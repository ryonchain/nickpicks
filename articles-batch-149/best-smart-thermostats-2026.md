## What to Know Before You Buy

**C-wire requirement:** Most smart thermostats require a C-wire (common wire) for continuous power. Check your current thermostat's wiring — if you see a wire labeled "C," you're good. If not, many models include adapters, or you can install a C-wire adapter (~$15) separately.

**Compatibility check:** Smart thermostats don't work with all HVAC systems. High-voltage systems (typically found in older homes with baseboard heating) require specialized thermostats. Use the manufacturer's compatibility checker before buying.

**Heat pump systems:** If you have a heat pump, verify the thermostat supports heat pumps with auxiliary/emergency heat. Ecobee and Nest both handle heat pumps well; some budget options don't.

**Utility rebates:** Many electric utilities offer $50–$150 rebates on ENERGY STAR smart thermostats. Check dsireusa.org or your utility's website before purchasing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much do smart thermostats actually save?**
The EPA estimates $145/year average savings. Savings depend on your current usage patterns — people who rarely adjust their thermostat see the most dramatic savings from auto-scheduling.

**Can I install a smart thermostat myself?**
Most smart thermostats are DIY-installable in 30–60 minutes with a screwdriver. The installation apps (Nest, Ecobee) walk you through wire-by-wire with photos. If you're uncomfortable with electrical work, HVAC technicians charge $75–$150 for installation.

**Do smart thermostats work without Wi-Fi?**
Yes — smart thermostats function as regular programmable thermostats without Wi-Fi. You lose remote control and learning features but maintain basic heating/cooling control.
