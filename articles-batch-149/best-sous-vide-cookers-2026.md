## Essential Sous Vide Accessories

**Vacuum sealer:** A FoodSaver FM2000 (~$60) or Anova Precision Vacuum Sealer (~$100) is the proper companion. Alternatively, use heavy-duty Ziploc freezer bags with the water displacement method for most proteins.

**Container:** A dedicated sous vide container (Rubbermaid 12-quart, ~$15, or LIPAVI 10L with rack, ~$30) beats a regular stockpot for large batches and keeping heat loss low.

**Container lid/cover:** Reduces evaporation on multi-hour cooks. Ping pong balls are the classic hack; fitted polycarbonate lids are the clean version.

**Cast iron pan:** For finishing/searing. Sous vide protein needs a hot sear after cooking — cast iron or stainless steel at maximum heat, 60–90 seconds per side.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is sous vide safe? Can I get botulism?**
Sous vide is extremely safe when practiced correctly. Pasteurization is achieved at lower temperatures held for longer — chicken at 140°F for 30 minutes is as safe as chicken at 165°F. The risk (anaerobic bacteria in vacuum-sealed bags) is only relevant if you hold food at unsafe temperatures, which sous vide eliminates.

**Can I use Ziploc bags instead of a vacuum sealer?**
Yes — for most proteins, heavy-duty Ziploc freezer bags work fine using the water displacement method to remove air. Vacuum sealing is better for long cooks (24+ hours) and for delicate fish.

**Why do I need to sear after sous vide?**
Sous vide doesn't produce browning (Maillard reaction) because it uses low temperatures. A 60–90 second sear in a very hot pan creates the crust and flavor compounds that make meat irresistible. Searing after (not before) sous vide is the correct order for best results.
