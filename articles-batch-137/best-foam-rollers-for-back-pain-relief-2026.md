## Foam Rolling for Back Pain: What the Research Says

### Thoracic Spine (Upper Back)
The most effective foam rolling for postural back pain targets the thoracic spine. Position the roller horizontally under your upper back, support your head with your hands, and gently extend over the roller at each thoracic segment. Hold each spot for 20–30 seconds. This is the #1 PT-recommended exercise for desk workers.

### Hip Flexors and Lower Back
Tight hip flexors (psoas, iliacus) cause anterior pelvic tilt that increases lumbar lordosis and lower back pain. Rolling the hip flexors in a prone position — roller under the front of the hip — relieves this chronic tightness better than most stretches.

### What Not to Roll
**Never roll directly on the lumbar spine (lower back).** Unlike the thoracic spine, which is supported by the ribcage, the lumbar vertebrae are free-floating and can be destabilized by rolling. Roll the glutes, piriformis, and thoracic spine instead; lower back tension usually releases as a downstream benefit.

### Density Guide
- **Soft (white foam):** Acute pain, physical therapy, beginners
- **Medium (standard EVA foam):** General use, most users
- **Firm (grid, knurled):** Performance users, myofascial work, regular rollers
- **Extra firm (black foam):** Advanced users with high pain tolerance; very targeted trigger point work

## FAQ

**Q: Can foam rolling fix back pain?**
A: Foam rolling can significantly reduce muscle tension that contributes to back pain, improve thoracic mobility, and help correct postural imbalances. It is not a treatment for structural causes of back pain (disc herniation, stenosis). Consult a physical therapist for diagnosis.

**Q: How often should I foam roll my back?**
A: Daily rolling for 5–10 minutes is safe and beneficial. Focus on thoracic spine and hip flexors. Avoid aggressive rolling on the same area two sessions in a row if it's very sore.

**Q: Will foam rolling make my back pain worse?**
A: Rolling over an acute injury or inflamed tissue can worsen pain. Start gentle (soft roller, light pressure) and stop if pain increases. For chronic muscle tension, temporary discomfort during rolling typically resolves to relief afterward.

**Q: How long does it take for foam rolling to help back pain?**
A: Many people feel immediate relief in thoracic mobility after a session. For chronic tightness, consistent daily rolling for 2–4 weeks typically produces meaningful improvement.
