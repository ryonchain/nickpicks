## Jump Rope Buying Guide

### Do You Need Bluetooth or Just a Counter?
- **Just a counter:** WOD Nation or Redify. Track current session reps. No phone, no app.
- **Bluetooth + app:** Crossrope, Tangram, DEGOL. Historical tracking, progress charts, structured workouts.
- **Workout library:** Crossrope is in a class of its own for structured jump rope programming.

### Speed Rope vs Weighted Rope
**Speed ropes** (thin cable): Best for speed, double-unders, competitive training. Faster rotation, harder to control for beginners.

**Weighted ropes** (thicker cable): Better cardio output, easier control for beginners, better for conditioning. Crossrope's system offers both via interchangeable cables.

### Rope Length
Set your rope length standing on the center of the rope: handles should reach your armpits. Most adjustable ropes allow easy trimming — cut once, permanent.

### What Cardio Benefits Does Jump Rope Provide?
Jump rope burns approximately 10–16 calories per minute at moderate intensity — comparable to running at a 7–8 minute mile pace. It also develops coordination, calf strength, foot speed, and cardiovascular fitness simultaneously.

## FAQ

**Q: How long should a beginner jump rope session be?**
A: Start with 1–2 minute intervals with rest periods. Build to 10 continuous minutes. Most intermediates aim for 15–30 minutes of actual jump time (broken into intervals with rest).

**Q: Can jumping rope replace running?**
A: For cardiovascular conditioning, jump rope provides comparable intensity. For distance running training specifically, they're complementary but not identical — running develops different muscles and movement patterns. For time-efficient cardio in small spaces, jump rope wins.

**Q: Is jump rope good for weight loss?**
A: Yes — it's one of the highest calorie-burn exercises available per minute. Combined with consistent tracking (a smart rope helps here), it's effective for creating a caloric deficit.

**Q: What surface should I jump rope on?**
A: Gym flooring, wood floors, rubber mats, or concrete outdoors. Avoid carpet (rope catches) and rough asphalt (cable wear). A 3/8" rubber mat under your feet protects joints and cables.
