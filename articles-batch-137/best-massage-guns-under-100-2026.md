## What to Look for in a Budget Massage Gun

### Amplitude: The Most Important Spec
Amplitude is how far the massage head travels per stroke. Budget guns under $50 often only offer 8–10mm; quality sub-$100 options deliver 12mm, which makes a genuine difference for deep muscle work. Don't buy a gun with less than 10mm amplitude.

### Stall Force: How Hard Can You Press?
Stall force determines how much pressure the motor can sustain before cutting out. Look for 40+ lbs for serious use. Under $40 guns typically offer 20–30 lbs — enough for surface work, not enough for dense glutes or hamstrings.

### Noise Level
Cheap motors run 70–80 dB — loud enough to interrupt conversation. Quality budget guns (Bob and Brad, RENPHO R4 Pro) run 45–55 dB. If noise matters, specifically check this spec.

### Attachments
At minimum: ball head (general use), flat head (dense muscles), fork head (spine-adjacent), and bullet head (trigger points). Most guns in this roundup include 5–6 heads.

## FAQ

**Q: Are budget massage guns as effective as Theragun?**
A: For light-to-moderate recovery work, quality budget guns (Bob and Brad C2 Pro, Ekrin B37S) deliver comparable results. Where premium guns shine is stall force and amplitude at high speeds — relevant for serious athletes doing high-volume training.

**Q: How long should I use a massage gun?**
A: 2 minutes per muscle group is the standard guideline. Keep the head moving; don't hold it stationary in one spot for more than 30 seconds.

**Q: Can I use a massage gun on my neck?**
A: Yes, on the neck muscles (trapezius, SCM), but avoid the cervical spine, bony prominences, and the carotid artery. Use a dampener head and low speed.

**Q: Is percussion therapy safe every day?**
A: Yes, for general muscle maintenance. Avoid using on acute injuries (fresh sprains, bruises, inflamed areas) or directly over joints.
