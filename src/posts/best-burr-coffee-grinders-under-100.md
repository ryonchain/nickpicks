## Why Burr vs. Blade

Blade grinders (the ones with a spinning metal blade like a blender) chop coffee into uneven particle sizes — some dust-fine, some boulder-coarse. Uneven particle size means uneven extraction: fine particles over-extract and go bitter while coarse particles under-extract and go sour. The combined result is both bitter AND sour — muddy, hollow coffee.

Burr grinders use two abrasive surfaces (burrs) that crush coffee beans between them at a calibrated distance. Every particle exits at the same size. Even extraction = cleaner, better-tasting coffee. The grind setting (the distance between burrs) determines particle size, which you adjust depending on brew method.

This is why coffee professionals universally say: upgrade the grinder before the brewer. A $30 burr grinder + $30 pour-over dripper beats a $200 drip machine + $20 blade grinder.

## Conical vs. Flat Burr: What's the Difference?

**Conical burr grinders** have a cone-shaped inner burr rotating inside a ring-shaped outer burr. They're more common, quieter, produce less heat during grinding, and tend to produce a slightly bimodal distribution (some larger and smaller particles mixed with the target size). The vast majority of home grinders under $150 use conical burrs.

**Flat burr grinders** have two parallel disc-shaped burrs facing each other. They produce a more uniform, unimodal particle distribution — all particles very close to the same size. This produces more clarity in the cup, especially at espresso grind. Flat burr grinders are more common in commercial and prosumer machines ($200+).

**For grinders under $100:** all options use conical burrs. Flat burr grinders at this price point exist but are generally lower quality. The conical vs. flat debate becomes meaningful above $200; under $100, focus on burr size, RPM, and adjustment consistency.

## Grind Setting Guide

Match grind size to your brew method — using the wrong grind size is the most common coffee mistake:

| Brew Method | Grind Size | Description |
|---|---|---|
| Turkish coffee | Extra fine | Flour-like powder |
| Espresso | Fine | Table salt texture |
| AeroPress (short steep) | Fine-medium | Slightly coarser than espresso |
| Pour-over (V60, Chemex) | Medium-fine | Granulated sugar texture |
| Drip coffee maker | Medium | Coarse sand texture |
| French press | Coarse | Sea salt texture |
| Cold brew | Extra coarse | Rock salt texture |

Start coarser than you think you need, then adjust finer if the coffee tastes weak or sour.

---

## What to Look For

**Burr size:** Larger burrs (40mm+) produce more consistent results and generate less heat. Budget grinders often have 38–40mm burrs; premium grinders run 50–64mm.

**RPM:** Lower RPM means less heat and better preservation of volatile compounds. Budget electric grinders run 450–600 RPM; professional grinders run 300 RPM or less.

**Grind setting adjustability:** More settings = finer control. 15+ settings is a minimum for versatility. Look for labeled or numbered settings rather than vague "coarse to fine" dials.

**Hopper size:** If you make multiple cups daily, a 0.5 lb+ hopper saves constant refilling. If you grind fresh for each cup, smaller is fine.
