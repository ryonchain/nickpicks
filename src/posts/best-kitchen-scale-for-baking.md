## Buying Guide: What Matters in a Baking Scale

### Accuracy and Increment Size
For baking, **1-gram increments** are the minimum standard. Coarser scales (2g or 5g increments) introduce meaningful errors in small-batch baking — a 5g error in yeast quantity changes your entire bread timeline.

Scales priced above ~$20 are generally accurate to 1g. Under $15, you'll see more drift at low weights.

### Maximum Capacity
Most home baking scales cap at 5 kg (11 lbs). That's sufficient for:
- Flour for a 2-loaf bread batch (~1.2 kg)
- Weighing dough for portion control
- Full cake recipe ingredients

For commercial baking or large batch prep, look for scales with 10–15 kg capacity.

### Display Visibility
The biggest real-world frustration with kitchen scales is a bowl blocking the display. The **OXO pull-out display** solves this elegantly. Backlit displays (like the KD-8000) help in dim kitchens.

### Platform Material
**Stainless steel** platforms clean more thoroughly and resist odors better than plastic. Worth paying a few extra dollars for if you bake with oils, fats, or sticky ingredients.

### Tare Function
All modern scales have a tare button. The key thing to check: does the tare button stay responsive when you're mid-recipe, adding ingredient after ingredient? Some cheaper models lose tare calibration after multiple uses in one session.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I really need a kitchen scale for baking?**
For bread, sourdough, croissants, macarons, and any recipe requiring precision — yes. A cup of flour measured by volume can range from 120g to 160g depending on how you scoop. That 33% variance directly affects the texture of your baked goods. For general cooking (soups, stews), a scale is nice to have but not essential.

**What's the difference between a kitchen scale and a postal scale?**
Postal scales are optimized for small weights (under 1 lb) with high precision. Kitchen scales handle larger weights up to 5–15 kg. For baking, a kitchen scale with 1g increments is what you want — postal scales run out of capacity quickly.

**How do I know if my scale is accurate?**
A nickel weighs exactly 5 grams — a useful calibration reference. Place a nickel on your scale; if it reads 5g, you're calibrated. For precision, a calibration weight set is inexpensive ($8–$15 on Amazon).

**How long do batteries last?**
Most digital scales use 2 AAA batteries that last 1–2 years with regular home use. Auto-off features (typically 3–5 minutes of inactivity) extend battery life significantly.

**Can kitchen scales measure liquids?**
Yes — in weight, not volume. Water is easy (1ml = 1g), but other liquids have different densities. Most bakers still measure liquids by volume (ml or cups) unless the recipe specifies weight.
