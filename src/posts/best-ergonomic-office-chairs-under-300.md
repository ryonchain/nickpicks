## What Makes a Chair Actually Ergonomic

Many chairs use "ergonomic" as a marketing word that means nothing. These are the adjustments and features that actually matter:

**Lumbar support:** Your lower back has a natural inward curve (lordosis). When you sit without lumbar support, this curve reverses as your lower back rounds forward. A lumbar support cushion or adjustable lumbar push keeps the curve in its natural position. Without it, you're damaging your spine slowly.

**Seat depth:** The seat pan should allow you to sit with your back against the backrest while leaving 2–4 finger-widths of space between the front of the seat and the back of your knees. Seats too deep force you to sit forward away from the backrest. Seat depth adjustment (or a seat with a sliding pan) matters more than most people realize.

**Seat height:** Your feet should rest flat on the floor with your knees at or slightly below hip height. Height adjustment range (typically 16–21 inches) should accommodate your height.

**Armrests:** Should be adjustable in height and ideally in width. Your shoulders should be relaxed (not raised) and forearms roughly parallel to the desk when typing. Fixed armrests that don't match your desk height are often worse than no armrests.

**Backrest adjustability:** Tilt and recline let you shift position throughout the day — movement is healthy. Lockable tilt at a slight recline (100–110°) reduces spinal compression compared to sitting bolt-upright at 90°.

## Chair Setup: Getting It Right After Assembly

Most people adjust their chair once and forget it. The right setup:

1. **Seat height:** Feet flat on floor, knees at or slightly below hip height. If your feet don't reach the floor, use a footrest.
2. **Seat depth:** Sit with back against backrest — 2-3 finger widths should fit between front edge of seat and back of knees.
3. **Lumbar support position:** Should press into your lower back at the natural inward curve, typically 2–4 inches above the seat.
4. **Armrests:** Adjust so your shoulders are relaxed (not raised) and forearms are roughly parallel to the desk.
5. **Monitor height:** With your chair properly set, your monitor should be at eye height without neck strain.

## FAQ

**Q: Is a $300 ergonomic chair worth it vs. a $100 chair?**
A: For people who sit 6-8+ hours per day, yes. The adjustability and build quality of a proper ergonomic chair makes a real difference in comfort, posture, and long-term back health. If you're sitting 2 hours a day, it matters less.

**Q: How long does a good office chair last?**
A: Commercial-grade chairs (HON, Steelcase, Herman Miller) last 10-15 years. Consumer-grade chairs in the $200-300 range typically last 3-7 years depending on use. Mesh backs tend to outlast foam.

**Q: Do I need a headrest?**
A: Headrests are useful for leaning back in a reclined position, not for sitting upright at a desk. Most people don't actually use them while actively working. They're a nice-to-have, not a must-have.
