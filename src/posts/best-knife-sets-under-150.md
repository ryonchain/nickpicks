## Buying Guide

### Forged vs. Stamped Knives
**Forged:** The blade is hammered from one piece of hot steel. Heavier, better balanced, typically holds an edge longer. Standard in German-style knives (Wüsthof, Henckels).

**Stamped:** Cut from a steel sheet and heat-treated. Lighter, less expensive. Can still be excellent — Victorinox Fibrox blades are stamped and outperform many forged knives.

For most home cooks, both styles work well. If you prefer lighter Japanese-style knives, stamped is often the right choice.

### German vs. Japanese Steel
**German steel** (HRC 56–58): Softer, more flexible, easier to sharpen at home with a honing rod. Edges are typically at 20–22 degrees per side.

**Japanese steel** (HRC 60–65): Harder, holds an edge longer, but more brittle. Edges at 10–15 degrees per side — razor sharp but more prone to chipping on hard bones.

For a first set bought by a non-chef, German steel is more forgiving.

### What Not to Buy
Avoid sets that compete primarily on piece count. A 20-piece set for $40 with unknown-brand steel will be frustrating and will require constant sharpening. Better to spend $100 on a Victorinox 8-piece than $40 on a 20-piece set.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I sharpen kitchen knives?**
Hone (with a honing rod) before most uses to realign the edge. True sharpening (removing metal) depends on use — for home cooks, 1–2 times per year is typical. Professional cooks sharpen more frequently.

**Can I put knife sets in the dishwasher?**
Most sets say dishwasher safe, but hand washing is strongly recommended. Dishwasher heat causes micro-corrosion, loosens rivets over time, and dulls edges faster. A 30-second hand wash after use extends knife life significantly.

**Is a knife block necessary?**
No. Magnetic knife strips are space-efficient and keep blades in better condition (knives aren't rattling against each other). Drawer inserts also work. Block sets are convenient if you have counter space.

**What's the difference between a chef's knife and a santoku?**
Chef's knives have a pointed tip and a curved belly for rocking cuts. Santoku knives are flat-edged with a sheepsfoot tip — better for push cuts and slicing vegetables. Many sets include both; if you can only have one, choose the chef's knife for versatility.

**Do I need a sharpening steel or a sharpening stone?**
A honing steel realigns a dulled edge — use it often. A sharpening stone (whetstone) actually removes metal and resharpens. For home cooks, a honing steel for weekly maintenance plus a professional sharpening once a year is the right workflow.
