## HDD vs. SSD for Backup: Which Should You Buy?

**External HDD (Hard Disk Drive):**
- Much lower cost per gigabyte (~$20–25 per TB)
- Higher capacities available (up to 20TB in portable/desktop form)
- Slower transfer speeds (USB 3.0 HDD: 80–130 MB/s)
- More vulnerable to physical shock when moving
- Best for: large backups (photo libraries, full system backups), desktop use

**External SSD (Solid State Drive):**
- Higher cost per gigabyte (~$80–120 per TB)
- Faster transfer speeds (USB 3.2 Gen2 SSD: 500–2000 MB/s)
- Shock-resistant, compact, durable
- Best for: frequent access, carrying in a bag, fast system backups

**The practical split:** Use an external SSD for your primary work backup (fast, portable, durable) and an HDD for archival storage of large media files where speed isn't critical.

## Backup Methods: What These Drives Should Do

A backup drive is only useful if it's actually used. The 3-2-1 backup rule:
- **3 copies** of your data
- **2 different storage media**
- **1 offsite** (cloud, remote location, or at another physical site)

For home office workers: your local files, plus an external drive backup, plus cloud storage (Google Drive, iCloud, Backblaze) covers the 3-2-1 rule reasonably well.

## Encryption: When It Matters for Backup

**Hardware encryption** (built into the drive firmware):
- Password required to access data even if drive is physically stolen
- No performance penalty
- AES 256-bit standard is considered unbreakable with current technology

**Software encryption** (via OS-level tools like BitLocker, FileVault, or VeraCrypt):
- Works with any drive
- Requires setup but is free
- Good option if your drive doesn't include hardware encryption

For most home office workers, software encryption via your operating system is sufficient. If you frequently carry a backup drive with sensitive client files or financial records, a drive with hardware encryption (or software encryption enabled) is worth the extra step.

## FAQ

**Q: How often should I back up?**
A: For important work files, daily or continuous backup is ideal. Weekly is acceptable for most home office use cases. Monthly is not enough — you'll lose a month of work if your drive fails.

**Q: Does an external SSD last longer than an HDD?**
A: SSDs have no moving parts, so they're more shock-resistant in the short term. For long-term archival storage in a drawer, HDDs can retain data for longer periods without being powered. Both can fail. Don't rely on a single backup drive regardless of type.

**Q: What's the difference between USB 3.0, 3.1, and 3.2?**
A: USB 3.0 = USB 3.2 Gen1 = 5 Gbps max. USB 3.1 Gen2 = USB 3.2 Gen2 = 10 Gbps max. USB 3.2 Gen2x2 = 20 Gbps. For SSDs, Gen2 or better is needed to take advantage of fast drive speeds. HDDs max out at USB 3.0 speeds anyway.
