## The Big Picture: Does Expensive Cast Iron Cook Better?

Cast iron's primary advantages — heat retention, even cooking, oven-safe versatility, and 50-year longevity — are properties of the material, not the brand. A $30 Lodge maintains heat as effectively as a $300 Le Creuset once at temperature.

Where premium brands differ:
- **Surface smoothness:** Cheaper cast iron has a rougher pebbly texture from sand casting. Premium brands machine the surface for a smoother cooking surface that's closer to nonstick from day one.
- **Weight:** Lodge is heavier than machined alternatives. The Field Company and Stargazer shave 10–20% off Lodge's weight.
- **Handle design:** Premium options have longer handles, helper handles, and better ergonomics.
- **Aesthetics:** Le Creuset enamel comes in colors. Finex has a distinctive octagonal shape. These matter if the pan goes from stove to table.

## Buying Guide

### Size Guide
- **8":** For one person, eggs, small pancakes, single servings
- **10–10.25":** The most versatile size — 2–4 eggs, a chicken breast, a full cornbread batch
- **12":** For families, searing multiple steaks, large sautés, oven frittatas
- **12"+:** Serious cooks, large batch cooking, skillet bread

### Bare Cast Iron vs Enamel Cast Iron
**Bare cast iron:** Requires seasoning, can't use with highly acidic ingredients long-term, can develop rust if left wet. In exchange: better high-heat searing, more nonstick over time with proper seasoning, completely restorable.

**Enamel cast iron:** No seasoning, acid-safe, doesn't rust, easier cleaning. In exchange: can't sear at maximum heat, can chip, cannot be restored from damage.

### Weight Considerations
A 10" cast iron weighs 4.5–5.5 lbs. If you're cooking daily or transferring from stovetop to oven frequently, the 0.5–1 lb difference between Lodge and Field Company becomes meaningful over months of use.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to season cast iron before first use?**
If it's pre-seasoned (Lodge and most modern bare cast iron), cook something fatty (bacon, fried chicken) in it for the first few uses. No full oven seasoning required unless the pan arrives with rust or uneven coating.

**How do I clean cast iron?**
Rinse with warm water while still warm and wipe with a towel. Mild soap is acceptable occasionally (the "no soap ever" rule is outdated — modern dish soap won't strip seasoning). Dry immediately and apply a very thin oil coat before storage.

**Why does my cast iron rust?**
Water left on bare iron creates rust. Dry thoroughly after washing and store in a dry place. Surface rust isn't a disaster — scrub with a chain mail scrubber, reseason, and you're back.

**Can I use cast iron on induction cooktops?**
Yes. Cast iron is magnetic and works on induction. All the pans in this guide are induction compatible.

**Is cast iron actually better than stainless steel for searing?**
Cast iron's high heat retention makes it excellent for searing proteins — it doesn't lose temperature as quickly when cold meat hits the pan. Stainless steel heats more evenly from the burner; cast iron stores and delivers more total heat once at temperature. Both achieve good sears; cast iron has a slight edge for thick steaks and crusty braises.
