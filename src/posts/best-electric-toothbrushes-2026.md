## Buying Guide: Oscillating vs. Sonic

### Oscillating-Rotating (Oral-B)
The brush head rotates while pulsing. Most clinical studies are based on this technology. The oscillating action directly scrubs plaque off tooth surfaces. Generally preferred by dentists.

### Sonic (Sonicare, Colgate)
The head vibrates at high frequency (30,000–62,000 strokes/minute). Sonic waves create fluid dynamics that disrupt plaque even in areas the brush doesn't directly touch — including between teeth. Generally gentler on gums and dental work. Some people find the sensation more comfortable.

**Bottom line:** Both work well. Choose oscillating if you want the most clinical evidence. Choose sonic if you have sensitive gums or prefer the feel.

### Key Features to Look For
- **Pressure sensor:** Protects against brushing too hard (the most common cause of enamel wear)
- **2-minute timer:** ADA-recommended brushing duration
- **Quadrant timer:** Prompts every 30 seconds to move sections
- **Battery life:** Two weeks minimum; 30 days is better

### Replacement Heads
Electric toothbrush heads should be replaced every 3 months (same as manual brushes). Oral-B and Sonicare replacement heads are widely available at pharmacies. iO heads cost more. Factor this into long-term cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How much better is an electric toothbrush than a manual one?**
Studies consistently show electric toothbrushes remove 21% more plaque and reduce gingivitis 11% more than manual brushes. The bigger advantage is consistency — an electric brush does the work for you, so technique matters less.

**How long should I brush with an electric toothbrush?**
Two minutes, twice a day. Most electric toothbrushes have built-in timers. Many apps track this over time. Two minutes is the ADA standard — most people brush for under 45 seconds manually.

**Are electric toothbrushes safe for braces?**
Yes. Oral-B and Sonicare both make orthodontic brush heads designed for brackets and wires. Soft sonic vibrations can actually help clean around brackets more effectively than manual brushing.

**How do I charge an electric toothbrush?**
Most models use an inductive charging stand (no direct plug into the brush). Place the handle on the stand and leave it. Most need charging once every 1–2 weeks. Don't leave on charge constantly — use it until the battery is low, then recharge.

**Are expensive electric toothbrush models worth the price?**
The premium (smart AI coaching) models add motivation and technique feedback — genuinely useful for people who want to improve brushing habits. But the Pro 1000 at $50 outperforms even expensive smart toothbrushes in the fundamental task: plaque removal. Buy the smart features for the coaching, not because the cleaning is dramatically better.
