## How Cold Brew Works

Cold brew is simple: coarse-ground coffee steeped in cold or room-temperature water for 12–24 hours. The slow, cold extraction pulls out coffee's sweeter, low-acid compounds while leaving behind the bitter oils that hot water extracts aggressively.

The result is a highly concentrated liquid (typically 2:1 coffee-to-water ratio) that you dilute before drinking — usually 1:1 with water or milk. Cold brew concentrate keeps in the refrigerator for up to 2 weeks, making it ideal for batch-brewing.

**Key variables:**

- **Steep time:** 12 hours gives a lighter, brighter cup. 18–24 hours produces richer, more chocolatey concentrate. Most people find 16–18 hours optimal.
- **Grind size:** Coarse grind (like sea salt) is critical. Finer grounds over-extract, go bitter, and clog filters.
- **Ratio:** A common starting point is 1 cup ground coffee to 4 cups water for concentrate. Adjust to taste.
- **Filter type:** Paper filters (like OXO) give a cleaner, clearer cup. Cloth filters (like Toddy) let more oils through for a slightly richer body.

## Cold Brew Tips

**Use coarse grind:** The most common cold brew mistake is grinding too fine. Aim for a consistency like coarse sea salt — roughly what you'd use for a French press.

**Steep at room temp for speed:** Room-temperature brewing cuts steep time to 12–14 hours vs. 18–24 hours in the fridge. The fridge method produces slightly cleaner flavor; room-temp is faster and still excellent.

**Dilute before drinking:** Cold brew concentrate is 2–3x the strength of regular coffee. Dilute 1:1 with water or milk, or more to taste. Drinking it undiluted will be intensely strong.

**Clean immediately after use:** Coffee oils go rancid quickly. Rinse all components immediately after use — don't let them sit.
