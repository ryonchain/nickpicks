## Why Get a Pressure Cooker?

Electric pressure cookers (including Instant Pot) dramatically reduce cooking time by trapping steam and building internal pressure, which raises the boiling point of water to around 250°F instead of 212°F. This accelerated cooking is particularly useful for:

- Dried beans (45 min vs. 2 hours on stove)
- Tough cuts of meat (chuck roast: 60 min vs. 3+ hours)
- Whole chicken (25 min)
- Stock and broth (45 min vs. 3–8 hours)
- Risotto (6 min under pressure + natural release)

Most electric pressure cookers also function as slow cookers, rice cookers, steamers, sauté pans, and yogurt makers — consolidating multiple appliances into one.

## Size Guide: Which Quart Size Is Right for You?

| Size | Best For | Capacity (food) |
|---|---|---|
| 3-quart Mini | 1–2 people, small kitchens, side dishes, smaller meals | 2–3 lbs meat, 4–6 cups |
| 6-quart | 3–6 people, most households, the "standard" size | 4–6 lbs meat, 6–8 cups |
| 8-quart | 6+ people, batch cooking, large roasts, big families | 6–8 lbs meat, 8–12 cups |

**The 6-quart is right for most people.** It handles family meals, has the best accessory availability, and the recipes found online are almost universally written for 6-quart sizing.

**Get the 8-quart if:** You regularly cook for 6+ people, frequently make stock (needs headroom), or batch cook for the week.

**Get the 3-quart if:** You're cooking for 1–2 people, have very limited counter or storage space, or only need it for side dishes.

## Common Instant Pot Mistakes to Avoid

**Not adding enough liquid:** Pressure cookers require at least 1 cup of liquid to build steam. Most recipes specify this, but it's the #1 beginner mistake.

**Overfilling:** Never fill above the 2/3 line (or 1/2 line for foods that expand — beans, grains). Overfilling prevents proper pressurization and can cause the valve to clog.

**Opening the lid before releasing pressure:** Always release pressure (quick release or natural release) before opening. The lid locks when under pressure — do not force it.

**Quick release vs. natural release:** Quick release (turning the valve immediately) is fine for most dishes. Natural release (letting pressure drop on its own over 10–20 minutes) is better for large roasts, beans, and dishes that can become tough with rapid pressure changes.
