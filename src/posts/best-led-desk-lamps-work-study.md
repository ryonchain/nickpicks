## Color Temperature: The Most Important Spec

Measured in Kelvin (K), color temperature determines whether your light looks warm, neutral, or cool:

- **2700–3000K (warm white):** Amber-toned light, similar to incandescent bulbs. Good for evening reading and winding down. Too yellow for focused work.
- **4000–4500K (neutral white):** Clean, natural light similar to daylight indoors. The sweet spot for focused work and studying. Reduces eye fatigue without being harsh.
- **5000–6500K (cool white/daylight):** Blue-shifted light. High alertness, good for early morning productivity. Can feel harsh for long sessions and may disrupt sleep if used in the evening.

**Best for most people:** A lamp with adjustable color temperature (warm to cool) lets you shift based on time of day and task.

## Clamp vs. Base: Which Style Is Right for You?

**Base lamp:** Sits on the desk surface. Stable, easy to position, no desk edge required. Takes up desk footprint. Good for most standard setups.

**Clamp lamp:** Attaches to the desk edge or monitor. Saves desk surface space. Lets you position the light directly above the work area. Requires a clampable desk edge (usually up to 1.5–2 inches thick).

**Monitor bar lamp:** Hangs on top of your monitor screen and shines downward onto your desk without creating glare in the lens. Increasingly popular for home office setups.

## Positioning Tips for Better Lighting

**Where to place your lamp:**
- Position the light to your non-dominant side to avoid casting a shadow when writing
- Aim for the work surface, not your face — direct light in your eyes causes more fatigue than helps
- Keep the lamp at a similar brightness to your monitor to reduce contrast fatigue

**Color temperature guidance by time of day:**
- **Morning (work start):** 4500–5000K — alerting, matches daylight
- **Afternoon (deep work):** 4000–4500K — focused but sustainable
- **Evening (reading, low-intensity tasks):** 3000–3500K — warmer, easier on eyes

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need a CRI rating above 90?**
A: For most work and study, CRI 80+ is fine. CRI 90+ matters more for tasks where accurate color rendering is important — art, design, product photography, or detailed crafting.

**Q: Is LED better than fluorescent for studying?**
A: Yes, significantly. Modern LED lamps are flicker-free (fluorescent has a micro-flicker that causes eye fatigue), more energy efficient, and offer adjustable color temperature. There's no reason to use fluorescent for desk lighting in 2026.

**Q: What wattage do I need?**
A: For a desk lamp, 6–12 watts LED is plenty for the work surface. More relevant than wattage is lumens (light output) and spread — look for 400–800 lumens with a wide spread.
