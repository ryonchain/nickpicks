## Compression Levels: What You Need

**15–20 mmHg (mild compression):**
- Best for: Travel, light activity, minor fatigue, mild swelling
- Who: Desk workers, travelers, casual athletes
- Feel: Light squeeze — noticeable but not constrictive

**20–30 mmHg (moderate compression):**
- Best for: Running, athletics, all-day standing, moderate edema, post-surgical
- Who: Runners, nurses, teachers, construction workers
- Feel: Firm — meaningful support that you'll notice on and off
- This is the most common athletic compression level

**30–40 mmHg (firm medical compression):**
- Best for: Deep vein thrombosis prevention, lymphedema, serious circulatory conditions
- Who: Medical patients — requires healthcare provider guidance
- Note: Consult a doctor before using 30–40 mmHg

For most athletic and travel use: **20–30 mmHg**.

## Sizing and Fit Tips

Compression socks MUST fit properly — an oversized compression sock provides little benefit; an undersized one causes discomfort and restricted circulation.

**Measure:** Calf circumference at widest point, ankle circumference at narrowest point, foot length.

**Size ranges:** Most brands use S/M/L/XL but some (especially European brands like CEP) use numerical sizes based on calf circumference. Always check the brand's sizing chart.

**Long vs. crew height:** Over-the-calf (long) provides calf compression. Crew height (shorter) is for foot and ankle only. For running recovery and blood clot prevention, use over-the-calf.
