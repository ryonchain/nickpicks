## Paddle Length: The First Decision

Paddle length depends on two factors: your height and your kayak's width.

| Kayak Width | Under 5'5" | 5'5"–5'11" | 6'0"–6'4" | Over 6'4" |

| Over 33" | 230 cm | 230 cm | 235 cm | 240 cm |

Most recreational sit-on-top kayaks (28"–32" wide) pair best with a 230 cm paddle for average-height paddlers.

---

## Blade Shape and Feather Angle

**High-angle paddling:** Vertical paddle shaft with short, wide blades. Efficient for power and fast sprints. Used by sea kayakers and racers.

**Low-angle paddling:** More horizontal shaft with longer, narrower blades. Less tiring for long-distance paddling. Best for recreational and touring kayakers.

**Feather angle:** The offset between the two blades, measured in degrees. Most modern paddles let you adjust feather from 0° (unfeathered) to 45° or 60°. Beginners: start at 0° and experiment. Advanced paddlers in wind: 45°–60° reduces wind resistance on the recovery blade.

---

## Blade and Shaft Materials

**Aluminum shafts/plastic blades (~$40–$80):** Heavy (32–40 oz), durable, and inexpensive. Aluminum gets cold in cold water. Fine for casual paddlers who use their kayak 5–10 times per year.

**Fiberglass blades (~$80–$200):** Significantly lighter than plastic. Better power transfer, smoother catch and exit. For paddlers who get out 10+ times per year, this is the sweet spot.

**Carbon fiber ($200–$400+):** Lightest option (18–22 oz vs. aluminum's 35+ oz). The weight difference over a 5-mile paddle is enormous. Best for touring, fitness paddlers, and sea kayakers.
