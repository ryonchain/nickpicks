## What's Realistic Under $50

A $50 pickleball paddle won't have the same precision craftsmanship as a $100+ paddle, but it should have:
- A composite (fiberglass) or graphite face — not cheap plastic
- A polymer honeycomb core — not aluminum or foam
- Proper grip size and cushioning
- USAPA-legal dimensions (if you care about tournament play)

What you sacrifice at this price: edge guard quality, handle balance refinement, and long-term durability under hard use. Recreational players who play 1–3 times per week rarely notice these trade-offs.

## Paddles to Avoid Under $50

**Wooden paddles:** Still sold in bargain bins and kids' toy sections. Heavy (15+ oz), no feel, damages balls faster, and punishes the arm on off-center hits. Not suitable for actual pickleball play.

**No-name paddles with no reviews:** A composite/graphite label doesn't guarantee actual composite or graphite. Stick with brands that have 100+ reviews and demonstrated product consistency.

**Overly heavy budget paddles (9+ oz):** Some cheap paddles are heavy because heavy = feels substantial to buyers but the weight isn't distributed for performance. Quality paddles achieve performance at 7.5–8.5 oz.

## When to Upgrade

Budget paddles make sense until:
- You're playing 3+ times per week regularly
- You're playing competitive ladder leagues or tournaments
- You notice your paddle face delaminating (layers separating)
- You want more spin, better dink control, or faster pop

At that point, invest in a $70–$100 mid-range paddle (Selkirk Latitude, Paddletek Bantam EX-L) — the difference in performance is immediately noticeable.
