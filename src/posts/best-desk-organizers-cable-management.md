## The Three Zones of Desk Organization

**Zone 1 — On the desk surface:** Only what you use every day should be here. Monitor, keyboard, mouse, a notepad if you use it. Everything else is clutter.

**Zone 2 — Under the desk:** Cable runs, power strips, and anything that needs to exist but doesn't need to be visible. This is where cable management trays and boxes do their work.

**Zone 3 — Off-desk storage:** Reference materials, supplies, and items you access weekly (not daily). Drawer organizers and desktop caddies for things that genuinely need to live on or near the desk.

Cable management almost always should target Zone 2. Getting power strips, excess cable slack, and adapters off your desk surface eliminates 80% of the visual clutter in most home offices.

## Building a System: The Order of Operations

1. **Start under the desk:** Mount a cable tray or cable box to hide your power strip. This eliminates the biggest source of visible cable chaos immediately.
2. **Route cables along desk edges:** Use adhesive cable clips along the back of your desk to keep cables in the same run rather than dangling freely.
3. **Bundle remaining desk cables:** Use Velcro ties to bundle any cables that share a path (monitor cable + lamp cable along the monitor arm, for example).
4. **Handle surface clutter:** Once cables are managed, address desk surface clutter with a desk organizer for small items.

## FAQ

**Q: What's the difference between cable management and cable hiding?**
A: Cable management structures your cables so they're organized and routable. Cable hiding conceals them from view. Good solutions do both — they route cables cleanly and hide them behind trays, raceways, or boxes.

**Q: Are Velcro ties or zip ties better for desk cable management?**
A: Velcro for anything you might need to change (desk setup, adding/removing devices). Zip ties for permanent bundling in inaccessible locations. For desk use, always Velcro.

**Q: Can I put a power strip inside a cable box safely?**
A: Yes, but ensure the box has adequate ventilation and that you don't overload the circuit. Cable boxes are designed for this purpose.
