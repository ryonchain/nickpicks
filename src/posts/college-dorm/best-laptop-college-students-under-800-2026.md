## What Actually Matters for a College Laptop

### Battery Life (Non-Negotiable)

Campus life means 8–10 hours away from an outlet. A laptop that needs charging by 2 PM fails the most basic college use case. Real-world battery life (not manufacturer claims) should be 8 hours minimum — 10–14 hours is ideal.

ARM-based chips (Apple M4, Snapdragon X) lead here. Intel and AMD have closed the gap significantly in their latest generations but still trail.

### Weight

You'll carry this laptop in a backpack to multiple classes every day. The difference between a 2.8 lb laptop and a 4.5 lb laptop becomes very real around week three of the semester. Under 3.5 lbs is the target.

### Display

A 13–15 inch display is the college sweet spot. Smaller feels cramped for long reading; larger is heavy and unwieldy. IPS or OLED panels with 300+ nits brightness are essential if you're ever outside or in a bright lecture hall.

### Performance by Major

- **Liberal Arts, Business, Pre-Med:** Any modern i5/Ryzen 5 with 8–16GB RAM handles Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Zoom, and browser tabs.
- **CS / Engineering:** 16GB RAM minimum, discrete GPU if running simulations or ML work.
- **Design / Architecture:** Look for higher color accuracy (100% sRGB or DCI-P3) and dedicated GPU.
- **Video / Music Production:** High RAM (16–32GB), fast SSD, and processing power matter more than portability.

## What Specs to Prioritize (and Ignore)

**Prioritize:**
- RAM: 16GB minimum for a laptop you'll use for 4 years. 8GB feels the pinch by junior year.
- SSD: 512GB minimum — most engineering and design programs fill up 256GB fast
- Battery: Real-world, not spec-sheet
- Display brightness: 300 nits minimum, 400+ nits for outdoor use

**Don't overpay for:**
- Processor generation numbers (i7 vs. i9 for typical coursework)
- Touch screens (unless you're a 2-in-1 user)
- Thunderbolt 4 ports vs. USB-C (won't matter for most students)
- Storage beyond 1TB (external drives are cheap)
