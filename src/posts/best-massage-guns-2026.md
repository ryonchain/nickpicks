## Buying Guide: Key Specs Explained

### Amplitude
The single most important spec: how deep the percussion head travels. More amplitude = deeper muscle penetration.
- **10 mm:** Surface relief, blood flow improvement
- **12 mm:** Adequate for most muscle groups
- **16 mm:** Deep tissue equivalent, reaches dense muscle layers

### Stall Force
How much pressure the gun can maintain before the motor stalls. Higher stall force means you can apply more body pressure without slowing the device. Theragun: 60 lbs stall force. Budget devices: 20–30 lbs. Relevant for dense muscle groups (glutes, quads).

### Noise Level
- **Under 50 dB:** Quiet — similar to normal conversation
- **50–65 dB:** Moderate — audible but not disruptive
- **65+ dB:** Loud — noticeable in quiet environments

### Attachments
Most guns include:
- **Ball:** General use, large muscle groups
- **Flat:** Dense muscles (quads, glutes)
- **Bullet/cone:** Trigger points, small areas
- **Fork:** Spine-adjacent muscles, Achilles
- **Thumb:** Lower back, pressure points

### Battery Life
2–6 hours per charge is typical. For most users (10–20 minutes per session), 2-hour battery lasts weeks before needing a charge. Longer battery matters most for physical therapists, athletes, or family sharing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do massage guns actually speed muscle recovery?**
Research supports percussion therapy for reducing delayed-onset muscle soreness (DOMS) and improving range of motion pre-workout. Most studies use 5–10 minute sessions. Effects are meaningful but modest — it's a recovery tool, not a replacement for sleep, nutrition, and rest.

**How long should I use a massage gun?**
Per muscle group: 1–2 minutes at 30–40% device pressure. Per session: 10–15 minutes total. Over-use on a single area causes increased soreness, not reduced. Start with 30 seconds per muscle group and adjust.

**Where should I NOT use a massage gun?**
Avoid: bony areas (spine, joints), bruised or injured tissue, open wounds, inflamed areas, varicose veins, lymph nodes. Use only on muscle belly — the meaty part, not tendons or bones.

**Is a $40 massage gun as good as a $230 Theragun?**
No. The difference is real: amplitude depth, motor power, and durability. But for light use (post-workout soreness in non-competitive adults), a budget gun provides meaningful benefit at a fraction of the cost. Serious athletes, physical therapy patients, or daily heavy users benefit most from premium devices.

**How do I know which attachment to use?**
Ball: Default choice for large muscles (quads, hamstrings, glutes, back). Flat: Dense muscles or firm pressure applications. Bullet: Trigger points, knots, small precise areas. Fork: Running the gun alongside the spine or Achilles tendon. When in doubt, use the ball.
