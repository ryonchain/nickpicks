## H2: Understanding Sleeping Bag Temperature Ratings

Sleeping bags use the EN/ISO 13537 standard to report temperature performance. This standard defines three ratings:

- **Upper Comfort Limit** — the warmest temperature a standard woman can sleep without overheating
- **Comfort (Lower Limit)** — the temperature at which a standard woman can sleep comfortably
- **Lower Limit (Transition)** — the temperature at which a standard man can sleep in a curled position without waking cold
- **Extreme** — survival limit; not a comfort rating

**Practical guide:** If a bag is rated to 20°F, a cold sleeper might be cold at 30°F while a warm sleeper is comfortable at 10°F. Most manufacturers recommend buying a bag 10°F colder than the lowest temperature you expect to encounter.

## H2: Down vs. Synthetic Sleeping Bag Insulation

**Down insulation** is the gold standard for warmth-to-weight ratio. Fill power measures loft — 550-fill is good, 700-fill is great, 850-fill is premium. The higher the fill power, the lighter and more packable the bag for the same warmth.

**Limitation:** Down loses insulating power when wet. Down bags require careful management in wet conditions. Hydrophobic down treatments (DownTek, Nikwax) improve wet performance significantly.

**Synthetic insulation** retains most of its warmth when wet. Dries faster than down. Heavier and less packable than equivalent-warmth down. Best for humid climates, kayaking trips, and situations where your bag might get wet.

**Verdict:** Dry climates → down. Wet climates or wet trips → synthetic. Most US western mountain camping → down is fine.

## H2: Sleeping Bag Accessories That Improve Warmth

**Sleeping bag liner** — adds 10–25°F of warmth to any bag. A silk liner adds 15°F and weighs 4 oz. Also keeps your bag cleaner between washes.

**Sleeping pad** — critical for insulation from ground cold. A R-value 3 pad for summer, R-4 for 3-season, R-5+ for winter. Your sleeping bag's temperature rating assumes you're on an insulating pad.

**Dry bag** — keep your sleeping bag protected in a dry bag inside your pack. A wet sleeping bag in an emergency situation is dangerous.

## FAQ

**Q: What sleeping bag temperature rating do I need?**
A: Match your bag rating to the coldest conditions you'll encounter, minus 10°F buffer. Summer camping in mountains (40°F nights) → 30°F bag. Spring/fall shoulder season (25°F nights) → 15°F bag.

**Q: How do I wash a down sleeping bag?**
A: Use a front-loading washer (never top-loading agitators) with down-specific soap (Nikwax Down Wash). Dry on low heat with dryer balls to break up clumps. Takes 2–3 dryer cycles to fully dry. Improper washing is the most common way to damage a down bag.

**Q: How long does a sleeping bag last?**
A: A quality down bag properly maintained lasts 15–20 years. Synthetic bags degrade faster — 5–10 years of regular use. Store both bags loosely in a large cotton storage sack, never compressed in the stuff sack.
