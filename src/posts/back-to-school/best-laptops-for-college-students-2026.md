## What to Look For in a College Laptop

**Battery life** is the #1 spec that matters day-to-day. Get 8+ real-world hours or you'll be hunting outlets between classes. Manufacturer claims are inflated — look for independent reviews that test at screen brightness of 200 nits with mixed workloads.

**Weight under 4 lbs** matters when you're crossing campus. Under 3.5 lbs is ideal. A 0.5 lb difference sounds minor until you're hauling it five days a week.

**16GB RAM** is the new minimum for students who run multiple apps, browser tabs, or creative software simultaneously. 8GB will bottleneck you within two years, especially as browsers and Electron-based apps grow more memory-hungry.

**256GB SSD minimum** — 512GB if you store video or large project files locally. Cloud storage helps, but a slow or full internal drive will drag down your whole system.

**Full HD (1080p) IPS display** — anything lower will strain your eyes after a semester of reading. OLED is a meaningful upgrade if your budget allows; the color accuracy and contrast make long study sessions noticeably more comfortable.

**Ports matter more than you think.** At minimum, look for USB-A, USB-C, and HDMI. Dongles are a tax on your time and bag space.

## Budget Tier (Under $500)

For students who primarily use Google Docs, a learning management system, and web-based research tools, the sub-$500 category covers all bases. The key is picking a machine with a modern processor — AMD Ryzen 5000 or 7000 series, or Intel 12th/13th Gen Core — and avoiding anything with an eMMC drive instead of a real NVMe SSD.

Chromebooks occupy the lower end of this tier and are worth considering if your campus and coursework are fully cloud-based. They offer exceptional battery life and near-zero maintenance overhead. However, if you need native Windows software — specific engineering tools, Adobe apps, or Office offline — stick with Windows.

## What to Know Before You Buy

**Check your school's software requirements.** Some engineering, nursing, or business programs require specific Windows-only applications. Confirm before committing to a Mac or Chromebook.

**RAM is usually not upgradeable** in thin laptops — what you buy is what you keep. Prioritize 16GB at purchase time rather than planning to upgrade later.

**Consider refurbished or last-year models.** Many of the laptops above have prior-generation versions available certified refurbished at 20–30% off. A refurbished Ryzen 5 model from a reliable seller often outperforms a new Ryzen 3 at the same price.

**Education discounts exist.** Dell, Apple, HP, and Lenovo all offer student pricing through their direct stores. Worth checking before defaulting to Amazon — sometimes the direct price is competitive or lower.

**AppleCare and extended warranties** are worth the cost for laptops you'll use daily for four years. Accidental damage coverage is especially valuable in the dorm and campus environment.
