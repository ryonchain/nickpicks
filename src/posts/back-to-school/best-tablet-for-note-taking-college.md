# Best Tablet for Note-Taking in College 2026: iPad vs Android vs Surface

Heading into a new semester with a pile of notebooks and highlighters is a tradition — but more students are ditching paper entirely in favor of a tablet and stylus. The problem is that the tablet market is more crowded than ever, and the wrong choice can leave you stuck with a device that fights your workflow rather than supports it.

## iPad vs Android vs Surface: Which Is Best for Note-Taking?

The honest answer: **iPad wins for most college students** — but each platform has legitimate advantages depending on your workflow.

---

### iPad (Best Overall for Note-Taking)

Apple's Notes, GoodNotes 5, Notability, and GoodNotes 6 are the best handwriting apps available on any platform. The Apple Pencil's latency is near-zero, and palm rejection is reliable out of the box. Ink rendering feels natural — strokes taper and thicken in ways that closely mimic a real pen. For students who annotate slides, sketch diagrams, or write equations by hand, this matters enormously in a long lecture.

iPadOS also handles multitasking well enough for most students: you can run GoodNotes and Safari side-by-side, reference your professor's PDF while writing notes over it, and switch between apps without losing your place.

**Best for:** Students who value the best handwriting experience, annotating PDFs, and have iPhones already in the Apple ecosystem.

**Limitation:** iPadOS still does not run full desktop software. If your major requires SPSS, AutoCAD, or Visual Studio, iPad is a supplement — not a replacement.

---

### Android Tablets (Best Value)

Samsung's Galaxy Tab S series comes closest to iPad-level polish on Android. The S Pen is included in most models (no extra purchase required), Note-taking apps like Samsung Notes, Nebo, and Squid are solid, and the price-to-hardware ratio is strong. Samsung DeX mode gives power users a desktop-like experience on external monitors.

One real advantage Android has over iPad: file management is far less restrictive. You can plug in a USB drive, move files freely, and connect to a wider range of peripherals without adapters. For students who need to work with a lab computer, submit files to department servers, or use external storage regularly, this flexibility is genuinely useful.

**Best for:** Students who want S Pen included, prefer Google ecosystem, or need a budget-friendly handwriting device.

---

### Microsoft Surface (Best for Windows Users)

The Surface Pro running full Windows 11 is technically the most capable tablet for coursework — you can run any desktop app on it. The Surface Pen works well with OneNote, which integrates tightly with Microsoft 365 (free for most students through their university). If your school issues Office licenses, you are already in this ecosystem.

The trade-off is weight and battery life. Surface Pro devices are heavier than comparable iPads and typically get fewer hours per charge under real-world conditions. The keyboard cover adds another layer of bulk. But if you need a single device that handles both note-taking and full software, nothing else on this list matches it.

**Best for:** Engineering or architecture students who need Windows-only software, and students who want a single device for everything.

## How to Choose: Key Buying Factors

Before you look at spec sheets, answer these four questions:

**1. What ecosystem are you already in?** If you use an iPhone and a Mac, an iPad will feel seamless — AirDrop, Handoff, and iCloud sync work without setup. If you are on Android and Windows, the iPad experience will feel more isolated.

**2. Do you need to run desktop software?** Check with your department. Many engineering, architecture, and social science programs require specific Windows applications. If yours does, the Surface Pro is the only tablet here that fully handles it.

**3. What is your budget — including accessories?** A base iPad at $349 sounds affordable, but add an Apple Pencil ($79–$129) and a keyboard case ($100+) and you are at $550–$600. A Samsung Galaxy Tab S9 FE comes with the S Pen in the box. Factor in total cost.

**4. How large does the screen need to be?** For reading and annotating dense academic PDFs — textbook pages, journal articles with small footnotes — bigger is better. A 14.6-inch Galaxy Tab S9 Ultra or a 12.9-inch iPad Pro gives you room to write notes in the margin without shrinking the text to illegibility.

## Essential Accessories to Buy With Your Tablet

The tablet itself is only part of the setup. These accessories make a meaningful difference:

- **Keyboard case or folio keyboard** — for typing papers and emails without carrying a separate laptop. Look for models with a trackpad.
- **Screen protector (matte/paper-like)** — reduces glare and adds friction that makes handwriting feel more natural. Particularly recommended for iPad users.
- **Stylus storage** — Apple Pencil Pro and Samsung S Pen are easy to lose. A case with a dedicated holder prevents the expensive accessory from rolling off a desk.
- **USB-C hub** — especially important for Surface Pro and iPad users who need to connect to classroom projectors, ethernet, or external storage.
