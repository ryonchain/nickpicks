## How Much Power Do Students Actually Need?

Power bank capacity is measured in milliamp-hours (mAh). Here is what that translates to in real usage:

| Device | Approximate Battery |
|--------|-------------------|
| iPhone 15 | 3,279 mAh |
| Samsung Galaxy S24 | 4,000 mAh |
| iPad Air | 7,606 mAh |
| MacBook Air M2 13" | 52.6 Wh (~14,000 mAh equivalent) |
| MacBook Pro 14" | 70 Wh (~19,000 mAh equivalent) |

**Rule of thumb:** A 10,000mAh power bank charges an iPhone about 2.5 times. A 20,000mAh pack can fully charge a thin laptop once.

For most students, a 10,000mAh bank covers a full day of phone use with room to spare. If you also carry a tablet or need to top up your laptop between classes, step up to 20,000mAh or higher. If you only need a safety net for your phone and earbuds, a compact 5,000mAh model fits in a jacket pocket and weighs almost nothing.

## USB-C Power Delivery: The Number That Matters Most

Capacity (mAh) tells you how much energy the bank holds. **Wattage (W)** tells you how fast it delivers that energy.

- **5W–10W:** Slow phone charging. Avoid for anything you need fast.
- **18W–30W:** Fast phone charging, slow laptop charging.
- **45W–65W:** Laptop-compatible charging. Will power most thin laptops.
- **100W+:** Powers most gaming laptops and MacBook Pros.

Students buying a power bank to charge their laptop should look for a minimum of **45W USB-C PD output**. Under that threshold, your laptop may not charge at all — or may charge slower than it drains under load. Always check the wattage spec on the USB-C port, not just the total output rating printed on the front of the device.

## Airline and TSA Rules You Need to Know

Before packing a large power bank for a flight home, check the capacity limit. The FAA allows power banks up to **100Wh in carry-on luggage without prior approval**. Banks between 100Wh and 160Wh require airline approval. Anything above 160Wh is prohibited on passenger aircraft.

To convert mAh to Wh, use this formula: **Wh = (mAh × voltage) / 1000**. Most power banks run at 3.7V, so a 27,650mAh bank at 3.7V is about 102Wh — just over the automatic limit. Check the label on the bank itself, as manufacturers are required to print the Wh rating.

For students who travel frequently, a 20,000mAh (roughly 74Wh) model keeps you well within the free carry-on range.

## Carry Weight vs. Capacity: Finding Your Balance

Power banks are dense. A 20,000mAh unit typically weighs 400–500 grams (roughly one pound). A 10,000mAh slim model can come in under 200 grams. If you are already carrying a laptop, textbooks, and a water bottle, adding a heavy power bank adds up fast.

Consider splitting the difference: carry a compact 10,000mAh bank daily and keep a larger 20,000mAh+ unit for long days, travel, or exam periods when you know you will be away from outlets for six or more hours. Some students keep a small MagSafe pack clipped to their phone case as a permanent top-up and leave the larger bank in the bag for when they need serious reserves.

## What to Skip

Avoid no-name power banks from unfamiliar brands selling 30,000mAh units for $15. Capacity ratings on cheap banks are routinely inflated — a bank claiming 30,000mAh may actually deliver 10,000mAh or less under real conditions. More importantly, poor-quality lithium cells have caused fires. Stick with established brands like Anker, Baseus, Otterbox, Belkin, or Mophie, all of which use quality cells and have proper protection circuits.

Also avoid buying a power bank without USB-C if you are purchasing new in 2026. USB-A-only banks are becoming obsolete as laptops, tablets, and newer phones increasingly rely on USB-C for both charging and data.
