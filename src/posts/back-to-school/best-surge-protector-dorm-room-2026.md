The average college student has a laptop, phone, tablet, wireless earbuds, a smart watch, and a bedside lamp — all competing for 2–3 outlets. A dorm room surge protector solves the outlet shortage, protects your devices from voltage spikes, and adds USB ports so you're not hunting for a charging brick every time you need to plug in.

The complication: many residence halls ban "extension cords" and require that power strips have a built-in surge protector. Understanding what your dorm actually allows before buying prevents the frustration of having it confiscated on move-in day.

## Dorm Power Strip Rules: What You Need to Know

Most universities require power strips that meet all of the following:
- **Built-in surge protection** (not a plain extension cord)
- **UL listed** (Underwriters Laboratories safety certification)
- **Grounded** (3-prong plug, not 2-prong)
- **No extension cord** — the strip must plug directly into the wall, not daisy-chain off another strip

Every recommendation on this list meets all four requirements. Look for the UL mark on the packaging — it's a small UL symbol, usually on the bottom of the strip.

Some universities also restrict strips over a certain amperage or prohibit strips that have switches on each individual outlet. When in doubt, your RA or the housing office can confirm what's approved.

## Joule Rating: Does It Actually Matter?

Joules measure how much surge energy a protector can absorb before failing. A higher joule rating means:
- More protection against large voltage spikes
- Longer protection lifespan before the protection wears out

For a student laptop, phone, and monitor: 1,000–2,000 joules is more than adequate. For expensive equipment (desktop PC, gaming setup, audio production gear): go higher. The APC at 1,080 joules is fine for most dorm setups. The Belkin at 4,320 joules is notable overkill, but dorms aren't known for electrical stability — extra margin doesn't hurt.

## What to Avoid

**Plain extension cords:** Not surge protectors. Not allowed in most dorms. Don't buy them thinking they're equivalent.

**Daisy-chaining strips:** Plugging one power strip into another. Prohibited in virtually all residence halls and creates a genuine fire hazard.

**Cheap no-brand strips:** If you can't find a UL rating on the packaging, skip it. A surge without real protection can fry everything plugged into it.

**Strips with individual outlet switches:** Some universities specifically prohibit these (they're associated with non-compliant wiring). Check before buying if your dorm has this restriction.
