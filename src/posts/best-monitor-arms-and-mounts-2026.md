## Why Use a Monitor Arm?

Most monitors ship with a fixed or minimally adjustable stand. That stand locks you into a position that may be too low, too far forward, or completely wrong for your chair and height. The result is neck strain, shoulder tension, and eye fatigue by the end of the day.

A monitor arm solves all of that:

- **Ergonomics:** Position the screen so the top third of your display is at eye level, reducing neck flexion.
- **Desk space:** Remove the bulky factory stand entirely and reclaim several square feet of usable surface.
- **Flexibility:** Tilt, swivel, and rotate with one hand. Switch to portrait mode for reading or coding without tools.
- **Dual monitor setups:** A dual arm holds two screens on a single mount point, eliminating two stands and creating a cleaner sightline between displays.

If you spend more than four hours a day at a desk, a monitor arm is not a luxury — it is a posture tool.

## Key Specs to Understand Before You Buy

**VESA Compatibility:** Nearly every arm uses the VESA mounting standard. Most monitors are 75x75mm or 100x100mm VESA. Check your monitor's spec sheet before purchasing. Some ultrawide or curved panels use non-standard mounts or have stands that are not removable.

**Weight Capacity:** Arms are rated in pounds or kilograms. A 24-inch 1080p monitor typically weighs 7–10 lbs. A 32-inch 4K panel can hit 15–20 lbs. Match the arm's rating to your actual monitor weight with some headroom.

**Clamp vs. Grommet Mount:** Clamp mounts attach to the edge of your desk — fast to install, no permanent modification. Grommet mounts go through a pre-drilled hole in the desk for a cleaner look and more stability. Most arms include both options.

**Gas Spring vs. Spring-Assisted:** Gas spring arms (like the Ergotron LX) use pressurized cylinders to counterbalance your monitor's weight. They hold position precisely and feel effortless to adjust. Spring-assisted arms use mechanical springs — cheaper, functional, but less smooth. You can feel the difference immediately.

**Cable Management:** Look for integrated cable channels or routing clips along the arm. They keep display cables, USB, and power cords hidden and prevent them from tangling as you reposition the monitor.

## Installation Tips

1. **Check your desk thickness** before buying. Most clamps accommodate 0.4–3.5 inch desk edges, but verify the spec sheet matches your desk.
2. **Remove the factory stand first.** Most monitors have four screws on the back under a plastic cover. Use the included VESA plate from the arm if your monitor's mounting holes are recessed.
3. **Route cables before final tightening.** Thread your display cable and power adapter through the arm channels before locking down the monitor head. Much easier than trying to thread them afterward.
4. **Set gas spring tension with the monitor attached.** Adjust the tension knob until the arm holds position with no drift up or down.
5. **Do not overtighten the desk clamp.** Snug is enough. Overtightening can crack glass desks or dent soft wood surfaces.

## FAQ

**Do I need VESA 100x100 or 75x75?** Check your monitor's manual or the manufacturer's website. Most monitors under 27 inches use 75x75mm; 27 inches and above often use 100x100mm. Many arms include adapter plates for both.

**Can I use a monitor arm with a glass desk?** Yes, but use the grommet mount if your desk has a hole, or add a rubber pad under the clamp to distribute pressure. Avoid aggressive tightening.

**Will a monitor arm work with an ultrawide?** It depends on the arm's weight limit and width rating. The Fully Jarvis and Ergotron LX both support wider panels — confirm the arm's maximum monitor width before purchasing.

**Is a gas spring arm worth the extra cost?** For most people, yes. If you adjust your monitor position regularly — shifting from sitting to standing, changing tasks, or sharing a desk — gas spring makes repositioning a one-hand, one-second action instead of a chore.

**What if my monitor does not have VESA holes?** A few monitors (mostly older iMacs and some Dells) have proprietary stands that are not removable. In those cases, a third-party VESA adapter bracket for your specific model may be available, but it is worth checking before purchasing an arm.
