# Best Cat Trees + Scratching Posts (2025)

Cats have two non-negotiable needs that most owners underestimate: vertical space and somewhere to scratch. Without a good cat tree, your furniture becomes the climbing gym. Without a quality scratching post, your couch becomes the scratching pad.

This guide covers the best cat trees and scratching posts of 2025, with recommendations for every living space, cat size, and aesthetic preference.

---

## Why Cat Trees Matter

Cats are natural vertical animals — in the wild, height means safety from predators and a strategic vantage point for hunting. Indoor cats need vertical territory just as urgently. A cat that can climb to a high perch above the household activity has a sense of control and security that reduces anxiety-driven behaviors (excessive meowing, aggression, over-grooming).

Multi-cat households especially benefit: vertical space multiplies territory without requiring more floor space.

---

## What Makes a Good Cat Tree?

**Stability:** This is the most important factor. A wobbly cat tree gets abandoned quickly — cats instinctively avoid platforms they don't trust. Look for a wide, heavy base plate, and thick-diameter posts.

**Height:** Taller is generally better. Cats prefer the highest perch available. 60"+ trees give cats meaningful vertical territory.

**Sisal coverage:** Cats scratch to mark territory, stretch their muscles, and maintain claw health. Sisal rope (not sisal fabric) offers the best durability and the texture cats prefer for scratching. More sisal coverage = longer post lifespan.

**Hiding spaces:** Enclosed condos give cats private retreat spaces — essential for anxious cats and multi-cat homes.

**Perch size:** For large cats (Maine Coons, Norwegian Forest Cats, large domestic shorthairs), perch size matters. Many cat trees have perches too small for cats over 12 lbs.

## Placement Tips

- **Near sleeping areas:** Cats scratch most when they first wake up. Place a post near where your cat sleeps.
- **Near furniture they're already scratching:** Redirect, don't relocate. Put the post next to the couch they're currently targeting.
- **By the front door:** Cats use scratching to mark territory near entry points — a post at the entrance often satisfies this instinct.
- **Multiple posts for multi-cat homes:** Give each cat access to their own scratching surface to prevent conflict.
