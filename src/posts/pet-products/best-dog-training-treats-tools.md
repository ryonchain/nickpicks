## What Makes a Good Training Treat?

**Small size:** Training treats should be pea-sized or smaller. Larger treats slow down training because the dog spends too long chewing. In a 15-minute session, you might give 50–100 treats — you want tiny ones.

**High value:** The treat needs to be more interesting than whatever the dog would rather be doing. For recall and distraction training, you need the highest-value treats you can find. For low-distraction indoor training, regular kibble may work.

**Soft texture:** Soft treats get eaten in 0.5 seconds; hard treats take 5–10 seconds of chewing — killing the timing precision that makes positive reinforcement work. Always train with soft treats.

**Ingredients:** Long ingredient lists with artificial preservatives, dyes, or fillers aren't ideal for training treats given in large quantities. Look for real protein as the first ingredient.

**Palatability:** Some dogs are driven by chicken; others need beef, fish, or something more exotic. Know your dog's preferences — a treat they don't love won't work as a motivator in distracting environments.

## Training Tips for Better Results

**Timing is everything.** The click or verbal marker must land within 1–2 seconds of the desired behavior to be effective. Practice clicking while your dog does everyday things before starting formal training.

**End on a win.** Always close a training session on a successful repetition of something the dog knows well. Ending on frustration makes the next session harder to start.

**Keep sessions short.** 3–5 minute sessions three times daily produce faster results than one 30-minute session. Mental fatigue sets in quickly, especially in puppies.

**Jackpot rewards.** Occasionally reward an exceptional performance with 5–10 treats in rapid succession — this reinforces that some behaviors are especially worth repeating.

**Separate treat value from difficulty.** Use high-value treats for new behaviors, distracting environments, and reliability training. Lower-value treats for known, easy behaviors in low-distraction environments.
