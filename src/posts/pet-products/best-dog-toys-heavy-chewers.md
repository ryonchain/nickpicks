If your dog destroys every toy in under an hour, you know the cycle: buy a toy, watch it get annihilated, sweep up the remains, repeat. Heavy chewers — typically large breeds, working breeds, and dogs with high prey drive — need toys built for sustained abuse, not the average pet store plush.

## Understanding Your Chewer's Level

Not all heavy chewers are the same. Kong and other manufacturers categorize chewers in levels:

- **Moderate chewers:** Average dogs that chew for fun, not destruction
- **Power chewers:** Dogs that can demolish most rubber toys given time
- **Extreme chewers:** Dogs that can break apart "indestructible" toys and have posed choking hazards with shredded material

For true extreme chewers, supervision is always recommended even with the toughest toys. No toy is indestructible under unlimited, unsupervised use.

---

## What to Look For

**Material:** Natural rubber (especially vulcanized rubber) is the toughest commonly available material. Nylon-based chews can also be durable. Avoid soft rubber, latex, and plush for power chewers.

**Size:** Always choose a toy larger than what your dog could swallow. A toy that's too small is a choking hazard, especially when pieces break off.

**Stimulation type:** Chewing alone can bore some dogs. Look for toys that also deliver treat rewards, unpredictable movement, or scent engagement to keep dogs interested without redirecting frustration to furniture.

**Digestibility:** For chews (rather than toys), consider whether you want a digestible option (bully sticks, antlers) or an inedible chew (rubber). Both have valid roles.

## Rotation Strategy for Heavy Chewers

Giving your dog access to all toys at once reduces novelty and increases frustration biting. Instead:

- Rotate 2–3 toys at a time, swapping every few days
- Reserve high-value toys (frozen Kong, Goughnuts) for periods when your dog needs to settle (crate time, alone time)
- Pair physical chew toys with training and exercise to address the underlying energy that drives destructive chewing
