## Why Sleep Tracking Matters

You can't improve what you don't measure. Sleep tracking reveals:

- **Sleep duration** — are you getting the 7–9 hours most adults need?
- **Sleep stages** — are you hitting enough deep sleep (N3) and REM for recovery and memory?
- **Sleep efficiency** — what percentage of time in bed are you actually asleep?
- **HRV (Heart Rate Variability)** — a proxy for nervous system recovery; the most actionable metric for athletes
- **Consistency** — irregular sleep timing ("social jet lag") disrupts circadian rhythm even when total hours are adequate

Armed with this data, targeted interventions (consistent sleep times, temperature control, blue light reduction, supplements) produce measurable improvements within 2–4 weeks.

## Types of Sleep Tracking Devices

**Wrist-worn (smartwatches/fitness trackers):** Most convenient because you already wear them. Accuracy is good for sleep duration and stages; less reliable for specific stage timing without dedicated sleep-optimized hardware.

**Ring-worn (Oura Ring):** Dedicated biometric focus with superior HRV accuracy. Less intrusive than a watch in bed.

**Bedside monitors (Withings Sleep Analyzer):** Placed under your mattress; no wearable required. Good option if you find wrist/ring devices uncomfortable.

**Smart alarm clocks:** Use sound or light to wake you at the lightest sleep stage in a window before your alarm — reducing grogginess.

## Sleep Hygiene Habits That Amplify Any Device

Trackers and aids work better alongside consistent habits:

- **Fixed wake time daily** (including weekends) — the most powerful circadian stabilizer
- **No screens 60+ minutes before bed** — or use blue light glasses/night mode
- **Cool room (65–68°F)** — optimal temperature for deep sleep
- **No caffeine after 1–2 PM** — caffeine's half-life is 5–6 hours
- **Dark room** — even small LED lights disrupt melatonin

## FAQ

**Q: How accurate are consumer sleep trackers?**
A: Wrist-worn trackers are about 75–80% accurate for sleep stage identification compared to clinical polysomnography (sleep lab). They're most reliable for tracking trends over time rather than any single night. Ring-worn devices like Oura show slightly better HRV accuracy due to closer vessel proximity.

**Q: Do sleep trackers cause "orthosomnia" (sleep anxiety)?**
A: Some users become overly focused on sleep scores, which increases bedtime anxiety and paradoxically worsens sleep. Use tracking as trend data, not a daily performance report. If scores stress you out, check weekly rather than nightly.

**Q: What's the most important sleep metric to track?**
A: Consistency of wake time and total sleep duration are the highest-leverage variables. HRV is the most actionable recovery metric for athletes. Sleep stages matter, but are harder to directly control than schedule consistency.
