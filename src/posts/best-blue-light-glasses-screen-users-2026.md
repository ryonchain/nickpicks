## The Science Behind Blue Light Blocking Glasses

Blue light (wavelengths between 400–500nm) is emitted in high concentrations by LED screens — monitors, phones, tablets, and TVs. The effects of chronic blue light exposure include:

- **Digital eye strain** — dryness, blur, and discomfort from reduced blink rate
- **Sleep disruption** — blue light suppresses melatonin production, delaying sleep onset
- **Headaches** — particularly from high-contrast screens in dark environments
- **Macular stress** — long-term exposure may accelerate age-related macular degeneration (evidence still emerging)

Blue light lenses come in two primary categories:
- **Clear lenses (day use):** Filter 20–50% of blue light with minimal color distortion. Ideal for office use where color accuracy matters.
- **Yellow/amber lenses (evening use):** Filter 90–99% of blue light at the cost of color shift. Best used 2–3 hours before bed.

## What to Look For in Blue Light Glasses

**Filter percentage:** Look for at least 40% blue light blocking for daytime use; 90%+ for evening/sleep optimization.

**Lens quality:** Anti-reflective coatings reduce glare independently of blue light filtering. UV protection is a bonus.

**Frame fit:** For all-day wear, lightweight frames (under 25g) with wide temples reduce ear and nose fatigue.

**Non-prescription vs. prescription:** Most blue light glasses are sold as non-prescription (0.00 magnification) — confirm this if you don't need vision correction.

## When to Wear Blue Light Glasses

**During the workday (9 AM – 5 PM):** Clear or lightly tinted lenses that don't distort color. Wear throughout your screen sessions to reduce cumulative eye strain.

**Evening use (after 7 PM):** Switch to amber/orange lenses if you're still on screens. The stronger blue light filtering helps preserve your natural melatonin cycle and makes falling asleep easier.

**Gaming sessions:** Wide-frame models like Gunnar's gaming line are purpose-built for marathon sessions with high-refresh screens.

**Note:** Blue light glasses don't replace the 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. This addresses the focusing fatigue that blue light glasses don't prevent.
