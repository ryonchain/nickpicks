## Buying Guide: What You Need to Know

### 2019 DOE Standard Change
**Important:** In 2019, the Department of Energy changed how dehumidifier capacity is measured. A "50-pint" unit sold before 2019 extracts more moisture than a "50-pint" unit sold today (tested under different temperature/humidity conditions). If you see older reviews mentioning "70-pint" vs. "50-pint" models with similar real-world performance, this is why. All picks above use the current DOE standard.

### Choosing the Right Capacity
Under current DOE standards:
- **30 pints/day:** Up to 1,500 sq ft with moderate humidity
- **50 pints/day:** 1,500–3,000 sq ft or 500–1,500 sq ft with severe humidity
- **70 pints/day:** 3,000+ sq ft or large spaces with severe humidity

When in doubt, size up — a larger unit running on low efficiency is more effective and cheaper to operate than a smaller unit running at maximum capacity constantly.

### Continuous Drain vs. Tank Emptying
All picks above support continuous drain via a 1/2-inch gravity drain hose. Connect to a floor drain or pump to a utility sink — you never empty the tank. Highly recommended for basement installations where the unit runs year-round. If no drain is convenient, tank emptying means daily or twice-daily manual emptying.

### Temperature Limitation
Standard compressor dehumidifiers work best above 65°F. Below 50°F, the coils may ice up and the unit stops working. For cold basements in winter (under 50°F), look for "whole-home" units or desiccant dehumidifiers (more expensive).

### Operating Cost
50-pint Energy Star dehumidifiers use ~450–550W when running. At average US electricity rates (~$0.15/kWh), running 12 hours/day costs approximately $0.81–0.99/day, or ~$25/month. Energy Star certified models use 15–20% less energy than non-certified alternatives.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What humidity level should a basement be?**
50% relative humidity is the ideal target. Below 40%: too dry for comfort. 50–55%: optimal for preventing mold and dust mites. Above 60%: mold risk increases significantly. Above 70%: conditions favor rapid mold growth on building materials.

**How long until a dehumidifier solves my damp basement?**
Initial moisture removal: 24–48 hours of operation. Getting the basement to target humidity (50%): typically 1–3 days. Ongoing maintenance: the unit runs periodically when humidity rises above the setpoint. First season may take longer if the walls and floor are saturated.

**Do I need to run my dehumidifier in winter?**
In cold climates: typically not, unless the basement is heated. Cold air holds less moisture and most basements are below 50°F in winter — conditions where compressor dehumidifiers ice up anyway. Resume operation in spring when humidity returns.

**Where should I place a basement dehumidifier?**
Central location for open basements. Near the humidity source (laundry room, crawl space door) for specific problem areas. Keep 6–12 inches clearance on all sides for air intake. Not directly against walls.

**Can a dehumidifier remove existing mold?**
No — a dehumidifier prevents mold growth by reducing humidity below the growth threshold. Existing mold must be physically removed (bleach solution for non-porous surfaces; professionals for drywall, carpet, or structural damage). After remediation, a dehumidifier prevents recurrence.
