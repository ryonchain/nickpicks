## What to Look For in a Student Laptop

**Battery life:** Aim for 8+ hours of real-world use. Anything under 6 hours means you'll be hunting for outlets by afternoon.

**Weight:** If you're carrying it across campus, under 3 lbs is ideal. Under 4 lbs is acceptable.

**RAM:** 8GB is the minimum for comfortable multitasking. 16GB is better for students running heavier software like video editing or coding IDEs.

**Storage:** 128GB is tight for four years. 256GB is the practical minimum; 512GB is comfortable.

**Display:** 1080p (Full HD) is the baseline. IPS panels offer better viewing angles than TN — important for long study sessions.

## Student Laptop Buying Guide

### Should you buy a Chromebook or Windows laptop?

The answer depends almost entirely on your software requirements. Ask your school or professor what software is required for your specific courses before you buy. If the answer is "just Google Docs and web browsers," a Chromebook is the smarter, cheaper choice. If there's any chance you'll need Windows software, buy Windows.

### How much RAM do you actually need?

8GB handles browser-based work fine. If you're planning to run video editing software, multiple IDEs, or virtual machines, upgrade to 16GB. Upgrading RAM after purchase is often impossible on modern thin laptops, so buy what you'll need.

### Should you buy AppleCare or an extended warranty?

For a MacBook Air going through four years of college, AppleCare+ is worth it — accidental damage coverage alone pays for itself once. For budget Windows laptops, manufacturer warranties are typically sufficient.

### New vs. refurbished?

Certified refurbished MacBooks from Apple and Lenovo offer significant savings. A refurbished MacBook Air M2 from Apple's certified store often costs $150–$200 less than new with a 1-year warranty. Worth considering if budget is a concern.
