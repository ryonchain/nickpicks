## What Dry Skin Actually Needs

**Humectants** draw water into the skin: hyaluronic acid, glycerin, urea, aloe vera.

**Emollients** fill gaps in the skin barrier and prevent water loss: ceramides, squalane, fatty acids (shea butter, jojoba oil), niacinamide.

**Occlusives** seal moisture in with a physical barrier: petrolatum (Vaseline), lanolin, dimethicone, beeswax.

The best moisturizers for dry skin layer all three. A product with only humectants pulls water into the skin but lets it evaporate without an emollient layer. A product with only occlusive seals in whatever moisture exists but doesn't attract new water. Look for formulas that hit all three categories.

**Fragrance is often the enemy.** Many moisturizers use fragrance (synthetic or natural) to feel luxurious. For dry, sensitized, or barrier-compromised skin, fragrance is a common irritant that undermines healing. Fragrance-free is almost always the better choice for dry skin.

## Day vs. Night Moisturizing Strategy

**Morning routine:** Focus on humectants (hyaluronic acid) and emollients, applied under SPF. The moisturizer should absorb well enough to not pill under sunscreen or makeup. Lighter formulas work better in the morning.

**Night routine:** Use your richest, most occlusive formula at night when your skin is in repair mode and doesn't need to sit under makeup. This is also when you'd layer a facial oil over your moisturizer if you use one, sealing in all the hydration.

**Layering tip:** Apply moisturizer within 60 seconds of washing your face while skin is still slightly damp. This locks in additional water from the rinse and maximizes the humectant effect.

## FAQ

**How much moisturizer should I use?**
A pea-sized amount for a lightweight gel; a nickel-sized amount for a cream. More isn't better — excess product that can't absorb sits on the surface and can cause breakouts.

**Should I use the same moisturizer morning and night?**
You can, but it's not optimal. Morning calls for a lighter formula that sits well under SPF and doesn't interfere with makeup. Night calls for your richest, most repairing formula. Using two products is better, but one good cream applied twice is fine for simplicity.

**My moisturizer pills under sunscreen — what do I do?**
Pilling happens when your moisturizer hasn't fully absorbed before you apply sunscreen. Wait 60–90 seconds after moisturizer before applying SPF, or look for an SPF moisturizer that combines both steps.

**Can moisturizer make skin oily?**
Not directly — but heavy occlusive moisturizers can feel greasy on oily or combination skin. If your skin is oily but also dehydrated (tight, flaky, or prone to congestion), look for gel-cream formulas with hyaluronic acid rather than shea butter or petrolatum-heavy creams.
