## Why Vitamin C Works

Vitamin C (L-ascorbic acid) inhibits melanin production, which reduces the appearance of dark spots, sun damage, and post-acne marks. It's also a potent antioxidant that neutralizes free radical damage from UV exposure, pollution, and blue light. Over 8–12 weeks of consistent daily use, most people see measurable improvement in skin tone, texture, and brightness.

**What makes a good vitamin C serum:**

- **Concentration:** 10–20% L-ascorbic acid is the clinical sweet spot. Under 8% is too weak. Over 20% increases irritation with diminishing returns.
- **pH:** L-ascorbic acid is most stable and effective at pH 2.5–3.5. Most reputable serums are formulated in this range.
- **Packaging:** Vitamin C oxidizes on contact with air and light. Dark glass bottles with droppers or airless pumps are non-negotiable. Avoid clear bottles.
- **Stabilizers:** Look for vitamin E (tocopherol) and ferulic acid — both extend vitamin C stability and enhance its antioxidant effect.

## How to Use Vitamin C Serum

**Apply in the morning.** Vitamin C's antioxidant action is most valuable in the daytime when your skin faces UV radiation and environmental stressors. Apply it after cleansing and toning, before moisturizer and sunscreen.

**Use sunscreen every day.** Vitamin C and SPF are the most powerful anti-aging combination in skincare. Without sunscreen, new UV damage will outpace the serum's brightening benefits.

**Start slow if you have sensitive skin.** Begin with every other day for two weeks, then work up to daily use. If you experience redness, flaking, or burning, switch to a lower-concentration formula or a vitamin C derivative (like ascorbyl glucoside).

**Give it 8–12 weeks.** Melanin production is slow to change. Most studies showing significant improvement in dark spots and skin tone measured results at 8–12 weeks of daily use. Don't abandon ship at week three.

## FAQ

**Can I use vitamin C with retinol?**
Yes, but use them separately. Apply vitamin C in the morning and retinol at night to avoid potential irritation and pH conflicts. Some users alternate nights with retinol if they're starting out.

**Why did my vitamin C serum turn orange/brown?**
Oxidized vitamin C turns yellow, then orange, then brown. An orange serum has lost most of its potency and can potentially cause skin staining. Discard it and store your next bottle away from heat and light.

**What's the difference between L-ascorbic acid and vitamin C derivatives?**
L-ascorbic acid is pure vitamin C — the most researched and most potent form. Derivatives like ascorbyl glucoside and sodium ascorbyl phosphate are more stable but require conversion in the skin and are less effective at the same concentration. L-ascorbic acid is the gold standard if your skin can tolerate it.

**Can vitamin C serum be used on dark skin?**
Absolutely — vitamin C is effective for hyperpigmentation on all skin tones, including deeper skin tones where dark spots from acne and sun damage are particularly common. Look for formulas with ferulic acid for enhanced results.
