## Swing vs. Bouncer: Which Do You Need?

**Baby swings** are motorized seats that move in a rhythmic arc (side-to-side, front-to-back, or both). They're larger, require power, and provide consistent, hands-free motion. Best for babies who need sustained movement to sleep or calm down.

**Baby bouncers** are lightweight fabric seats that respond to your baby's natural movement — every kick or wiggle bounces the seat gently. No motor required. Compact, portable, and easy to move room to room. Best for alert, interactive babies who want to see the world and be near you.

**Key consideration:** Not every baby loves every type of motion. A side-to-side swing might soothe one baby while another only responds to front-to-back. If possible, borrow both to test before committing.

---

## What to Look For

**Weight limit:** Most swings support up to 25–30 lbs. Most bouncers cap at 20 lbs. Check the specific limits — your baby will outgrow a light-limit bouncer faster.

**Motion variety:** Multiple swing directions and speeds give you more options for finding what your baby responds to. Some swings combine rocking with vibration.

**Sound options:** White noise, heartbeat sounds, and lullabies are common. Some sync with Spotify or have Bluetooth speakers.

**Portability:** Full-size swings are bulky and hard to move. Look for foldable designs if space or mobility matters.

**Power source:** Swings run on AC adapter, batteries, or both. Battery-only swings are convenient but expensive to run (and batteries die at 3am). AC adapter capability is strongly preferred.

## Baby Swing Safety Guidelines

- Always use the 3-point or 5-point harness, even if baby seems secure
- Never leave a sleeping baby unattended in a swing for extended periods — transfer to a firm, flat sleep surface when they fall asleep
- Keep the swing away from stairs, counters, and elevated surfaces
- Never place a swing on a table, bed, or elevated surface
- Stop using the swing when your baby can sit up unassisted or reaches the weight limit

## FAQ

**Q: Can my newborn use a swing from birth?**
A: Yes, as long as the swing reclines to a near-flat position for the first few weeks (before neck muscles develop). Check that the seat reclines at least 130–135 degrees. Use the newborn insert included with most swings.

**Q: How long can I leave my baby in a swing?**
A: Limit sessions to 30 minutes at a time. Extended time in a semi-reclined seat can put stress on a newborn's spine and restrict breathing if their head falls forward (positional asphyxia). Always transfer to a flat surface for extended sleep.

**Q: What if my baby doesn't like the swing?**
A: Many babies need a few tries to adapt. Try different speeds, motion directions, or adding white noise alongside the swing. If the baby still resists, a bouncer with different motion dynamics may work better.
