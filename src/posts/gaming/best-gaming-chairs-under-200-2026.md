## Ergonomic vs. Racing Style: What Actually Matters

The "racing chair" aesthetic (bucket seat shape, high side bolsters, pillow lumbar) looks great but isn't always the most comfortable for long sessions. Here's the real breakdown:

- **Ergonomic gaming chairs** borrow office-chair design — flatter seat base, adjustable lumbar, forward tilt. Better for people who sit upright and want posture support.
- **Racing-style chairs** have sculpted side bolsters that cradle you in position. Great for leaning back during casual play; less ideal if you hunch forward toward your monitor.
- **Seat cushion density:** High-density foam lasts years. Cheap chairs collapse within months of daily use. Check reviews for mentions of "sinking" after 6 months.
- **Lumbar support:** A built-in adjustable lumbar pillar (not a separate pillow) is the gold standard under $200.

## What to Look for When Buying a Gaming Chair Under $200

### Lumbar Support Type
Built-in adjustable lumbar > external adjustable pillow > fixed pillow. If a chair only comes with a lumbar pillow that ties on, it will migrate over time.

### Seat Cushion Density
Sit (or read reviews from) people who've used the chair 6+ months. Foam compression is the #1 failure point on budget chairs.

### Armrest Dimensions
1D armrests (height only) are fine for casual use. If you're a competitive player who rests their wrists on armrests while aiming, look for 3D or 4D adjustment.

### Weight and Height Fit
Most chairs list a weight capacity but not an ideal height range. The sweet spot for most racing chairs is 5'5"–6'1". Taller or shorter gamers should specifically look for chairs that list torso length fit.
