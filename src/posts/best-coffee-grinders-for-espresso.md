## Why Espresso Grinders Are Different

For espresso, you need:
1. **Very fine grind** — finer than any pour-over or drip setting, approaching flour texture
2. **Precise, micro-adjustable settings** — a 5-second change in extraction time might require a single step of grinder adjustment
3. **Consistent particle size (low fines)** — too many fine particles cause channeling, bitterness, and inconsistent pours
4. **Low retention** — old grounds in the grinder contaminate fresh extractions

Most budget blade grinders and even many entry burr grinders marketed for drip coffee don't reach espresso fineness at all. A grinder specifically designed for espresso is not optional — it's the foundation.

## Buying Guide

### Burr Type: Flat vs Conical
**Flat burrs** (Breville Smart Grinder Pro, DF64): Produce a more uniform particle size distribution, often preferred for clarity in espresso flavor. Tend to generate slightly more heat and have higher retention.

**Conical burrs** (Baratza Encore ESP, Timemore, 1Zpresso): Generally lower retention, less heat buildup, and many produce a sweeter, more textured espresso. Both produce excellent espresso — this is a preference distinction, not performance.

### Grind Steps: How Many Do You Need?
For espresso, more micro-adjustment steps = more dialing precision. **40+ steps** in the espresso range is the minimum. The Baratza Encore ESP's 280 steps and the 1Zpresso JX-Pro's 90 clicks per rotation are excellent. The Breville's 60 settings cover the range adequately.

### Retention
Low retention = less stale ground coffee contaminating fresh shots. Single-dose grinders (Baratza Encore ESP, DF64) have near-zero retention. Hopper-based grinders (Breville) retain 0.5–2g between shots. For home use, minor retention is generally fine.

### Workflow Speed
If you make 2–4 espresso drinks per morning, any electric grinder works fine. For home cafes pulling 10+ shots per session, a faster grinder (higher RPM or larger burrs) reduces friction.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use any burr grinder for espresso?**
No. Most budget burr grinders (Oxo Brew, basic Capresso) don't reach espresso-fine settings. You need a grinder that explicitly reaches espresso fineness. Check specifications: if it doesn't mention espresso on the spec sheet, assume it doesn't grind fine enough.

**What grind size is espresso?**
Espresso grinds are similar to fine table salt or very fine sand — much finer than drip coffee. The correct size depends on your specific espresso machine and target extraction time (25–30 seconds for a double shot is the standard starting point).

**How do I dial in espresso with my grinder?**
1. Pull a shot and time it (target: 25–30 seconds for a 2:1 output ratio)
2. Too fast = too coarse — adjust grinder finer
3. Too slow = too fine — adjust grinder coarser
4. Make single micro-adjustments and repull until timing is in range

**Does the grinder or the espresso machine matter more?**
Both matter, but many baristas argue the grinder is more important. A great grinder + mediocre machine often produces better espresso than a mediocre grinder + great machine. This is why the Baratza Encore ESP at $195 can be paired with a $600 entry espresso machine and produce excellent results.

**How often should I clean my espresso grinder?**
Brush burrs after each session to remove loose grounds. Full disassembly and burr cleaning every 2–4 weeks for daily espresso use. Replace burrs every 200–500 lbs of coffee depending on burr material and use intensity.
