## How Sous Vide Works (Quick Overview)

1. Season protein, seal in a vacuum bag (or ziplock with the water displacement method)
2. Set your immersion circulator to target temperature
3. Cook for the prescribed time — 1 to 48 hours depending on the protein
4. Quick sear in a screaming hot pan or with a torch for Maillard crust

Results: edge-to-edge perfect doneness you cannot achieve any other way, extreme timing flexibility (the food holds temperature safely), and consistent repeatability.

## Buying Guide

### What Wattage Do You Need?
- **750–800W (Nano):** Fine for 4–8 quart pots, single or double portions. Slower preheat, perfectly adequate once at temperature.
- **1,000–1,100W (Precision Cooker, Joule, Inkbird):** Faster preheat, handles larger containers up to 10+ gallons. Ideal for meal prep batches.

Higher wattage also helps recover temperature faster when adding room-temperature food — important for large batches.

### App vs Manual Control
- **App control:** Access to tested recipe guides, visual doneness indicators, remote monitoring, and timer management. Requires account creation.
- **Manual control:** Set temperature and time directly on the device. Works offline, no account required. Less guided.

For beginners, app access to tested time/temperature guides is genuinely helpful. Anova's app is the best in the category for recipe quality.

### Container Size
Most circulators clip to any container. For home sous vide:
- A 6–8 quart stockpot handles 2–4 steaks or a whole chicken breast batch
- A 12-quart container is better for full rib racks or large batches
- Cambro 12-quart polycarbonate containers are the most popular dedicated sous vide vessels

### Vacuum Sealing vs Ziplock
A vacuum sealer produces the best results — more reliable seal, fully submerges. But a standard ziplock bag using the water displacement method (zip closed except 1 inch, lower slowly into water to push air out, then seal) works for most proteins at short cook times (under 4 hours). At longer cooks or high temperatures, a proper vacuum seal is recommended.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is sous vide safe? What about bacteria?**
Yes, when done correctly. Pasteurization happens at specific time + temperature combinations, not at boiling point. Chicken at 145°F for 9 minutes is FDA-safe; at 140°F, you need 30 minutes. The Anova and Joule apps include pasteurization guidance. The key: cook at 130°F+ for proteins, and sear immediately after to kill any surface bacteria reintroduced during bag opening.

**What can I cook sous vide?**
- Steak (most popular — perfect medium-rare every time)
- Chicken breast (solves the dry chicken problem)
- Pork chops, pork tenderloin
- Salmon and fish (130°F for 45 min = silky, butter-soft texture)
- Eggs (65°C for 1 hour = jammy, custard-like)
- Vegetables (74°C for 45 min for carrots, beets)
- Custards and crème brûlée bases

**Does sous vide require a vacuum sealer?**
No. The water displacement method with a ziplock bag works for most applications. For cook times over 4–6 hours or very high temperatures, a proper vacuum seal is more reliable.

**How long does sous vide take?**
Depends on the protein and thickness. General guidelines:
- Steak (1.5"): 1–4 hours
- Chicken breast: 1–4 hours
- Pork chop: 1–4 hours
- Short ribs: 24–72 hours (this is where sous vide is transformative)
- Salmon: 30–45 minutes

**Can I sous vide from frozen?**
Yes — add 30–60 minutes to the standard cook time. This makes sous vide excellent for meal prep: vacuum seal and freeze, cook from frozen when needed.
