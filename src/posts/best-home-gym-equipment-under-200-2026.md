## Buying Guide: Priorities for Small Spaces

### Square Footage First
Before buying, measure your space. You need roughly:
- **Pull-up bar:** Fits in any doorway
- **Kettlebell swings:** 6-foot ceiling, 4×4 clear floor space
- **Resistance band exercises:** 4-foot reach in all directions
- **Stationary bike:** 4×2 feet footprint

### Compound Movements First
Buy equipment that enables compound (multi-joint) movements — they provide the most training value per piece. Priority order:
1. Pulling (pull-up bar, resistance bands)
2. Pushing (push-up handles, dumbbells)
3. Hinging (kettlebell)
4. Squatting (resistance bands, dumbbells)
5. Cardio (bike, jump rope)

### Progressive Overload Matters
The most important principle in home gym: you need the ability to increase resistance as you get stronger. Adjustable dumbbells and adjustable kettlebells solve this. Fixed-weight gear becomes useless once you outgrow it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I build real muscle with home gym equipment?**
Yes. Progressive overload is what drives muscle growth — not the equipment itself. With adjustable dumbbells, resistance bands, and a pull-up bar, you can progressively overload every major muscle group and build significant strength.

**What's the single best piece of home gym equipment?**
Adjustable dumbbells (if you have the budget) or a pull-up bar (if you need the most value per dollar). A pull-up bar at $25 enables one of the best back/bicep exercises and costs less than a single gym day pass.

**Do I need a gym mat?**
For floor exercises (ab work, stretching, pushups): yes — a $20–30 exercise mat makes a meaningful difference in comfort. For weight training: not required but helpful for noise reduction in apartments.

**Can I use resistance bands and bodyweight instead of weights?**
Absolutely. For beginners and intermediate trainees, resistance bands + bodyweight provides sufficient stimulus for months to years of progress. Weights become more important for advanced strength training.

**Is a squat rack necessary?**
For barbell squats and bench press: yes. For dumbbell/kettlebell work: no. Most home gym users under $500 skip the rack and use goblet squats (kettlebell) and dumbbell variations instead.
