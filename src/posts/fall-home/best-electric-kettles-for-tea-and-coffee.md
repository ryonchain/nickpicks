## Why Temperature Matters More Than You Think

Most electric kettles boil water. The right electric kettle holds specific temperatures:

- **White and green tea:** 160–175°F — boiling water scorches delicate leaves and turns green tea bitter
- **Oolong:** 185–195°F
- **Black tea and dark roast coffee:** 200–212°F
- **Pour-over coffee:** 200°F is the SCAA standard for optimal extraction
- **French press:** 195–205°F

A variable temperature kettle eliminates the guesswork of "boil and wait 30 seconds" and delivers consistent results for whatever you're brewing.

What separates good kettles from great ones:
- **Temperature hold:** Can it maintain your target temperature for 30–60 minutes?
- **Gooseneck vs. standard spout:** Gooseneck provides a slow, controlled pour for coffee brewing; standard spouts fill cups faster but aren't precise
- **Build material:** Stainless steel interior vs. plastic — steel doesn't leach or retain odors
- **Capacity:** 0.8–1 L is standard; 1.2–1.5 L for larger households

## What to Look For When Buying

**Match spout to brewing method:** Gooseneck spouts give you control over pour rate and direction — essential for pour-over, V60, and Chemex. Standard spouts pour faster and work fine for French press, teapots, and cups.

**Variable temperature is worth it if you drink anything other than black tea:** The difference in green tea quality at 170°F versus 212°F is significant. One ruined batch of $20 sencha pays for a mid-range variable temp kettle.

**1 hour hold vs. 30 minutes:** If you're a morning brewer who sometimes forgets about the kettle, 60-minute hold is meaningfully more convenient.

**Stainless steel interior is non-negotiable:** Plastic-lined kettles impart a faint taste, especially when new. Every model on this list uses stainless steel.
