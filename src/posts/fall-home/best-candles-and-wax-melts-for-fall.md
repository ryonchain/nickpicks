## Fall Fragrance: What Actually Works

Fall candle shopping is overwhelming because every brand releases seasonal lines in August. The problem: most fall candles smell like synthetic "pumpkin spice" spray rather than actual cinnamon, apple, or wood smoke. What makes a good fall candle:

**Scent throw:** The strength of the fragrance when the candle is burning (hot throw) vs. cold (cold throw). Look for reviews that specifically mention room-filling scent rather than "nice when you're right next to it."

**Burn time:** Jar candles in the 7–14 oz range typically burn 50–80 hours. Larger jars hit 100+ hours. Wax melts burn until the fragrance exhausts — usually 8–12 hours per cube.

**Wax type:**
- **Paraffin:** Most common; strong scent throw; not the cleanest burn
- **Soy:** Slower burn, cleaner smoke, often slightly softer scent throw; preferred by eco-conscious buyers
- **Coconut-soy blend:** Best of both — strong scent throw with cleaner burn

**Wick type:** Cotton wicks are the standard and burn cleanly. Wood wicks crackle pleasantly but require more trimming and can tunnel if not managed.

## What to Look For When Buying

**Match candle size to room size:** A 7 oz single-wick candle won't adequately fragrance a large living room. Use a 3-wick or large jar candle (14+ oz) for open-concept spaces; a single-wick 7–9 oz for bedrooms and bathrooms.

**Burn the first time correctly:** On a new candle, let it burn until the entire top layer is melted (usually 2–4 hours) before extinguishing. This prevents tunneling — the hollow center that wastes half your candle.

**Trim the wick before each burn:** A 1/4 inch wick produces a clean, even flame. Long untrimmed wicks cause excessive soot, flickering, and faster wax consumption.

**Soy vs. paraffin is a real tradeoff:** Soy burns slower and cleaner; paraffin typically has stronger scent throw. Soy-blend candles try to split the difference — they're the most common format in the mid-price range.
