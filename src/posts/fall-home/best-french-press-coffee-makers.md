## Why French Press Makes Better Coffee

French press is the simplest, most straightforward coffee brewing method available — and for many coffee drinkers, it's also the best. Unlike paper-filter methods (pour-over, drip), the metal filter in a French press allows coffee oils and fine particles through, producing a fuller-bodied, richer cup. No paper filters means no additional cost and no filter-specific flavor.

The tradeoff: French press produces more sediment in the cup, and the coffee continues extracting after pressing, so it needs to be poured immediately rather than left in the press.

Key buying factors:

- **Material:** Glass is traditional and lets you see the brew; it's fragile and loses heat. Stainless steel is durable and retains heat better. Double-wall stainless is the premium option.
- **Filter quality:** A fine mesh filter that seals tightly to the carafe walls reduces sediment. Cheaper presses have loose mesh that lets grounds slip past.
- **Size:** 12 oz (3-cup) for solo brewing; 34 oz (8-cup) for 2–4 people; 51 oz (12-cup) for 4–6 people or batch brewing.
- **Ease of cleaning:** The filter stack (mesh screen + spiral plate + plunger) collects grounds. Models with fewer components and wider openings are significantly easier to clean.

## What to Look For When Buying

**Pour immediately after pressing:** Regardless of French press material, once you press the plunger, coffee continues extracting against the grounds. If you don't pour it right away, it over-extracts and becomes bitter. Use a stainless press or a thermal carafe if you need it to stay hot.

**Coarse grind is required:** French press coffee needs a coarse, even grind (like rough kosher salt). Fine grounds pass through the mesh filter and create excessive sediment and bitterness. If you buy a French press, invest in a burr grinder — pre-ground coffee sold at drip-grind fineness will not produce good results.

**Filter quality separates good French presses from frustrating ones:** A loose, floppy mesh filter that lets grounds slip past the carafe wall produces gritty, over-extracted coffee. Prioritize models where the filter is described as fitting tightly to the carafe wall.

**Cleaning matters for daily use:** A French press you use every morning needs to be cleaned every morning. Wide-mouth carafes and filter systems with fewer components are meaningfully easier to deal with.
