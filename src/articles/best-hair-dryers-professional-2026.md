## Hair Dryer Buying Guide

### Wattage and Motor Speed

More watts = more airflow = faster drying. But wattage isn't the whole story — motor quality and technology matter more than raw wattage at higher price points.

- Under 1,500W: Avoid for regular use (slow, inefficient)
- 1,500–1,875W: Standard range for consumer dryers
- 1,875W: U.S. household circuit maximum for standard appliances
- 2,000W+: Professional dryers on 20-amp circuits (not for most home use)
- Dyson/Shark FlexStyle: Lower wattage but higher RPM motor — different metric

### Ionic vs. Non-Ionic

Ionic dryers emit negative ions that break down water molecules faster and seal the hair cuticle — reducing frizz and drying time. All premium dryers are ionic. Avoid non-ionic dryers unless budget is the sole constraint.

## FAQ

**Is the Dyson Supersonic actually worth $400–$500?**
For daily use on fine, damaged, or color-treated hair: yes — the heat damage reduction and time savings compound over years of use. For occasional use or healthy hair: the BaBylissPRO or Shark FlexStyle delivers 85–90% of the result at 30–45% of the cost.

**What's the difference between 1,875W consumer dryers and salon dryers?**
U.S. standard household circuits (15 amp) cap at approximately 1,875W for appliances. Salon dryers operate on 20-amp circuits and can reach 2,000–2,200W. The difference in drying speed is real but modest for most hair types.

**How often should I replace a hair dryer?**
Professional-grade dryers (BaBylissPRO, Dyson): 5–10 years. Budget consumer dryers: 2–4 years. Replace when motor becomes inconsistent or heating element fails.
