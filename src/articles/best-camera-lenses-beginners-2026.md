**Why the 50mm f/1.8 wins for beginners:**
- The f/1.8 aperture lets in 3× more light than a kit lens at f/3.5 — portraits and indoor shots look completely different
- 50mm focal length (on full-frame) sees the world close to how your eyes do — easy to visualize shots before raising the camera

- Lightweight and small — lives on the camera and never gets left at home
- Forces you to move your feet instead of zooming — teaches compositional thinking

## Buying Guide: Choosing Your First Upgrade Lens

### Step 1: Match the mount
Lenses only work on their intended camera mount. Sony E lenses don't fit Nikon Z cameras. Always check compatibility before buying. Adapters exist but add complexity and may slow autofocus.

### Step 2: Prime vs. zoom
Primes (fixed focal length) are sharper, smaller, and cheaper for a given aperture. Zooms are more versatile but cost more for the same aperture. Beginners often benefit from a prime because it forces creative decisions about composition.

### Step 3: Focal length for your subject
- 24–35mm: landscapes, street, environmental portraits
- 50mm: street, documentary, portraits with context
- 85–135mm: flattering portraits, compressed backgrounds
- 70–300mm: wildlife, sports, distant subjects

### Step 4: Third-party brands are legitimate now
Sigma, Tamron, Viltrox, and Tokina all produce lenses that match or exceed OEM quality in their price range. Don't pay a brand premium if a third-party option performs better.

### Step 5: Used lenses are a serious option
Camera lenses hold their value well, and a used lens in good condition performs identically to new. Check eBay, MPB, or KEH for legitimate used inventory.

## FAQ

**Q: Should I buy a zoom or a prime lens as my first upgrade?**
For most beginners, a 50mm f/1.8 prime (or equivalent for your sensor size) teaches more about photography than a zoom upgrade. It costs less, performs better in low light, and forces you to compose by moving rather than twisting a zoom ring.

**Q: Does the brand of lens matter?**
For autofocus reliability and lens correction in-camera, native brand lenses have an edge. But third-party lenses from Sigma, Tamron, and Viltrox offer comparable or superior optical quality at lower prices, with native AF on major mounts.

**Q: Can I use a Canon lens on a Sony camera?**
With an adapter (such as the Sigma MC-11 or Metabones adapter), some Canon EF lenses work on Sony E-mount bodies with AF. However, native Sony or Sigma/Tamron E-mount lenses are faster and more reliable.

**Q: What aperture do I need for portraits?**
f/1.4–f/2.0 produces shallow depth-of-field backgrounds (bokeh). f/1.8 hits the sweet spot: affordable, great low-light performance, and pleasing background separation. f/2.8 is sufficient for studio work with controlled lighting.

**Q: Is image stabilization important in a prime lens?**
Modern mirrorless cameras have excellent in-body image stabilization (IBIS) that works with any lens. Lens-based stabilization (OSS, IS, VR) adds another layer but isn't critical if your body has IBIS. For static subjects in low light, both IBIS and lens IS help.
