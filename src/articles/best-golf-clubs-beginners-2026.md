## What Beginners Actually Need in Golf Clubs

The biggest mistake beginners make: buying the clubs pros use. Tour clubs are designed for players who make nearly perfect contact on every swing. Beginners need the opposite — maximum forgiveness for off-center hits, large sweet spots, and higher launch angles to compensate for imperfect swing paths.

### Forgiveness Features to Look For
- **Cavity-back irons** (weight distributed around the perimeter): forgive off-center hits better than muscle-back "blade" irons
- **Large club faces**: increase the sweet spot size
- **Wide soles**: reduce digging into the ground on fat shots
- **High MOI (Moment of Inertia)**: a measure of forgiveness — higher is better for beginners

### What Should Be in a Beginner Set
A proper beginner set (14 clubs maximum by rule) should include:
- **Driver** (1 club for long tee shots)
- **Fairway wood** (3-wood for long approach shots)
- **Hybrid** (replaces hard-to-hit long irons)
- **Irons 5–9** (mid and short approach shots)
- **Pitching wedge** (shorter approach shots)
- **Sand wedge** (bunker shots, short chips)
- **Putter** (putting on the green)
- **Bag** (to carry/roll all of the above)

## Beginner Golf FAQ

**Should I buy new or used clubs?**
New complete beginner sets often provide better value than used individual clubs because they're matched in shaft flex and grip size. Mismatched used clubs make learning harder, not easier.

**Do I need all 14 clubs?**
No — most beginners do well with 8–10 clubs. You'll rarely use a 3-iron, 4-iron, or 2-wood correctly as a beginner. Complete sets intelligently skip these.

**When should I upgrade?**
When you consistently break 90 (roughly 18 months to 2 years of regular play), you'll have enough swing consistency to benefit from fitted clubs. Before that, the clubs aren't your limiting factor.
