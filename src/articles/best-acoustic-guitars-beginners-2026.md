## Buyer's Guide

### Acoustic vs. Classical Guitar
**Acoustic (steel-string):** Bright, loud, versatile. Best for pop, rock, country, folk.
**Classical (nylon-string):** Softer on fingers, quieter. Best for classical, flamenco, fingerpicking.

Most beginners start with steel-string acoustic unless they know they want classical.

### Body Sizes
- **Dreadnought**: Most common, best for strumming, loudest
- **Concert/Grand Concert**: Smaller, comfortable for smaller players
- **Mini (3/4 size)**: For kids or travel

### Solid vs. Laminate Wood
**Solid wood top**: More resonant, improves with age, worth the extra cost
**Laminate**: Cheaper, more durable to humidity changes, doesn't improve with age

### Do I Need a Setup?
Most guitars benefit from a professional setup ($40–$80 at a music store) — lowering the action, adjusting the truss rod, checking intonation. Premium beginner guitars (Taylor Academy, Seagull) often don't need one; budget guitars almost always do.

### Essential Accessories for Beginners
1. **Tuner** — clip-on chromatic tuner or Fender tune app
2. **Picks** — variety pack (light, medium, heavy)
3. **Capo** — lets you play simple chords in different keys
4. **Extra strings** — strings break; buy extra Ernie Ball or D'Addario
5. **Guitar strap** — even if you sit while playing
6. **Humidifier** — crucial for acoustic guitars in dry climates (prevents cracking)
7. **Gig bag or case** — protect your investment

## FAQ

**How much should I spend on a first guitar?**
$150–$300 is the sweet spot. Under $100 is often discouraging (hard to play, bad tone). Over $400 as a beginner is wasteful. The Yamaha FG800 ($199) and Fender CD-60S ($199) are the standard recommendations at every price.

**Is it better to start on acoustic or electric?**
Acoustic builds finger strength faster. Electric is easier to press the strings. If you want to play rock or metal, start electric. For everything else, acoustic is the traditional starting point.

**How long does it take to learn guitar?**
Most people can play simple songs and chord progressions within 3 months of consistent practice (15–30 minutes daily). Playing fluently takes 1–3 years.

**Should I take lessons or learn online?**
Either works. Online resources like JustinGuitar.com are excellent and free. In-person lessons provide correction and accountability. Many beginners do both — YouTube for songs, occasional lessons for technique.

**Do I need to know music theory?**
No, but understanding basic concepts (notes, chords, scales) helps enormously. Many successful guitarists know very little theory; others know a lot. Start with chords and songs you love.
