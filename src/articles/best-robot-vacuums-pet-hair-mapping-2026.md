## What to Look For: Buying Guide for Pet Owners

### Suction Power (Pascal Ratings Explained)

Pascal (Pa) is the unit of pressure used to measure robot vacuum suction. Here's a practical breakdown:

- **Under 1,500 Pa:** Fine for bare floors and light debris. Struggles with pet hair on carpet.
- **1,500–2,500 Pa:** Handles pet hair on hard floors reliably. Inconsistent on medium-pile carpet.
- **3,000–5,000 Pa:** The sweet spot for pet owners. Pulls embedded hair from low and medium-pile carpet without issue.
- **6,000–10,000 Pa:** High-end territory — genuinely useful for thick rugs and heavy shedders, not just a spec sheet number.

For most homes with one or two pets, 3,000–5,000 Pa hits the right balance of cleaning power and battery efficiency. High-pile rugs or multiple large dogs justify going higher.

### Mapping Technology: LiDAR vs. Camera vs. Gyroscope

How a robot navigates determines how thoroughly it cleans — and whether "recharge and resume" works reliably.

**LiDAR:** The gold standard. Spins a laser to build a precise floor plan in minutes, even in the dark. Maps persist across sessions and enable reliable room-by-room scheduling.

**Camera-based (vSLAM):** Uses visual landmarks to map the space. Works well in lit rooms but struggles in the dark or in open-plan layouts. Less precise than LiDAR but cheaper to produce.

**Gyroscope/random bounce:** No real mapping — the robot bounces until it statistically covers the floor. Misses corners, re-covers cleaned areas, and can't resume after recharging. Avoid for homes over 800 sq ft.

### Battery Life vs. Home Size

| Home Size | Minimum Runtime Needed | Key Feature |
|-----------|----------------------|-------------|
| Under 1,000 sq ft | 60–90 min | Single charge usually sufficient |
| 1,000–2,000 sq ft | 90–120 min | Recharge and resume helpful |
| 2,000–3,500 sq ft | 120–180 min | Recharge and resume required |
| 3,500+ sq ft | 180+ min or multi-dock setup | LiDAR mapping essential |

### Self-Empty Docks: Worth the Extra Cost?

For pet owners, yes. A self-emptying dock vacuums the robot's onboard bin into a larger container after each run. Without one, you're emptying the dustbin daily. Docks with HEPA filtration help allergy-prone households most. Expect to spend $80–$150 more over a base model.

### Rubber Roller Brushes vs. Bristle Brushes

The most underappreciated spec for pet owners. Bristle brushes grab hair — then hair wraps around the bristles until the brush seizes up. You end up cutting it off with scissors weekly.

Rubber roller extractors use flexible fins that scoop hair into the bin without giving it anything to wrap around. Every pet owner should prioritize this feature over every other spec except suction.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What suction power do I need for pet hair on carpet?

For pet hair on carpet, aim for 3,000 Pa or higher. Anything under 2,000 Pa will struggle with embedded pet hair on medium-pile carpet. The Roborock S8 MaxV Ultra at 10,000 Pa is overkill for most, but 3,000–5,000 Pa covers pet hair on any carpet type reliably.

### Do robot vacuums really work for pet hair?

Yes, modern robot vacuums are highly effective for daily pet hair maintenance. Tangle-resistant rubber roller brushes (vs. bristle brushes) are the key feature — they grab pet hair without wrapping around the brush. Look for "rubber extractor" or "tangle-free" in the specs. Daily scheduled runs prevent buildup so the vacuum never faces an overwhelming amount at once.

### How long does a robot vacuum battery last for a large home?

Most robot vacuums cover 1,500–2,500 sq ft per charge with 90–180 minute runtimes. For homes over 2,000 sq ft, prioritize models with "recharge and resume" — the robot docks when low, charges, then picks up exactly where it stopped. LiDAR mapping is required for this feature to work reliably.
