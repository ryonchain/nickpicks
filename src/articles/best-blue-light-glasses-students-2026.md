## Buying Guide: Do Blue Light Glasses Actually Work?

### The Science

The scientific consensus is nuanced. Blue light from screens does affect the circadian rhythm (suppressing melatonin) and may contribute to eye strain. However, most eye strain from screens is likely caused by reduced blink rate (causing dry eyes) and sustained near-focus (accommodation fatigue) rather than blue light wavelengths specifically.

**What the research suggests:**
- Blue light glasses may improve sleep quality when worn in the evening (2–3 hours before bed)
- Clear-lens blue light glasses show weaker effects on eye strain in controlled trials
- Amber/yellow tints show stronger melatonin-related benefits

**The 20-20-20 Rule:** The most evidence-backed intervention for digital eye strain is the 20-20-20 rule — every 20 minutes, look at something 20 feet away for 20 seconds. Blue light glasses complement, but don't replace, this practice.

### Clear vs. Amber/Tinted Lenses

**Clear lenses:** Filter a narrower blue light spectrum, don't change color perception. Better for daytime use and color-sensitive work (design, photography).

**Amber/yellow lenses:** Filter a broader spectrum, improve contrast, notably impact evening use by blocking the blue light frequencies most associated with melatonin suppression. Best for studying at night.

### With or Without Prescription

- **Without Rx:** Plano (non-magnifying) blue light glasses available from all brands, any student can use
- **With Rx:** Felix Gray, Warby Parker, and Zenni Optical offer prescription blue light lenses — the most practical option for students who already wear corrective lenses
