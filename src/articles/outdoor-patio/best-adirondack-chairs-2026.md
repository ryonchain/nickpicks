**Bottom line:** For most buyers, the **POLYWOOD Classic Adirondack** (~$350) is the easiest yes — it looks great, requires zero maintenance, and the warranty backs it for life. If you love the feel of real wood, go cedar; if budget is tight, the Lifetime poly chair is a solid starter pick.

The Adirondack chair has been an American patio staple since 1903. The classic slatted back, wide armrests, and reclining seat are iconic. But the material you choose dramatically changes how much work you'll put in and how long it lasts.

---

## Material Breakdown: What You're Really Choosing

### Wood Adirondack Chairs
The original material. Cedar, pine, teak, and eucalyptus are common.

- **Cedar:** Naturally rot-resistant and aromatic. Needs sealing every 1–2 years but holds up well. Lighter than hardwoods.
- **Teak:** Premium hardwood with natural oils that repel water. The most durable wood option, but also the most expensive.
- **Pine:** Cheapest wood option. Requires the most maintenance — paint or stain annually, or it deteriorates quickly.

**Best for:** buyers who love the authentic look and feel of wood and don't mind annual upkeep.

### Poly Resin / HDPE Adirondack Chairs
Made from recycled plastics compressed into lumber-like boards. The best outdoor furniture innovation of the last 30 years.

- Won't rot, crack, splinter, warp, or fade
- Available in 20+ colors, permanently embedded in the material
- Heavier than wood — stays put in wind
- Virtually maintenance-free; spray with a hose and done

**Best for:** buyers who want zero maintenance and a chair that will outlast them.

### Metal / Aluminum Adirondack Chairs
Powder-coated aluminum or steel. Lightweight and stackable, but the classic Adirondack aesthetic is harder to achieve.

- Won't rot or absorb moisture
- Lightweight (aluminum) or heavy-duty (steel)
- Can get hot in direct sun
- Steel can rust if coating chips

**Best for:** buyers who want lightweight portability or a more industrial aesthetic.
