## Propane vs. Electric vs. Natural Gas: Choosing the Right Fuel

**Propane (LP gas):** Most common residential outdoor heater fuel. Standard tanks (20 lb / 5 gallon) are widely available at hardware stores and gas stations. No electrical outlet needed. High BTU output (30,000–46,000 BTU typical) heats large open areas. Requires good ventilation — not for enclosed spaces.

**Electric/Infrared:** Plugs into standard outlet. No combustion gases, no ventilation concerns — safe for screened-in porches and covered patios. Infrared heating (rather than forced air) heats objects and people directly rather than heating air that blows away in wind. More efficient than resistance heating but limited BTU ceiling vs. propane.

**Natural gas:** Requires a dedicated natural gas line (permanent installation). Best for commercial settings or homeowners with existing gas infrastructure. Eliminates propane tank logistics but significant setup cost.

**Wall-mounted infrared:** High-end option for commercial patios. Permanently mounted, directional infrared heating is extremely efficient and weather-resistant.

## Placement and Safety Guidelines

**Clearance:** Most propane heaters require at least 3 feet of clearance above the heater and 2 feet on all sides from combustible materials. Read your specific product manual.

**Ventilation:** Never use propane or natural gas heaters in enclosed spaces or spaces with limited air flow. Carbon monoxide accumulation is a serious safety risk.

**Stability:** Use on level surfaces. Always engage the tip-over safety switch. Never leave running unattended in high wind conditions.

**Propane storage:** Store propane tanks outside in a well-ventilated area. Never store indoors or in a garage.

**Winter storage:** Disconnect propane and store heater in a covered, dry location. Most heaters benefit from a weather cover year-round when not in use.
