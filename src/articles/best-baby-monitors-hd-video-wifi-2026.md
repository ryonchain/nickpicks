## Baby Monitor Buying Guide

### WiFi vs. Dedicated Radio (FHSS)

**WiFi monitors** let you check on your baby from anywhere via smartphone, even when you're at work or out at night. They require a stable home internet connection and are only as reliable as your WiFi router.

**Dedicated radio monitors** use a direct encrypted signal between the camera and parent unit — no internet required. They're more reliable for in-home use but can't be accessed remotely.

**Best choice:** If you want remote access from outside the home, choose WiFi. If you want the most reliable in-home signal with no internet dependency, choose FHSS radio.

### Night Vision: What to Look For

True night vision uses infrared LEDs — check that the monitor shows detail at the face and hands level, not just a silhouette. The Nanit Pro is the benchmark for night vision quality; most others are adequate but noticeably less detailed.

### Privacy and Security

A camera in your baby's room needs strong security. Look for:
- AES encryption on video transmission
- Two-factor authentication on the app
- No-subscription local storage option (reduces cloud exposure)
- A track record of security patches from the manufacturer

Avoid no-name brands on baby monitors — security audits matter here.

### Do You Need Sleep Tracking?

Sleep tracking (Nanit) is genuinely useful for:
- Sleep training — understanding sleep patterns helps parents make data-driven adjustments
- Tracking total sleep across naps and night sleep
- New parent reassurance with breathing motion monitoring (with breathing wear)

It's a nice-to-have, not a must-have. Most parents manage well without it.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How far can a baby monitor reach?**
Dedicated RF monitors like the Infant Optics DXR-8 Pro reach 1,000 ft in open space (300–500 ft through walls). WiFi monitors work anywhere you have internet — no distance limit.

**Can baby monitors be hacked?**
Any internet-connected device can theoretically be compromised. Buy from reputable brands with strong security records, enable two-factor authentication, and use unique passwords. Eufy, Nanit, and Infant Optics have strong security track records.

**When should I stop using a baby monitor?**
Most parents use monitors until around 2–4 years old, or when the child consistently sleeps through the night and can call out if they need help. There's no firm rule — use it as long as it provides value.

**Do I need a baby monitor with two-way audio?**
Two-way audio lets you soothe your baby with your voice without entering the room. It's especially useful for sleep training. Most modern monitors include it — only skip it if cost is a primary concern.

**Can I use a regular security camera as a baby monitor?**
Yes — cameras like the Wyze Cam or Eufy SoloCam work fine as DIY baby monitors. You lose dedicated features like night light, lullabies, and sleep tracking, but the video quality is often better. Good for a second room or cost-conscious parents.
