## Luggage Tag Buying Guide

### Material matters more than you think

**Silicone** is the most practical all-around material. It's waterproof, flexible (won't crack in cold cargo holds), brightly colorable, and cheap enough to replace if it breaks. The main downside is that it can look cheap on premium luggage.

**Leather** signals quality and is comfortable to handle, but it's not fully waterproof and can stiffen in cold weather. Genuine full-grain leather will outlast bonded or PU leather by years.

**Hard plastic (ABS)** is rigid and good for customizable insert cards. The tradeoff is brittleness — a hard impact can crack the shell. Look for thick-walled shells over thin ones.

**Metal** (aluminum or stainless steel) is the most durable option for the contact card itself. Laser engraving is permanent. The downside: if your information changes, you need a new tag.

### Closures: what actually holds under stress

The most common failure point for luggage tags is the attachment closure, not the tag itself. Strap loops made from the same silicone as the tag body tend to degrade over time. Look for:

- **Metal grommet-reinforced loop** — the loop passes through a metal eyelet, distributing stress rather than concentrating it at a single point
- **Adjustable buckle strap** — lets you tighten around thicker handle bars
- **Cable loop** — the most secure option, used on metal tags; rated for significant tensile load

Avoid cheap plastic S-hooks — they routinely open under the stress of bag handling.

### What to write on your tag

| Field | Recommended | Avoid |
|-------|-------------|-------|
| Name | First name + last initial | Full address |
| Phone | Mobile number | Home landline |
| Email | Personal email | Work email |
| Address | City/country only | Full street address |

### How many tags do you need?

At minimum, one external tag per piece of luggage. For checked bags, consider adding an AirTag or Tile inside the bag as a digital backup — external tags can fall off. For carry-ons, a tag is still useful for airline staff during gate checks.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What information should I put on a luggage tag?**
Include your name, phone number, and email address. Skip your home address — it tells strangers your house is empty while you travel. An email is safer than a phone number for international trips where your number may not work.

**Are leather luggage tags worth it?**
Leather tags last longer than plastic and look better with wear, but they're typically not waterproof. For checked baggage that gets soaked on tarmacs, silicone or hard-shell plastic tags are more practical. Leather is ideal for carry-ons and personal bags.

**Do I need a luggage tag if my bag has a built-in tag holder?**
Yes — many built-in tag holders use paper cards that fall out or become unreadable. An external tag is more visible to baggage handlers and airport staff who need to identify your bag quickly.

**What size luggage tag is best?**
Standard luggage tags are 4x3 inches — large enough to be readable at a distance. Oversized tags (5x3 or larger) are more visible but add bulk. For a carry-on you're stowing overhead, a slim profile tag is less likely to snag on bin edges.
