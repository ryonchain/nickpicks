## Camera Bag Buying Guide

### How to Choose the Right Capacity

| Kit Size | Recommended Capacity |
|---------|-------------------|
| 1 body + 1–2 lenses + accessories | 15–20L |
| 1 body + 3–4 lenses + laptop | 20–30L |
| 2 bodies + 4–6 lenses + flash | 30–45L |
| Multi-day adventure kit | 45–60L |

### Camera Access Speed: The Overlooked Factor

Most photographers default to rear-panel access backpacks without considering how often they want to access the camera during a shoot. For street and event photography, rear-panel access requires stopping, removing the bag, unzipping, and re-packing every time you switch lenses. Consider:

- **Side-zip access** (Peak Design): camera out in <3 seconds while wearing the bag
- **Front-panel access** (Manfrotto messenger): camera out in <2 seconds
- **Rear-panel access** (most backpacks): camera out in 15–30 seconds, requires removing bag

### Weatherproofing: What It Actually Means

"Water-resistant" means the fabric repels light rain and splashes. "Weatherproof" with an included rain cover handles sustained rain. Neither rating means the bag is waterproof — sustained heavy rain will eventually penetrate any bag. For shooting in rain, a rain cover is essential; consider keeping your most sensitive gear (lenses) in sealed plastic bags inside the bag as an additional layer of protection.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I buy a dedicated camera bag or use a regular bag with a camera insert?**
A dedicated camera bag has built-in protection and organization. A regular bag with a camera insert (like a Peak Design Capture Clip or a Tenba BYOB insert) is more versatile — you carry one bag for all purposes and the insert provides camera protection inside it. If you carry a camera daily but don't want to switch bags, the insert approach is worth considering.

**How do I protect cameras in a bag from humidity?**
Silica gel desiccant packets ($5–$10 for a pack) absorb moisture inside a sealed bag. Keep 2–3 packets in your camera compartment and replace them after 2–3 months of use. In humid climates or when storing gear long-term, a dry box with humidity control is more reliable than a bag.

**Are expensive camera bags worth it?**
The Peak Design Everyday Backpack at $300 protects a $2,000+ camera kit and gets used every day for years. Amortized over 5 years of daily use, that's $0.16/day of protection for expensive gear. Premium bags are worth the investment when you use the camera regularly. For occasional hobbyists, an Amazon Basics bag at $40 is perfectly adequate.
