## Memory Card + Storage Buying Guide

### Card Speed Ratings Explained

| Rating | Min Sustained Write | Best For |
|--------|-------------------|---------|
| V10 / Class 10 | 10 MB/s | Basic 1080p video |
| V30 | 30 MB/s | 4K video (standard bitrate) |
| V60 | 60 MB/s | 4K high bitrate / some 6K |
| V90 | 90 MB/s | 8K / ProRes / RAW video |
| CFexpress Type A | 700+ MB/s | 8K RAW / ALL-I / simultaneous recording |
| CFexpress Type B | 1,700+ MB/s | Cinema cameras / Nikon Z series |

Always buy one step above what your camera technically requires — headroom matters when the camera's buffer is full and must write quickly.

### Storage Capacity for Common Workflows

| Use Case | File Size Per Hour | Recommended Card |
|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| 4K H.264/HEVC | 5–8 GB | 256 GB V30 |
| 4K H.264 high quality | 15–25 GB | 256 GB V60 |
| 4K ProRes | 40–100 GB | 512 GB V60+ |
| 8K RAW | 250–500 GB | 512 GB CFexpress |
| RAW photos (24MP) | 40–80 MB/shot | 256 GB V30+ |

### Portable SSD vs External HDD

Portable SSDs are faster (1,000+ MB/s vs 120–160 MB/s for HDD), more durable (no moving parts), and significantly smaller. External HDDs are cheaper per GB (2TB HDD: ~$60 vs 2TB SSD: ~$110) and better for long-term archival where you're not actively writing and reading frequently. The right strategy: use portable SSDs for active project working storage, then archive to external HDDs once projects are complete.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How do I know if my card reader is fast enough to use my card's full speed?**
Most laptop USB ports limit to USB 3.0 (~625 MB/s). A V60 SD card maxes out at 200 MB/s, so a USB 3.0 port is sufficient. For CFexpress cards (800 MB/s+), you need a USB 3.2 Gen 2 or Thunderbolt port with a matching CFexpress reader to achieve maximum transfer speeds.

**Should I format my memory card in the camera or on my computer?**
Always format in the camera. Different cameras use different file system structures (FAT32, exFAT, proprietary) and formatting in-camera ensures the card is optimized for how that specific camera writes files. Computer-based formatting can cause slow write speeds or recording errors.

**How do I protect footage from card failure in the field?**
Three habits: (1) Use cameras with dual card slots and enable simultaneous recording. (2) Never fill a card completely — stop at 80% capacity and swap. (3) Back up footage to a portable SSD immediately after each shooting session, before leaving the location. A lost card after backing up is inconvenient; a lost card before backing up is a disaster.
