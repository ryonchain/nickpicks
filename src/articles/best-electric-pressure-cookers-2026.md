## Electric Pressure Cooker Buying Guide

### What Size Do You Actually Need?

| Household Size | Recommended Capacity | Why |
|----------------|---------------------|-----|
| 1–2 people | 3 Qt | Smaller portions, less waste, easier to store |
| 3–4 people | 6 Qt | Most popular size, handles family meals |
| 5+ people or batch cookers | 8 Qt | Large roasts, soups, batch meal prep |

**Rule of thumb:** Never fill a pressure cooker more than 2/3 full (pressure needs headroom). For soups and grains, fill only to the 1/2-full line. An 8 Qt is often necessary for families of 4 who batch cook.

### Understanding Pressure PSI

| PSI | Speed vs. 15 PSI (stovetop standard) |
|-----|--------------------------------------|
| 10 PSI (low) | ~25% longer cook times |
| 12 PSI (typical electric) | ~15% longer cook times |
| 15 PSI (Ninja Foodi, stovetop) | Fastest — stovetop speed |

Most electric pressure cookers max out at 10–12 PSI. The Ninja Foodi's 15 PSI matches stovetop performance.

### Common Meals and Approximate Cook Times

## FAQs

**Is pressure cooking safe?**
Modern electric pressure cookers have 10+ safety mechanisms including a pressure release valve, lid-lock indicator, overheat protection, and steam release shield. They are significantly safer than older stovetop pressure cookers. Follow the fill limits and use the correct liquid amounts, and safety incidents are extremely rare.

**Does pressure cooking destroy nutrients?**
Pressure cooking is actually one of the most nutrient-preserving cooking methods because the short cook time reduces nutrient degradation. Compared to long boiling or roasting, pressure cooking retains more vitamins and minerals.

**Can I use my Instant Pot as a slow cooker?**
Yes, all Instant Pots include a slow cook function. However, electric pressure cookers don't match the performance of dedicated slow cookers — the heat distribution is slightly different. For most slow-cook recipes, the results are acceptable but not identical to a Crock-Pot.

**What foods should I NOT pressure cook?**
Avoid pressure-cooking dairy (it scorches and burns), battered foods, pasta in large quantities (starch foams), and extremely lean fish fillets (they overcook in seconds at pressure). These are better suited to other cooking methods.
