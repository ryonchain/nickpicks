## Why Percussion Therapy Works for Recovery

Percussion massage guns work by delivering rapid, repetitive strokes (percussions) deep into muscle tissue — typically 20–40mm of amplitude at 1,200–3,200 RPM. This breaks up knots, increases blood flow, and reduces lactic acid buildup faster than foam rolling or static stretching alone. Clinical research supports percussion therapy for reducing perceived soreness and improving range of motion post-exercise.

Key specs to understand before buying:
- **Amplitude:** How deep the head travels (16mm = surface; 12mm = deeper tissue)
- **Stall force:** How much pressure the motor handles before stalling — higher = better for deep work
- **Speed settings:** More isn't always better; what matters is smooth, controllable range
- **Noise level (dB):** Important if you use it at the office or late at night

## Buying Guide: What to Look for in a Massage Gun

### Amplitude (Stroke Depth)
This is the most important spec. Standard models offer 10–12mm. Premium models like Theragun deliver 16mm. More amplitude = deeper tissue penetration for larger muscle groups. For neck and shoulder work, 10mm is plenty. For glutes, hamstrings, and back, 12–16mm makes a real difference.

### Stall Force
Stall force measures how much pressure the motor handles before it stops. Higher stall force (40+ lbs) means you can lean into the device on dense muscles without it cutting out. Low stall force models (<25 lbs) are fine for light use but frustrating during serious recovery work.

### Speed Range
5 speeds across 1,400–3,200 PPM gives you the flexibility to start light (warm-up or sensitive areas) and finish heavy (deep recovery). More speed options ≠ better; smooth, responsive speed control matters more.

### Noise Level
Budget guns often run 70–80 dB — loud enough to hear through walls. Premium models run 45–60 dB, quiet enough to use in a bedroom without waking a partner.

### Battery Life
Plan on 2–3 sessions per day at 15–20 minutes each. Models with 3-hour battery life will need nightly charging. 5–8 hour models give you a week of moderate use between charges.

### Attachments
Ball head: general use on large muscle groups. Fork head: spine-adjacent muscles and Achilles. Flat head: dense muscle groups. Thumb head: trigger point work. Dampener: bony areas and sensitive spots.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long should I use a massage gun on one area?**
2 minutes per muscle group is the standard guideline. Move continuously — don't hold in one spot for more than 30 seconds. For warm-up, 30–60 seconds per area is enough.

**Can a massage gun replace a foam roller?**
They serve similar purposes but are not identical. Massage guns are faster and reach deeper. Foam rollers cover larger surface areas and are better for fascial compression. Most athletes use both.

**Is it safe to use a massage gun on the neck?**
Yes, on the muscles of the neck (traps, SCM), but avoid bony areas, the spine itself, and the carotid artery. Use the dampener head and low speed. If you have cervical issues, consult a PT first.

**When should I use a massage gun — before or after a workout?**
Both. Before: 30 seconds per muscle group to increase blood flow and reduce warm-up time. After: 2 minutes per sore area to flush lactic acid and reduce DOMS.

**Are cheap massage guns worth it?**
Budget models (RENPHO, Sharper Image) work for light soreness and casual use. For serious athletes or anyone doing high-volume training, the stall force and amplitude of premium models pays off in recovery quality.

**Can I use a massage gun on injured muscles?**
Do not use percussion therapy directly on acute injuries (sprains, strains within 48–72 hours), bruises, or inflamed tissue. Consult a doctor or physical therapist for injury-specific guidance.
