## What to Look for in a Panini Press

### Plate Type and Material

**Ridged plates:** Create grill marks and drain fat away from food. Essential for authentic panini look and grilled meats.

**Smooth plates:** Better for quesadillas, crepes, and things where you don't want ridges. Some presses include both.

**Cast aluminum with nonstick coating:** Most common; heats quickly and is easy to clean.

**Removable plates:** The most convenient option — plates detach for dishwasher cleaning and can sometimes be swapped between ridged and smooth.

### Upper Plate Float vs. Fixed Height

A floating upper plate adjusts to the height of whatever is inside — essential for thick ciabatta, layered sandwiches, and burgers. Fixed-height upper plates compress everything to the same thickness, which can flatten thick fillings unevenly.

### Temperature Control

Many basic panini presses are thermostat-free — they just heat to a single temperature. Better models offer adjustable settings from warm (for reheating) through high (for searing). Temperature control gives you flexibility across different foods.

### Wattage

- **Under 1000W:** Slow heating, fine for sandwiches
- **1200–1500W:** Standard; handles most tasks well
- **1800W+:** Restaurant-speed heating; great for thick meats

Cleaning a non-removable panini press with fixed plates is annoying — you're wiping the hot plates with a damp cloth. Removable, dishwasher-safe plates make cleanup trivial. Pay the slight premium for this feature.

### Do You Cook More Than Sandwiches?

If you'll primarily use this for grilling meats and vegetables in addition to sandwiches, the **Cuisinart Griddler** is the right pick — its five configurations cover everything. For dedicated panini and quesadilla use only, the **Hamilton Beach** is more than sufficient.

Different breads toast at different rates. Sourdough needs slightly lower heat than ciabatta. Without temperature control, you're guessing. If you're particular about your panini, invest in adjustable temperature.

## FAQ: Panini Presses

**How long does it take to press a panini?**
At optimal temperature (~375–400°F), 3–5 minutes for a standard panini. Thicker sandwiches with cold fillings take up to 7 minutes.

**Do I need to butter the bread before pressing?**
Yes — a light coating of butter or olive oil on the exterior of the bread creates the characteristic golden, crispy crust. Without it, the bread toasts but doesn't get the same glossy result.

**Can I use a panini press for waffles?**
Only with waffle plates — some presses (like the Cuisinart Griddler) sell optional waffle plate inserts. Standard ridged plates will not produce proper waffles.

**How do I prevent cheese from oozing onto the plates?**
Keep cheese inside the sandwich, away from the edges. Don't overfill — leave ½ inch of clearance on all edges.

**How do I clean non-removable plates?**
Let the press cool to warm (not hot). Wipe with a damp cloth. For stuck bits, place a damp paper towel inside and close the press for 2–3 minutes to steam loosen residue. Never submerge in water.
