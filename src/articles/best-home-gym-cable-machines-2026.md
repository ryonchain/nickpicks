## Cable Machine Exercise Guide

**Upper body (cable-specific advantages):**
- **Cable flyes:** Constant pectoral tension vs. dumbbell (tension drops at top)
- **Cable rows:** Variable angle options, smooth resistance
- **Tricep pushdowns:** Most commonly performed cable exercise
- **Face pulls:** Essential for shoulder health and posture
- **Cable curls:** Constant bicep tension at top of movement

**Lower body and core:**
- **Cable pull-throughs:** Hip hinge pattern with constant tension
- **Cable woodchops:** Anti-rotation core strength
- **Standing cable crunches:** Core with resistance
- **Cable hip abduction:** Glute development

**Single-cable exercises (for single pulley systems):**
Any bilateral cable exercise can be performed unilaterally — simply add the movement to both sides individually.

## Cable Machine Buying Guide

**Weight stack size:**
For most home gym users, 150–200 lbs per stack is sufficient. With a 2:1 cable ratio, a 150 lb stack produces 75 lbs of resistance at the handle (typical for cable exercises).

**Pulley positions:**
More adjustment points = more exercise variety. 16+ positions cover everything from floor-level ankle work to overhead pulls.

**Footprint:**
- Compact functional trainers: 47"×27" — fits in 8'×8' space minimum
- Full-size commercial: 72"×48" — requires 12'×12' minimum

**Weight ratio (1:1 vs. 2:1):**
- 1:1: What you stack = what you lift. Less range of motion.
- 2:1: Lift half the stack weight with twice the range of motion. Better for exercises.

## FAQ

**Can a home cable machine replace a commercial gym?**
For cable exercises specifically — yes. For the sheer variety and heaviness of commercial gym cable stacks, you'll need to spend $2,000+ for true commercial equivalence.

**What accessories do I need?**
At minimum: a single handle, a rope attachment, and a straight bar. A full set adds ankle strap, V-bar, and lat pulldown bar. Most are sold as accessory sets for $30–$60.

**How much space does a functional trainer need?**
Budget 8'×8' minimum for a compact model. Include standing room — you'll work up to 4 feet away from the machine on many exercises.

**Is a functional trainer worth it vs. resistance bands?**
Resistance bands are effective for the price but don't replicate the smooth, consistent resistance curve of cables. For serious training, cable machines are worth the investment.
