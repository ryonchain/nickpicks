## Beginner's Guide to Choosing a Mirrorless Camera

### APS-C vs. Full Frame vs. Micro Four Thirds — Which Sensor Size?
**APS-C** (most picks here): ~25×17mm sensor. Best balance of image quality, lens size, and price. The sweet spot for beginners.
**Full Frame** (Sony A7 series, Canon R6, Nikon Z6): 35×24mm sensor — professional quality, better low light. $1,500+ body-only. Not for beginners.
**Micro Four Thirds** (OM System, Panasonic): ~18×14mm. Smaller sensor but more compact system, excellent for video and travel. Lower low-light ceiling.

### What Lens to Buy First
The 16–50mm or 18–55mm kit zoom is the right first lens for most beginners — covers wide to short telephoto, great for learning. The second lens most people love: a fast prime (like the 35mm f/1.8 or 50mm f/1.8) — dramatically better low-light and that shallow depth-of-field "blurry background" look.

### Autofocus Matters More Than Megapixels
All these cameras have 20–26MP, which is more than enough for any display or print. The real differentiator is autofocus: Sony's tracking is the current gold standard. For action and moving subjects, prioritize AF performance over specs.

### Video vs. Photo Priority
For video: Sony ZV-E10 II or Nikon Z30 — designed for creators. For photos: Fujifilm X-T30 II or Sony A6100. For both: Sony ZV-E10 II is the safest choice.

## FAQ

**Is a mirrorless camera worth it over a DSLR in 2026?**
Yes. The major manufacturers (Sony, Canon, Nikon, Fujifilm) have all shifted R&D to mirrorless. DSLR lenses are still supported via adapters, but new lens development is mirrorless-only. Mirrorless also offers superior AF, video, and electronic viewfinders.

**Do I need to shoot in RAW?**
Not immediately. Modern JPEG processing is excellent — all these cameras produce great JPEGs out of the box. Shoot JPEG first, learn composition and exposure, then add RAW when you start editing. Don't make photography complicated before you've learned the basics.

**How long will a beginner camera last?**
A Sony APS-C body with good lenses will serve most photographers for 5–10 years. The lenses are the long-term investment — buy quality glass, it outlasts camera bodies. The body matters less than people think once you're above a basic threshold.

**What memory card should I buy?**
For most beginner cameras: a UHS-I U3 V30 card (64–128GB). Brands: Sony, SanDisk, or Lexar. Avoid cheap no-name cards — they cause lost photos and camera errors. A 128GB V30 card runs $15–25 and handles 4K video without buffer issues.
