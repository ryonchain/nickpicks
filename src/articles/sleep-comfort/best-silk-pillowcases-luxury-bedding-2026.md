## Buying Guide

### Understanding Silk Quality

**Momme weight**: The density of silk. Higher = heavier, more durable fabric. 19mm (minimum), 22mm (recommended), 25mm (premium durability).

**Mulberry vs. wild silk**: Mulberry silk comes from silkworms raised on mulberry leaves in controlled conditions — the result is a finer, more uniform fiber. Wild silk is coarser and less consistent. All pillowcases on this list use mulberry silk.

**Grade 6A**: The highest silk grade, indicating superior fiber length, whiteness, and uniformity. Grade 6A is what reputable brands use. Lower grades are often found in unbranded alternatives.

**The burn test**: Genuine silk burns slowly, smells like burning hair, and produces a crushable ash. Polyester satin melts, beads, and smells synthetic. If you're unsure about a "silk" pillowcase you own, this test is definitive.

### Why Thread Count Matters Less Than Weave

For cotton sheets, weave type matters more than thread count:

- **Percale** (1-over-1 weave): Crisp, lightweight, breathable. Better for hot sleepers.
- **Sateen** (4-over-1 weave): Silky, heavier, warmer. Better for cool sleepers who want a luxury feel.
- **Linen**: Most breathable, initially rough, softens significantly over time.

High thread counts (800+) are often achieved by using thinner, lower-quality threads twisted together — genuine quality is better indicated by the fiber source and weave.

### Silk Benefits for Skin and Hair

**Hair**: Reduces friction-caused breakage and frizz. Particularly beneficial for curly, color-treated, and chemically processed hair. Hair maintains its natural oils throughout the night rather than being absorbed by cotton.

**Skin**: Reduces mechanical creasing from sleeping on rough surfaces. Beneficial for preventing sleep wrinkles, though not as impactful as consistent sun protection and moisturizer use.

**Temperature**: Silk is naturally temperature-regulating — neither as warm as flannel nor as cool as percale cotton, but adapts to body temperature.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do silk pillowcases actually prevent hair breakage?**
Yes. Cotton pillowcases create friction against hair strands, causing cuticle damage, frizz, and breakage — particularly for curly, color-treated, or chemically processed hair. Silk's smooth surface allows hair to slide without resistance, reducing mechanical damage overnight. Multiple studies and dermatologists support this benefit.

**What momme weight should I look for in a silk pillowcase?**
Momme (mm) is the weight measurement for silk — higher momme means denser, more durable fabric. 19 momme is the minimum for quality pillowcases. 22–25 momme is ideal for durability and feel. Above 25 momme starts to feel heavier and less breathable. Avoid anything under 16 momme marketed as "silk" — it will pill and degrade quickly.

**Is silk or satin better for hair and skin?**
Silk (a natural protein fiber) is superior to satin (usually polyester with a satin weave). True silk is naturally breathable, hypoallergenic, and smooth at the fiber level. Satin can look similar but doesn't have silk's moisture-regulating or amino acid properties. Price is the giveaway: genuine silk pillowcases cost $40+.

**Can you machine wash silk pillowcases?**
Some high-quality silk pillowcases (Slip, Fishers Finery) are machine washable on delicate cycle in cold water. Most manufacturers recommend hand washing or a dedicated silk detergent (Woolite, Heritage Park Silk Wash) to preserve fiber integrity. Never use a dryer — hang or lay flat to dry.

**What is the best sheet material for hot sleepers?**
Percale cotton and bamboo-derived lyocell (Tencel) are the best sheet materials for hot sleepers. Percale is crisp and breathable; bamboo/lyocell is softer with excellent moisture wicking. Sateen and microfiber trap heat. Linen is the most breathable option of all, though it feels rougher initially.
