## Buying Guide: What to Look For

### Shell Material

**Percale cotton**: The classic hot-sleeper choice. Crisp, breathable, gets softer with every wash.

**Tencel/lyocell**: Derived from eucalyptus. More moisture-wicking than cotton. Best for active sweaters.

**Bamboo viscose**: Good moisture management, soft feel, less durable than Tencel.

**Sateen/microfiber**: Avoid if you sleep hot. Beautiful feel, but traps heat.

### Fill Options

**Down (600+ fill power)**: Best breathability of any fill, excellent loft-to-weight. Not hypoallergenic. Needs occasional fluffing.

**Down alternative (polyester)**: Hypoallergenic, easy-care. Retains more heat than down. Look for thinner fills.

**Tencel fill**: Most breathable non-down option. Good for allergy sufferers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What makes a comforter 'cooling'?**
Cooling comforters achieve lower sleep temperatures through breathable natural fibers (bamboo, cotton, Tencel), moisture-wicking fill (down, down-alternative), phase-change material (PCM) that absorbs body heat, and open-weave construction that allows airflow. The best options combine two or more of these strategies.

**What fill power should hot sleepers look for in down?**
Higher fill power (600+) means more loft with less fill material, which creates better airflow through the comforter. For hot sleepers, a 700–800 fill power lightweight down comforter is the gold standard — maximum loft, minimum insulation.

**Is bamboo actually cooler than cotton?**
Bamboo-derived viscose (often called "bamboo") is more moisture-wicking than standard cotton but not necessarily cooler at the surface. TENCEL (lyocell from eucalyptus) outperforms both for moisture management. Percale-weave cotton is still better than satin or sateen weaves for breathability.

**Can a cooling blanket replace air conditioning?**
No, but it can reduce how hard your AC needs to work. Most cooling blankets lower perceived skin temperature by 2–5°F through moisture wicking and airflow — enough for comfort in 72–76°F rooms, less effective above 80°F.

**Should hot sleepers use a top sheet?**
Yes. A lightweight percale or Tencel top sheet acts as a moisture-wicking barrier between your body and the comforter, extending how long the cooling effect lasts before the comforter saturates with body heat.
