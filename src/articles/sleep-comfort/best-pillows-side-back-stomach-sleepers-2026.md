## Pillow Buying Guide

### Choosing by Sleep Position

**Side sleepers** need 4–6 inches of loft to bridge shoulder width. Too low and the neck bends toward the mattress. Medium-firm fill with good loft stability is essential.

**Back sleepers** need medium loft (3–5 inches) with gentle contouring. The pillow should cradle the cervical curve, not push the head forward or let it drop back.

**Stomach sleepers** need the lowest possible loft (1–3 inches) and softest fill. Any height causes neck rotation and strain over 6–8 hours.

### Fill Materials Compared

**Down**: Luxurious, lightweight, compressible. Not hypoallergenic. Requires more care. Best for stomach sleepers.

**Memory foam (solid)**: Consistent support, no fluffing needed. Retains heat. Can't adjust height. Good for back/side sleepers.

**Shredded foam**: Adjustable, breathable, machine washable. Less luxurious feel. Best all-position option.

**Latex**: Responsive, durable, naturally cool. Heavier. Typically more expensive. Good for side and back sleepers.

### Pillow Size Guide

- **Standard (20×26")**: Fits most people and all beds.
- **Queen (20×30")**: Slightly longer — good for people who move a lot.
- **King (20×36")**: Best for king beds to prevent falling off the edge.

## Common Pillow Mistakes

**Using two pillows**: Stacking pillows doubles loft and kinks the neck forward. One properly fitted pillow is always better than two.

**Never replacing pillows**: Pillow fill breaks down even when it looks fine. Test by folding in half — if it stays folded, it's dead.

**Ignoring mattress firmness**: A firm mattress requires less pillow loft (the mattress provides more pushback at the shoulder). A soft mattress requires more pillow loft because shoulders sink in.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What pillow loft is best for side sleepers?**
Side sleepers need a high loft (4–6 inches) to fill the gap between the shoulder and head. Look for firm or medium-firm pillows that maintain height throughout the night without compressing flat.

**Can the wrong pillow cause neck pain?**
Yes. A pillow that's too high forces the neck into a flexed position; too low leaves it unsupported. Neck pain that eases after a few hours of being upright is often a pillow problem, not a structural issue.

**How often should you replace your pillow?**
Most pillows should be replaced every 1–2 years. A quick test: fold your pillow in half. If it doesn't spring back, the fill has broken down and it's no longer providing adequate support.

**Are memory foam pillows good for all sleep positions?**
Memory foam works best for back and side sleepers who need consistent support. Stomach sleepers typically need softer, lower-loft pillows — solid memory foam is usually too firm and tall for prone sleeping.

**What is the best pillow for hot sleepers?**
Latex and shredded-foam pillows with breathable covers sleep cooler than solid memory foam. The Purple Harmony Pillow's open-grid design is specifically engineered for temperature-neutral sleep.
