## Buying Guide

### White, Pink, or Brown Noise?

- **White noise**: All frequencies equal. Works well for masking high-frequency sounds (voices, TV).
- **Pink noise**: More bass than white. Sounds like rain. Many people find it more soothing for sleep.
- **Brown noise**: Heavy bass. Sounds like deep wind or thunder. Preferred for high-anxiety sleepers.

Start with pink or brown if white noise feels harsh. All three mask disruptive sounds effectively.

### Electronic vs. Mechanical Fan

**Electronic**: More sound variety, consistent output, no moving parts to fail. Sounds slightly synthetic.

**Mechanical fan (Dohm, SNOOZ)**: The real fan creates a more natural, warm sound that many people prefer. Limited sound variety — basically one sound at multiple speeds.

### Volume and Masking

Effective masking requires the white noise to be louder than the ambient intrusions you're masking. Typical traffic in suburban areas peaks at 65–70 dB at the bedroom — your machine needs to reach 60+ dB to be effective. All machines on this list achieve this.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does white noise actually improve sleep?**
Yes — studies consistently show white noise reduces sleep onset time and decreases nocturnal awakenings. It works by masking sudden sound spikes (traffic, roommates, snoring partners) rather than eliminating sound. The consistent background sound prevents the brain from startling awake at loud interruptions.

**What is the difference between white, pink, and brown noise?**
White noise contains all frequencies at equal intensity — sounds like static. Pink noise has more energy at lower frequencies — sounds like rain. Brown noise (also called red noise) has even more bass — sounds like deep wind or distant thunder. Many people find pink and brown noise more soothing than white, but all three mask disruptive sounds effectively.

**How loud should a white noise machine be?**
The AAP recommends no louder than 50 dB for infants. For adults, 50–65 dB is the effective masking range — loud enough to cover ambient sounds, low enough to avoid long-term hearing risk. Most machines max out at 70–85 dB; you'll use them at 50–60% of max.

**Are white noise machines better than apps?**
Dedicated machines produce continuous, non-looping sounds and don't drain your phone battery or generate blue light. Phone apps are adequate for travel. At home, a dedicated machine is more reliable and doesn't interfere with sleep hygiene by keeping your phone active by the bed.

**Can white noise machines help with tinnitus?**
White noise machines are often recommended by audiologists for tinnitus management. The consistent background sound makes the tinnitus less perceptible, reducing the psychological impact. Look for machines with a broad frequency range and precise volume control.
