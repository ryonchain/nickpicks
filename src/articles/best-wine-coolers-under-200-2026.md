## Wine Cooler Buying Guide

### Thermoelectric vs. Compressor

| Feature | Thermoelectric | Compressor |
|---------|----------------|------------|
| Noise level | Nearly silent | Low hum |
| Vibration | None | Slight |
| Works in warm rooms (>75°F) | No | Yes |
| Energy use (small units) | Lower | Higher |
| Best for | Cool-room placement | Any environment |

**Rule of thumb:** If your kitchen stays under 75°F, thermoelectric is preferred (quieter, no vibration). If you're placing the cooler in a garage, basement, or warm room, buy a compressor model.

### Single-Zone vs. Dual-Zone

- **Single-zone:** One temperature throughout. Best for people who mostly drink one type (all reds or all whites).
- **Dual-zone:** Two independently controlled compartments. Best for people who drink both red and white regularly.

### Temperature Guidelines

| Wine Type | Storage Temperature | Serving Temperature |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Red wine (full-body) | 55–58°F | 60–65°F |
| Red wine (light-body) | 55°F | 55–60°F |
| White wine (dry) | 50–55°F | 45–50°F |
| Sparkling/Champagne | 45–50°F | 40–45°F |
| Dessert wine | 50°F | 45–50°F |

## FAQ

**Can I store wine in a regular refrigerator?**
Short-term (days to weeks): yes. Long-term: no. Regular refrigerators run 35–38°F (too cold for wine), have no humidity control (corks dry out), and the vibration and odors from food affect wine over time.

**Do wine coolers use a lot of electricity?**
Small thermoelectric units (8–18 bottles) use approximately 50–80W — roughly $3–$5/month. Compressor units use slightly more, approximately $5–$10/month. Much less than a regular refrigerator.

**How many bottles do I actually need to store?**
If you buy wine case by case (12 bottles), a 12–24 bottle cooler makes sense. If you collect wine and buy by the case regularly, look at 38–54 bottle capacity.
