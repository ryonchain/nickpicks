## Why Solar Path Light Quality Varies So Much

The difference between good and bad solar path lights comes down to three components:

**Battery capacity**: Cheap lights use thin 600 mAh batteries that deplete by midnight. Good lights use 1,200–2,000 mAh batteries that last all night even after a partly cloudy charging day.

**Solar panel efficiency**: Small, low-grade panels charge slowly and can't recover after cloudy days. Monocrystalline silicon panels (used in quality lights) are more efficient in partial shade and indirect sun.

**Weatherproofing**: IP44 means protected from splashing water — insufficient for rain. IP65 means fully dust-tight and protected from water jets — the right rating for outdoor lights that will face storms.

## Buying Guide: Choosing Solar Path Lights

### Understanding Lumens for Path Lights

Pathway lighting doesn't need to be bright — 10–30 lumens per stake is standard for border marking. For functional illumination that actually lights the path for walking, 50–100 lumens per stake is better. Security lighting requires 300–2,500 lumens. Match lumens to purpose.

### IP Rating for Outdoor Use

At ground level, path lights face splashing from rain, hoses, and sprinklers. IP44 is adequate for light rain exposure but not jets of water. IP65 handles direct water spray from any angle. For stakes near lawn sprinklers or in rainy climates, IP65 is recommended.

### Battery Capacity and Winter Performance

In winter, days are shorter and sun is lower in the sky, reducing charging efficiency. Lights with 600 mAh batteries may not fully charge on a December day in northern regions, leading to early shutoff. 1,200–2,000 mAh batteries provide a buffer for cloudy periods.

### Solar Panel Positioning

All solar path lights require direct sun on the panel for 6–8 hours daily. Panels pointing north or shaded by trees/structures will underperform. Most stake lights have the panel integrated into the top — the stake must point toward maximum sun exposure. Wall-mount lights often have separate adjustable panels.

### Spacing for Visual Effect

For garden paths: stake lights every 6–8 feet on alternate sides creates rhythm without clutter. For a border look: every 3–4 feet on one side. For security lighting: space motion sensors so coverage zones overlap.

## FAQ

**Do solar path lights work in winter?**
Yes, with caveats. Cold temperatures actually improve battery performance. The issue is shorter daylight — fewer hours to charge. Lights in northern regions with snow cover on panels won't charge until the snow is cleared. Quality lights with larger batteries perform better through cloudy stretches.

**How long do solar path lights last?**
The solar panel and LED components last 5–10 years. The battery is the weak point — NiMH batteries (standard in most solar lights) degrade after 2–3 years of daily charge/discharge cycles. Some manufacturers sell replacement batteries.

**Do I need to do anything to install solar path lights?**
Installation is typically push-the-stake-in installation with no tools. The first charge usually requires 8 hours of direct sun before first use. Turn on the switch on the bottom/back of each light.

**Why are my solar lights dim after one year?**
Battery capacity degradation is the most common cause. After 2–3 years of daily cycling, NiMH batteries hold less charge, resulting in shorter runtime or dimmer output. The fix is battery replacement if the unit allows it, or replacement of the whole unit if sealed.
