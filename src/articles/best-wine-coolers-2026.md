## Wine Storage Guide

**Serving vs. storage temperature:**

| Wine Type | Serving Temp | Long-Term Storage |
|-----------|-------------|-------------------|
| Full-body red (Cabernet, Malbec) | 60–65°F | 55°F |
| Light red (Pinot Noir, Beaujolais) | 55–60°F | 55°F |
| Full-body white (Chardonnay) | 50–55°F | 55°F |
| Light white/Rosé | 45–50°F | 55°F |
| Sparkling/Champagne | 40–45°F | 55°F |

For a dual-zone cooler: set the upper zone to 40–50°F for whites and sparkling, lower zone to 55–65°F for reds.

**Compressor vs. thermoelectric:**
- **Compressor:** More efficient at maintaining temp in warm rooms. Slight vibration (minimized in quality models). Better for larger coolers.
- **Thermoelectric:** Completely silent, zero vibration (ideal for aged wines). Less efficient in warm ambient temps. Best for smaller countertop units.

**What ruins wine in improper storage:**
1. **Temperature fluctuation** — larger problem than absolute temperature
2. **Vibration** — disturbs sediment, accelerates oxidation
3. **UV light** — causes premature aging
4. **Low humidity** — dries cork, allows oxidation (ideal: 50–70% RH)

## FAQ

**What temperature should I store red wine?**
For long-term aging, 55°F is ideal for all wine types. For immediate drinking, serve reds at 60–65°F.

**Can I store wine in a regular refrigerator?**
For short periods (2–4 weeks), yes. A regular fridge is too cold and too dry for long-term storage — it'll dry corks and mute flavors over time.

**How do I know if a wine cooler will fit under my counter?**
Standard under-counter height is 34". Wine coolers designed for under-counter installation are typically 32–33" tall. Measure your opening height and check the product spec carefully.

**Is a wine cooler worth it for casual drinkers?**
If you regularly buy bottles to enjoy within 1–3 months: a basic 18-bottle thermoelectric unit at $100–$150 is worthwhile. If you're building a collection for aging: dual-zone compressor model is the right investment.
