## Linen Bedding Buying Guide

### Why Choose Linen Over Cotton?

| Property | Linen | Cotton |
|----------|-------|--------|
| Breathability | Excellent | Good |
| Temperature regulation | Excellent | Fair |
| Initial softness | Lower | Higher |
| Softness with age | Improves significantly | Decreases |
| Durability | 2–3× cotton lifespan | Standard |
| Wrinkle resistance | Low (wrinkles easily) | Moderate to good |
| Price | Premium | Wide range |

### Linen Weight Guide (GSM)
- **140–160 GSM**: Very lightweight, hot climates, summer only
- **170–190 GSM**: Ideal all-season weight, most buyers
- **200–240 GSM**: Heavier, more structured, cooler climates

### Caring for Linen Bedding
- Wash in cold or warm water (not hot — heat damages linen fibers)
- Tumble dry on low or air dry — over-drying causes brittleness
- Remove from dryer slightly damp to reduce wrinkles
- Iron if desired (high heat, steam) or embrace the natural wrinkled aesthetic
- Don't use fabric softener — it coats fibers and reduces linen's natural moisture-wicking

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is linen bedding scratchy?**
Pure linen can feel coarse initially, especially compared to high-thread-count cotton. After 5–10 washes it softens noticeably. Stone-washed or enzyme-washed linen (Bokser, Quince) starts softer. Linen-blend sets (Mellanni, Cariloha) also start with more softness due to the blended fibers.

**Does linen keep you cool at night?**
Yes, significantly more than cotton at equivalent weight. Linen's hollow fiber structure and natural moisture-wicking actively draw heat and moisture away from the body. Hot sleepers consistently report improved sleep quality after switching to linen.

**How long does linen bedding last?**
Quality linen bedding lasts 15–20 years with proper care — roughly twice the lifespan of comparable cotton. Linen fibers become stronger (not weaker) when wet, which means washing actually reinforces rather than degrades the fabric over time.

**What's the difference between French, Belgian, and Lithuanian linen?**
All three regions grow high-quality flax in similar climatic conditions. French and Belgian linen are traditional premium sources and command higher prices. Lithuanian linen is high quality and more affordable. In practice, the fabrication method (how the yarn is spun and woven) matters as much as the source country.
