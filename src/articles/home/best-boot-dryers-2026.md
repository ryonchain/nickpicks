    answer: "Electric boot dryers use a motor to circulate warm or room-temperature air through tubes that insert into the boot's interior. The forced air circulation removes moisture from the inside out — much faster and more thorough than leaving boots near a radiator or at room temperature.   - question: "What's the difference between heated and non-heated boot dryers?"
    answer: "Heated dryers use a heating element plus a fan — faster drying (2–4 hours for very wet boots) but can damage heat-sensitive materials if overheated. Non-heated (fan-only) dryers use room temperature air — gentler, safe for all materials including Gore-Tex and delicate leather, but slower (4–8 hours). For ski boots, insulated winter boots, and leather work boots, non-heated or low-heat dryers are recommended. For rubber boots and synthetic materials, either works."
  - question: "Can a boot dryer damage boots?"

Good boot dryers operate at 100–120°F maximum, well below damage thresholds. Air-only dryers (no heat) are safe for everything. For ski boots with heat-moldable liners, avoid heat dryers or use the lowest setting — heat may reshape the liner's custom fit."

## When a Boot Dryer Pays for Itself

**Work boots:** Heavy leather or composite-toe work boots that get wet daily dry in 2–4 hours vs. 24–48 hours passively. Dry boots at the start of the next shift. Materials last significantly longer when not subjected to repeated wet-dry stress.

**Ski and snow boots:** Ski boots with heat-moldable liners are expensive ($400–$1,000+). Drying them properly each night maintains the custom fit and prevents liner breakdown. A $30 boot dryer protects a $500 investment.

**Kids' school boots and shoes:** Children arrive home with wet boots constantly in winter. A 4-port dryer handles the family's footwear overnight.

**Running shoes:** Post-run shoes dry in 2–3 hours — ready for the next morning's run.

The average boot dryer pays for itself in extended footwear life within the first year of regular use.
