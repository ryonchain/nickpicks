## Hiking Boot Buying Guide

### Boot Height
- **Low-cut (shoe):** Best for fast day hikes on maintained trails. Lighter, more agile.
- **Mid-cut:** Slight ankle support, good all-around option for moderate terrain.
- **High-cut:** Full ankle support — best for backpacking with heavy loads and off-trail terrain.

### Waterproofing
GORE-TEX is the gold standard. Proprietary membranes (M Select DRY, NTX, OutDry) are generally comparable. Non-waterproof options dry faster but won't keep feet dry in stream crossings or sustained rain.

### Fit Tips
- Try on with the socks you'll hike in (medium or heavy wool is most common)
- Your heel should not lift when you walk — heel slippage causes blisters
- You should have a thumb's width of space in front of your longest toe for downhill trail comfort
- Lace bottom laces tightly (foot hold), upper laces moderately (circulation)

### Break-In Period
Most leather and synthetic leather boots need 20–50 miles to fully break in. Wear them on short local hikes before a long trip.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Are waterproof hiking boots worth it?**
For most hikers, yes. GORE-TEX keeps feet dry in rain, morning dew, and stream crossings. The trade-off is slightly reduced breathability, which is rarely noticeable except in very hot, dry conditions.

**How long should hiking boots last?**
400–1,000 miles depending on terrain and boot construction. Watch for outsole wear, midsole compression (boots feel "flat"), and delamination as signs it's time to replace.

**Can I use trail running shoes instead of hiking boots?**
For maintained trails and day hikes without a heavy pack, trail runners are excellent alternatives. For off-trail, heavy loads, or technical terrain, boots provide better support and protection.
