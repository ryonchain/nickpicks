## Cast Iron Cookware Buying Guide

### Bare Cast Iron vs Enameled: Which to Choose?

| Factor | Bare Cast Iron | Enameled Cast Iron |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| Initial cost | Lower | Higher |
| Maintenance | Must season | None required |
| Acidic foods | Reacts (avoid tomatoes, wine) | No reaction |
| Non-stick | Develops over time | Better from start |
| Metal utensils | Yes | No (avoid scratching) |
| Searing performance | Excellent | Excellent |
| Aesthetics | Industrial | Colorful/decorative |
| Repair | Re-season if needed | Can chip (not repairable) |

### The Myth of "Cast Iron Is Hard to Maintain"
Lodge bare cast iron maintenance is simple:
1. Clean immediately after cooking while still slightly warm
2. Hand-wash with minimal soap (brief soap exposure doesn't destroy seasoning)
3. Dry completely with a paper towel or on low heat
4. Apply a very thin coat of any cooking oil to the cooking surface
5. That's it — 2 minutes of care per use

### Building Seasoning Over Time
New cast iron improves with every high-fat cooking session. For fastest seasoning development:
- Fry bacon, sauté vegetables in butter, fry eggs
- Avoid boiling water or steaming in new cast iron — water fights seasoning
- After 10–15 cooking sessions, the surface becomes noticeably more non-stick

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Lodge as good as Le Creuset?**
For bare cast iron (no enamel): Lodge is comparable or better than anything in Le Creuset's bare line, at a lower price. For enameled cast iron (Dutch ovens, braisers): Le Creuset uses a more premium enamel system, but Lodge Enameled delivers 80% of the performance at 25% of the price. Staub enameled is what professionals actually use when they want to spend Le Creuset money.

**Can cast iron be used on induction cooktops?**
Yes — all bare and enameled cast iron is induction-compatible because the material is magnetic. Cast iron is actually one of the best materials for induction cooking because of its heat retention.

**What's the best size cast iron skillet to start with?**
10.25-inch (the Lodge standard). It handles a 12-oz steak, four chicken thighs, or six eggs simultaneously — the most versatile size for a household of 2–4. Go 12-inch if you regularly cook for more than 4 people.

**Can I use soap on cast iron?**
Yes, briefly. The myth that soap ruins cast iron dates to an era when soaps contained lye. Modern dish soap used briefly and rinsed immediately won't damage a well-seasoned pan. The key is drying completely and oiling lightly after washing. Never soak cast iron in water or let it air-dry wet.
