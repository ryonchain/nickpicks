## Fuzzy Logic vs Basic Rice Cookers: Is It Worth It?

**Basic rice cookers** ($20–$50) use a simple thermostat: when the temperature reaches the boiling point of water (100°C), the cooker switches to keep-warm. They work reliably for plain white rice but struggle with variations in water ratio, altitude, or specialty rice types.

**Fuzzy logic rice cookers** ($100–$250) use microcomputers to monitor temperature, adjust cooking time, and compensate for variables like altitude, pan size, and water absorbed. They cook every rice variety — white, sushi, brown, GABA, porridge — with precision.

**Is fuzzy logic worth the extra $100?**
- If you only cook plain white rice daily: basic is sufficient.
- If you cook sushi rice, brown rice, or mixed grains: fuzzy logic is worth every cent.
- If rice is central to your diet: invest in the Zojirushi.

## What Size Rice Cooker Do You Need?

Rice cooker capacity is measured in **uncooked cups** (roughly 180ml per cup). Rice doubles in volume when cooked.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is Zojirushi worth the price?**
For households that eat rice multiple times per week, yes — definitively. The consistency improvement over budget cookers is substantial, and Zojirushi cookers last 10–15+ years, making the per-use cost negligible over time.

**Can I cook brown rice in a basic rice cooker?**
You can, but results are inconsistent. Brown rice requires a longer cook time and different water ratio, and basic cookers' simple thermostats can't compensate. Fuzzy logic cookers have dedicated brown rice settings that produce significantly better results.

**What's the difference between Zojirushi and Tiger?**
Both are premium Japanese brands with excellent reputations. Zojirushi's fuzzy logic algorithm is generally considered slightly superior for specialty rice varieties. Tiger's Tacook simultaneous cooking plate is a genuine differentiator. Price-wise, Tiger typically runs $30–50 less than equivalent Zojirushi models.

**Does a rice cooker save energy vs stovetop?**
Yes — rice cookers use a concentrated heat element targeted at the pot, while stovetop burners lose heat to the air around the pot. A typical rice cooker uses 400–700W for 20–30 minutes, comparable to a stovetop but with automatic shut-off.
