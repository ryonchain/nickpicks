## How LED Teeth Whitening Works

Whitening gels contain either hydrogen peroxide or carbamide peroxide as the active bleaching agent. LED accelerator lights (typically blue 460 nm wavelength) activate the peroxide gel, speeding up the oxidation reaction that breaks down stain molecules in tooth enamel. The LED doesn't whiten teeth on its own — it accelerates gel efficacy, allowing shorter session times or lower gel concentrations.

**Active ingredient comparison:**
- **Hydrogen peroxide (HP):** Faster-acting, typically used in 6–16% at-home concentrations
- **Carbamide peroxide (CP):** Converts to HP in the mouth; 10% CP ≈ 3.5% HP; used at 10–35% concentrations

**Realistic expectations:** At-home LED kits can whiten teeth 3–8 shades over 1–2 weeks. Professional in-office whitening typically achieves 8–12 shades but costs $300–$800.

## Buying Guide

### Concentration vs. Sensitivity Trade-Off
Higher peroxide concentration = faster results = more sensitivity risk. Users with enamel erosion, existing sensitivity, or gum recession should start with 10% CP or 6% HP formulas. If teeth are healthy and you want the fastest results, 35% CP (AuraGlow) or professional-grade formulas are appropriate.

### LED Light Quality
Not all LED lights are equal. Look for devices specifying 460 nm blue LED wavelengths — the clinically studied frequency for peroxide activation. Cheaper devices use unspecified white LEDs that have minimal activation effect.

### Gel Application Method
Tray-based systems can overflow onto gums, causing irritation. Brush-tip applicators (Snow) or closed mouthpiece systems (GLO) minimize gum contact. If you have sensitive gums, prioritize precise application methods.

## FAQ

### How long do teeth whitening results last?
Professional and at-home whitening results typically last 6 months to 2 years, depending on dietary habits (coffee, tea, red wine stain teeth faster), tobacco use, and maintenance whitening frequency. Touch-up sessions every 2–3 months maintain results without requiring full retreatment.

### Are LED whitening kits safe for enamel?
Whitening gels are safe when used as directed. Overuse or leaving gel on for longer than recommended can temporarily soften enamel. Never use professional-concentration gels (35%+) more frequently than labeled. Using fluoride toothpaste after whitening sessions helps remineralize enamel.

### Can people with dental work (crowns, veneers) use whitening kits?
Whitening gels only whiten natural tooth enamel — crowns, veneers, bonding, and fillings will not lighten. This can result in visible color mismatch between natural teeth and dental work after whitening. Consult your dentist before whitening if you have visible dental restorations.
