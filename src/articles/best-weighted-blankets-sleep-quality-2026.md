## Weighted Blanket Buying Guide

### How to Choose the Right Weight

The standard recommendation is **10% of your body weight** — a 150 lb person starts with a 15 lb blanket. But this is a floor, not a ceiling. Many people prefer slightly more pressure than 10%:

| Body Weight | Starting Point (10%) | Consider Going Up To |
|------------|---------------------|---------------------|
| 100–120 lb | 10–12 lb | 15 lb |
| 120–150 lb | 12–15 lb | 18–20 lb |
| 150–180 lb | 15–18 lb | 20 lb |
| 180–220 lb | 18–20 lb | 22–25 lb |
| 220+ lb | 20–22 lb | 25 lb |

**For children:** Use 5–8% of body weight, and never use a weighted blanket for children under 2 years old. Always consult a pediatric occupational therapist before using weighted products with children who have medical conditions.

If you're brand new to weighted blankets, start at 10% — you can always try a heavier option if you want more pressure. Going too heavy too soon can feel restrictive and disrupt sleep rather than improving it.

### Glass Beads vs. Poly Pellets

The fill material determines how a weighted blanket drapes, feels, and holds up over time.

**Glass beads** are the current standard. They're small (roughly the size of a sugar grain), dense, and distribute evenly across sewn pocket grids — producing consistent pressure across your body. They don't degrade or compress over time.

**Poly pellets** (small plastic beads) are cheaper to manufacture. They're bulkier, tend to shift toward pocket edges, and make a faint plastic-rustling sound when you move. For nightly use, glass beads are worth the small price premium.

### Fabric Materials and Heat Retention

The cover material dramatically affects how warm you sleep — this is where most people go wrong:

| Material | Temperature | Notes |
|----------|-------------|-------|
| Microfiber / fleece | Warm | Great for cold sleepers; avoid if you run hot |
| Cotton percale | Neutral–cool | Best all-season choice for most people |
| Bamboo / Tencel | Cool | Best cover for hot sleepers using glass-bead blankets |
| Chunky-knit cotton | Coolest | Open weave allows airflow; significantly cooler than flat covers |
| Polyester batting layers | Warm | Common in budget blankets; avoid thick layers if heat is a concern |

### Washability — What to Know Before You Buy

- **Up to 15 lbs:** Most home washing machines handle these fine. Front-loaders are gentler than agitator top-loaders.
- **15–20 lbs:** Depends on your machine's capacity. If your washer is under 4.5 cubic feet, use a laundromat for anything over 15 lbs.
- **20+ lbs:** Commercial washer required, or buy a removable-cover design and wash only the cover at home.

The removable-cover approach (Gravity, Luna) is the most practical: wash the cover weekly, and take the inner blanket to a laundromat once a month.

### Size Selection

- **Twin (48"×72"):** Best for solo use. Works on any bed size — layer it on your side if you share a bed.
- **Queen (60"×80"):** Covers one person on a queen or king bed. Not designed for sharing — pressure per person drops when two people share one blanket.
- **Full:** A catch-all size between twin and queen, good for solo use with extra coverage.

If your partner doesn't want a weighted blanket, buy a twin size and layer it on your side only.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What weight weighted blanket should I get?**

The general guideline is 10% of your body weight, but this is a starting point — personal preference matters more. A 150 lb person might find 15 lbs ideal, while some prefer 20 lbs. Start at 10% of body weight and go up if you want more pressure. Children should use 5–8% of body weight, and weighted blankets are not safe for children under 2.

**Do weighted blankets actually help with sleep?**

Research shows weighted blankets can reduce anxiety and improve sleep onset, particularly for people with anxiety, autism, or sensory processing differences. The mechanism is "deep pressure stimulation" — similar to the calming effect of being hugged. A 2020 Journal of Clinical Sleep Medicine study found weighted blankets significantly reduced insomnia severity. Results vary — some people sleep better immediately, others find the weight disruptive.

**Are weighted blankets safe for hot sleepers?**

Standard glass-bead weighted blankets trap more heat than regular blankets, which is a problem for hot sleepers. Look for cotton-filled or bamboo materials, open-weave knit construction (like Bearaby), or blankets with cooling covers. Avoid polyester-heavy options if you run warm. Cotton and Tencel/bamboo covers stay significantly cooler than microfiber.
