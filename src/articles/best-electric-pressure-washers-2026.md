## PSI, GPM, and Cord Length: What Actually Matters

**PSI (pounds per square inch)** measures the water pressure. Higher PSI strips more grime but can damage soft surfaces. For home use:
- **1,200–1,700 PSI**: cars, patio furniture, window screens
- **1,800–2,300 PSI**: decks, fences, driveways, siding
- **2,400–3,200 PSI**: heavy concrete, oil stains, stubborn mildew

**GPM (gallons per minute)** measures water volume. PSI × GPM = cleaning units (CU) — the real measure of cleaning power. A 2,000 PSI / 1.5 GPM washer (3,000 CU) outperforms a 2,300 PSI / 1.0 GPM model (2,300 CU).

**Cord length** determines your operating radius without an extension cord. Under 25 ft is limiting for most driveways. 35 ft is the practical minimum for front-to-back exterior washing. Extension cords rated below 12 AWG can trip GFCI outlets and damage motors.

**Hose length** adds to your reach beyond the cord. 20–25 ft is standard; 30 ft hoses significantly reduce how often you need to reposition.

## Buying Guide: Choosing the Right Electric Pressure Washer

### PSI vs. GPM: The Full Picture
Don't shop on PSI alone. A 2,500 PSI / 1.0 GPM washer (2,500 CU) cleans slower than a 2,000 PSI / 1.5 GPM unit (3,000 CU). When comparing models, multiply PSI × GPM to get cleaning units — use that as your primary metric.

### Cord and Hose Length
Measure your intended cleaning area. A 20 ft hose + 35 ft cord gives you roughly 55 ft of total reach from your outlet. For most house washing, a second outlet on the far side of the house will be necessary. Cordless models solve this at the cost of runtime.

### Surface Compatibility
- **Car paint / vinyl**: 1,200–1,800 PSI, wide nozzle (25°–40°), 18-inch distance
- **Wood decking**: 1,500–2,000 PSI, 25° nozzle, with-the-grain direction
- **Concrete / pavers**: 2,000–3,000 PSI, 15° or turbo nozzle, 6–10 inch distance
- **Brick / siding**: 1,500–2,000 PSI, 25°–40° nozzle, top-to-bottom direction

### Motor Type
Universal brush motors are the most common in budget models — they're inexpensive but generate more heat and wear faster. Induction motors (Karcher) run cooler and quieter. Brushless motors (Greenworks) offer the best longevity. For occasional homeowner use, any type is fine; for frequent or extended sessions, invest in induction or brushless.

### Nozzle Sets
Five-nozzle sets (0°, 15°, 25°, 40°, soap) cover every residential application. Variable-pressure wands (like Karcher's VPS) simplify operation but are surface-specific. Turbo/rotary nozzles add 50% effective pressure and are worth adding for concrete work.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use a regular extension cord with an electric pressure washer?**
Use only 12 AWG or heavier cords (the lower the gauge number, the thicker the wire). Undersized cords cause voltage drop, overheat the motor, and trip GFCI outlets. Length matters too — keep extension cords under 50 ft.

**How do I store a pressure washer to prevent pump damage?**
Run pump antifreeze (propylene glycol) through the system before storing below freezing temperatures. For summer storage, leave the trigger gun connected so residual pressure doesn't build up in the hose.

**Are electric pressure washers safe for wooden decks?**
Yes, with the right nozzle (25°) and technique. Always spray with the wood grain, keep the wand moving, and maintain 12–18 inches of distance. Too close or too narrow a nozzle will raise the grain and damage the wood.

**How long do electric pressure washer motors last?**
Budget models typically last 50–100 hours of use. Mid-range models with induction or brushless motors often reach 200–500 hours. The pump is usually the first component to wear; replacement pumps cost $30–$80 for most brands.

**Can electric pressure washers clean mold off concrete?**
Yes — combine a 2,000+ PSI washer with a detergent nozzle and a concrete-safe cleaner (sodium hypochlorite or specialized concrete cleaner). Apply detergent first, dwell 3–5 minutes, then rinse with the 15° nozzle.
