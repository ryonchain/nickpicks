## What Blue Light Glasses Actually Do

Blue light (roughly 400–490nm wavelength) is emitted by screens, LED lighting, and sunlight. The concern around screens is twofold: eye strain during extended use, and disruption of melatonin production that affects sleep quality.

The sleep science is better established: light in the 460–480nm range does suppress melatonin. Wearing amber-tinted glasses (which block more of the visible blue spectrum) in the 1–2 hours before bed has shown measurable improvement in sleep onset in some studies.

The eye strain claim is less clear — most researchers attribute screen-related eye strain to reduced blink rate and focusing fatigue rather than blue light specifically. That said, many users report subjective improvement, and clear or lightly tinted blue light lenses don't cause harm.

## Buying Guide: Which Blue Light Glasses Are Right for You?

**Define your use case first.** Daytime screen work → clear or very light tint (maintains color accuracy). Evening use before sleep → amber or orange tint (higher blocking percentage). Both → buy two pairs, one for each context. If you type heavily at the same time, pairing glasses with an [ergonomic keyboard](/articles/best-ergonomic-keyboards-home-office-2026/) addresses two of the most common screen-work discomforts simultaneously.

**Clear vs. tinted.** Clear lenses offer minimal blocking but zero color distortion — suitable for design work and video calls where you don't want to look like you're wearing orange glasses. Tinted lenses block more but shift your color perception.

**Prescription vs. non-prescription.** If you already wear glasses, the most practical approach is adding blue light coating to your next prescription pair (Zenni Blokz or LensaBlue add-on). If you have perfect or corrected vision, non-prescription blue light glasses are widely available.

**The biggest thing you can actually control:** Regardless of which glasses you buy, using Night Shift (iOS/macOS), Night Mode (Android), or f.lux (Windows/Mac) to shift your screen's color temperature in the evening is free, evidence-backed, and affects more hours than glasses alone. Your physical [home office lighting](/articles/best-home-office-lighting-under-50/) environment matters too — the right light temperature reduces blue light exposure before glasses even come into play.
