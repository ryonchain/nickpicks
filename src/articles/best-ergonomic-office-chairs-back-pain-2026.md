## Ergonomic Chair Buying Guide for Back Pain

### The 4 Adjustments That Matter Most

**1. Lumbar support position:** Should sit at the natural curve of your lower back — typically between your waistband and the middle of your back. Height-adjustable lumbar is non-negotiable if you're buying specifically for back pain relief.

**2. Seat height:** Feet should rest flat on the floor with your knees at 90–100 degrees. If your chair goes too high, your feet dangle; too low, your knees rise above your hips and compress your lower back.

**3. Seat depth:** Leave 2–3 finger widths between the edge of the seat and the back of your knees. Seats that are too deep cut off circulation; too shallow and you lose thigh support.

**4. Armrests:** Arms should rest at 90–100 degrees with relaxed shoulders. Armrests that are too high force your shoulders up; too low and you lean sideways, straining your lower back.

### Mesh vs Foam Backrest

**Mesh:** Better breathability, conforms to your back's shape, lighter weight. Best for warm environments or users who run hot. Mesh loses tension over time — check warranty on this specifically.

**Foam/padding:** More cushioned initial feel, better for cold climates. Dense foam holds its shape longer than mesh, but poor airflow causes heat buildup over long sessions.

### When to Buy Used

Herman Miller and Steelcase chairs hold up for 15–20 years. The used market for certified-refurbished Aeron and Leap V2 chairs is strong — you can often find excellent condition units at 40–60% off retail from office liquidators. This is the smartest value move for anyone who wants premium ergonomics without the premium price.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long should I sit before taking a break?**
50 minutes of sitting followed by a 10-minute movement break is a well-supported interval. Even the best ergonomic chair can't fully compensate for continuous sitting — movement breaks are essential.

**Should I pair my chair with a standing desk?**
Yes, when possible. An ergonomic chair handles your sitting hours well; a sit-stand desk adds the ability to break up seated time without leaving your workstation.

**What's the difference between lumbar support and lumbar adjustment?**
Lumbar support is any pad or curve that pushes against your lower back. Lumbar adjustment means that support can move — up, down, or deeper into your back. Adjustment is what makes it personalized and actually effective.
