A dorm TV needs to be small (32" max for most rooms), affordable, and have streaming apps built in — because nobody uses a cable box in college anymore. Here are the best options for 2026.

---

## What Size TV for a Dorm Room?

Dorm rooms are small. Standard viewing distances:

| Room Type | Distance to TV | Best Size |
|-----------|----------------|-----------|
| Single dorm | 6–8 feet | 24–32" |
| Double dorm | 8–10 feet | 32–40" |
| Suite / apartment | 10+ feet | 43–55" |

**Check your school's policy** — some dorms limit TV sizes or prohibit certain mounting methods.

## Dorm TV Buying Checklist

- [ ] **Size check** — measure your desk or wall space first
- [ ] **Smart TV OS** — Roku, Fire TV, or Google TV (all good; pick based on your ecosystem)
- [ ] **HDMI ports** — at least 2; 3 if you have a console + streaming stick + laptop
- [ ] **USB port** — useful for direct media playback from a flash drive
- [ ] **Wall mount** — check if your dorm allows wall mounting; VESA compatibility matters
- [ ] **Power cord length** — measure from the outlet to your ideal TV position

---

## What About a Monitor Instead?

A gaming monitor can double as a TV if you add an Amazon Fire Stick or Apple TV. Monitors often have:
- Better response times for gaming
- USB-C connectivity for laptops
- No built-in tuner (not needed if you only stream)

**Best monitor-as-TV option**: Dell 27" S2722DC — USB-C, IPS panel, looks great for streaming and work.

## Dorm TV Accessories to Consider

- **HDMI cable** (~$8–$12) — You'll need one to connect a game console
- **TV wall mount** (~$20–$30) — Saves desk space
- **Soundbar** (~$50–$80) — Built-in TV speakers are weak; a compact soundbar makes a real difference
- **Fire TV Stick** or **Chromecast** — For non-smart TVs or upgrading an old dumb screen
