## Trail Camera Buying Guide

### Standard vs. Cellular: Which Do You Need?

**Standard (SD card only):** Lower cost, no monthly fees. Requires physically visiting the camera to retrieve images. Fine for hunters who check cameras regularly, or for locations with easy access.

**Cellular:** Images delivered to your phone in real-time or on a schedule. Requires a data plan ($5–$15/month). Essential for remote locations, property security, or hunters who want to know exactly when and what is on their land without driving hours to check.

### No-Glow vs. Low-Glow vs. White Flash

**No-Glow (black flash infrared):** LEDs emit 940nm infrared — completely invisible to humans and animals. Night images are black-and-white. Best for: spooky deer, property security where you don't want to reveal camera locations.

**Low-Glow (red flash infrared):** LEDs emit 850nm infrared — a very faint red glow visible to humans in the dark, minimally visible to wildlife. Night images are slightly better quality than black flash. Best for: most hunting applications.

**White Flash:** Uses a white LED flash that illuminates color night images at the cost of startling nearby animals and being visible. Best for: situations where color detail matters more than stealth.

### Key Specs Explained

- **Trigger speed:** Time from motion detection to photo capture. Under 0.3 seconds captures fast-moving deer mid-stride. Over 0.7 seconds often captures the tail end of an animal exiting the frame.
- **Detection range:** Distance at which the PIR sensor triggers. Longer isn't always better — a camera triggering at 100 feet on distant movement will create thousands of false-trigger images from wind and branches.
- **Burst mode:** Camera takes 2–10 photos per trigger. Useful for capturing multiple animals in a group.

## FAQ

**Do I need a special SD card for trail cameras?**
Use a Class 10 or faster SD card rated for video recording. Cheap Class 4 SD cards frequently cause missed images in burst mode and video corruption. SanDisk Endurance cards are purpose-built for trail cameras and highly recommended.

**How long do batteries last in trail cameras?**
Standard AA alkaline batteries: 3–6 months at moderate trigger frequency. Using lithium batteries in cold weather extends life significantly. Rechargeable NiMH batteries can be used but have shorter life in cold temperatures.

**Are trail cameras legal everywhere?**
Legal on private property for personal use in all US states. On public lands (National Forests, BLM), regulations vary by state and jurisdiction — some states prohibit trail cameras on public land during hunting seasons. Check local regulations before deploying.
