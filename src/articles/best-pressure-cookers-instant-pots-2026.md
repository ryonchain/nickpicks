## Pressure Cooker Buying Guide

### Electric vs. Stovetop: Which Is Right for You?
**Electric** (Instant Pot, Ninja Foodi): safer for beginners, set-and-forget convenience, additional cooking functions. Lower max pressure (10–12 PSI) means slightly longer cook times than stovetop.

**Stovetop**: higher pressure (15 PSI) = 25–30% faster cooking, more responsive heat control, works on any heat source. Requires attention during cooking.

If you're new to pressure cooking, start electric. If you're already comfortable with stovetop cooking and want maximum performance, go stovetop.

### Size: Getting Capacity Right
- **3 Qt**: 1–2 people, small kitchens
- **6 Qt**: 3–5 people, most households (most popular size)
- **8 Qt**: large families, batch cooking, or big cuts of meat (whole chickens, large roasts)

Don't under-size — pressure cookers should never be filled more than 2/3 full. An 8 Qt pot is often necessary even for 4-person families when cooking large quantities.

### Pressure PSI and Cook Time
Most electric pressure cookers max out at 10–12 PSI. At these pressures:
- Chicken breast: 10–12 minutes
- Beef stew: 35–40 minutes
- Dried beans (soaked): 8–10 minutes
- Dried beans (unsoaked): 25–30 minutes

Stovetop models at 15 PSI cut these times by 20–30%.

### Safety Features to Look For
Modern electric pressure cookers have 10+ safety mechanisms. Key ones:
- **Pressure release valve**: quick release vs. natural release
- **Lid-lock indicator**: confirms sealed before pressurizing
- **Overheat protection**: shuts off if temperature exceeds safe range
- **Steam release shield**: prevents burns during quick release

### Multi-Cooker Functions Worth Paying For
- **Sauté mode**: brown meat before pressure cooking — dramatically improves flavor
- **Slow cook**: replaces a separate slow cooker
- **Yogurt mode**: makes homemade yogurt cheaply
- **Air crisp/fry**: replaces a separate air fryer
