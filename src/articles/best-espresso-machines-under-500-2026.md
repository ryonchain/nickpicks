## Buying Guide: What to Look for in a Home Espresso Machine

### Pump Pressure
Espresso requires 9 bars of pressure during extraction. Most machines are rated at 15–20 bars, which gives headroom to reach 9 bars consistently. **Don't buy anything rated below 9 bars** — it won't produce real espresso.

### Boiler Type
- **Thermoblock:** Heats quickly (15–45 sec), smaller thermal mass. Fine for 1–2 cups. Hard to steam milk and brew simultaneously.
- **Single boiler:** Needs temperature adjustment between brewing and steaming. Requires waiting.
- **Dual boiler (premium):** Independent control for brew and steam simultaneously. Usually $600+ territory.
- **Thermojet (Breville):** Best of both worlds under $500 — Breville's proprietary tech heats in 3 seconds.

### Steam Wand: Auto vs. Manual
- **Manual:** Requires technique. Once mastered, gives more control over texture. Takes 2–4 weeks to learn.
- **Auto (Breville Bambino Plus):** Insert the wand, set temperature, walk away. Consistently good results from day one.

### Built-in Grinder?
Fresh-ground coffee is 40–60% better than pre-ground (degassing starts within minutes of grinding). If budget allows, De'Longhi La Specialista Arte includes a grinder. Otherwise, budget $50–100 for a basic burr grinder like the Baratza Encore.

### Pod vs. Ground
- Pods: convenience, consistency, higher per-cup cost, limited variety
- Ground: lower per-cup cost, infinite coffee variety, skill required

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a separate grinder?**
For the best results, yes. Even a $50 burr grinder dramatically improves shot quality. However, the De'Longhi La Specialista Arte includes a built-in grinder and is worth considering if you want a complete setup.

**What's the difference between espresso and coffee?**
Espresso is brewed under pressure (9+ bars), which produces a concentrated 1–2 oz shot with crema. Coffee makers use gravity or gentle pressure. You can't make true espresso in a standard drip coffee maker.

**How do I make a latte at home?**
1. Pull a double espresso shot (~2 oz)
2. Steam 6–8 oz of milk to 140–150°F with micro-foam
3. Pour steamed milk over espresso, holding back foam with a spoon
4. Spoon foam on top

**What coffee do I use for espresso?**
Any dark or medium-dark roast ground fine. Dedicated espresso blends are labeled specifically, but a medium-dark Colombia or Brazil also works beautifully. Avoid light roasts — they tend to be sour at espresso extraction pressures.

**How do I steam milk for lattes at home?**
