# Best Cellular Shades and Blinds in 2026

Cellular shades (also called honeycomb blinds) are the most energy-efficient window treatment available. The hexagonal cells trap air, creating insulation that reduces heat transfer through windows. They also provide privacy, light control, and a clean modern look. Key decisions: single cell vs. double cell, light filtering vs. blackout, and cordless vs. corded.

---

## Single Cell vs. Double Cell

**Single cell:** One layer of honeycomb cells. More affordable. Adequate for most rooms.

**Double cell:** Two layers of cells nested together. Significantly better insulation (R-value roughly double single cell). Worth it for rooms with large south-facing windows or extreme climates.

**Triple cell:** Three layers. Maximum insulation. Typically used in very cold climates or for maximum energy savings.

---

## Light Filtering vs. Blackout

**Light filtering:** Diffuses light, provides daytime privacy, allows ambient glow through the shade. Best for living rooms, offices, and spaces where you want natural light without glare.

**Room darkening:** Blocks 85–95% of light. Good for bedrooms where you want dark sleeping conditions but not complete darkness.

**Blackout:** Blocks 99%+ of light. For nurseries, media rooms, daytime sleepers.

## Installation Guide

**Inside mount (preferred):** Shade mounts inside the window frame. Clean look. Requires at least 1.5-inch depth inside the frame.

**Outside mount:** Shade mounts on the wall above the window. Use when inside mount isn't possible, or when you want to make the window appear larger. Extend 2–3 inches past the frame on each side.

**Measurement tip:** Measure width at top, middle, and bottom of window frame — windows are rarely perfectly rectangular. Use the narrowest measurement for inside mount, with 1/4" deducted for clearance.

## Energy Savings

According to the U.S. Department of Energy:
- Cellular shades on south-facing windows reduce solar heat gain by 60–70% in summer
- Insulating shades reduce window heat loss by 40% in winter

For a home with 10 average-sized windows, the energy savings can offset the cost of cellular shades within 2–4 years in extreme climates.
