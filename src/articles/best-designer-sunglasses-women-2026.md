## How to Choose Sunglasses for Your Face Shape

### Oval Face
Lucky — most frames work. Avoid frames wider than your forehead; go narrower and balanced.

**Best picks:** Aviators, square frames, wayfarers, cat-eyes

### Round Face
Aim for angular, longer frames to add definition and lengthen the face.

**Best picks:** Rectangular frames, cat-eyes, narrow ovals

### Square Face
Soften strong angles with rounder frames. Avoid rectangular shapes that emphasize angles.

**Best picks:** Round frames, oval frames, cat-eyes

### Heart Face (wider forehead, narrower jaw)
Balance the wider top with bottom-heavy styles or cat-eyes that draw attention downward.

**Best picks:** Cat-eyes, round frames, aviators

## UV Protection: What the Labels Mean

- **UV400:** Blocks 99–100% of UV-A and UV-B rays (wavelengths up to 400nm). This is what you need.
- **Polarized:** Reduces glare from reflective surfaces (water, snow, roads). Doesn't add UV protection — look for both.
- **No UV rating:** Avoid these. Dark tint without UV protection actually causes more eye damage (pupils dilate behind dark lenses).

**Rule:** Any reputable designer brand provides UV400 protection. Cheap non-branded sunglasses often don't — worse than no sunglasses at all.

## FAQ

**Q: Are expensive sunglasses worth it?**
A: For UV protection, not necessarily — a $20 pair with proper UV400 coating protects as well as a $400 pair. The premium pays for frame quality (durability, materials), optics quality, and design. If you lose sunglasses regularly, start with mid-range ($50–$150) until you have a track record of keeping them.

**Q: Do designer sunglasses come with warranties?**
A: Most include a 1-year warranty against manufacturing defects. Some brands (Maui Jim, Oakley) offer repair programs for scratched lenses.

**Q: Can I get prescription lenses in designer frames?**
A: Yes. Most opticians can fit prescription lenses into any frame. For luxury frames, this can add $100–$400 in lens costs depending on your prescription.
