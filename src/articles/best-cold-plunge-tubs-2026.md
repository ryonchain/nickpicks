## Cold Plunge Basics: What Actually Matters

### Target Temperature
Research supports 50–59°F (10–15°C) as the effective range for most protocols:
- **Anti-inflammation:** 55–60°F for 10–15 minutes, 3–5x weekly
- **Norepinephrine release (mood/focus):** 50–55°F for 2–4 minutes
- **Andrew Huberman Protocol:** 11 minutes total per week, divided across 2–4 sessions
- **Wim Hof Method:** Longer exposures at 40–50°F

Below 40°F risks hypothermia without controlled conditions. Above 65°F produces minimal cold shock response.

### Chiller vs. Ice Method
- **Ice method:** Fill tub, add 40–80 lbs of ice per session. Cost: $5–15/session. No electricity required. Temperature not regulated.
- **Chiller system:** Circulates and chills water to a set temperature. Cost: $1,500–5,000 upfront. No ice purchase required. Consistent temperature every session.

For daily users, a chiller system pays back within 6–18 months versus ice cost.

### Sanitation
Cold water alone doesn't sanitize. All systems require:
- Ozone, UV, or chemical (bromine/chlorine) filtration
- Water changes every 7–14 days minimum without filtration
- Cover/lid to prevent contamination between sessions

## Cold Plunge Protocol Guide

### Beginner Protocol (First 2 Weeks)
- Temperature: 55–60°F
- Duration: 2–3 minutes
- Frequency: 3x weekly
- Focus: Building tolerance, learning breath control, completing sessions without exiting early

### Intermediate Protocol (Week 3+)
- Temperature: 50–55°F
- Duration: 4–6 minutes
- Frequency: 5x weekly
- Focus: Incorporating breathing protocols (box breathing, slow exhale)

### Research-Backed Protocol (Huberman)
- Temperature: 50–59°F
- Duration: 11 minutes total per week
- Sessions: 2–4 sessions/week
- Timing: Morning (before training) for alertness; post-training controversial for hypertrophy (may blunt adaptation)

### Safety Rules
- Never cold plunge alone if new to the practice
- Do not plunge immediately after vigorous exercise (risk of cardiac stress)
- Exit immediately if you experience uncontrolled shivering, loss of dexterity, or confusion
- People with cardiovascular disease should consult a physician first
