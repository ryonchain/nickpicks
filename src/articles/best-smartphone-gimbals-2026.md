## Smartphone Gimbal Buying Guide

### 3-Axis vs 2-Axis: Why You Want 3-Axis

2-axis gimbals stabilize pitch (up-down tilt) and roll (side-to-side tilt) only — they don't correct the yaw (horizontal rotation) that happens when you swing the camera left or right while walking. 3-axis gimbals eliminate all three axes of movement. All five gimbals in this guide are 3-axis; skip any product that doesn't specify 3-axis stabilization.

### Stabilization Modes Explained

- **Lock mode:** Camera stays pointed at a fixed direction regardless of how you move the gimbal body.
- **Follow mode:** Camera follows your pan movements smoothly but corrects for tilt and roll.
- **Pan follow:** Only pans follow your movement; tilt stays level.
- **POV mode:** Camera follows all movements (used for intentional creative tilts).
- **Vortex/360°:** Camera spins 360° for creative effects.

### What to Look for in Companion Apps

The app makes or breaks the creative features. Look for:
- **Subject tracking:** Face/body lock that automatically pans to follow a moving subject
- **Story templates:** Automated shot sequences that combine movement types
- **Timelapse modes:** Especially motion timelapse (gimbal moves while shooting timelapse)
- **Manual control:** Exposure, ISO, and focus accessible from within the app

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use a gimbal for live streaming?**
Yes — most gimbals work alongside streaming apps like Instagram Live or YouTube Live. The DJI OM 6's Mimo app has a built-in live streaming mode. For other apps, the gimbal stabilizes the physical phone while the streaming app runs independently.

**Do gimbals work for photography as well as video?**
Gimbals primarily benefit video because stabilization eliminates motion blur during handheld video recording. For still photography, the electronic shutter in modern phones is fast enough that a gimbal provides minimal benefit. The timelapse and hyperlapse modes are worth using for photography, however.

**How long does gimbal calibration take?**
Initial calibration (balancing the phone on the gimbal before first use) takes 2–5 minutes. After that, the same phone attaches and is immediately balanced — calibration is only needed when switching to a phone with a different weight.
