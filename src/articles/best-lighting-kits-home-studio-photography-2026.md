## Studio Lighting Buying Guide

### Key Light vs Fill Light vs Background Light

The three-point lighting setup is the foundation of professional portrait photography:

1. **Key light** (main light, typically softbox at 45° to subject): Provides primary illumination. Harder for dramatic shadows; softer (larger diffuser) for even, flattering light.
2. **Fill light** (opposite side, lower power): Reduces shadow depth without eliminating it. A reflector (white foam board) can serve as a no-cost fill light.
3. **Background light** (aimed at backdrop): Separates subject from background. Can be colored with gels for creative effects.

Two-light kits cover key + fill. Start there before adding a third background light.

### Softbox Size and Light Quality

Larger softboxes produce softer, more flattering light. The "softness" comes from the light source size relative to the subject distance:
- 24" softbox at 3 feet: medium-soft (good for portraits)
- 48" octabox at 3 feet: very soft (beauty/commercial portrait quality)
- 24" softbox at 6 feet: harder (more directional shadows)

The Godox SL-60W's Bowens mount gives access to large octaboxes (48"+) that produce window-light quality.

### Color Temperature Consistency

For video, all lights in the same scene must match color temperature precisely — even a 200K difference between two lights creates color shift visible to the camera. Either match all lights at 5600K daylight or use bi-color lights (like the Neewer LED panels) to fine-tune all units to the same setting.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need blackout curtains for home studio photography?**
For the best control, yes — external light creates color temperature inconsistency and reduces your control over light ratios. Heavy blackout curtains (~$30–$50) solve this. If that's not possible, scheduling shoots during cloudy overcast days produces more consistent ambient light that's easier to manage alongside studio lights.

**What's the best background color to start with?**
White is the most versatile starting backdrop — it's used for clean product photography, can be lit to pure white or toned to gray, and works for most portrait styles. Black is the second most useful for low-key dramatic portraits. Add color backdrops once you've established the basics.

**Can I use these lights outdoors?**
Continuous LED lights are usable outdoors in shaded areas, but direct sunlight overwhelms most studio lights — even a 60W LED at full power adds minimal visible fill in bright sun. For outdoor fill flash, battery-powered strobes (Godox AD200) are more effective than continuous LED lights.
