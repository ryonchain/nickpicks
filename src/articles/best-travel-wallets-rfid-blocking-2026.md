## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is RFID skimming a real threat to travelers?**
RFID skimming is a real but relatively rare threat. Contactless credit cards (those with a tap-to-pay symbol) emit a radio signal that can be read by a nearby RFID reader within a few inches. In crowded travel environments (airports, transit systems, tourist areas) the risk is elevated compared to everyday life. RFID-blocking materials add a low-cost layer of protection with no downside.

**Does an RFID-blocking wallet affect contactless payment?**
Yes — an RFID-blocking wallet prevents contactless payment while the card is inside it. To use tap-to-pay, simply remove the card from the wallet. This is the intended behavior: the RFID block prevents unauthorized reads, while intentional payment requires removing the card.

**What cards use RFID technology?**
Contactless credit and debit cards (featuring the tap symbol — four curved lines), newer passports (US passports issued after 2007 contain an RFID chip), transit cards, and some hotel key cards. Standard magnetic stripe cards and chip-only cards do not use RFID and don't require blocking protection.

**What is the difference between a travel wallet and a regular wallet?**
Travel wallets are designed for the specific needs of travelers: passport slots (the distinctive large slot), multiple card slots for foreign currency cards and loyalty cards, document pockets for boarding passes and hotel confirmations, and RFID blocking. Regular wallets typically lack passport capacity and document organization.
