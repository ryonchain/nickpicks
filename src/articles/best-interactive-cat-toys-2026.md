## What to Look for in Interactive Cat Toys

### Match Toys to Hunting Style
Cats have distinct hunting preferences:
- **Aerial hunters:** prefer wand toys with feathers, things that fly
- **Ground hunters:** prefer toys that move along the floor (Hot Pursuit, flopping fish)
- **Ambush hunters:** prefer toys that appear unexpectedly (PetFusion Ambush)

Watch your cat's natural play style and prioritize accordingly.

### Rotation Is Key
No single toy sustains engagement indefinitely. Keep 4–6 toys in rotation, introducing different types on different days. Store unused toys in a sealed bag with catnip — when reintroduced, they smell new again.

### Play Session Duration & Frequency
Two 10–15 minute interactive play sessions per day is the vet-recommended baseline for indoor cats under 5 years old. One before dinner and one before your bedtime (reducing nighttime zoomies) is optimal timing.

### Safety
Avoid toys with small parts that can detach and be swallowed. Feather wand toys should only be used supervised — the long wire can be a strangulation risk if left unattended.

## FAQ

**Q: My cat ignores all toys — what's wrong?**
A: Nothing is wrong. Cats often don't play with toys when left with them. Movement is what triggers prey drive — a still toy is not interesting. Try a wand toy you operate manually. If your cat responds to that, they'll engage with automated toys too.

**Q: How do I reintroduce toys my cat has lost interest in?**
A: Store the toy for 2 weeks, then bring it back. Add a spritz of catnip spray or store it in a bag with dried catnip. Novel scent restores interest. Alternatively, introduce it in a new room.

**Q: Should I leave interactive toys out all the time?**
A: Manual wand toys: no (safety risk unattended). Automatic toys: yes, but rotate them. Puzzle feeders: rotate as meal replacements. Flopping fish: fine to leave out, rotate with breaks.

**Q: Are laser pointers bad for cats?**
A: The problem with lasers is the frustration of never actually catching the prey. Always end a laser session by redirecting to a physical toy the cat can "catch" to complete the hunt cycle. Cats need the catch and release resolution.
