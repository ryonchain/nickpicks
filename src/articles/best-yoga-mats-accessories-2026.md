## Yoga Mat Buying Guide

### Thickness: What to Choose

| Thickness | Best For | Tradeoff |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| 1–2mm | Travel, hot yoga towel overlay | Almost no joint cushioning |
| 3–4mm | Regular practice, studios | Light cushioning, good stability |
| 5–6mm | Joint sensitivity, hard floors | Less tactile floor feedback |
| 6mm+ | Injury recovery, maximum cushioning | Can feel "floating" in balance poses |

**Recommendation for most beginners:** 5–6mm. **Recommendation for experienced practitioners:** 3–4mm (better proprioceptive feedback).

### Materials Compared

| Material | Grip | Eco | Durability | Easy to Clean | Price |

| PVC | Good (dry only) | Low | High | Very easy | Low |
| Natural rubber | Excellent (dry + moist) | Medium | Medium | Medium | Medium |
| TPE | Good | Medium | Medium | Easy | Medium |
| Cork + rubber | Good | High | Medium | Easy | High |

### Hot Yoga Considerations

Hot yoga (Bikram, Baptiste power yoga) generates significant sweat. Standard mats become dangerously slippery. For hot yoga, either:
1. Use a **hot yoga towel** over any mat (Manduka Yogitoes, Shandali)
2. Choose a mat with polyurethane surface (Liforme, Lululemon The Mat 5mm) that grips when wet

Open-cell rubber mats (Jade, eKO) are adequate for warm vinyasa but will saturate in dedicated hot yoga conditions.

## FAQ

**How often should I replace my yoga mat?**
PVC mats: every 1–3 years with regular practice. Natural rubber: 3–5 years. Manduka PRO: indefinitely (lifetime guarantee). Replace when the surface loses grip, shows pilling, or develops thickness loss in high-wear areas.

**How do I clean a yoga mat?**
Most mats: wipe with a damp cloth + diluted mat spray after each use. Deep clean monthly with mild soap and water, laid flat to dry. Never machine wash unless the manufacturer specifies it — most open-cell rubber mats will degrade. Do not dry in direct sunlight.

**What's the best mat for bad knees?**
A 6mm mat (Manduka PRO, Gaiam Premium) provides the most joint cushioning. For tabletop and kneeling poses, fold the edge of the mat to double thickness, or place a folded blanket under the knee.

**Do I need yoga blocks if I'm flexible?**
Flexibility doesn't eliminate the need for blocks — support and extension are different from flexibility. Blocks are useful for maintaining alignment in poses where flexibility doesn't yet allow full expression. Even advanced practitioners use blocks in restorative and yin yoga contexts.
