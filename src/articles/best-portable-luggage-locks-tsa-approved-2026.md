# Best TSA-Approved Luggage Locks in 2026

A TSA-approved luggage lock lets you secure your checked bags without risking having them cut off for inspection. When TSA needs to inspect a bag, they use a master key to open Travel Sentry-approved locks, inspect the contents, and re-lock the bag. Without a TSA-approved lock, agents cut non-approved locks and leave your bag with no lock for the remainder of the trip.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What does TSA approved mean for luggage locks?**
TSA-approved locks (also called Travel Sentry-approved) have a master keyway that allows TSA agents to open the lock with a master key for inspection, then re-lock it without cutting the lock or damaging your luggage. Non-TSA locks will be cut off if TSA needs to inspect your bag. TSA-approved locks display the Travel Sentry logo (a red diamond) or the Safe Skies logo.

**Do luggage locks actually prevent theft?**
Luggage locks deter opportunistic theft and add time to any attempted bag breach. They do not stop a determined thief — zippers can be opened and re-closed without cutting a lock, and cut-resistant locks can eventually be overcome with enough time. The primary value is deterrence: a locked bag signals it belongs to someone security-conscious and is not worth the risk of detection.

**Can I use a luggage lock on a carry-on bag?**
Yes — TSA does not require carry-on bags to be unlocked for airport security. The bag goes through X-ray, and TSA will ask you to open specific compartments if needed. A lock on a carry-on is useful for preventing opportunistic theft during flight when the bag is in the overhead bin.

**Should I use a combination or keyed luggage lock?**
Combination locks are strongly preferred for travel — you can't lose the combination, and you don't need to carry a key. Keyed locks require carrying a spare key (losing the key means buying a new lock) and are impractical for sharing luggage access with a travel partner. Combination locks are standard for checked baggage.
