## How to Choose a Level 2 EV Charger

### Amperage: What You Actually Need

Most EVs have a maximum onboard charging rate of 32–48 amps on AC Level 2. A 40-amp charger is enough for nearly all current EVs. Only a handful of vehicles (some Tesla Model S/X, and some high-end European EVs) can accept more than 48 amps at home.

- **32 amps**: ~24 miles/hour. Fine for most drivers who add less than 200 miles/day.
- **40 amps**: ~30 miles/hour. Covers almost all daily driving needs.
- **48 amps**: ~38 miles/hour. Maximum for most home chargers; useful for road-trip recovery.

### Smart vs. Dumb Chargers

**Smart chargers** (ChargePoint, JuiceBox, Wallbox, Emporia) connect to WiFi and let you:
- Schedule charging for off-peak hours
- Monitor energy use and cost per session
- Receive remote alerts
- Integrate with voice assistants
- Participate in utility demand-response programs (sometimes paid)

**Dumb chargers** (Grizzl-E) just charge. They're faster to install, cheaper, and have zero points of failure related to software or connectivity.

**Recommendation:** If you have a time-of-use electricity plan (most utilities offer these), a smart charger pays for itself. If your rate is flat 24/7, a dumb charger is a perfectly rational choice.

### Hardwire vs. Plug-In

Both work. Plug-in (NEMA 14-50) is easier to relocate if you move. Hardwire is cleaner-looking and required by some insurance policies. If you're renting, get a plug-in version.

### Installation Costs

Expect $200–$800 for a licensed electrician to install, depending on:
- Distance from your electrical panel to the garage
- Whether you need a panel upgrade (adds $1,500–$3,000)
- Local labor rates

Check your electric utility website — most offer $100–$500 rebates on Level 2 charger purchases, and some cover installation too.
