  - question: "What's the difference between fabric and latex resistance bands?"
    answer: "Fabric bands (usually cotton with latex inside) stay in place on skin and clothing without rolling or pinching — ideal for glute and leg work. Latex bands provide a smoother, more consistent stretch and are available in longer lengths for upper body pulling and mobility work. Most home exercisers benefit from having both: fabric loop bands for lower body, latex bands for upper body and stretching."
  - question: "How do I know which resistance level to start with?"
    answer: "For beginners, start with light to medium resistance (10–20 lbs). The right level for an exercise should make the last 2–3 reps of a 12–15 rep set feel challenging but achievable. If you're breezing through all reps, go heavier. If you can't complete 8 reps with good form, go lighter. A set with 4–5 resistance levels lets you scale appropriately as you get stronger."
  - question: "Are resistance bands effective for building muscle?"
    answer: "Yes, though with caveats. Bands provide accommodating resistance — load increases at the end of the movement, which is mechanically different from free weights. Studies show bands build comparable muscle to weights for most exercises, especially when sets are taken close to failure. They're particularly effective for glutes, shoulders, and single-leg exercises. For heavy compound lifts, bands complement but don't fully replace barbells."
  - question: "How long do resistance bands last?"
    answer: "Latex bands typically last 1–3 years with regular use, depending on storage and usage. Keep them out of direct sunlight and extreme heat, which degrade latex. Fabric bands last longer — 3–5 years is common. Snap resistance bands immediately if you see cracks, tears, or discoloration. Many quality sets (like Rogue and Fit Simplify) include replacement guarantees."
