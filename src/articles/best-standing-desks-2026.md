## What to Look for in a Standing Desk

### Height Range
Most standing desks fit users between 5'3″ and 6'3″. If you're outside this range, check the minimum and maximum heights explicitly. Taller users need desks that extend to 50″+; shorter users need ones that lower to 24″ or below.

### Stability and Wobble
The single biggest quality indicator. Wobble at standing height ruins the experience — especially with dual monitors. Four-leg frames are most stable; premium two-leg frames (FlexiSpot E5/Uplift) are next; budget single-motor two-leg desks are most prone to wobble.

### Weight Capacity
A typical setup (desktop, dual monitors, laptop stand, accessories) weighs 80–150 lbs. Most desks handle this easily. If you have a heavy CRT or broadcast monitor setup, check capacity explicitly.

### Motor Count
Dual motor desks raise more smoothly and last longer than single motor alternatives. At the budget tier, single motor is the norm. Stepping up to dual motor adds $50–100 and significant reliability.

### Desktop Material
Options include MDF (standard, cheapest), solid wood, bamboo, and glass. Bamboo is durable and eco-friendly; solid wood is premium and heavy; MDF is fine for most users.

### Memory Presets
4 memory presets is the minimum worth having. It lets you save your exact sitting and standing heights and switch instantly.

## Standing vs. Sitting: Do Standing Desks Actually Work?

The short answer: yes, with important caveats. Standing desks don't make you burn significantly more calories, and standing all day is as bad as sitting all day. The benefit comes from **alternating** between positions — sit for 50 minutes, stand for 10, repeat. Anti-fatigue mats help significantly if you're standing on hard floors.

The real benefits are:
- **Reduced lower back pain** (the most consistently reported benefit)
- **Better afternoon energy** (standing naturally prevents the post-lunch slump)
- **Improved focus** for some task types
- **Better posture** when standing at proper height
