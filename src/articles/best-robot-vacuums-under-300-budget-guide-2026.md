## What Matters in a Robot Vacuum Under $300

### Suction Power (Pa)
Pa (Pascal) measures suction. Under $300, look for at least 1,500 Pa for hardwood floors; 2,000+ Pa for carpets or pet hair. Higher Pa doesn't always mean better results in real conditions — brush roll design and edge cleaning matter too.

### Navigation Type
**Bump-and-go:** Older random-pattern navigation. Misses spots, takes longer, drains battery faster.
**Gyroscopic:** Moves in systematic rows — better coverage, still no room mapping.
**LiDAR/camera mapping:** Creates a floor map, optimizes routes. The best navigation available — typically $300+ but starting to appear at $250–$300.

### Self-Emptying Base
Self-emptying bases collect debris from the robot's dustbin into a larger bag (30–60 day capacity). Eliminates the need to empty after every run — a genuine quality-of-life upgrade. Available in this price range from iRobot and Eufy G-series.

### Pet Hair Performance
Pet hair tangles brush rolls. Look for tangle-free brush designs or models specifically marketed for pets. The Shark IQ series and Eufy G30 variants perform best on pet hair.

## Common Mistakes When Buying a Budget Robot Vacuum

**Don't base decisions on suction Pa alone.** A 4,000 Pa robot with bad brush design will underperform a 2,000 Pa robot with good brush rolls on carpet.

**Prepare your floors.** Robot vacuums need cables off the floor, small rugs secured, and floor clutter minimized — or they'll get stuck and stop.

**Check ceiling height for self-emptying bases.** Some bases are 18"+ tall. Measure your doorways and furniture clearances before ordering.

**Understand what "smart mapping" means.** True room mapping (LiDAR or camera-based) is different from "smart navigation" — the latter often means systematic row patterns, not actual floor plan creation.
