## Why Immersion Blenders Beat Countertop Blenders for Soups

Making soup with a countertop blender means transferring hot liquid in batches, using a kitchen towel to hold the lid down while the steam builds pressure, and dealing with inevitable spatters across the kitchen. It also means washing the blender pitcher.

An immersion blender does the same job in 60 seconds: put it in the pot, blend until smooth, rinse under the faucet. For any recipe that starts in a pot — soup, sauce, curry, gravy — an immersion blender is the right tool.

**What to look for:**
- **Motor power:** 200W is adequate for soft-cooked soups. 400W+ handles fibrous vegetables and tougher ingredients. 600W+ gives you Vitamix-level results for restaurant-quality purées.
- **Variable speed:** Multiple speed settings give you control from gentle blending (avoid splashing) to maximum power (smooth purée).
- **Blade guard design:** A wide bell-shaped guard submerges more food per stroke. Look for guards with holes to draw in food from all sides.
- **Cord length:** For tall stockpots, a cord under 5 feet is frustrating. 6 feet is the practical minimum.
- **Accessories:** Whisk attachments, chopper bowls, and measuring cups extend what you can do with a single tool.

## Tips for Perfect Soup Purées

**Cook vegetables thoroughly.** Soft-cooked vegetables (past fork-tender) purée dramatically more smoothly than barely-cooked ones. Don't rush the cooking.

**Blend in the pot.** If you're making a chunky soup, remove 1/3 of the solids, blend the rest until smooth, then add the chunks back. This gives you a texture contrast between smooth broth and tender vegetable pieces.

**Control splash by going slow.** Start blending on low speed with the head fully submerged. Only increase speed once everything around the blade is already incorporated.

**Add cream or butter at the end.** Heat breaks down dairy emulsification — add heavy cream or butter after blending and off the heat for the silkiest result.
