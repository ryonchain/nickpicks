## Fish Oil Buying Guide

**Read EPA/DHA, not total fish oil:** Many products advertise "1000mg fish oil" on the front but contain only 180mg EPA + 120mg DHA — a low dose. Always check the Supplement Facts panel for individual EPA and DHA amounts.

**rTG > EE for absorption:** Re-esterified triglyceride form absorbs significantly better than ethyl ester. For therapeutic use, rTG is worth the premium. For budget-conscious daily maintenance, ethyl ester with a high EPA/DHA dose is still effective.

**Freshness testing:** IKOS and IFOS are the two main independent organizations that test fish oil for oxidation, heavy metals, and purity. A 5-star IFOS rating or IKOS approval means you can trust freshness and purity claims.

## FAQ

**What is the difference between triglyceride and ethyl ester fish oil?**
Triglyceride (TG) form fish oil is more bioavailable — studies show 70% better absorption than ethyl ester (EE) form. Natural fish oil is in TG form. Most cheap fish oils are processed into EE form, which is cheaper to produce. Re-esterified triglyceride (rTG) form, found in Nordic Naturals and similar premium brands, combines high EPA/DHA concentration with natural TG absorption.

**How much EPA and DHA should I take daily?**
Most research on cardiovascular support uses 1,000–4,000mg of combined EPA+DHA per day. General health maintenance is typically 500–1,000mg combined. Check the Supplement Facts panel for EPA and DHA amounts specifically — total fish oil mg is a misleading metric used to inflate numbers on budget products.

**How do I know if my fish oil is rancid?**
Rancid fish oil smells strongly of fish and tastes noticeably fishy or bitter. Quality fish oil should have minimal odor. Cut a softgel open to check — fresh fish oil smells mild and slightly oceanic, not pungent. Store in the refrigerator after opening to slow oxidation.

**Is fish oil worth taking every day?**
Fish oil is widely used for cardiovascular, joint, and cognitive support. Customers report reduced joint stiffness and inflammation as common benefits. Research supports omega-3 supplementation for those who do not regularly eat fatty fish (salmon, mackerel, sardines) 2–3 times per week.
