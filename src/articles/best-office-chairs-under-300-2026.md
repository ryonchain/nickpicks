## What Actually Matters in an Office Chair

**Lumbar support.** A fixed lumbar cushion is better than nothing, but adjustable lumbar is a meaningful upgrade. It should press into the natural curve of your lower back — not your upper back.

**Armrest quality.** At minimum, 2D armrests (height-adjustable) prevent shoulder strain. 4D armrests are ideal if you type for long periods — they let you position your forearms level with the keyboard.

**Seat depth.** Your back should touch the backrest with 2–3 fingers of space behind your knees. Chairs with adjustable seat depth (sliding pan) are rare under $300 but worth finding.

**Breathability.** Foam seats trap heat. Mesh backs and mesh seats run significantly cooler for long sessions.

## FAQ: Office Chair Questions

**Do ergonomic chairs actually help?**
Studies consistently show adjustable lumbar support reduces lower back pain and discomfort during prolonged sitting. The key is actually adjusting the chair to your body — a $300 chair left at default settings does nothing.

**How long should an office chair last?**
Quality chairs at $200–$300 typically last 5–8 years with daily use. Pneumatic cylinders and casters are the most common failure points — both are replaceable for under $30.

**Should I get a chair or a standing desk?**
Both. Alternating between sitting and standing is better than either alone. See our [standing desks guide](/articles/best-standing-desks-under-400-2026) for pairing recommendations.
