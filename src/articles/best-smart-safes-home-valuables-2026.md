## How to Choose a Smart Safe for Home Valuables

### Fire Protection vs. Quick Access: Understand the Trade-Off

Home safes generally fall into two camps: fireproof document safes (thicker walls, heavier, slower to open) and quick-access security safes (faster biometric or keypad access, thinner steel, no fire rating). A fireproof safe like the SentrySafe is not designed for sub-second handgun access — the door mechanism prioritizes heat sealing over speed. Conversely, a handgun safe like the Vaultek VT20i is not fire-rated. If you need both fire protection and quick firearm access, you need two safes, or a large gun safe with a fire rating (which starts around $500+).

### Biometric Reliability: What Matters

Fingerprint readers vary significantly by quality. The cheapest biometric safes use optical sensors that struggle with dirty or slightly injured fingers. Mid-range safes like the RPNB use capacitive sensors that are more accurate. Premium safes (Vaultek, Verifi) use high-resolution capacitive or FBI-certified sensors with the lowest false-rejection rates. Regardless of sensor quality, always register at least 4 fingerprints per authorized user (both thumbs, both index fingers) and ensure every safe has a PIN keypad and physical key backup. A biometric safe you can't open in an emergency is a liability.

### Size and Placement

Measure before you buy. The most common mistake is purchasing a safe that doesn't fit the intended location — under a desk, in a closet, or in a nightstand. Interior capacity in cubic feet sounds precise but doesn't tell you interior dimensions. For handgun safes, measure your firearm with any attached accessories (flashlight, optic). For document safes, decide whether you need hanging file capacity (the SentrySafe accommodates standard files; smaller safes typically don't).

### Bolting: Non-Negotiable for Lightweight Safes

Any safe under 150 lbs should be bolted down. A 60-lb safe can be carried out in 30 seconds by a single person. Pre-drilled anchor holes are standard on all safes in this guide — use them. Bolt into concrete when possible. For wood subfloors, use lag screws with washers at stud locations. For handgun safes placed in drawers or shelves, the included security cable at minimum prevents easy grab-and-go theft.
