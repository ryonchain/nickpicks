## Home Gym Flooring Buying Guide

### Thickness Guide

| Use Case | Recommended Thickness |
|----------|----------------------|
| Yoga / stretching | 3/8"–1/2" foam or rubber |
| Cardio machines (treadmill, bike) | 3/8" rubber |
| Functional training / HIIT | 1/2"–3/4" |
| Free weights, dumbbells | 3/4" rubber |
| Barbell / powerlifting | 3/4"+ vulcanized rubber |
| Olympic lifting with drops | 3/4" rubber + 4" bumper platform |

### Material Comparison

| Material | Durability | Cushion | Noise Reduction | Price |
|----------|-----------|---------|-----------------|-------|
| Vulcanized rubber | Excellent | Moderate | Excellent | High |
| Recycled rubber | Good | Good | Good | Medium |
| EVA foam | Moderate | Excellent | Moderate | Low |
| Horse stall mats | Excellent | Good | Good | Low |

### Horse Stall Mats: The Budget Hack
3/4" horse stall mats from farm supply stores (Tractor Supply, Rural King) are the same material as premium gym rubber — at $1.50–2.00/sq ft. The trade-offs: strong initial rubber odor (airs out in 1–2 weeks), limited sizing options, and heavier to install. Many serious home gym builders use them for their barbell areas.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to glue down gym flooring?**
For rolled rubber over concrete, a gym flooring adhesive or double-sided tape along the edges prevents creep. Interlocking tiles generally stay in place with equipment weight.

**How thick should garage gym flooring be?**
For concrete garage floors with standard home gym equipment: 3/4" rubber minimum. For heavy barbell training: 3/4" rubber with a 4" bumper plate landing zone.

**Will gym flooring damage my floors?**
Rubber mats are generally safe on concrete. On hardwood, check that the rubber backing is labeled "safe for hardwood" — some rubber compounds can stain wood floors over time.
