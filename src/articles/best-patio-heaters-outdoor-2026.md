## Propane vs. Electric: Which Patio Heater Type Is Right for You?

### Propane Heaters
- **Output:** 40,000–46,000 BTU for full-size units — dramatically more heat than electric
- **Best for:** Open, uncovered patios without an electrical outlet; large outdoor dining areas
- **Run time:** ~9–10 hours per 20 lb propane tank
- **Requires:** 20 lb propane tank (included or purchased separately); safe to use outdoors only
- **Caution:** Never use inside or in an enclosed space — propane combustion produces CO

### Electric / Infrared Heaters
- **Output:** 1,500W (5,100 BTU equivalent) — sufficient for covered patios and targeted heating
- **Best for:** Covered patios, garages, and spaces within reach of an outlet
- **Cost to run:** ~$0.20 per hour at average US electricity rates
- **Requires:** 110V or 240V electrical outlet; longer extension cords for flexibility
- **Advantage:** No fuel management; instant on/off; usable under covered areas without ventilation concerns

**Rule of thumb:** Open uncovered patio → propane. Covered patio with an outlet → electric infrared.

## Buying Guide: Choosing a Patio Heater

### BTU Output by Space Size

| Space | Recommended BTU |
|-------|----------------|
| Small balcony or table area (<50 sq ft) | 10,000–15,000 BTU |
| Medium patio (50–150 sq ft) | 25,000–40,000 BTU |
| Large open patio or deck (150+ sq ft) | 40,000–46,000 BTU |
| Covered enclosed patio | Electric infrared (1,500W is sufficient) |

### Wind and Open vs. Covered Spaces

Propane heaters heat the air around them. In a breeze, that heated air moves away quickly — reducing effective coverage. In very exposed, windy spots, a glass tube (as on the Hiland) helps, but propane heaters still underperform in persistent wind. Electric infrared heats objects directly through radiation, which is less affected by air movement. Covered patios naturally block wind; open exposed areas benefit from propane's higher BTU output.

### Safety for Different Fuel Types

**Propane:** Never use propane heaters indoors or in enclosed spaces. Combustion produces CO (carbon monoxide). Keep 3 feet of clearance from overhead structures when operating.

**Electric:** Safe for covered areas, screened porches, and semi-enclosed spaces as long as the heater has outdoor (weather-resistant) IP rating. Check for IPX4 minimum.

### Portability Needs

Full-size propane heaters (AZ Patio, Fire Sense) are meant to stay in one spot — they're heavy (36–50 lbs) and connect to a large propane tank. If you want to move a heater seasonally or between spaces, choose a model with wheels. Tabletop propane units are genuinely portable.

## FAQ

**How much does it cost to run a propane patio heater?**
A 20 lb propane tank costs $20–30 to refill (approximately $3–4 per gallon, tanks hold ~4.7 gallons). At 41,000 BTU output with ~9 hours of run time, that's roughly $2.50–3.50 per hour for full-output operation. Dialing back to 50% output doubles run time.

**How long will a propane tank last?**
A 20 lb propane tank provides approximately 9 hours of runtime at full 41,000 BTU output. At 75% output, expect 12 hours. Keep a spare tank on hand for events longer than one evening.

**Can I use a patio heater under a pergola?**
Electric infrared heaters: yes, safe under pergolas and covered patios with proper 3-foot clearance. Propane heaters: consult the manufacturer's clearance requirements — typically 3 feet from combustible materials above and to the sides. Most propane heaters are not approved for enclosed or low-ceiling spaces.

**Do patio heaters work in below-freezing temperatures?**
A 41,000 BTU propane heater maintains comfortable temperatures (50°F+) in outdoor spaces down to about 20°F ambient — closer to the heater. Below 20°F, you're fighting the physics of heating an open space. Electric 1,500W heaters are less effective in sub-freezing temperatures for open outdoor areas.
