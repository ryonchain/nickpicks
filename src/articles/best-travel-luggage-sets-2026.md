## How to Choose a Luggage Set

**1. Shell Material: ABS vs. Polycarbonate**
ABS is lighter and more scratch-resistant but cracks under sharp impact. Polycarbonate is more expensive but flexes instead of cracking when bags are dropped or crushed. For frequent travelers or anyone who checks bags regularly, polycarbonate is worth the additional cost. For carry-on-only travelers, ABS is perfectly adequate.

**2. Wheel Quality**
This is the most frequent failure point in budget luggage. Multi-directional spinner wheels (four-wheel systems) let you roll luggage beside you rather than dragging it behind — dramatically easier in crowded terminals. Test by pushing an empty bag: it should roll without pulling to one side and should track straight when released. Budget sets with poor wheel quality are identifiable within the first airport test run.

**3. Handle System**
A handle that wobbles at full extension is surprisingly fatiguing over long distances. Look for handles that lock into at least two height positions and feel solid when pushed laterally. Travelpro's PowerScope system is the gold standard; Samsonite's are the reliable mid-tier benchmark.

**4. Set Size Configuration**
Most three-piece sets include a 20-inch carry-on, 24-inch medium checked, and 28-inch large checked bag. Verify dimensions against your primary airline's size limits — low-cost carriers increasingly restrict carry-on to 18 or 19 inches. If you almost never travel with a third bag, a two-piece set at a higher per-piece quality level may make more sense than three lower-quality pieces.

## FAQ

**Is hardshell or softshell luggage better for checked bags?**
Hardshell is better for protecting fragile contents and resists getting wet in rain or snow on the tarmac. Softshell packs more flexibly and compresses for tight overhead bins. For checked bags, hardshell is generally the better call; for carry-ons that need to fit in full overhead bins, softshell can be more forgiving.

**What size carry-on is safe for most airlines?**
22 x 14 x 9 inches (including wheels and handles) is the standard domestic US allowance. International carriers, especially budget airlines in Europe and Asia, often restrict to smaller dimensions. A 20-inch carry-on from a reputable brand typically stays within limits but always verify against your specific airline.

**How long should a mid-range luggage set last?**
A $150–$300 set from a reputable brand should handle 3–5 years of regular travel (10–20 trips annually) without structural failure. Wheels and zippers are the first failure points — brands with available replacement parts extend useful life significantly.

**Are TSA locks on luggage actually useful?**
TSA-approved locks prevent opportunistic theft while allowing TSA agents to inspect bags without destroying your lock. They don't prevent determined theft but add a meaningful deterrent. Any set in the $100+ range should include them as standard.
