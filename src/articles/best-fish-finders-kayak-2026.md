## Buyer's Guide

### Transducer Types for Kayaks

**Traditional CHIRP:** Best for identifying fish and general depth. Standard on most units.

**DownScan / ClearVü / DownVü:** High-frequency imaging directly below the boat. Near-photographic views of structure.

**SideScan / SideVü / SideScan Imaging:** Images to the sides. See 30–60 feet on each side. Excellent for finding drop-offs, timber, submerged grass beds.

**LiveScope / Mega Live:** Real-time moving sonar image. See fish moving in real time. Game-changing technology. Requires premium units ($600+).

### Display Size Guide
- **3.5"**: Portable/backup; hard to read in sunlight
- **5"**: Most popular for kayaks — good balance of info and compact size
- **7"**: Easiest to read; heavier; better for larger kayaks or side-imaging

### Kayak Mounting Options
1. **RAM Mount**: Universal ball-mount system. Most popular. ~$20–$40.
2. **Scotty mount**: Common on fishing kayaks. Strong, adjustable.
3. **Flush mount**: Permanent installation in kayak hull. Cleanest look.
4. **Suction cup**: Not recommended — vibration causes issues.

### Transducer Placement
- **Thru-hull**: Mount inside the hull using epoxy. Protects transducer from damage. Some signal loss.
- **Through-hull**: Drill through hull (better signal). More complex install.
- **Shoot-through**: Stick inside hull with foam/oil. Easy, signal varies.
- **Kayak scupper**: Mount in scupper holes. Convenient; common for fishing kayaks.

## FAQ

**Do fish finders work in shallow water?**
Yes, but very shallow water (under 2 feet) can cause "clutter" near the surface. Most units work well in 2+ feet. ClearVü and DownScan imaging work better in 8–60 ft depth.

**What's the difference between 2D sonar and ClearVü?**
2D/CHIRP sonar shows traditional arches and lines that require interpretation. ClearVü (and similar DownScan) produces photo-like images of structure below — much easier to "see" the bottom.

**Do I need GPS on a fish finder?**
Not required, but highly recommended. GPS lets you mark waypoints (productive spots), track your path, and get speed data. Units with charts add navigation capability for unfamiliar water.

**How do I power a fish finder on a kayak?**
Most fish finders run on 12V. Options:
- Small 12V lithium battery ($30–$80) — cheapest, easily portable
- Larger AGM/lithium deep cycle — longer runtime
- Most kayak fish finders run 8–12 hours on a 7Ah battery

**What fish finder do tournament kayak anglers use?**
Most tournament anglers use Garmin Echomap UHD2 or Humminbird Helix with LiveScope/Mega Live live sonar. These units ($600–$1,500+) show fish moving in real time.
