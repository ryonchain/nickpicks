## How to Choose a Wet/Dry Vacuum

**Tank size guide:**
- **2–5 gallon:** Compact, portable — tool connection, spot cleanup, small spaces
- **6–9 gallon:** Home garage, woodworking, general cleanup — the right size for most homeowners
- **12–16 gallon:** Workshop, job site, flooding, heavy debris — for serious use

**Suction power:** Measured in HP (amperage × efficiency) and "water lift" (inches). Water lift = how powerfully it pulls at low airflow. For fine dust collection from power tools, higher water lift matters more than peak HP.

**Filtration for fine dust:** Standard filters capture large debris adequately. For drywall, MDF, concrete, or plywood dust, use a HEPA or fine-dust filter — inhaling fine particles is a serious long-term health risk.

**Wet pickup:** Remove the filter before vacuuming liquids — a wet filter pushes water back out and can damage the motor. All models include a float shut-off that stops suction when the tank is full of liquid.

**Accessories to look for:**
- Floor nozzle (standard)
- Crevice tool for tight corners
- Dusting brush for shelves and cabinets
- 2.5" to 1.25" reducer for smaller tool ports
- Extension wands for high shelves and ceilings

---

## Maintenance Tips

1. **Clean or replace filter regularly** — a clogged filter dramatically reduces suction. Tap filter against a trash can between uses
2. **Empty before full** — vacuuming with a full tank strains the motor
3. **Run dry before storing** — remove and wipe the tank after wet pickup to prevent mold
4. **Check hose connections** — air leaks at fittings reduce suction; ensure all connections are seated
5. **Store accessories inside** — keep nozzles and brushes inside the tank when not in use
