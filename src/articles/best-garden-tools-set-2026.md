## What Every Gardener Actually Needs

### The Essential 5 Hand Tools
Every garden kit should start with these:

1. **Trowel** — Hand digging, planting seedlings, moving bulbs
2. **Transplanting trowel** (narrow, pointed) — Precision planting in tight spaces
3. **Hand cultivator/fork** — Breaking up soil, aerating roots
4. **Hand weeder** — Removing tap-rooted weeds (dandelions, thistles)
5. **Pruning shears** — Cutting stems, deadheading flowers, light branch trimming

If you only buy one additional tool: a **kneeler pad**. Kneeling on bare ground for 20 minutes causes discomfort most beginners don't anticipate.

### Material Guide: What Actually Lasts

**Forged stainless steel**: Strongest, most durable, doesn't rust. Most expensive.
**Cast aluminum**: Rust-proof, light, good for most gardeners. Budget to mid-range.
**Coated carbon steel**: Strong but rusts if not cleaned. Polish after use.
**Zinc-cast/plastic**: Avoid. Bends and breaks in real soil.

### Handle Material Matters

**Ash wood**: Traditional, absorbs shock, comfortable. Requires oiling once per season.
**Fiberglass**: Extremely durable, doesn't rot. Harder grip feel.
**Ergonomic plastic**: Best for joint concerns. Radius Garden's radial handle is the best example.

### Don't Skip the Gloves
Rose thorns, splinters, irritating plants (euphorbia, poison ivy), pesticide exposure — gloves protect hands you use for everything else. The best gardening gloves have reinforced fingertips and fit snugly so you feel what you're touching. Touchscreen-compatible gloves exist if you need your phone while working.

## FAQ

**What garden tools should I buy first?**
Trowel, hand cultivator, and a kneeler. Those three cover 80% of everyday garden tasks. Add pruning shears and a weeder when you need them.

**Are expensive garden tools worth it?**
For tools you use daily, yes. A $15 trowel bends in heavy clay soil and lasts 2 seasons. A $45 forged trowel lasts 20 years. The break-even on quality tools is fast. For tools you use occasionally, budget aluminum is fine.

**How do I maintain garden tools?**
Clean soil off metal after each use. Oil wooden handles once per season (boiled linseed oil). Sharpen edges annually (hoes, trowels benefit from edge maintenance). Store in a dry location — hanging on wall hooks or in a tool bag is better than lying in a damp shed.
