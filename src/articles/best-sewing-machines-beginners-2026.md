## What to Look For

### Mechanical vs Computerized

**Mechanical:** Dials and levers set all functions. Simpler, more durable, easier to repair. Best for beginners who want to learn the basics.

**Computerized:** LCD screen, automatic stitch selection, memory. More features, slightly more complex. The Brother CS6000i is the best example of a beginner-friendly computerized machine.

### Key Features to Prioritize

1. **Automatic needle threader:** The single best feature for beginners — threading a needle by hand is frustrating and time-consuming.
2. **Drop-in bobbin:** Top-loading (drop-in) bobbins are much easier to manage than front-loading.
3. **Speed control:** A variable speed control or speed limiter helps beginners go slow while learning.
4. **Stitch variety:** 12–60 stitches is plenty. You'll use straight stitch, zigzag, and buttonhole 90% of the time.

### Common Beginner Mistakes

- **Buying too cheap:** Machines under $50 often jam, break threads, and skip stitches. The $60 Singer M1500 is the floor for a reliable beginner machine.
- **Ignoring threading:** Most sewing problems are caused by incorrect threading — top thread, bobbin, or both. Rethread completely before assuming the machine is broken.
- **Wrong needle for fabric:** Use a stretch needle for knits, a universal for wovens, a sharp needle for microfiber. Wrong needle = skipped stitches.

## FAQ

The Brother CS6000i — automatic needle threader, 60 stitches, excellent instructions, and it's one of the most popular machines for learning.

**Is Singer or Brother better for beginners?**
Both are excellent. Brother has a better reputation for computerized beginner machines; Singer wins for heavy-duty mechanical machines.

$100–$200 is the sweet spot. Under $100 means compromising on build quality; over $200 as a beginner means features you won't use yet.
