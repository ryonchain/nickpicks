## FAQ

**What is the difference between RGB and RGBIC LED strips?**

RGB strips treat the entire strip as one zone — every LED changes to the same color at the same time. RGBIC (IC stands for integrated circuit) strips have independent control chips along the length, so different segments can display different colors simultaneously, creating gradient or chasing effects. For a gaming desk with dynamic lighting, RGBIC looks significantly more impressive. For simple bias lighting behind a monitor, basic RGB is usually enough and costs less.

**Can you cut LED strip lights to a custom length?**

Yes, most LED strips can be cut at marked scissor points — usually every 2–4 inches, indicated by a dotted line and scissor icon on the strip itself. Always cut only at these marks; cutting between them severs a circuit and can leave a dead segment at the end of your strip. If you need to reconnect cut sections later, snap-on LED strip connectors are sold cheaply on Amazon and don't require any soldering.

**Do LED strip lights use a lot of electricity?**

No — LED strips are very energy-efficient. A typical 16.4-foot RGB strip running at full white brightness draws around 24–36 watts. At the US average electricity rate of roughly $0.16 per kWh, running a strip for 6 hours a day costs about $0.01–$0.02 per day, or under $7 for a full year. Colored modes at partial brightness draw even less, so there's no meaningful reason to switch them off for energy savings.
