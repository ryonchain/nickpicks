## Buying Guide: Tripod Buying Checklist

### Max load rating
Always buy a tripod rated to at least 50% more than your camera + heaviest lens. A 2 kg body + 500g lens = 2.5 kg kit. Buy a tripod rated to 4 kg minimum.

### Head type
Ball heads are the most common: fast to reposition, good for stills and video. Pan-tilt heads (two separate knobs) are slower but offer more precise framing — preferred for studio and product photography.

### Leg sections
3-section legs are stiffer and faster to deploy. 4-section legs pack smaller. For travel: 4-section. For local landscape work: 3-section.

### Leg locks
Twist locks: compact, but slower. Flip locks: faster, easier in cold weather. Both work; pick based on preference.

### Center column
A column that tilts or goes horizontal unlocks macro, overhead, and low-angle work. If you shoot in these scenarios, pay for it.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need a tripod for mirrorless cameras?**
Yes. In-body stabilization handles handheld shooting for most focal lengths, but it can't replace a tripod for long exposures, video work, macro photography, or telephoto shooting in low light.

**Q: What's the difference between a ball head and a pan-tilt head?**
A ball head uses a single ball-in-socket to position the camera in any direction — fast to adjust, good for stills and travel. A pan-tilt head has separate controls for horizontal and vertical movement — slower but more precise, preferred for video and architecture photography.

**Q: Can I use a $40 tripod with a $1,000 camera?**
Technically yes, but the risk of damaging your gear increases with a cheap tripod. Budget tripods fail at leg locks, center columns, and ball head clamps. A $80–$100 tripod is a much safer baseline for real camera systems.

**Q: How heavy should a travel tripod be?**
Most photographers tolerate 1.0–1.5 kg for travel. Anything above 2 kg and you'll start leaving it behind. Compact carbon fiber tripods hit 0.8–1.2 kg but cost significantly more.

**Q: Do I need a quick-release plate?**
Yes. A quick-release plate (Arca-Swiss standard is most common) attaches to the camera bottom and clicks in/out of the tripod head. Without one, you're screwing the camera in by hand every time. Most tripods in this list include one.
