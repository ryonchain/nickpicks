## How to Choose a Food Processor

### Size Guide

- **3-cup mini:** Garlic, herbs, nuts, small sauces — occasional use
- **7–9 cup:** 1–3 person households, moderate weekly prep
- **11–14 cup:** Families of 4+, regular meal prep
- **16 cup:** Batch cooking, large families, serious home cooks

### Motor Power and Longevity

Motor power matters for longevity as much as performance. A 720W motor running at 50% capacity will last longer than a 400W motor at 90% capacity doing the same task. If you process tough vegetables or bread dough regularly, buy more motor than you think you need.

### Blade Quality

Stainless steel blades hold an edge significantly longer than cheaper alloys. Cuisinart and Breville use high-quality blades. Replacement blade sets for both brands are readily available for under $30 — a valuable long-term consideration.

### What Food Processors Can't Do

Food processors are not blenders. They don't make smooth liquids (use a blender). They're not designed for continuous high-speed operation (motors overheat after several minutes). They don't whip cream or beat eggs (use a stand mixer or hand mixer).

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a food processor if I have a KitchenAid stand mixer?**
They serve different purposes. A KitchenAid handles mixing (doughs, batters, whipped cream) but doesn't slice or shred. A food processor handles chopping, slicing, and shredding but doesn't whip air into mixtures. They complement each other.

**How long do food processors last?**
A Cuisinart Custom 14 with normal use lasts 10–15 years. The motor is the weak point — avoid running it continuously for more than 2–3 minutes or overloading it with extremely stiff doughs. Let it cool between heavy tasks.

**Is a food processor worth it?**
If you cook 4+ times per week and regularly prep vegetables, cheese, or large batches, yes — it saves 20–30 minutes per session. If you cook twice a week and buy pre-cut vegetables, probably not.
