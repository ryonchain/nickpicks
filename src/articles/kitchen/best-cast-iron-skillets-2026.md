Cast iron is the only cookware that gets better with use. A properly seasoned cast iron skillet from 1970 is more non-stick than one bought yesterday. It sears steak better than stainless, handles oven temperatures that destroy non-stick coatings, and weighs enough to stay put while you cook. The same pan works on induction, gas, electric, campfire, and in the oven at 500°F.

The question isn't whether you need cast iron. It's which one to buy.

## Why Cast Iron?

**Heat retention:** Cast iron's thermal mass holds temperature through cold food additions — a steak doesn't drop the pan below searing temp when it hits the surface. This creates a better crust than thin stainless or aluminum.

**Versatility:** Start on the stovetop, finish in the oven, serve at the table. No other pan covers this range without special considerations.

**Non-stick over time:** A well-seasoned cast iron is naturally non-stick. The polymerized oil layer builds over years of cooking and improves with use.

**Durability:** A cast iron skillet maintained properly lasts indefinitely. Many people cook daily on skillets that are 50+ years old.

**Price:** The best cast iron skillets cost $25–$50. There is essentially no diminishing return on investment past that point for traditional cast iron.

## Cast Iron Care: The Right Way

**After every use:**
1. Wash with hot water while still warm (food releases easily)
2. Use a stiff brush or chainmail scrubber — minimal soap
3. Dry immediately and thoroughly (rust forms fast)
4. Apply a thin layer of oil, heat briefly on the stovetop, let cool

**Never:**
- Soak in water (rust)
- Leave wet
- Put in the dishwasher
- Use cold water on a hot skillet (thermal shock can crack)

**Reseasoning if rust appears:**
1. Scrub off rust with steel wool (it's OK — you're starting fresh)
2. Dry completely
3. Apply a thin layer of oil all over
4. Bake upside-down at 450°F for 1 hour
5. Repeat 2–3 times

## What to Cook in Cast Iron

**Best uses:**
- **Steak:** Preheat empty for 5–8 minutes before adding the steak. The high heat creates a crust non-stick pans can't match.
- **Cornbread:** The classic. Put the skillet in the oven while it preheats so the batter sizzles when it hits the surface.
- **Fried chicken:** Cast iron holds temperature through each batch better than any other pan.
- **Frittata:** Start on stovetop, finish in the oven. Cast iron handles the transition seamlessly.
- **Skillet cookies:** The "pizookie" (cast iron chocolate chip cookie) is a genre unto itself.

**What cast iron isn't ideal for:**
- Delicate fish (tends to stick even when well-seasoned)
- Acidic dishes cooked for a long time (tomato sauce for 30+ minutes will react with bare iron, potentially stripping seasoning)
- Foods that need quick temperature changes
