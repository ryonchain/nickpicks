## How to Choose a Drip Coffee Maker

### Brew Temperature Is Everything

The Specialty Coffee Association (SCA) certifies coffee makers that maintain 197–204°F throughout the brew cycle. At 185°F (most budget machines), coffee is under-extracted — it tastes flat and weak even with proper dosing. At 212°F (boiling), coffee is over-extracted and bitter. If you're spending more than $80 on a coffee maker, verify it brews at 200°F.

### Glass Carafe vs. Thermal Carafe

Glass carafes sit on a hot plate to stay warm — which is convenient but scorches the coffee after 20–30 minutes. Thermal stainless carafes hold temperature for 2–4 hours without external heat, preserving flavor significantly longer. If you brew in the morning and drink throughout the day, a thermal carafe is worth the premium.

### Programmable Delay Brew

If you want coffee ready when you wake up, programmable delay brew is the feature. Most machines above $50 include it. Fill the water and grounds the night before, set a wake time, and coffee is ready when you need it.

### Capacity

- 1–2 people: 5–9 cup capacity
- 3–4 people: 10–12 cup capacity
- 5+ people or office: 14 cup capacity

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the best budget coffee maker under $100?**
The Cuisinart DCC-3200 (~$70) is the best budget option that includes all useful features: programmable delay, adjustable keep-warm, and 14-cup capacity.

**How important is SCA certification?**
Meaningfully important. SCA certification means the machine is independently verified to brew at the correct temperature (197–204°F) with proper extraction time. It's the clearest signal that a machine will produce consistently good coffee.

**Can a cheap coffee maker produce good coffee?**
Yes, but it requires offsetting the lower brew temperature — use slightly more grounds (1.25–1.5× the standard dose) and accept that some nuance will be lost. Fresh, quality beans and a burr grinder matter more than the machine at entry-level price points.
