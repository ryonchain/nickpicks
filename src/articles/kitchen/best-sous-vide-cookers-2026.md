Sous vide cooking delivers results that no other method can match for precision. A steak cooked sous vide reaches exactly 130°F edge-to-edge, every time, without overcooking. Chicken breast stays moist and tender instead of dry and tough. Salmon turns custard-like. The technique — sealing food in a bag and cooking it in a temperature-controlled water bath — was once exclusive to fine dining restaurants. Now a home sous vide circulator costs under $100.

This guide explains the technique and covers the best circulators available in 2026.

## How Sous Vide Works

A sous vide circulator clamps to the side of a pot or container, heats water to a precise temperature (within 0.1°F), and keeps it circulating. You seal food in a zip-lock or vacuum-sealed bag, submerge it, and let time and temperature do the rest.

**The key insight:** With conventional cooking, you're trying to hit a target temperature at the center of food while the outside overcooks. With sous vide, every part of the food is exactly at your target temperature. There's no "window" of doneness — food stays at perfect temperature for as long as it's in the water bath.

Results: medium-rare steak that's uniformly pink edge-to-edge. Chicken that's pasteurized and tender at 145°F instead of dried out at 165°F. Eggs with yolks the texture of custard.

The final step — a quick, high-heat sear in a cast iron pan or on a grill — creates the Maillard reaction browning that makes food look and taste like restaurant quality.

## Temperature and Time Guide

| Food | Temperature | Time |
|------|------------|------|
| Steak (medium-rare) | 130°F / 54°C | 1–4 hours |
| Chicken breast | 140°F / 60°C | 1.5–4 hours |
| Salmon | 122°F / 50°C | 30–45 min |
| Eggs (soft-cooked) | 145°F / 63°C | 1 hour |
| Pork chops | 140°F / 60°C | 1–4 hours |
| Pork shoulder | 165°F / 74°C | 24–36 hours |
| Short ribs | 135°F / 57°C | 48–72 hours |

The wide time ranges are forgiving — sous vide food doesn't overcook quickly (it won't overcook at all until extended windows close). You have hours, not minutes, of flexibility.

## Common Sous Vide Questions

**Do I need a vacuum sealer?** No. The water displacement method works well for zip-lock bags: close the bag most of the way, lower it into water slowly, and the water pressure pushes air out. Seal when you've expelled as much air as possible. For most applications, this is good enough.

**Is it safe?** Yes. Sous vide pasteurizes food through a combination of temperature and time. Chicken at 140°F for 1.5 hours is fully pasteurized — much lower than the conventional 165°F, which is an instant-kill temperature. The USDA tables confirm this.

**What container should I use?** Any deep pot or food-safe container that fits the circulator clamp. A 12-quart polycarbonate container with a lid is ideal — the lid reduces heat loss and evaporation during long cooks.

**Does it taste different?** Yes — in ways that are immediately obvious. Steak cooked to 130°F throughout tastes fundamentally different from steak grilled to 130°F at the center with an overcooked exterior. The texture is more tender, the flavor more uniform.

## First Recipes to Try

**Perfect steak:** Season a ribeye, seal in a bag, cook at 130°F for 2 hours, sear in cast iron for 45 seconds per side with butter, garlic, and thyme. The first time most people do this, they consider it a revelation.

**Chicken breast:** At 140°F for 1.5 hours, chicken breast is fully cooked and unbelievably moist. Season with salt, garlic, and herbs before sealing. Sear quickly or serve directly.

**Soft-boiled eggs:** 145°F for exactly 1 hour produces a set white and runny, jammy yolk. Perfect every single time.
