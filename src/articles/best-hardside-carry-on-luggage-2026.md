## Hardside Carry-On Buying Guide

### Shell materials: polycarbonate vs. aluminum vs. ABS

**Polycarbonate (PC)** is the standard for quality hardside luggage above $150. It's derived from the same material used in bulletproof glass and helmet visors. PC shells flex under impact rather than cracking, which distributes stress and prevents structural failure. It's lighter than aluminum and tougher than ABS.

**Aluminum** is the most premium option. It won't crack or scratch as easily as plastic — it dents. Dents are cosmetic and don't affect structural integrity. Aluminum is significantly heavier (8+ lbs for a carry-on) and more expensive ($600–$1,500). It's the choice for travelers who want the ultimate in durability and are willing to pay for it.

**ABS plastic** is the cheapest hardside material. It's rigid but brittle — it cracks rather than flexes under impact. Suitable for budget options used occasionally, but not recommended for frequent travel.

**PC/ABS blends** offer a middle ground — more flexible than pure ABS, less expensive than pure PC. Most mid-range hardside bags ($100–$200) use a PC/ABS mix.

### Interior packing systems

A hardside bag's interior packing system matters as much as the shell. Look for:

- **Compression straps** on both halves — prevents clothing from shifting
- **Divider panels** with separate compartments for shoes, toiletries, and clothing
- **Zippered interior pockets** for small items that would otherwise float in the main compartment
- **Laundry separator** — a zippered partition for worn clothing

### Scratches: what to expect and how to minimize them

Every hardside bag will show surface scratches after normal use. This is unavoidable and cosmetic only — scratches don't affect the bag's structural integrity or water resistance. To minimize:

1. **Choose textured finishes** over smooth gloss — the micro-grooves or ribbing on textured shells scatter light and make scratches less visible
2. **Choose matte finishes** over gloss — glossy surfaces show every mark, matte surfaces absorb scratches more gracefully
3. **Use a luggage cover** for gate-checked bags — even a simple fabric cover prevents most surface scratches during rough handling

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the best material for hardside luggage?**
Polycarbonate is the best all-around material — it's lighter than aluminum, flexes slightly under impact (which prevents cracking), and is more scratch-resistant than ABS plastic. Aluminum is the most premium and durable but significantly heavier.

**Will a hardside carry-on crack if it falls?**
Polycarbonate shells are designed to flex rather than crack — they handle falls better than ABS plastic. True cracking from a single fall is rare with quality polycarbonate. The weakest points on any hardside bag are the zipper teeth and wheel housings.

**Is hardside or softside luggage better for carry-on?**
Hardside protects fragile contents better and is waterproof. Softside is lighter, has external pockets, and can flex to fit in tight overhead bins. For carry-ons that never leave the cabin, softside is practical. For bags that get gate-checked frequently, hardside is more protective.

**Do hardside bags scratch easily?**
Yes — all hardside luggage picks up surface scratches from normal use. This is expected and cosmetic only. Textured surfaces show scratches less than smooth gloss finishes.
