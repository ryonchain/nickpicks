## How Dull Do Your Knives Get? (And Why It Matters for Choosing a Sharpener)

First, understand what sharpening does: it removes metal from the blade to recreate a fine, V-shaped edge. Different tools remove different amounts of metal at different grits:

- **Coarse (80–120 grit):** Fast metal removal; for very dull or damaged edges
- **Medium (220–400 grit):** Standard resharpening for typical home-use dullness
- **Fine (800–1500 grit):** Refines and polishes an already reasonably sharp edge
- **Honing (diamond or ceramic rods):** Realigns the edge without removing much metal — this is maintenance between sharpenings

**Most home knives need:** A medium sharpening every 2–3 months, plus honing (with a honing rod) after every few uses.

## Types of Knife Sharpeners

### Electric Sharpeners

Pull the knife through motorized abrasive wheels in multiple stages (coarse → fine → hone). Fast (60–90 seconds), easy, and very consistent.

**Pros:** Fastest; consistent results; good for beginners  
**Cons:** Remove more metal per sharpening; less control over final angle; not suitable for Japanese single-bevel knives

### Manual Pull-Through Sharpeners

Fixed abrasive slots you pull the blade through. Pre-set sharpening angle means no technique required.

**Pros:** Inexpensive; compact; fast for quick touch-ups  
**Cons:** Fixed angle may not match all knife geometry; removes more metal than whetstones

### Whetstones (Water Stones)

Flat abrasive blocks used with water or oil. Requires technique and practice but produces the best, most customizable edges.

**Pros:** Best possible edge; works on all knives; long-lasting tool  
**Cons:** Learning curve; takes 5–15 minutes per knife; requires practice

### Honing Rods (Sharpening Steel)

Not sharpeners — honing rods realign the blade's microscopic edge without removing metal. Used weekly or before each cooking session to maintain sharpness between full sharpenings.

---

## Key Features to Compare

### Number of Sharpening Stages

Quality electric sharpeners use 2–3 stages:
1. Coarse diamonds — reshapes edge
2. Fine abrasive — refines
3. Honing/stropping — polishes and removes burrs

Three stages produce the best results for Western (European) knives.

### Sharpening Angle

Most Western knives are sharpened at 15–20° per side. Many electric sharpeners are pre-set at 20° — this works well for German steel knives (Wüsthof, Henckels). Japanese knives (Global, Shun) are typically sharpened at 10–15° and require a sharpener or whetstone that accommodates that angle.

### Diamond vs. Ceramic Abrasives

Diamond abrasives are harder and faster-cutting; they work on the most difficult steels. Ceramic abrasives produce a slightly finer finish. Premium sharpeners use diamond for coarse stages and ceramic for fine/polish stages.

### Don't Confuse Honing with Sharpening

A honing rod (or honing steel) doesn't sharpen — it straightens the microscopic edge that bends with use. Use a honing rod before every cooking session; use a sharpener when the honing rod no longer restores the feel of a sharp knife.

### How Often Should You Sharpen?

For home cooks who cook 3–4 times per week:
- **Hone:** Before each use or every 1–2 uses
- **Sharpen with electric/pull-through:** Every 2–3 months
- **Full whetstone session:** Once or twice a year

## FAQ: Knife Sharpeners

**Can I sharpen a serrated knife?**
Yes, but it requires a special tool. The Chef'sChoice Trizor XV (Stage 3) and the Spyderco Sharpmaker both handle serrated knives. Standard pull-through sharpeners do not.

**Do electric sharpeners damage knives?**
They remove more metal per session than whetstones but produce a very good edge. On cheap knives, this doesn't matter. On high-end knives, minimize electric sharpening to when truly necessary — use a honing rod for regular maintenance.

**What angle should I sharpen at?**
Western knives: 17–20° per side. Japanese knives: 10–15° per side. If you don't know what angle your knife was factory-sharpened at, 15° per side is a safe all-around choice.

**Is a $10 pull-through sharpener worth it?**
For inexpensive kitchen knives: yes. For premium chef's knives ($100+), invest in a better sharpener that gives you more control.

**What's the best way to test if a knife is sharp?**
The paper test: hold a sheet of printer paper and slice down through it. A sharp knife cuts cleanly with a satisfying zip. A dull knife tears or catches.
