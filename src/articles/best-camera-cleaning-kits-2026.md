## Understanding Camera Cleaning: What Needs Cleaning

### Lens glass (front and rear elements)
Fingerprints, dust, and moisture collect on the front element. The rear element is more critical to image quality — contamination here is closer to the sensor and has greater effect on image sharpness. Clean with a lens-safe cleaning fluid and lint-free wipes.

### Sensor
The image sensor collects dust whenever you change lenses (and sometimes just from use). Sensor dust appears as soft dark spots in images — most visible in a clear sky at small apertures (f/8–f/16). Cleaning is more involved than lens cleaning and requires care, but is entirely manageable as a DIY task.

### Camera body exterior
Grip rubber, weather seals, and body panel seams collect dust and grime. Regular exterior cleaning prevents buildup from working into seams and dials.

### Viewfinder eyepiece and LCD
The EVF eyepiece and rear LCD accumulate skin oils and dust. These don't affect image quality but affect your ability to compose and review shots accurately.

## Buying Guide: Camera Cleaning Routine

### How often should I clean my sensor?
Inspect monthly if you change lenses frequently, or whenever you notice dark spots in images. Take a test shot of a clear sky or gray wall at f/11–f/16 to reveal dust — it appears as soft-edged dark circles.

### Safe cleaning order
1. **Blower first** — always start with air. Loose dust comes off without any contact risk.
2. **Brush second** — for stubborn loose particles, use the Arctic Butterfly or a similar sensor brush.
3. **Wet swab last** — only when blower + brush leave visible contamination.

### What to avoid
- Never use canned compressed air (propellant damages sensors)
- Never use household glass cleaner (solvents attack lens coatings)
- Never use paper towels or T-shirts (scratches glass)
- Never blow with your mouth (introduces moisture and oils)

## FAQ

**Q: Is it safe to clean my own camera sensor?**
Yes, for most photographers. Modern mirrorless cameras expose the sensor to the room during lens changes anyway — the sensor isn't fragile. Use the correct tools (blower, then swabs if needed), follow the process, and work slowly. Hundreds of thousands of photographers clean their own sensors without issue.

**Q: How do I know if my sensor needs cleaning?**
Take a test shot of a blank, evenly lit surface (gray wall, clear sky) at f/11–f/16. Review at 100% on screen. Dark soft-edged blobs or spots indicate sensor dust. Clean spots (clear or the same texture as the surface) mean the sensor is fine.

**Q: Will sensor cleaning void my warranty?**
Typically no — camera warranties cover manufacturing defects, not user-caused damage from improper cleaning. Using the correct tools (sensor swabs with Eclipse fluid) carries no warranty risk. Spraying canned air might, technically, but isn't something manufacturers typically investigate.

**Q: What size sensor swab do I need?**
Full-frame (36×24mm): full-frame swabs. APS-C (approximately 23×15mm): APS-C swabs. Micro Four Thirds (17.3×13mm): MFT swabs. Using a full-frame swab on an APS-C camera is fine — it's just wider than necessary.

**Q: Can I clean the rear lens element the same way I clean the front?**
Yes, but with extra care. The rear element is critical to image quality and closer to the sensor. Apply minimal fluid, use a clean swab or wipe, and avoid pressing hard. If the rear element has significant contamination, consider a professional lens service.
