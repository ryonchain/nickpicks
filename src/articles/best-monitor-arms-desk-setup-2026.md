A monitor arm does something a monitor stand can't: it frees your desk surface completely and lets you position your screen at exactly the right height, angle, and distance for your posture. The difference is real — a properly positioned monitor at eye level reduces neck strain compared to a screen sitting flat on a desk at the wrong height.

This guide covers five monitor arms for every budget — from the $35 entry-level pick to dual-arm setups — with weight limits, VESA compatibility, and clear guidance on when single vs. dual makes sense.

---

## What to Know Before You Buy

**VESA compatibility:** Almost all monitors support VESA mounting, but the pattern varies. Common sizes: 75×75mm (smaller screens), 100×100mm (most monitors), and 200×200mm (large displays). Check your monitor's manual or the rear panel — VESA mounts are standardized bolt-hole squares.

**Weight capacity:** Monitor arms have rated weight limits. Most 24–27" monitors weigh 8–12 lbs. Large 32"+ or ultrawide monitors can weigh 15–25 lbs. An underpowered arm sags over time and loses its set position. Always verify your monitor's weight before buying.

**Desk clamp vs. grommet:** Most arms mount via a desk clamp (tightens on the edge) or a grommet hole (bolts through a hole in the desk). Clamps work on most desks; grommets are more stable but require a hole.

**Single vs. dual:** Dual monitor arms hold two screens on one pole. They save space but require both monitors to weigh within the dual arm's limit. If your monitors are different sizes, confirm the arm handles mismatched weights and positions independently.

**Gas spring vs. friction:** Gas spring arms move with light pressure and hold position without tightening a knob. Friction arms use a manually tightened bolt to hold position — fine for static setups, annoying if you adjust frequently.

## Single vs. Dual: Which Do You Need?

**Single arm** is right if you work primarily from one screen. It frees the most desk space per dollar, installs in minutes, and positions one monitor exactly where you want it.

**Dual arm** is right if you use two monitors regularly. A dual arm on one pole keeps both screens in a coordinated arrangement — easier to cable-manage and more visually consistent than two separate clamps. (If you prefer stands over arms, see our guide to [dual monitor stands](/articles/best-monitor-stands-dual-setup-2026/).) The trade-off: dual arms are harder to position if your monitors are significantly different sizes or weights.

**Mixed-size monitors:** If you're pairing a 27" primary with a 24" secondary, ensure the dual arm's per-arm weight limit accommodates both independently. Most dual arms (including the HUANUO) allow different heights per arm, so a height-mismatched setup works fine.

## Is a Monitor Arm Worth It?

Yes, for two reasons: ergonomics and desk space.

**Ergonomics:** Most monitor stands don't allow enough height adjustment for proper posture. The screen top should be at or slightly below eye level, with the screen at arm's length. A monitor arm makes this adjustment trivial.

**Desk space:** Lifting the monitor off the desk frees the footprint of the stand — typically 8–10 inches of depth. On a standard desk, that's meaningful real estate for a keyboard, notebook, or second monitor.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What VESA size do I need for a monitor arm?**
Most monitors use 75×75mm or 100×100mm VESA patterns. Check your monitor's manual or the rear mounting plate — the bolt holes form a square. Most 24–27" monitors use 100×100mm. Both sizes are supported by the arms in this guide.

**How much weight can a monitor arm hold?**
Budget single arms typically handle 14–17 lbs. The Ergotron LX supports up to 25 lbs, covering most monitors up to 34". Most 24–27" monitors weigh 8–12 lbs. Always verify your monitor's weight before buying.

**Are monitor arms worth it?**
Yes — for two reasons: ergonomics (easy eye-level positioning to reduce neck strain) and desk space (removes the monitor stand footprint, freeing 8–10 inches of desk depth).

**What's the difference between gas spring and friction monitor arms?**
Gas spring arms move with fingertip pressure and hold position without tightening — push the monitor where you want it. Friction arms use a manually tightened knob. Gas spring is significantly more convenient if you adjust frequently.

**How do I install a monitor arm without drilling into my desk?**
Use the desk clamp attachment (included with all arms in this guide). It tightens onto the desk edge — no drilling required. Grommet mounting is the alternative for desks with pre-drilled holes and provides more stability for heavier setups.

**Is the Ergotron LX worth the price?**
