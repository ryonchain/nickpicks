## Running Shoe Buying Guide

### How to Pick Your Shoe

**Know your gait:** Neutral runners (most people) can wear any shoe. Overpronators benefit from stability features. Underpronators (supinators) need extra cushioning.

**Match shoe to purpose:** Daily training shoes should prioritize comfort and durability over speed. For racing or tempo work, prioritize weight and energy return.

**Consider drop:** Drop is the height difference between heel and forefoot. High drop (10–12mm) suits heel strikers. Low drop (4–8mm) suits midfoot and forefoot strikers.

**Size up:** Running shoes typically size 0.5 sizes larger than casual footwear — your foot swells during running and you need toe room to prevent black toenails.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How long do running shoes last?**
300–500 miles is the standard guideline. Track your mileage with a running app to know when to replace.

**Should I buy running shoes online or in a store?**
Both work, but first-time buyers benefit from a specialty running store's gait analysis. Once you know your gait type and preferred brands, online offers better selection and pricing.

**Can I use running shoes for walking?**
Yes — running shoes are typically more cushioned than walking shoes and perform well for extended walking.

**What's the difference between trail and road running shoes?**
Road shoes have smoother outsoles designed for pavement. Trail shoes have aggressive lugs for grip on dirt, gravel, and mud. Don't swap them — road shoes lack the protection for rocky terrain.
