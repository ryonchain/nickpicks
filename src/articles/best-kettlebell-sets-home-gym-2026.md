## How to Choose the Right Kettlebell

### Adjustable vs. Fixed Weight
Adjustable kettlebells save floor space and money if you use multiple weights. Fixed cast iron is preferred by athletes doing high-rep work because the balance is consistent and the shape is traditional. Beginners often benefit from adjustable; advanced users often prefer a rack of fixed bells.

### Starting Weight Guide
- **Women new to kettlebells:** 18–26 lbs (8–12 kg) for swings and deadlifts; 9–13 lbs for presses
- **Men new to kettlebells:** 26–35 lbs (12–16 kg) for swings; 18–26 lbs for presses
- **Intermediate women:** 26–44 lbs (12–20 kg)
- **Intermediate men:** 35–53 lbs (16–24 kg)

### Handle Diameter
33mm is standard for most sport-specific work and fits most hands. 35–38mm handles require more grip strength and can cause fatigue in high-rep sets.

### Surface Finish
- **Raw cast iron:** Best feel, requires chalk, can rust
- **Powder coat:** Best durability, good chalk adhesion
- **Vinyl coated:** Floor-friendly, less chalk adhesion, can crack over time
- **E-coat:** Excellent rust resistance, smooth feel

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How many kettlebells do I need?**
Most people use 2–3 weights. A beginner set of 3 bells (light/medium/heavy) covers the majority of movements. An adjustable handles this in one unit.

**Can I use a single kettlebell for a complete workout?**
Yes. One appropriately weighted bell covers swings, goblet squats, single-arm presses, rows, and carries. A 35-lb bell covers most male beginner movements; 26 lbs for most female beginners.

**What's the difference between a kettlebell and a dumbbell for the same exercise?**
The offset center of mass in a kettlebell creates a different loading pattern, especially in ballistic movements. Swings, cleans, and snatches work uniquely with a kettlebell because the bell naturally arcs and shifts momentum. Dumbbells are better for strict presses and isolation.
