## Training Equipment Buying Guide

### Treats: High-Value vs. Low-Value

Training effectiveness depends heavily on treat value relative to the distraction level. Use a hierarchy:
- **Low distraction (home, no visitors)**: Kibble, Cheerios
- **Medium distraction (yard, street walk)**: Commercial training treats (Zukes Mini Naturals, Charlee Bear)
- **High distraction (dog parks, recall off leash near distractions)**: Real meat (chicken, beef, hot dog pieces)

Always match treat value to distraction level — asking a dog to return from a squirrel chase with kibble is setting up failure.

### Understanding Marker Training

The clicker (or verbal marker "yes!") creates a bridge between behavior and reward. The sequence: behavior happens → mark instantly → deliver treat within 1–3 seconds. The marker removes timing dependency from treat delivery — the click says "that's exactly it" even if the treat arrives 2 seconds later.

To charge the clicker: click → treat, click → treat, 15–20 times. Now the click has meaning.

### Building Reliability Before Adding Distractions

The common mistake: training a command in the living room, then expecting compliance at the dog park. Build behavior reliability on a gradient:
1. Training environment (no distractions)
2. Quiet yard
3. Quiet street
4. Moderate distraction (neighborhood walk)
5. High distraction (dog park, pet store)

Move to the next level only when you have 90%+ compliance at the current level.

## FAQ

### What equipment do I need to start training a puppy?

Minimum kit: treat pouch ($15), clicker ($6), 15-foot long line ($20), and appropriate treats. Total under $45. Skip e-collars and advanced tools until basic foundation behaviors (sit, down, stay, recall, leave-it) are reliable.

### How long should training sessions be?

Short and frequent beats long and occasional. Puppies: 3–5 minutes, 3–4 times daily. Adult dogs: 10–15 minutes, 1–2 times daily. Mental fatigue sets in — longer sessions produce diminishing returns and frustrated dogs.

### Can I train without treats?

Yes, using play, praise, and life rewards (going outside, getting the ball thrown, greeting guests). But treats are the most efficient reinforcer for building new behaviors quickly because they're delivered fast and are immediately consumable. They're a tool, not a dependency.
