## Pet Camera Buying Guide

### Do You Need a Treat Dispenser?

Dogs: Yes, the treat dispenser has genuine behavioral value. It creates a positive association with the camera, provides a positive reward for calm behavior spotted remotely, and helps dogs with separation anxiety (you can respond to barking with a reward for quiet). The interaction channel (see bark → dispense treat) is genuinely useful for training.

Cats: The treat dispenser works for food-motivated cats, but many cats ignore it entirely. Cats are less treat-motivated than dogs and the sound of the treat launcher can startle rather than attract. Skip the treat dispenser unless your cat is highly food motivated.

### What to Look for in Video Quality

- **Resolution**: 1080p minimum; 2K is noticeably sharper for reading expressions and behavioral cues
- **Night vision**: Color night vision (Furbo 360°, Wyze v3) is significantly better than standard IR for actual pet monitoring. Standard IR makes everything gray and flat.
- **Field of view**: 130°–160° covers most rooms without panning; 360° rotation solves large-room coverage

## FAQ

### Can I use a regular security camera for pet monitoring?

Yes, and some security cameras (Wyze, Arlo, Ring) work fine for pet monitoring. The differences: dedicated pet cameras have bark-alert AI trained specifically on pet sounds, treat dispensers, and app interfaces designed around pet behavior (activity history, etc.). For basic monitoring, any 1080p indoor camera works.

### How far away can pets hear two-way audio?

At typical room distances (8–15 feet), two-way audio is clearly audible to most dogs and cats. The speaker quality varies by device — Furbo and Petcube have more natural sound that pets respond to better than compressed, tinny audio on budget cameras.

### Do cameras help with separation anxiety?

Somewhat — they let you monitor anxiety levels and respond to barking with your voice or a treat. But they don't treat the underlying condition. For dogs with clinical separation anxiety, cameras are monitoring tools, not solutions. Behavioral training and veterinary consultation are the appropriate treatment path.
