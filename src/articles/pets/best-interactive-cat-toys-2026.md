## Cat Toy Buying Guide

### The Prey Sequence: Why It Matters

Cats need to complete the full hunting sequence to feel satisfied: **stalk → chase → pounce → catch → kill.** Laser toys that never let the cat "catch" anything leave cats perpetually frustrated — they develop anxiety from an unresolvable prey drive. Always follow laser or motorized wand sessions with a physical toy the cat can physically grab and "kill" (stuffed mouse, soft toy they can kick and bite).

### Rotation Strategy: Preventing Toy Boredom

Cats habituate to toys quickly. Keep 8–10 different toys and rotate them in and out every few days — toys that were "boring" last week become interesting again after two weeks in the closet. Novelty is the primary driver of engagement.

### Age and Energy Level

- **Kitten (0–2 years)**: High energy, needs multiple play sessions, wands and motorized toys
- **Adult (2–8 years)**: Moderate energy, daily 10–15 minute active sessions + passive toys
- **Senior (8+ years)**: Lower energy, prefer toys requiring less jumping (floor-level puzzle toys, slow-moving lasers)

Overweight or arthritic cats need exercise but can't jump to high surfaces — floor-level toys only.

## FAQ

### How long should I play with my cat each day?

Twice-daily 10–15 minute active play sessions are ideal for adult cats. More for kittens (up to 30 minutes in shorter bursts). Use the wand or motorized toy for active sessions; passive enrichment toys supplement throughout the day.

### My cat shows no interest in any toys. What's wrong?

First, check: is the cat unwell (vet check if new behavior)? Second, try different movement types — some cats respond to fast ground-level movement, others to slow aerial. Try a real feather (not synthetic) wand or a crinkle tunnel. Rotate toys to restore novelty. If a cat truly shows no interest in any enrichment, consider environmental enrichment alternatives (window perches, bird feeders outside windows).

### Are laser pointers safe for cats?

Laser pointers are safe physically — the standard red pointer cat toys are well below the power level that causes eye damage at normal use distances. The psychological issue is frustration from never catching the "prey." Always end laser sessions with a physical catch. Some cats develop obsessive laser-chasing behavior — if your cat is constantly hunting reflections or shadows, reduce laser play.
