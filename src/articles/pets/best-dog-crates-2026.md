# Best Dog Crates in 2026: Wire, Plastic & Furniture-Style for Every Breed

Crate training is one of the most effective tools for housetraining, travel, and giving anxious dogs a safe den. A dog that accepts its crate voluntarily has a portable home — a place to decompress during visitors, recover after surgery, or sleep safely during car trips.

But the wrong crate for the wrong dog creates problems: flimsy wire that an anxious dog destroys, plastic that traps heat in summer, or undersized crates that prevent natural standing and turning. This guide covers which type of crate matches which situation.

## Dog Crate Buying Guide

### Sizing: Getting This Right Matters

The crate should be large enough for your dog to:
1. Stand up without hunching
2. Turn around in a full circle
3. Lie down with legs extended

But not so large that one corner becomes a bathroom. Use a divider panel (included with most wire crates) to shrink the usable space for puppies — expand as they grow.

**Size chart (approximate):**
- XS (18"–22"): Dogs under 15 lbs (Chihuahua, toy breeds)
- S (24"): Dogs 15–25 lbs (Shih Tzu, Beagle, Poodle mini)
- M (30"): Dogs 25–40 lbs (Cocker Spaniel, Border Collie)
- L (36"): Dogs 40–70 lbs (Labrador, Boxer, medium-large breeds)
- XL (42"): Dogs 70–90 lbs (German Shepherd, Husky, Standard Poodle)
- XXL (48"–54"): Dogs 90+ lbs (Great Dane, Mastiff, Giant Breeds)

### Placement in the Home

Position the crate in a family-activity area (living room, kitchen) rather than a utility room. Dogs are social — isolation increases anxiety. The crate should feel like a den within the pack's territory, not exile.

### Crate Training vs. Punishment

A crate should never be used as punishment — it must remain a positive space. If a dog is forced into a crate as punishment, they will resist crating and the training collapses. The crate should always be introduced with positive reinforcement: treats thrown inside, meals fed inside, short durations with praise.

## FAQ

### How long can I leave a dog in a crate?

General guidelines: 1 hour per month of age for puppies (maximum 4–5 hours before bladder control develops). Adult dogs: maximum 8 hours, and only if they've been exercised beforehand. Dogs left crated 8–10 hours regularly need additional enrichment and exercise outside those hours.

### Should the crate door stay open at night?

Once crate training is complete, many dogs voluntarily sleep in their crate with the door open. This is ideal — the crate becomes a self-selected safe space rather than confinement.

### Can two dogs share a crate?

Generally no. Crates should be sized per individual dog. Two dogs sharing a crate can become resource-protective of the space, overheat, or fail to have individual retreat space during stress. Two crates side by side is a better solution.
