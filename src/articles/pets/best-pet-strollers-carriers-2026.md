## Buying Guide

### When Does a Pet Actually Need a Stroller or Carrier?

Real use cases (not novelty):
- **Senior dogs (8+ years)**: Joint pain limits distance but they still want to accompany you
- **Post-surgery recovery**: Dogs need outdoor mental stimulation while restricted from walking
- **Brachycephalic breeds** (French Bulldogs, Pugs): Overheat and exhaust easily in summer
- **Small dogs on long urban outings**: 5-mile walks exceed their physical capacity
- **Cats with outdoor enrichment goals**: Outdoor stimulation in a controlled environment
- **Airport travel**: Keeping pets close during long airport waits

### Weight Limits: The Critical Spec

Always verify the weight limit before purchasing. Most pet strollers cap at 30–45 lbs. Bikes trailers typically handle 50+ lbs. Backpack carriers are generally limited to 15–20 lbs. Weigh your pet and leave 15–20% margin below the maximum for safety and realistic load distribution.

### Climate and Ventilation

In summer heat, mesh panels are non-negotiable — enclosed strollers without ventilation become dangerously hot. For winter use, look for strollers with optional weather shields. For dogs that overheat easily (brachycephalic breeds, heavy double-coated breeds), prioritize maximum mesh coverage over enclosed sides.

## FAQ

### Will my dog accept a stroller?

Most dogs need an introduction period. Place the stroller in your home for a week with treats inside — let them explore voluntarily. Short first rides around the block with positive reinforcement (treats, praise) build positive associations. Most dogs adapt within 2–4 weeks. Cats typically take longer and prefer a covered, semi-enclosed carrier design.

### Can I use a baby stroller for my dog?

Technically yes, but not recommended — the flat seat position isn't designed for pet posture, there's no internal tether for safety, and the fabric gets damaged by claws. Pet-specific strollers have mesh floors (for ventilation and claws), internal tethers, and drainage-friendly materials.

### What's the best carrier for flying with a small dog?

In-cabin airline travel requires a soft-sided carrier that fits under the seat (typically under 18" x 11" x 11"). The Sherpa Travel Original Deluxe carrier and Sleepypod Air are the two most consistently airline-approved options — verify specific airline dimensions before purchase as requirements vary.
