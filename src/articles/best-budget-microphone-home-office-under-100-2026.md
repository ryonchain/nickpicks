## What Else You Need

**Boom arm (essential):** A $20–$40 scissor arm clamps to your desk and positions the mic correctly — 6–8 inches from your mouth at a slight downward angle. Desktop stands sit too low and too far. The [best desk setup upgrades](/articles/best-desk-upgrades-under-50-2026/) guide includes desk accessories that pair well with a new mic setup.

**Pop filter ($10):** Reduces plosive bursts (P, B, T sounds) that create thumps in recordings. Clip to the boom arm between you and the mic.

**Acoustic treatment (optional):** If your room sounds hollow (parallel walls, no carpet), a small foam panel behind your monitor reflects less sound. Not required for video calls; helpful for podcast recordings.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the best USB microphone under $100 for home office use?**
The Blue Yeti Nano ($80) is the best under $100 for most home office users — it delivers broadcast-quality sound, has a physical mute button and gain control, and works plug-and-play on Windows and Mac. Budget pick: Fifine K670 (~$35).

**Do I need a USB or XLR microphone for a home office?**
USB for simplicity. A USB mic plugs directly into your computer, no audio interface required. XLR offers higher quality and upgrade paths, but requires an audio interface ($50–$150 extra). For video calls and podcasting under $100, USB is the right choice.

**What's the difference between cardioid and omnidirectional microphone patterns?**
Cardioid captures sound from the front only, minimizing background noise behind and to the sides — ideal for home offices where background noise is a concern. Omnidirectional captures sound from all directions, suitable for conference room calls or interviews.

**Is the Blue Yeti worth it for home office calls?**
The full-size Blue Yeti ($130) is overkill for home office calls — the Blue Yeti Nano delivers 95% of the quality at $80. For basic Zoom calls, even the Fifine K670 ($35) is a massive upgrade over built-in laptop microphones.

**What accessories do I need with a USB microphone?**
A boom arm ($20–$40) positions the mic at the correct 6–8 inch distance from your mouth. A pop filter ($10) reduces plosive sounds (P, B). Both accessories improve audio quality more than upgrading the microphone itself at the sub-$100 level.
