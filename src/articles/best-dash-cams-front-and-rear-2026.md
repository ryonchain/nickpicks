# Best Dash Cams 2026: Front-Only and Front-and-Rear Picks

A dash cam is cheap insurance. A 30-second clip from a $50 camera has settled at-fault disputes, cleared wrongful hit-and-run accusations, and documented insurance fraud. The question isn't whether to get one — it's which setup fits your situation: a discreet front-only unit or a full front-and-rear system that covers your blind zones.

Here are the five best options for 2026, from a compact plug-and-play to a multi-channel system.

---

## What to Look For in a Dash Cam

**Video resolution** matters most in low-light and at highway speeds. 1080p is the minimum — 1440p or 2K is better for reading license plates. 4K is available but generates large files and requires more storage.

**Night vision** quality separates budget cams from solid performers. Look for Sony STARVIS or similar CMOS sensors paired with wide-aperture lenses (f/1.8 or lower).

**Parking mode** records when your car is parked and detects motion or impacts. Requires a hardwire kit or buffered parking mode battery — check what's included.

**Storage and loop recording** — all dash cams loop-record over old footage. 64GB or 128GB cards work well for most users; buy a high-endurance microSD card rated for continuous write cycles.

## Front-Only vs. Front-and-Rear: Which Do You Need?

**Choose front-only if:**
- You park in a garage or low-risk area
- You want the simplest install possible
- Budget is under $100

**Choose front-and-rear if:**
- You've had a rear-end collision before
- You frequently park on the street
- You drive rideshare or need passenger documentation

For most daily drivers, a good front-only camera like the Garmin Mini 2 or Vantrue E1 Lite covers 90% of incident scenarios. If you've been rear-ended or park on busy streets, the N4 Pro's three-channel coverage is worth the premium.
