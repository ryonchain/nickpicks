## Buying Guide: How to Choose a Portable Fan

**Power source:** Plug-in fans deliver stronger, more consistent airflow and are best for stationary desk or bedroom use. Battery-powered fans offer flexibility for outdoor or room-to-room use but require recharging and produce less airflow at equivalent noise levels.

**Noise level:** For sleep use, look for fans rated below 35 dB on their lowest setting. Dreo, ARCTIC, and Vornado lead on this metric. Most fans get significantly louder above medium speed — low-speed noise is the number that matters for bedrooms.

**Oscillation:** Fixed fans cool one zone; oscillating fans cool a room. For desk use, fixed is fine. For living rooms and bedrooms, oscillation makes a noticeable difference.

**Blade vs. bladeless:** Bladeless fans (Dyson) are safer for children and pets, easier to clean, and produce smoother airflow — but at significantly higher cost. Bladed fans deliver more CFM per dollar.

**Clip-on vs. freestanding:** Clip-on fans are best when you need targeted personal cooling with flexible placement. Freestanding fans are better for general room cooling.

## FAQ

**How much airflow do I need to cool a room?**
For a 10x12 bedroom: a 3-speed fan with a 10-inch blade is adequate. For a 15x20 living room: look for a tower fan with Vortex or 360° airflow. Square footage is a rough guide — ceiling height, insulation, and outside temperature all affect what's enough.

**Are bladeless fans actually better?**
They're safer and easier to clean. Airflow quality is smoother with less buffeting. But for raw CFM output per dollar, bladed fans win. Bladeless is worth it if you have children, pets, or if aesthetics matter significantly.

**How long do portable fan batteries last?**
On low speed: 12–24 hours for a 10,000 mAh battery. On high speed: 3–5 hours. Real-world battery life depends on temperature, voltage, and age. Plan for about 60–70% of the rated maximum in real conditions.

**Can a portable fan replace air conditioning?**
No — fans move air, they don't lower room temperature. In humid conditions, a fan provides some evaporative cooling comfort but does not actually cool the air. Fans are most effective when combined with AC or when outside temperatures are below ~85°F.
