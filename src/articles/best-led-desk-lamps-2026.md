## LED Desk Lamp Buying Guide

### Color Temperature: What Kelvin Setting You Actually Need

| Task | Best Color Temperature |
|------|------------------------|
| Evening reading (low eye strain) | 2700K–3000K (warm white) |
| General work / writing | 4000K–4500K (neutral white) |
| Detailed work / design | 5000K–5500K (cool white) |
| Video calls (natural skin tone) | 4500K–5000K |

### Brightness (Lux) Guidelines

- **Reading a book:** 250–500 lux at surface level
- **Computer work:** 300–500 lux (ambient + lamp combined)
- **Detail work (fine print, crafts, electronics):** 750–1000 lux

### CRI (Color Rendering Index)

CRI measures how accurately a light source renders colors compared to natural daylight (CRI 100). For most desk work, CRI 80+ is adequate. For design, photography, or any color-sensitive work, look for CRI 90+.

### Flicker and Eye Strain

Cheap LED lamps flicker at frequencies the eye can't consciously detect but that still cause fatigue over time. All five picks above are verified flicker-free. Avoid no-name lamps without flicker specs.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is LED light bad for your eyes at a desk?**
Quality LED light with proper color temperature and no flicker is not harmful. The eye strain most people experience from desk lighting comes from poorly positioned lights, excessive brightness contrast between screen and background, or flickering — not from LEDs specifically.

**What color temperature is best for studying?**
4000K–5000K is the research-supported sweet spot for alertness and focus without excessive eye strain. Save warmer settings (2700K–3000K) for evening wind-down reading.

**How bright should a desk lamp be?**
For standard desk work, 300–500 lux at the surface is sufficient. Use a lux meter app on your phone to verify — place it where your work sits and check the reading.
