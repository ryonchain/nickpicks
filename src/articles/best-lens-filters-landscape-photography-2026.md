## Understanding the Three Core Landscape Filters

### Circular Polarizer (CPL)
Eliminates reflections from water and glass, reduces sky haze, and saturates colors — especially green foliage and blue sky. The effect can't be replicated in post. Essential for any outdoor photography where water or bright sky is involved.

### Neutral Density (ND) Filter
Reduces the amount of light entering the lens by a fixed number of stops (1-stop, 3-stop, 6-stop, 10-stop are common). Allows slow shutter speeds in daylight — for silky waterfalls, smooth waves, and light trail shots. Higher-quality NDs preserve color neutrality without color cast.

### Graduated Neutral Density (GND)
Half clear, half ND — transitions in the middle. Used to balance bright skies against darker foregrounds when the exposure range exceeds what the sensor can capture. Available in soft-edge (gradual transition) and hard-edge (sharp transition) variants.

## Buying Guide: Filter Selection for Landscape Photography

### What thread size do I need?
Filters screw onto your lens's front thread. Check your lens cap for the thread diameter in millimeters (e.g., ⌀77mm). If you have multiple lenses with different thread sizes, buy filters for the largest size and use step-up rings for smaller lenses.

### Should I buy screw-in or slot-in filters?
Screw-in: Simpler, compact, and less expensive per filter. Best for photographers with one or two primary lenses. Slot-in: More flexible, repositionable gradients, works across all lenses. Best for landscape photographers with multiple focal lengths.

### Order of filter operations (stacking)
When stacking CPL + ND: put the CPL closest to the lens (inner position) so you can rotate it independently. Reverse order reduces effectiveness.

### Filter thread sizes to know
Common landscape lens sizes: 67mm, 72mm, 77mm, 82mm. Carry a step-up ring set ($10–$15) so one filter covers multiple lenses.

## FAQ

**Q: Can I replace a polarizing filter with software?**
No. The polarizing effect (removing reflections, saturating skies) is a physical phenomenon that requires the filter to be present at capture time. There is no post-processing equivalent.

**Q: What's the most useful filter to buy first?**
A circular polarizer (CPL). It has the most visible, consistent effect across landscape photography — in bright sun with water or blue sky present, the difference between shots with and without a CPL is dramatic. Start here before buying ND filters.

**Q: Do I need ND filters if I have in-camera ND?**
Some cameras (Sony ZV-E1, Fujifilm X-H2S) have built-in variable ND. This is genuinely useful for video and casual long exposures. For high-quality stills with maximum flexibility, external glass ND filters still produce cleaner results and more precise stop control.

**Q: Will a CPL fit on my lens?**
CPL filters screw into the front filter thread of your lens. They don't work with very wide (full-frame 14–16mm) lenses or fisheyes, where the front element protrudes or curves beyond reach of the filter thread. Check your lens's front element design before purchasing.

**Q: How do I calculate exposure with ND filters?**
Each stop of ND doubles the exposure time. A 1/100s base exposure through a 10-stop ND becomes 1/100 × 2^10 = approximately 10 seconds. Use an ND calculator app (available free for iOS and Android) for quick calculation in the field.
