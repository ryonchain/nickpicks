## Water Flosser Buying Guide

### Water Flosser vs String Floss

**String floss:** Inexpensive, no device required, effective when used correctly. Problem: most people don't floss consistently or correctly.

**Water flossers:** Higher upfront cost, requires counter space or charging, but removes more plaque, reaches places string floss misses (implants, bridges, orthodontics), and dramatically increases compliance — people actually use water flossers consistently because they're faster and more comfortable.

**The research:** A 2013 study published in the Journal of Clinical Dentistry found water flossing is 52% more effective at reducing gingivitis than string floss, and 2x more effective at reducing gingival bleeding.

### Key Specs to Evaluate

**Pressure range:** Look for 40–100+ PSI with multiple settings. Starting at low pressure is important for sensitive gums.

**Reservoir size:** Smaller (5–7 oz) = more refills, portable. Larger (17–22 oz) = full sessions without refilling, countertop size.

**Tips:** More tips included = more value, especially if multiple family members share or if you have orthodontics.

**Corded vs cordless:** Corded = more power, larger reservoir, constant power. Cordless = travel-ready, no outlet needed, more convenient.

### Who Especially Benefits From a Water Flosser

- Braces wearers: String floss around brackets is tedious; water flossers clean effectively without the frustration
- Implant or bridge patients: Water flossers reach under bridges and around implants where floss struggles
- Gum disease patients: Higher pressure settings and consistent use support gum health
- Seniors: Easier to use than string floss for those with limited dexterity
- People who struggle with consistency: Faster and more pleasant than string floss increases compliance

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should I use a water flosser or regular floss?**
Both. Dentists recommend using a water flosser in addition to — not instead of — string floss. Water flossers remove food debris and reduce bacteria; string floss physically removes plaque from tooth surfaces. Together, they're more effective than either alone.

**Can a water flosser replace brushing?**
No. Water flossers complement brushing by cleaning between teeth and below the gumline. Use your toothbrush first, then water flosser, then mouthwash.

**How often should I use a water flosser?**
Once daily is the dentist recommendation. Some users with gum disease or orthodontics use it twice daily.

**Can children use a water flosser?**
Yes, from about age 6+. Use the lowest pressure setting and supervise younger children. The Waterpik brand offers kid-specific tips for smaller mouths.

**How do I clean my water flosser?**
Empty the reservoir after each use to prevent bacteria growth. Deep clean monthly: run white vinegar through the system, then flush with water. Replace tips every 3–6 months.
