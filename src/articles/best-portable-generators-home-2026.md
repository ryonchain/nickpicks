## How to Choose a Portable Generator for Home Use

**1. Calculate Your Wattage Needs First**
The most important step before any purchase. List the appliances you need to run during an outage and find their wattage (usually on a label on the device or in the manual). Add them up for running wattage. Then find the appliance with the highest starting wattage (motors need 2–3x their running wattage to start) and add that to your total. That's your minimum generator size. Refrigerators: ~150W running, ~600W starting. Window AC: ~1,000–1,500W running, ~2,200W starting.

**2. Inverter vs. Conventional**
Inverter generators produce stable, clean power (low total harmonic distortion) safe for laptops, phones, televisions, and medical equipment without additional conditioning. They're quieter and more fuel-efficient but cost more per watt. Conventional generators are louder, less fuel-efficient, and produce power fluctuations — fine for power tools, fans, and incandescent lights, but add a UPS or surge protector before powering sensitive electronics.

**3. Fuel Type Considerations**
Gasoline generators are the most common and affordable, but gasoline has a shelf life of 3–12 months and supply can be disrupted in regional disasters. Propane generators burn a fuel with indefinite storage life that's widely stocked. Dual-fuel models (Champion, DuroMax) provide resilience by accepting either — strongly recommended for emergency preparedness use cases.

**4. Runtime and Fuel Tank Size**
Runtime at 50% load is the most meaningful spec for outage scenarios — it tells you how many hours you get between refuels when running essential appliances. For overnight operation without waking up to refuel, look for 8+ hours at 50% load. For extended multi-day outages, calculate how many gallons you'll need and confirm you can safely store that quantity.

## FAQ

**Can I run a portable generator inside my home or garage?**
Never. Carbon monoxide poisoning is the leading cause of non-fire generator-related deaths — the odorless gas builds to lethal concentrations within minutes in enclosed spaces. Always operate generators outdoors, at least 20 feet from any window, door, or vent. Install a CO detector inside your home as an additional safety measure.

**Do I need a transfer switch to use a portable generator?**
For powering appliances directly via extension cords, no transfer switch is needed. For connecting a generator to your home's wiring — powering outlets through your main panel — a properly installed transfer switch or interlock kit is legally required and essential for safety. Back-feeding power into utility lines without a transfer switch can electrocute utility workers.

**How often should I run my generator to keep it in working condition?**
Run your generator under load for 30 minutes every 30–90 days, and use fresh fuel or a fuel stabilizer if it will sit for more than 30 days. Stale gasoline is the most common reason generators fail to start in an actual outage. Change the oil after the first 20 hours and every 50–100 hours thereafter.

**How loud is a portable generator, and does it matter?**
Generator noise ranges from 52 dB (quiet inverter models) to 98 dB (large conventional generators). At 20 feet, 52 dB is about the volume of a normal conversation; 72 dB is about as loud as a vacuum cleaner; 98 dB approaches the level of a motorcycle. Most municipalities have noise ordinances — if you're running at night in a residential area, inverter models in the 52–65 dB range are meaningfully more neighborly.
