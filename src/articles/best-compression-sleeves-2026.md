## Compression Sleeve Buying Guide

### Understanding Compression Levels

Compression is measured in mmHg (millimeters of mercury), the same unit used for blood pressure:

- **8–15 mmHg:** Mild compression. Over-the-counter, good for mild swelling and daily fatigue.
- **15–20 mmHg:** Moderate compression. Best for travel, mild varicose veins, prolonged standing.
- **20–30 mmHg:** Firm compression. Medical-grade. Excellent for athletic performance, recovery, moderate varicose veins.
- **30+ mmHg:** Prescription-grade. For serious medical conditions — consult a doctor.

For athletic use and everyday wellness, 20–30 mmHg is the sweet spot.

### Graduated vs Non-Graduated Compression

**Graduated compression** is tighter at the ankle/wrist and progressively looser toward the knee/elbow. This gradient drives blood upward toward the heart — the therapeutic mechanism. Always look for graduated compression in a calf, leg, or arm sleeve.

**Non-graduated (uniform) compression** is consistent throughout. It adds warmth and mild support but doesn't provide the same circulatory benefit.

### Sizing Is Everything

A compression sleeve that's too loose does nothing. A sleeve that's too tight can impair circulation. Every reputable brand provides a sizing chart based on circumference measurements:
- **Calf sleeves:** Measure the widest part of your calf
- **Knee sleeves:** Measure 4–6" above the center of your kneecap
- **Arm sleeves:** Measure the widest part of your bicep

Always measure and cross-reference the brand's chart before buying.

### Material Considerations

- **Nylon/spandex:** Lightweight, moisture-wicking, best for athletic use
- **Neoprene:** Warmer, more insulating, common in knee sleeves, can retain heat
- **Copper-infused:** Antimicrobial benefit, minimal therapeutic claim beyond odor control

### When to Wear Compression Sleeves

- **During exercise:** For performance support and injury prevention
- **After exercise:** For accelerated recovery (wear 1–2 hours post-workout)
- **During travel:** For long flights or drives over 4 hours
- **All day at work:** For people who stand or sit for extended periods

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do compression sleeves actually work?**
Yes, for the right use cases. Graduated compression is clinically validated to improve venous blood flow, reduce muscle oscillation during exercise, and decrease swelling. The evidence is strongest for recovery use and circulation support. Athletic performance benefits are real but modest.

**Can I sleep in compression sleeves?**
Generally not recommended for healthy people — your circulatory system doesn't need help when horizontal. For medical conditions like lymphedema, consult your doctor. Athletes should remove compression sleeves after their post-workout recovery window (1–3 hours).

**How long should I wear a compression sleeve after a run?**
Wear for 1–3 hours post-run for optimal recovery benefit. Beyond that, the benefit diminishes and wearing compression for too long can cause skin issues.

**How often should I wash compression sleeves?**
After every use. Compression fabrics retain sweat and bacteria — regular washing maintains hygiene and, paradoxically, extends sleeve life by preventing fabric breakdown from salt and oils.

**Will compression sleeves help with shin splints?**
Yes, calf compression sleeves reduce tibial vibration during running, which is a contributing factor in shin splints. They won't cure underlying causes (overtraining, poor footwear, biomechanics), but they reduce pain and support recovery.
