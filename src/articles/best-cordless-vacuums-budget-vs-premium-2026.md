## What You're Actually Paying For at Each Price Point

**Under $100:** Basic suction, small dust bin, short battery life (20–25 min), no HEPA filter. Works for small apartments, light use, or as a secondary vacuum.

**$100–$200:** Better suction, HEPA filtration on most models, 35–45 min runtime, larger dust bins. This is the sweet spot for most households.

**$200–$400:** Stronger motors, true auto-boost features, better attachments, more reliable motors rated for 5+ years.

**$400+:** Laser particle detection, AI-powered suction adjustment, self-emptying bases, whole-home battery life. Real quality improvements — but only matter if you vacuum daily on mixed surfaces.

## Budget vs Premium: The Real Decision Matrix

### Buy Budget (Under $200) if:
- You vacuum weekly or less frequently
- Your home is under 1,200 sq ft
- You have mostly hard floors with minimal carpet
- You want a secondary/quick-cleanup vacuum
- Pet hair is occasional, not constant

### Buy Mid-Range ($200–$400) if:
- You vacuum 2–3x per week
- Your home is 1,200–2,000 sq ft
- You have a mix of carpet and hard floors
- You have pets with moderate shedding
- HEPA filtration is important (allergies, asthma)

### Buy Premium ($400+) if:
- You vacuum daily or every other day
- Your home is 2,000+ sq ft
- You have pets that shed heavily year-round
- You want the best possible suction for thick carpet
- The auto-emptying feature genuinely saves you time

## What You Don't Need to Pay For

**Extra attachments you won't use:** Premium bundles include 8–10 attachments. Most users use 2–3 consistently. Don't pay extra for attachments you'll lose or never open.

**"HEPA" marketing without full sealing:** Some budget models claim HEPA filters but aren't fully sealed — dust escapes around the filter. Fully sealed HEPA (marked on the spec sheet) is what matters for allergy sufferers.

**High wattage numbers:** Wattage measures power draw, not suction. Look for airwatts (AW) or air displacement specs instead.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is the Dyson V15 worth $650 over a $200 Shark?**
For most households: no. The Dyson laser detection is genuinely impressive and the build quality is class-leading, but the Shark IZ540H Stratos at $280 gets within 15–20% of Dyson's real-world performance at 40% of the price. The V15 justifies its cost only if you're deeply bothered by invisible fine dust or vacuum daily on high-pile carpet.

**How long should a cordless vacuum battery last?**
Lithium-ion batteries typically hold 80% capacity after 500 charge cycles. At one charge per week, that's nearly 10 years before meaningful degradation. Most vacuums fail on motor or brush roll before the battery becomes an issue.

**Are self-emptying cordless vacuums worth it?**
For large homes with pets: yes. For smaller spaces or occasional use: no. The self-emptying feature adds $300–$500 to the price. If you vacuum daily in 2,000+ sq ft, hands-off emptying saves meaningful time and effort weekly.

**Can cordless vacuums replace corded?**
For most households: yes. Modern cordless motors (especially Dyson digital motors) match or exceed corded performance on all but the thickest pile carpet. The main cordless limitation is runtime — for homes over 2,500 sq ft cleaned in one session, a corded or robot vacuum as primary with cordless as secondary makes sense.
