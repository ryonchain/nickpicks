## Corded vs. Cordless: Closing the Gap in 2026

For most DIYers and home renovation work, cordless circular saws are now the clear choice:

- **No extension cord management** — major advantage on rooftops, job sites, or large properties
- **Modern 18V/20V brushless motors** — deliver 5,500–6,500 RPM, matching most corded saws
- **Battery ecosystem investment pays off** — same batteries power multiple tools (drill, jigsaw, reciprocating saw, etc.)
- **Portability for ripping sheet goods** — take the saw to the material instead of wrestling 4x8 sheets to a table

Where corded still wins: sustained heavy cutting (full day ripping hardwood), lowest cost entry point, and applications where battery swaps are impractical.

## Key Specs to Evaluate

**Blade size:** 7-1/4" is the standard. Cuts through 2-5/8" material at 90° (enough for doubled 2x4 framing). 6-1/2" blades are lighter but cut slightly less deep. Most DIYers want 7-1/4".

**Motor type:**
- *Brushless motor:* More efficient, longer runtime per charge, longer motor lifespan, and typically more powerful. Worth the $30–50 premium — especially for frequent use.
- *Brushed motor:* Adequate for occasional use. Less efficient, shorter motor life, but lower price point.

**Battery voltage:** All major brands have moved to 18V (DeWalt calls it 20V MAX — same thing). More important is the Ah (amp-hour) rating of the battery: 4.0Ah or higher for extended cutting sessions.

**Cutting depth and bevel capacity:** Standard 7-1/4" saws cut 2-5/8" at 90°. Check bevel range — 56° is standard; look for a positive bevel stop at 45°.

**Left vs. right blade:** Most circular saws have the blade on the right. Left-blade models (Milwaukee, some Makita) give right-handed users better sightline to the cut line. Personal preference matters here.

## Safety Fundamentals for Circular Saw Use

Always follow these safety practices:

1. **Wear safety glasses and ear protection** — circular saws kick debris at high speed and generate 100+ dB
2. **Support your workpiece properly** — saw should cut through unsupported wood, not pivot on a support point
3. **Check for nails and fasteners** — hitting metal destroys blades and creates dangerous kickback
4. **Keep the blade guard functional** — never remove or tie back the retractable blade guard
5. **Wait for the blade to stop completely** before setting the saw down
6. **Watch for kickback** — kickback happens when the blade binds in the cut; keep your footing stable and never reach over an active cut

**Q: Should I buy a bare tool or a kit?**
A: Buy a kit if you're starting a new battery ecosystem. Buy a bare tool if you already own compatible 18V batteries (a 4.0Ah or larger battery for sustained cutting is recommended).

**Q: Can I use any 7-1/4" blade in a cordless circular saw?**
A: Generally yes — arbor size (5/8") is standard across most 7-1/4" saws. For best results with cordless, use thin-kerf blades (1/16" vs. standard 3/32" kerf) — they remove less material per pass, requiring less motor effort and extending battery runtime per charge.

**Q: Is a cordless circular saw powerful enough for framing?**
A: Yes — modern brushless 18V saws handle standard lumber framing without issue. Extended framing sessions (all day cutting full 2x lumber) may require multiple battery swaps. Keep 2 batteries charged for heavy work days.
