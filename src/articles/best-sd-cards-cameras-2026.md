# Best SD Cards for Cameras in 2026 — Speed & Capacity Guide

SD cards are easy to overlook — until a slow card causes your camera to buffer mid-burst, or a failed card loses a day's worth of irreplaceable images. The right card makes a real difference in how your camera performs: faster write speeds unlock longer burst buffers and uninterrupted 4K recording, while reliability comes down to the brand and build quality behind the label.

The key specs to understand are the speed class (V30, V60, V90 — minimum write speeds in MB/s), rated read and write speeds, and whether the card is UHS-I or UHS-II. UHS-II cards have a second row of pins and transfer data much faster, but your camera needs to support UHS-II to take advantage. Most cameras released after 2022 support at least UHS-I V30; check your camera's manual before buying.

## What to Look For

- **Match the card to your camera's bus speed:** A UHS-II card in a UHS-I camera performs identically to a UHS-I card. Know your camera's specification before paying the UHS-II premium.
- **V-class rating matters for video:** V30 is sufficient for 4K at most bitrates. V60 handles high-bitrate cinema formats. V90 is required for sustained 8K recording. Don't overspend on V90 if you only shoot V30-compliant video.
- **Read speed vs. write speed:** Camera performance during shooting depends on write speed. Read speed only affects how fast you import images to your computer — important for workflow but irrelevant to in-camera performance.
- **Capacity planning:** 128GB is the practical sweet spot for most photographers — large enough for a full shooting day, small enough that one lost or failed card doesn't lose everything. Buy two 128GB cards rather than one 256GB.
- **Counterfeit risk:** Buy SD cards from Amazon's fulfilled listings or directly from brand stores. Counterfeit cards from third-party sellers are a real problem — they report false capacities and fail under normal use.
- **Card readers matter:** Even a fast UHS-II card transfers at UHS-I speeds through a UHS-I reader. If you're buying a fast card for import speed, pair it with a UHS-II reader.
