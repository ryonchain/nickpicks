# Best Camera Straps in 2026 — Neck, Sling & Wrist Styles

The strap that came in the box with your camera was designed to hold your gear — not to make shooting more comfortable or your camera more accessible. A proper aftermarket strap changes how you carry and work with a camera, reducing fatigue on long shooting days and giving you faster access than a standard around-the-neck hang.

The three main styles each suit different shooting scenarios. Neck straps distribute weight across both shoulders for low-profile carrying. Sling straps let the camera rest at your hip and swing up to shooting position quickly. Wrist straps are minimal and keep the camera ready for fast, grab-and-go moments. The best choice depends on your camera weight, how long you're shooting, and your shooting style.

## What to Look For

- **Attachment method:** Standard lug connectors work with any camera; tripod socket mounts (used by sling straps) require an accessible, unobstructed tripod port — check before buying if you use an L-bracket or battery grip.
- **Quick-release value:** If you mount to a tripod frequently, a quick-release system (like Peak Design's Anchors) saves significant time and frustration every single day.
- **Strap width and padding:** For cameras heavier than 2 lbs with a lens attached, look for at least 1.5-inch width with genuine padding — thin straps cause shoulder and neck fatigue surprisingly quickly.
- **Sling vs. neck:** Sling straps give faster access and reduce neck strain; neck straps keep the camera more central and work better in crowds where a swinging camera at your hip is a liability.
- **Security features:** Any strap used in urban or crowded environments benefits from a locking mechanism or safety tether — standard connectors can come unclipped if something snags them.
- **Camera weight:** Wrist straps work well for lighter mirrorless bodies; heavy DSLRs with grips need a proper shoulder or sling strap — don't underestimate how much a heavy camera fatigues a wrist over hours of shooting.
