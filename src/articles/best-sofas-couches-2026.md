## What Makes a Great Sofa

**Frame:** Hardwood (kiln-dried) lasts 10–15 years. Softwood or engineered wood lasts 3–5 years before the joints loosen. Most budget sofas under $600 use softwood or particleboard — fine for light use, poor for daily family use.

**Cushion fill:** High-resiliency (HR) foam stays firm for years. Standard foam compresses quickly. Down-wrapped cushions feel luxurious but require regular fluffing. For daily-use sofas, look for HR foam with a density rating of 1.8–2.0 lb/ft³ minimum.

**Fabric:** Performance fabrics (Crypton, Sunbrella) handle spills and stains. Standard woven fabrics look better but need professional cleaning. Velvet looks stunning but shows wear and pet hair quickly.

**Seat depth:** Standard is 21–24 inches. Deep seats (25+ inches) are lounging-friendly but harder for shorter people to sit upright.

## What to Measure Before Buying

1. **Room clearance:** Leave 18" of walkway around all sides. Measure doorways and hallways for delivery.
2. **Seat height:** Standard is 17–19". Higher (20"+) is easier to stand from.
3. **Seat depth:** 20–22" for upright sitting, 24–28" for lounging.
4. **Weight capacity:** Most sofas support 300–400 lbs per seat.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the best sofa brand?**
For value: IKEA (Kivik series). For mid-range quality: Article, Joss & Main, AllModern. For investment pieces: Pottery Barn, Restoration Hardware, Room & Board.

**How long should a sofa last?**
A well-built sofa lasts 10–15 years. Budget sofas under $400 typically last 5–7 years with light use. Sofas with 8-way hand-tied springs and kiln-dried hardwood frames last longest.

**Is it worth spending more on a sofa?**
Yes, up to a point. The $800–1,500 range delivers significantly better longevity than the under-$500 range. Above $2,000, you're paying for brand premium and fabric options — the construction gains are smaller.

**What fabric is best for a sofa with dogs?**
Performance fabrics (Sunbrella, Crypton, Pottery Barn Eco Performance) are easiest to clean. Microfiber resists staining but catches fur. Avoid velvet, linen, or loose weaves with pets.
