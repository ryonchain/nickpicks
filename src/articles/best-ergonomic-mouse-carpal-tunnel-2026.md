## Why Standard Mice Cause Wrist Problems

Your arm naturally rests in a "handshake" position — forearm at roughly 45–90° from horizontal. Standard flat mice force your forearm to rotate palm-down (pronation), which:

- Compresses the median nerve (the nerve affected in carpal tunnel syndrome)
- Increases tension in the tendons of the forearm and wrist
- Adds stress to the radiocarpal joint with every click and movement

Over hours of daily use, this repetitive stress accumulates. Ergonomic mice address this three ways:

1. **Vertical mice**: Hold the mouse in a handshake grip, keeping the forearm at a neutral angle
2. **Trackball mice**: The hand stays stationary while the thumb or fingers move the ball — no forearm movement at all
3. **Contoured mice**: Standard flat format but with sculpted support that reduces pressure points and encourages a more natural hand arch

## Tips for Carpal Tunnel Prevention Beyond the Mouse

An ergonomic mouse helps, but full prevention requires attention to your full setup:

**Wrist position:** Keep wrists flat (not bent up or down) when mousing. A thin wrist rest helps, but don't lean your weight on it while actively moving.

**Mouse pad placement:** Position the mouse so your upper arm hangs naturally from your shoulder — no reaching forward or to the side.

**Take micro-breaks:** The 20-20-20 rule works for wrists too — every 20 minutes, rest your hands for 20 seconds.

**Consider a desk keyboard tray:** Lowering the keyboard so elbows are at 90° reduces forearm pronation even before the mouse becomes ergonomic.
