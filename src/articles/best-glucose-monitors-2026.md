Blood glucose monitoring has been transformed over the past decade. Traditional fingerstick glucometers — reliable, inexpensive, and clinically accurate — remain the standard for diabetic management. But continuous glucose monitors have added an entirely new layer of insight: real-time glucose curves that show exactly how food, exercise, stress, and sleep affect your metabolic health.

This guide covers both technologies to help you find the right tool for your situation.

## Understanding Your Options

**When to use a traditional glucometer:** If you're managing Type 2 diabetes with diet and oral medications, test strips provide adequate information (fasting glucose, post-meal checks) at low ongoing cost. They're the right tool for most people not on insulin.

**When to consider a CGM:** If you take insulin (especially fast-acting), if your doctor has prescribed more frequent monitoring, if you're struggling with hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) episodes, or if you want detailed metabolic data for optimization purposes.

**Insurance and cost:** Most insurance plans cover CGMs for insulin-dependent diabetics. Traditional glucometers are almost universally covered. Check your formulary before purchasing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What blood sugar level is considered diabetic?**
Fasting blood glucose of 126 mg/dL or higher on two separate tests indicates diabetes. 100–125 mg/dL fasting indicates prediabetes. Below 100 mg/dL is normal.

**Can I use a CGM without having diabetes?**
Yes — the Dexcom Stelo and Abbott Libre Sense are available over-the-counter for non-diabetic users interested in metabolic health monitoring. Many health-focused individuals use CGMs for 2–4 week periods to understand their personal glucose responses to different foods.
