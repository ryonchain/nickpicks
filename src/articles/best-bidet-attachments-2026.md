## Buying Guide: Electric vs. Non-Electric Bidets

### Non-Electric Bidets (Attachments)
Best for: Renters, budget buyers, households without a nearby outlet, first-time bidet users.

**Pros:** Inexpensive ($30–$100), no electrical requirement, simple installation (5–15 min), very reliable with minimal failure points.

**Cons:** Cold water only (unless you add a warm water T-connection), no heated seat, no air dryer — you'll still use some toilet paper.

### Electric Bidet Seats
Best for: Homeowners who want the full experience, people with mobility limitations, anyone who's used a bidet in Japan.

**Pros:** Heated seat, warm water on demand, air dryer (reduces TP use to near zero), adjustable nozzle position, often self-cleaning.

**Cons:** Require an electrical outlet within reach, more expensive ($150–$700+), more complex installation (30–60 min), must fit your specific toilet model.

## Installation: What You Need to Know

### Non-Electric Installation (10 minutes)
1. Turn off the toilet water supply valve
2. Remove the toilet seat
3. Attach the bidet mounting bracket to the toilet
4. Reconnect the seat on top of the bracket
5. Attach the T-connector to the fill valve
6. Run the supply hose from T-connector to bidet
7. Turn water back on and test

Tools required: Usually none. Some T-connectors need hand-tightening only.

### Electric Installation (30–60 minutes)
Same as above, plus:
- Connect the dedicated supply line to the bidet seat
- Plug into a nearby GFCI-protected outlet
- Most bathrooms built after 1975 have an outlet within reach

## FAQ

**Q: Will a bidet fit my toilet?**
A: Most bidet attachments fit standard two-piece toilets. Measure your toilet bowl length (round = 16.5 in, elongated = 18.5 in) and confirm compatibility before buying. One-piece and French curve toilets may need specific models.

**Q: Do bidets use a lot of water?**
A: A typical bidet wash uses 1/8 of a gallon. A standard toilet flush uses 1.6 gallons. Bidets use significantly less water than the toilet paper production process they replace.

**Q: Is the water pressure adjustable?**
A: Yes, on all quality models. The pressure range varies — most users settle on 40–60% of maximum.

**Q: Can I install a bidet in an apartment?**
A: Non-electric attachments don't require any permanent modification and can be removed when you move. They're ideal for renters. Electric seats require an outlet, which most bathrooms have, and are also removable.

**Q: Do bidets actually clean better than toilet paper?**
A: Studies consistently show water cleaning is more effective at removing fecal matter than dry paper. Gastroenterologists and colorectal surgeons routinely recommend bidets for patients with hemorrhoids, fissures, or post-surgical recovery.
