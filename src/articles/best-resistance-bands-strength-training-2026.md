## Types of Resistance Bands

**Tube bands with handles**: Handles on each end, attachable anchors. Best for exercises that mimic dumbbell movements — curls, rows, presses, lateral raises. Stackable sets let you combine bands for more resistance.

**Loop bands (flat or circular)**: Closed loop. Best for lower body — squats, glute bridges, lateral band walks, clam shells. Also used for pull-up assistance when looped around a bar.

**Fabric (cloth) bands**: Similar to mini loops but wider and fabric-covered. Won't roll up the leg during squats, don't snap like latex. Best for glute activation and lower body workouts.

**Figure-8 bands**: Useful for ankle resistance or wrist exercises. Specialized use case.

## Buying Guide: Choosing Resistance Bands

### Tube vs. Loop vs. Fabric

**Tube bands with handles** are the most versatile for upper body work — they mimic cable machine exercises and allow a full range of grip positions. **Loop bands** are best for lower body and accessory work. **Fabric bands** are the most comfortable for glute work and don't roll. Many home gym setups include all three types.

### Resistance Range

Beginners typically start with 10–25 lbs for upper body, 15–35 lbs for lower body. Intermediate lifters working toward heavy pulls need 50–100+ lbs. Stackable tube systems (Bodylastics, Whatafit) allow progressive overload as you get stronger.

### Anti-Snap Safety

Tube bands under tension can snap and whip at 20–30 mph when they fail. Bands with internal safety cords (Bodylastics) prevent this. If you're training alone with high-resistance stacked bands, anti-snap protection is worth the premium.

### Door Anchor Quality

The door anchor bears all the force of your pull exercises. Cheap anchors fail at the strap attachment point. Look for metal rings reinforced at the anchor head. For heavy resistance, a heavy-duty door anchor rated for 200+ lbs provides appropriate safety margin.

## FAQ

**Can resistance bands build muscle?**
Yes. Muscle growth requires progressive tension — bands provide this. Studies show comparable hypertrophy between band training and free weight training when resistance is sufficient and progressive. Bands are particularly effective for isolation exercises.

**How long do resistance bands last?**
Natural latex bands: 1–2 years with regular use (check for cracking or loss of elasticity). Fabric bands: 3+ years. Replace latex bands at the first sign of small cracks or surface degradation.

**What resistance level should a beginner start with?**
For upper body: 10–20 lbs tube bands. For lower body: light-to-medium loop bands (10–20 lbs). Start lighter than you think you need — proper form matters more than heavy resistance.

**Can I use resistance bands with a pull-up bar?**
Yes. Loop a large loop band over the pull-up bar, place your foot or knee in the loop, and perform assisted pull-ups. Start with a heavier band and progress to lighter bands as you get stronger.
