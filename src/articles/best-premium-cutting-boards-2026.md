### The Wood vs Plastic Hygiene Debate
Research from UC Davis found that wooden cutting boards absorb bacteria into the wood grain where it dies — bacteria drawn into wood fiber do not multiply and eventually die. Plastic boards accumulate knife cuts that harbor bacteria in grooves that can't be cleaned. Both materials are acceptable; the key variable is maintenance. A well-oiled, well-maintained wood board is bacteriologically safe. A deeply knife-scored plastic board is not.

### How to Oil and Maintain a Wood Cutting Board
1. **Initial oiling**: apply food-grade mineral oil liberally to all surfaces (including sides and bottom); let absorb for 6–8 hours; wipe off excess; repeat 3 times before first use
2. **Ongoing oiling**: monthly for boards used daily; oil when the wood looks dry or lighter than normal
3. **Cleaning**: hand-wash with mild soap and warm water; dry immediately; never submerge or put in dishwasher
4. **Sanitizing**: spray with white vinegar and let sit for 2 minutes, then rinse; or sprinkle with coarse salt, rub with a halved lemon, rinse

### Sizing Guide
- **Small (8"×10"–10"×14")**: side prep, fruit, single-serve portions
- **Medium (12"×18")**: the daily driver — covers 80% of kitchen prep
- **Large (14"×20"+)**: carving boards, pizza, large batch prep, pastry rolling

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is John Boos worth the price?**
Yes, for end-grain buyers. A John Boos Cherry or Maple end-grain board, properly maintained, lasts 25–30 years — the cost-per-year is lower than replacing cheap boards every 2–3 years. The aesthetics improve with age. If you cook seriously, it's worth the investment.

**What oil should I use on a cutting board?**
Food-grade mineral oil is the standard recommendation — it's flavorless, odorless, non-rancid, and safe. Avoid cooking oils (olive, vegetable, coconut) which eventually turn rancid. "Board creams" (mineral oil + beeswax, like Boos Block Cream) condition and provide surface protection. Apply oil with a soft cloth, let absorb, wipe off excess.

**Can I cut raw meat on a wood cutting board?**
Yes, safely, if you clean it properly after each use with hot water and soap. The research-backed safe approach is: use one side for raw proteins and the other side for produce, clean with soap immediately after raw protein contact, and sanitize with vinegar or a dilute bleach solution weekly. Plastic boards aren't automatically safer for raw meat.

**How do I remove knife scars and stains from a cutting board?**
Sand with 120-grit sandpaper along the grain, then 220-grit to smooth. Wipe clean. Apply mineral oil generously. End-grain boards can be completely resurfaced this way and returned to near-new condition. Edge-grain boards can also be sanded, though the process is more involved for deep scarring.
