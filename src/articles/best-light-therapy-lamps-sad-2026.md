## How to Use a Light Therapy Lamp

### Timing
Use it within 1 hour of waking, ideally during the first 30 minutes. Evening use can disrupt sleep (it suppresses melatonin). Morning use aligns circadian rhythm with daylight timing.

### Duration
10,000 lux lamp: 20–30 minutes per session
5,000 lux lamp: 40–60 minutes (less convenient but same dose)

### Distance
Most clinical recommendations use 10,000 lux measured at 12 inches. Smaller lamps require closer positioning. Don't stare directly at the lamp — look at your phone, eat breakfast, or read nearby.

### When to expect results
Most users notice improvement within 1–2 weeks of daily use. Some respond in 3–4 days; others need 3–4 weeks. If there's no improvement after 4 weeks of consistent use, consult a physician — other SAD interventions (therapy, medication) may be indicated.

### Side effects
Headaches, eyestrain, and mild irritability are reported in a small percentage of users, especially the first week. Starting with shorter sessions (10 minutes) and building up usually resolves this.

## FAQ

**Q: Does light therapy work for non-seasonal depression?**
A: Evidence is strongest for SAD. Some research supports use for non-seasonal depression as an adjunct to therapy and medication, but it's less definitive. Consult a psychiatrist before using it as a primary treatment for non-seasonal depression.

**Q: Can I use a light therapy lamp if I have bipolar disorder?**
A: Light therapy can trigger manic episodes in some people with bipolar disorder. Do not use without talking to your psychiatrist first.

**Q: Are light therapy lamps safe for eyes?**
A: UV-filtered lamps at recommended distances and durations are considered safe. Do not stare directly into the lamp. People with retinal conditions or certain medications (some antibiotics, lithium) that cause light sensitivity should consult a doctor first.

**Q: What's the difference between 5,000 lux and 10,000 lux lamps?**
A: 10,000 lux requires only 20–30 minutes of daily exposure; 5,000 lux requires double the time (40–60 minutes). 10,000 lux lamps are more practical for most users. Outdoor natural daylight on a sunny day is 32,000–100,000 lux for reference.
