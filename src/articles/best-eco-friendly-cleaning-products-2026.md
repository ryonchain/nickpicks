## Eco Cleaning Products Buying Guide

### Understanding Certifications

**EPA Safer Choice:** Products that meet EPA standards for ingredient safety across human health and environmental impact. This is the most credible third-party certification for cleaning products in the US.

**EWG Verified:** Environmental Working Group's rigorous standard — all ingredients must meet strict toxicity, health concern, and disclosure thresholds. Look for the EWG Verified mark on cleaning products.

**B Corporation:** Certifies the company's overall social and environmental practices — not just the product formula. Brands like Seventh Generation, Blueland, and ChicoBag are B Corps.

**USDA Certified Biobased:** Verifies the percentage of carbon-based ingredients derived from renewable biological resources (plants) rather than petroleum.

### Ingredients to Avoid

Conventional cleaning products can contain:

- **Quaternary ammonium compounds (quats):** Disinfectants linked to antibiotic resistance and respiratory irritation. Found in many "antibacterial" sprays.
- **Synthetic fragrances:** May contain phthalates (endocrine disruptors) — not fully disclosed under trade secret protections.
- **Sodium lauryl sulfate (SLS):** A foaming agent that can cause skin irritation in sensitive individuals.
- **Phosphates:** Contributes to algae blooms in waterways when washed down drains.
- **Chlorine bleach:** Effective disinfectant but produces harmful chloroform and other byproducts.
- **Parabens:** Preservatives with potential hormone-disrupting properties.

### Reducing Plastic Packaging

The most impactful plastic reduction step for cleaning products:

1. **Switch to concentrate systems** (Branch Basics, Blueland) — eliminate ongoing plastic bottle purchases.
2. **Choose refillable options** — some stores offer cleaning product refill stations.
3. **Choose brands using recycled plastic** (Method, Seventh Generation) over virgin plastic packaging.
4. **Buy in bulk** — fewer bottles overall, even if each is larger.

### Do Eco Cleaners Actually Work?

For most everyday cleaning tasks — kitchen counters, sinks, glass, bathroom surfaces — yes, eco-friendly products clean as effectively as conventional alternatives. The formulas use the same basic chemistry (surfactants to lift dirt, acids or bases to cut through grime) with plant-derived rather than petroleum-derived ingredients.

The exception is disinfection: most eco cleaners are not EPA-registered disinfectants. For high-pathogen situations (illness in the home, raw meat contact surfaces), Seventh Generation Disinfecting Spray or occasional diluted bleach are still the most effective options.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Are eco-friendly cleaning products as effective as conventional ones?

For everyday cleaning, yes. Modern plant-based formulas clean kitchen grease, soap scum, and everyday grime as well as their conventional counterparts. Where they underperform is as disinfectants — most eco products aren't EPA-registered to kill pathogens. For disinfection, Seventh Generation (with thymol) is the best eco alternative.

### What certifications should I look for on eco cleaning products?

The most credible certifications in the US are EPA Safer Choice (ingredient safety across health and environment) and EWG Verified (strict transparency and ingredient standards). Third-party certifications are essential — many products greenwash without independent verification.

### Are "natural" and "eco-friendly" cleaning products the same thing?

No. "Natural" is an unregulated marketing term with no legal definition — a product can legally claim to be "natural" while containing synthetic fragrances, petrochemical surfactants, and preservatives. Look for EPA Safer Choice or EWG Verified certifications rather than relying on label claims.

### Is eco-friendly cleaning more expensive?

Upfront, sometimes — but concentrate systems like Branch Basics and Blueland cost significantly less per use over time than buying single-use spray bottles. ECOS and Method are competitively priced with mainstream brands at most retailers.

### Can I make my own eco-friendly cleaning products?

Yes. White vinegar, baking soda, castile soap, and water cover most cleaning tasks. Vinegar diluted with water (1:1) makes an excellent glass and surface cleaner. Baking soda is a gentle abrasive for sink scrubbing. Castile soap diluted in water handles dishes and general surfaces. The main limitation is that none of these are EPA-registered disinfectants.
