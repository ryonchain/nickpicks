## Why Water Flossers Are Worth It

String flossing works but most people don't do it consistently — or do it correctly. Water flossers solve the compliance problem: a 60-second water flosser routine is faster, easier, and (for many people) more enjoyable than 2–3 minutes of proper string flossing technique.

For specific populations, water flossers aren't just convenient — they're clinically superior:

- **Braces wearers:** String floss can barely reach around brackets; water flossers flush the entire bracket surface and the wires
- **Implant patients:** Water flossers clean around implant margins without the risk of string disrupting the soft tissue seal
- **Periodontal patients:** Sub-gingival tips deliver antimicrobial rinse below the gumline
- **Arthritis patients:** No fine-motor dexterity required
- **Anyone with a bridge:** Water flossers clean under pontics that string floss can't reach

## How to Use a Water Flosser Correctly

Most people get suboptimal results because they use the wrong technique:

1. **Aim at the gumline, not the tooth.** Direct the tip at a 90-degree angle to the gumline, not the tooth surface.
2. **Work from back to front.** Start at the back molars and work toward the front teeth — don't chase debris around randomly.
3. **Pause briefly between teeth.** The tip should pause 1–2 seconds between each tooth for thorough flushing.
4. **Lean over the sink.** Water will exit your mouth — this is normal and means it's working.
5. **Use it after brushing.** Brush first to loosen debris; flosser then flushes it out.
