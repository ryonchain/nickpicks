## Why the Lamp Matters as Much as the Polish

Gel nails have become the go-to at-home manicure format because they last 2–4 weeks without chipping. But an uncured or under-cured gel is worse than no gel at all — it stays tacky, lifts early, and can cause skin sensitization with repeated exposure to uncured photoinitiators.

The lamp determines whether your gel cures fully, cures evenly, and cures without discomfort. Cheap, underpowered lamps save $15 at purchase and cost you weeks of frustrating lift, peeling, and incomplete cure.

What to look for in a nail lamp:
- **LED beads positioned to reach thumb and pinky** (many cheap lamps have poor coverage for outer fingers)
- **36W minimum for standard gels; 54W+ for builder gels**
- **Timer presets** (10s/30s/60s for different formula needs)
- **No-heat/low-heat mode** if you experience heat spikes

## Getting the Best Results from Your Nail Lamp

**Thin coats cure better.** Gel needs to cure from the top down; thick coats cure at the surface but remain sticky underneath. Two thin coats beat one thick coat every time.

**Wipe the inhibition layer.** Gel cures with a tacky surface layer (inhibition layer). Wipe with isopropyl alcohol after each cure cycle; this is normal, not an error.

**Cap the free edge.** Wrap the tip of each nail with gel and cure — this seals the edge and dramatically extends how long your manicure lasts before chipping at the tips.

**Clean your lamp's interior.** Gel spills and dust on the reflective interior reduce curing efficiency. Wipe monthly with a cotton pad and isopropyl alcohol.
