## RGBIC vs. RGB: The Key Distinction

Most budget strip lights use **RGB** — every LED on the strip displays the same color at the same time. **RGBIC** (independent control) uses an IC chip that allows different segments of the same strip to display different colors simultaneously. This is the difference between a strip that lights solid purple and one that flows through a rainbow gradient.

Key factors to evaluate:

- **Color modes:** RGB = one color at a time. RGBIC = multiple colors simultaneously. RGBWW adds warm white LEDs for better white-tone output. Check whether the warm white is separate from the color diodes or blended.
- **Length options:** Most rooms (bedroom accent, under-bed, behind TV) need 16.4 ft (5m). Whole-room perimeter lighting typically requires two strips joined or a longer run. Check whether extensions are available.
- **Lumens output:** Accent and ambient lighting: 200–400 lumens per meter is appropriate. Task or functional lighting from strips (under-cabinet, workspace): 400–800+ lumens per meter.
- **App and voice control:** Most modern strips support Alexa and Google Assistant. Apple HomeKit support is rarer and usually requires Philips Hue or Matter-certified hardware. Wi-Fi direct (no hub) is more convenient than Bluetooth-only.
- **Music sync:** Built-in mic sync reacts to ambient sound in the room. Some premium options (Govee Pro) use dedicated microphones or integrate with phone audio. Quality of sync varies significantly between brands.
- **Adhesive quality:** The single most common failure point. Cheap strips peel within weeks on painted walls, especially in warm rooms. Look for 3M-backed adhesive or strips that include mounting clips.

## What to Look For When Buying

**Decide on RGB vs. RGBIC first.** If you want gradient rainbow effects and multi-color zones, you need RGBIC. If you just want accent color in one tone at a time, RGB is cheaper and simpler. The price gap has narrowed to ~$5–10 for shorter runs.

**Wi-Fi vs. Bluetooth.** Bluetooth-only strips require your phone to be nearby and can't be automated as easily. Wi-Fi strips work independently once set up and integrate with voice assistants and automations properly. Worth the slight extra cost.

**Test your wall surface before committing.** Porous, textured, or freshly-painted walls will peel adhesive within weeks. Budget for mounting clips or command strips regardless of what adhesive the strip claims.

**Length and cuttability.** Measure your installation before buying. Check whether the strip is cuttable (and where) and whether extensions are available from the same brand if you end up short.
