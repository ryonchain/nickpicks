## How to Choose a Motion Sensor Light

### Lumens: How Bright Is Enough?

For pathways and entry steps, 100–400 lumens is adequate. For covering a driveway or large backyard, you want 1,500 lumens minimum — ideally 2,000–3,000. More lumens is generally better for deterrence, but position matters: a bright light aimed at your neighbor's bedroom window creates problems. Choose adjustable-head fixtures so you can direct the beam precisely.

### Camera Combo vs. Light Only

A camera floodlight does two jobs: it deters intruders with light and it records what the light reveals. If you've had package theft, a past break-in attempt, or just want evidence if something happens, the $100–$180 premium over a light-only fixture is worth it. For low-traffic areas like side yards or back fences, a light-only solution like the LEONLITE or Mr. Beams is simpler and cheaper to maintain.

### Hardwired vs. Battery

Hardwired lights are brighter, more reliable, and require no battery changes. If you have an existing outdoor junction box, hardwired is almost always the better choice. Battery-powered lights like the Mr. Beams are best reserved for locations with no existing wiring — sheds, fence posts, rental properties where electrical work isn't permitted.

### Placement Tips

Cover every point of entry: front door, back door, garage, and any gate. Mount lights 7–10 feet high so they illuminate a wide area and are out of easy reach. Point the motion sensor toward the most likely approach path — a driveway sensor aimed at the street will catch a car turning in; one aimed at your own yard will miss it. Test sensitivity settings before finalizing placement to avoid constant false triggers from trees or passing traffic.
