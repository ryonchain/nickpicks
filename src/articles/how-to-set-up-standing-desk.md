Most people unbox their [standing desk](/articles/best-standing-desk-under-500/), crank it to roughly shoulder height, and wonder why their back hurts after ten minutes. The problem isn't the desk — it's the setup. Get the ergonomics wrong and your body will stage a protest until you sit back down permanently. Follow these five steps and you'll have a workstation that actually supports the way you work.

---

## Step 1: Set Your Sitting Height First

Before you ever touch the standing preset, nail your seated position. This is your foundation.

**The elbow-at-90° formula:** Sit in your [ergonomic chair](/articles/best-ergonomic-chair-under-300/) with your feet flat on the floor and your back supported. Relax your arms at your sides, then bend your elbows to 90°. Your fingertips should land right at keyboard height. Raise or lower the desk surface until your wrists are neutral — flat, not bent up or down — when your fingers rest on the keys.

**Monitor eye-level rule:** The top third of your screen should sit at or just below eye level. The cleanest fix is a monitor arm, which lets you dial in both height and depth independently of the desk surface. The [Ergotron LX Monitor Arm](/articles/ergotron-lx-monitor-arm-review/) holds position without creep and clears your desk surface entirely.

## Step 2: Set Your Standing Height

The same elbow rule applies: stand upright, let your arms fall naturally, bend to 90°, and set the desk to meet your hands there. For most people this puts the desk surface roughly at hip-to-belly-button height.

**Anti-fatigue mat: non-negotiable.** Standing on a hard floor puts compressive load on your joints that will have you sitting down before your timer goes off. The [Topo Comfort Mat by Ergodriven](https://www.amazon.com) includes a raised ridge and footrest that keeps you shifting weight without thinking about it. ## Step 3: Program Your Memory Presets

Once you've confirmed your sitting and standing heights, save them immediately. On most desks this means holding the preset button for 3–5 seconds until it beeps. Label them: "1 = sit, 2 = stand."

Make transitions brainless. Apps like **Stretchly** (free, cross-platform) or **Stand Up!** (Mac) pop up gentle reminders on a schedule. Pair the reminder with a single button press and switching positions becomes a 10-second habit.

---

## Step 4: Tame the Cables

A rat's nest of cables actively discourages you from adjusting desk height because you're afraid of yanking something loose. Fix it:

1. Route power and data cables separately to reduce interference.
2. Use a cable spine or sleeve along the desk leg to bundle the run from surface to floor.
3. Mount a power strip to the underside of the desk so it travels with the surface.
4. Leave a deliberate 12-inch loop before the floor anchor so the desk can rise without pulling tight.

A proper [cable management kit](https://www.amazon.com) typically includes cable raceways, adhesive clips, velcro ties, and a cord sleeve — enough to go from chaos to clean in under an hour.

---

## Step 5: Build the Habit (The 30/30 Rule)

The simplest system that actually sticks: **30 minutes sitting, 30 minutes standing**, alternating throughout the day. Start with 20 minutes standing per hour and build up over two weeks as your feet adapt.

Anchor the habit to something you already do: stand when you take a call, sit when you're in deep focus work, stand when you check email. Pair it with your timer app and it becomes automatic within a week.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even with the right setup, a few predictable errors undercut the whole system:

- **Setting your standing height too high.** The most common mistake. If your shoulders are shrugging or your elbows are above 90°, lower the desk. Comfort beats posture-textbook perfection.
- **Skipping the anti-fatigue mat.** Standing on a hard floor adds compressive load to your knees and lower back. Without a mat, most people sit back down within 15 minutes. A quality anti-fatigue mat like the [Topo by Ergodriven](https://www.amazon.com) keeps you standing longer without thinking about it.
- **Forgetting to recheck monitor height at standing position.** Your monitor arm height that worked for sitting will likely be too low when you're standing. Confirm eye level matches the top third of your screen in both positions.
- **Using only one preset.** If you have to manually dial the desk height every time you switch, you won't switch. Program both presets immediately — sit and stand locked in before you put anything on the desk.
- **Skipping the habit-building phase.** The first week feels awkward. Start with 20 minutes standing per hour and build from there. Rushing to 50/50 on day one leads to sore feet and abandonment.

## You're Ready

Dial in your sitting height, lock in your standing height, program those presets, clean up the cables, and commit to a simple alternating schedule. Start with Step 1 right now — pull out a chair, bend your elbows, and measure. Five minutes of setup today means years of actually using the thing you paid for.

The first week, your feet will complain and your energy patterns will shift — you may feel more alert after standing sessions and more focused after sitting ones. That's normal. By week two, switching positions feels automatic and the fatigue disappears. That's when you start to understand why people who set this up correctly never go back to a static desk. The payoff is real, but only if you get the ergonomics right from day one.
