## Understanding Temperature Ratings

Sleeping bag temperature ratings follow the EN/ISO 13537 standard, which provides three numbers: **Comfort** (the temperature at which a cold sleeper stays comfortable), **Lower Limit** (the temperature a warm sleeper stays comfortable), and **Extreme** (the survival limit — not a comfort rating, do not camp at this temperature).

Most bags marketed with a single temperature rating (like "20°F bag") list the Lower Limit, not the Comfort rating. Cold sleepers should add 10–15°F to any stated rating. A "20°F" bag might require a 30–35°F night for a cold sleeper to sleep comfortably.

**Shell material** affects moisture resistance — DWR (durable water repellent) treated shells shed condensation better. **Fill type** determines warmth-to-weight ratio: down has the best warmth-to-weight but loses insulating ability when wet; synthetic insulation maintains warmth when wet and dries faster.

## Buyer's Guide

### Down vs. Synthetic: Which Should You Choose?

**Choose down if:** You camp in dry conditions, you value packability and weight above all else, and you're willing to pay more. Down's warmth-to-weight ratio is unmatched, and a quality down bag stored properly lasts decades.

**Choose synthetic if:** You camp in wet or humid conditions, you need the bag to perform if it gets wet (synthetic retains warmth even damp), you're on a budget, and easy care (machine washable) matters. Synthetic is heavier but more resilient.

### What Temperature Rating Do You Actually Need?

Match your bag's comfort rating (not lower limit) to the coldest night you expect to sleep in. For most three-season US camping (spring through fall), a 20°F bag is the right choice — it handles cold snaps while remaining comfortable on warmer nights with partial unzipping. For winter camping, step down to 0°F or colder. For summer-only camping, a 32°F or 40°F bag saves significant weight and cost.

### Mummy vs. Rectangular vs. Quilt

Mummy bags are the most thermally efficient — the tapered shape reduces dead air space your body has to heat. Rectangular bags offer more room to move and feel less claustrophobic for some sleepers, but run colder for the same insulation weight. Quilts (no hood, no back insulation panel) are the ultralight backpacker's choice when paired with an insulated sleeping pad — but require technique and a high tolerance for cold.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How should I store my sleeping bag?**
Never store a sleeping bag compressed in its stuff sack long-term — this degrades both down and synthetic fill over time. Store in a large cotton storage sack (usually included) or hung in a closet. Compression bags are for carrying on trail only.

**How do I wash a down sleeping bag?**
Use a front-loading washer on gentle cycle with a down-specific detergent (Nikwax Down Wash Direct, Granger's Down Wash). Never use a top-loader with an agitator — it damages down clusters. Dry on low heat with two or three tennis balls to break up clumping. Dry completely before storing — damp down grows mildew.

**What sleeping bag liner should I use?**
A silk or fleece liner adds 5–15°F of warmth and keeps the bag clean, extending time between full washes. A silk liner adds roughly 5°F and weighs 4–5 oz. A fleece liner adds 10–15°F at 12–15 oz. In summer, a liner alone may be sufficient without a bag.
