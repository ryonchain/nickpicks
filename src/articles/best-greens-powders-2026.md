## Greens Powder Buying Guide

**Ingredient transparency is critical:** Many greens powders use proprietary blends that list combined weights rather than individual ingredient doses. A "greens blend: 5,000mg" could contain 4,990mg of cheap wheat grass and 10mg of premium spirulina. Choose brands that disclose individual ingredient amounts.

**Taste affects compliance:** The best greens powder is the one you'll actually drink every day. If a product is unpleasant, you'll stop using it. The flavored options (Berry, Chocolate, Mint) significantly improve adherence for most users.

**Probiotics in greens powders:** Many greens powders add probiotic strains, but CFU counts are typically far lower than dedicated probiotic supplements. Consider them a bonus, not a replacement for a standalone probiotic.

## FAQ

**Do greens powders actually work?**
Greens powders can contribute to daily vegetable intake and provide micronutrients, antioxidants, and digestive enzymes. They are not a replacement for whole vegetables but are widely used as a convenient complement to a balanced diet. Ingredient quality and dose matter significantly between brands.

**What is the difference between AG1 and cheaper greens powders?**
AG1 (Athletic Greens) contains 75 ingredients including probiotics, adaptogens, and digestive enzymes at a premium price point (~$3/serving). Budget alternatives like Amazing Grass or Nested Naturals offer basic greens blends at $0.50–$1.00/serving with fewer total ingredients. The right choice depends on your budget and how comprehensive you want the formula.

**When is the best time to take greens powder?**
Most customers take greens powder first thing in the morning on an empty stomach or alongside breakfast. There is no single best time — consistency matters more than timing. Some products contain probiotics that are best taken with food.

**Are greens powders safe to take daily?**
Yes, greens powders made from whole-food-derived ingredients are widely considered safe for daily use by healthy adults. Check for third-party certifications and consult a healthcare provider if you take blood thinners, as some greens contain vitamin K.
