## Podcast Microphone Buying Guide

### USB vs XLR — The Real Difference
USB microphones have a built-in analog-to-digital converter. XLR microphones require an external audio interface. USB is simpler; XLR offers more control over the signal chain, better preamps, and higher headroom. For most podcasters, USB is genuinely sufficient. XLR becomes relevant when you care about studio-grade recordings or run a professional show.

### Condenser vs Dynamic Microphones
- **Condenser:** More sensitive, captures room ambiance, better for treated/quiet rooms
- **Dynamic:** Less sensitive, rejects background noise, better for untreated rooms

If you're recording in a bedroom with hard floors and minimal acoustic treatment, a dynamic (like the MV7 or SM58) will sound cleaner because it ignores more room reflection.

### Essential Accessories
1. **Boom arm:** Gets the mic off the desk, reduces desk vibration noise, allows precise positioning
2. **Shock mount:** Reduces vibration transfer from desk typing
3. **Pop filter:** Reduces plosives (P and B sounds) that distort recordings
4. **Acoustic panels:** 4–6 foam panels behind the mic transform room reverb

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need an audio interface to start podcasting?**
No. USB microphones (Blue Yeti, Shure MV7, Elgato Wave:3) connect directly to a computer. An interface is only needed for XLR microphones, which require an external preamp and analog-to-digital converter.

**What's the best podcast microphone under $100?**
The Samson Q2U ($70) is a remarkable value — it's a dynamic microphone with both USB and XLR outputs. Audio quality is significantly better than its price suggests.

**Can I use AirPods as a podcast microphone?**
Technically yes, but the audio quality is noticeably lower than any dedicated microphone. For serious content, even a $50 USB mic will sound dramatically better.
