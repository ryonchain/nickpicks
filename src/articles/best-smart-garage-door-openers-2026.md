## How to Choose a Smart Garage Door Opener

### Retrofit vs. Full Replacement

If your current opener works reliably, a retrofit add-on (myQ, Meross, Tailwind) is almost always the right call. You spend $30–$100 and gain full smartphone control. A full replacement makes sense when your opener is more than 10–15 years old, is chain-drive and noisy, or lacks a battery backup. New belt-drive units like the RYOBI and LiftMaster options are meaningfully quieter and more reliable than anything made before 2015.

### Smart Home Platform Compatibility

Check your current smart home setup before buying. Apple HomeKit users should choose Meross — it's the only retrofit option with native HomeKit at no extra cost. Amazon Alexa households can use any pick on this list. Google Home works with myQ, Meross, and LiftMaster natively. If you use automation platforms like Home Assistant, look for models with IFTTT support or check community integrations before purchasing.

### Security and Privacy

All the openers here use rolling-code encryption (codes change after every use, so replay attacks don't work) and TLS-encrypted cloud connections. The main vulnerabilities are human: weak WiFi passwords, shared access codes left active for former house-sitters, or leaving the app logged in on a shared device. Enable activity notifications so you know every time your door opens, and review access logs monthly.

### Backup Power

A garage door opener that loses power in a storm is useless. Both full-replacement units here (RYOBI and LiftMaster) include battery backup. If you go the retrofit route, your existing opener's battery backup (if it has one) remains active. If your current opener lacks backup power, a UPS battery on its outlet is a cheap insurance policy.
