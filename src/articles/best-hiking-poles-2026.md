# Best Hiking Poles in 2026

Trekking poles reduce knee impact on descents by 20–30%, improve stability on uneven terrain, and provide a rhythm for uphill climbing. They're not just for beginners — serious backpackers and trail runners use them consistently. The key choices: carbon fiber (lighter but can crack on impact) vs. aluminum (heavier but more durable), collapsible (3-section twist/flick-lock) vs. folding (folds to compact length), and tip types.

## Locking Systems

**FlickLock / Flick-Lock (snap collar):** Squeeze the collar, slide the section, release to lock. Very reliable. Adjustable with gloves. Industry standard for quality poles.

**Twist-lock:** Rotate the section to lock. Less reliable than flick-lock — can loosen under load. Found in budget poles.

**SpeedLock (Leki):** Lever-style lock on the side of the section. Very fast to adjust. Leki's proprietary system.

---

## Grip Materials

**Cork:** Best for long hikes. Molds to hand shape, doesn't slip when wet, doesn't cause blisters. Premium option.

**EVA foam:** Soft, doesn't transfer cold, good for winter. Popular for cold weather and trail running.

**Rubber:** Least comfortable for extended use. Found in budget poles and winter poles (doesn't conduct cold).

---

## Wrist Strap Technique

The wrist strap is not for holding the pole — it's for transferring load to the wrist. Insert your hand through the strap from below, then grip the handle. This means your wrist pushes against the strap on the downstroke, reducing grip fatigue significantly.
