## Probiotic Buying Guide

**CFU at expiration, not manufacture:** Always verify the label says CFUs are guaranteed "through expiration" or "at time of use" — not just "at time of manufacture." Viable bacteria counts drop over time, and a product manufactured with 100B CFU may have far fewer at the time you take it.

**Strain diversity vs. strain research:** More strains isn't always better. A product with 2 well-researched strains at meaningful doses may outperform a 50-strain product where each strain is present in trace amounts. Prioritize research-backed strains over marketing-driven strain counts.

**Refrigerated vs. shelf-stable:** Refrigerated probiotics can be more potent if the cold chain is maintained. Shelf-stable products using delayed-release capsules or spore-forming strains are practical for travel and daily compliance. Both can be effective when manufactured and stored correctly.

## FAQ

**How many CFUs do I need in a probiotic?**
Most clinical research uses 1–10 billion CFU per day for general gut health maintenance. Higher CFU counts (30–100 billion) are not necessarily better — strain specificity and viability at the time of consumption matter more than raw CFU numbers. Look for products that guarantee CFU counts at expiration, not just at manufacture.

**Do I need to refrigerate my probiotic?**
Some probiotics require refrigeration to maintain viability (particularly Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium strains). Many modern products use freeze-drying, acid-resistant capsules, or spore-forming strains (like Bacillus coagulans) that are shelf-stable at room temperature. Check the product label for storage instructions.

**What probiotic strains should I look for?**
Lactobacillus acidophilus and Bifidobacterium longum are the most researched strains for digestive health. Lactobacillus rhamnosus GG is widely studied for immune support and diarrhea prevention. Bifidobacterium lactis supports bowel regularity. Diverse multi-strain products are generally preferred over single-strain products for broader gut microbiome support.

**When is the best time to take a probiotic?**
Most research suggests taking probiotics with or shortly before a meal — food buffers stomach acid, improving survival of live cultures to reach the intestine. Some spore-based probiotics (Bacillus strains) are acid-resistant and can be taken any time. Follow the label instructions for each specific product.
