## How Induction Cooking Works

Induction cooktops use a copper coil beneath the glass surface to generate an oscillating magnetic field. That field induces eddy currents in any ferromagnetic cookware placed on top — cast iron, stainless steel, or induction-compatible non-stick. The pot heats; the glass doesn't. The moment you remove the pan, heat generation stops instantly.

**Cookware compatibility:** Your pans must be induction-compatible. Test with a magnet — if it sticks to the bottom of the pan, it works. Cast iron, enameled cast iron, and most stainless steel works. Aluminum, copper, and glass cookware does not unless the manufacturer has added an induction-compatible base layer.

**Wattage and speed:** Single-burner units typically draw 1,200–1,800W. Most home circuits are 15A (1,800W max), so 1,800W units run at full circuit capacity. A 1,800W induction cooktop boils 6 cups of water in approximately 4 minutes versus 6–8 minutes on a standard electric coil.

## Buying Guide: Choosing a Portable Induction Cooktop

### Single vs. Double Burner

Single-burner units ($35–$100) fit any countertop and standard 15A circuit. Double-burner units ($130–$180) effectively replace a small range and handle two pots simultaneously — but running both zones at full power (3,600W combined) requires two separate 15A circuits. RVs and small apartments may be limited to one zone at a time.

### Power Settings and Temperature Control

Entry-level units offer 8 power settings. Mid-range units like the Duxtop 9600LS offer 15 discrete power levels with temperature presets. For precision cooking (tempering, candy-making, sauce reduction), more granular settings matter. For boiling pasta and sautéing vegetables, 8 settings is sufficient.

### What Cookware Works

- **Works:** Cast iron, enameled cast iron, magnetic stainless steel, induction-rated non-stick
- **Doesn't work:** Pure aluminum, copper, ceramic, glass, non-magnetic stainless
- **Quick test:** If a magnet sticks firmly to the bottom of the pan, it's induction-compatible.

### Circuit Requirements

Standard US home circuits are 15A (1,800W). A 1,800W single-burner cooktop draws the full circuit — avoid running a microwave or other high-draw appliance on the same circuit. For double burners at full power, use two separate 15A circuits or limit yourself to one zone at a time.

## FAQ

**Can I use a cast iron skillet on an induction cooktop?**
Yes. Cast iron is ideal for induction — it's highly magnetic and retains heat well. Flat-bottomed cast iron works best; slightly warped pans may heat unevenly.

**Is induction cooking safe for an apartment?**
Yes. The cooking surface stays cool to the touch — only the pan gets hot. There's no open flame, no gas combustion, and reduced risk of contact burns. Many renters prefer induction over exposed coil electric burners for safety.

**How much does induction cooking cost to run?**
Induction is roughly 84–90% energy efficient (versus ~40% for gas). Boiling a pot of water on a 1,800W induction unit costs approximately $0.02–$0.04 per use at average US electricity rates.

**Will an induction cooktop work during a power outage?**
No. Induction requires electricity. For off-grid cooking, a propane camping stove is the appropriate backup.
