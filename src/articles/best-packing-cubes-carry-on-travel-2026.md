## Packing Cubes Buying Guide

### Compression vs. Non-Compression

**Compression cubes** have a second zipper that squeezes the cube to a fraction of its original volume after packing. This is critical for carry-on travel with a full week's worth of clothes — the space savings can be 30–40%.

**Non-compression cubes** organize without compressing. Better for rigid suitcases where you want to fill the space evenly, and for items that don't compress well (structured clothing, shoes).

For carry-on maximization: at least one or two compression cubes for clothes.

### How Many Cubes Do You Need?

A practical 5-day carry-on system:
- **1 large compression cube** — shirts, pants, lightweight layers
- **1 medium cube** — underwear and socks
- **1 small cube** — accessories (cables, adapters, sunglasses)
- Optional: **1 toiletry cube** or just use a quart bag

A 3-cube set (large + medium + small) is the minimum useful configuration. 4-piece sets are the sweet spot.

### Sizing for Standard Carry-Ons

Most carry-on bags conform to the 22" × 14" × 9" standard. Packing cubes sized for this don't leave awkward gaps. Check that the cubes you buy are sized for carry-ons, not full-size checked luggage (those cubes waste space in a smaller bag).

### Material Matters

- **15D nylon** (Osprey): lightest, plenty strong for normal use, less abrasion-resistant
- **Recycled ripstop nylon** (Eagle Creek): durable, slightly heavier than 15D, good balance
- **400D canvas** (Peak Design): most durable, most water-resistant, heaviest

For most travelers, recycled ripstop nylon is the best balance of weight and durability.

## Pro Carry-On Packing Tips

**Roll, don't fold.** Rolling clothes (especially synthetic and knit fabrics) reduces wrinkles and packs more efficiently into compression cubes.

**Wear your heaviest items.** Jacket, heaviest shoes, heaviest pants — wear them on the plane rather than packing them.

**Pack a second smaller bag.** A personal item (under-seat bag) + a carry-on is two bags. Use one for clothes, one for tech and daily-access items.

**Liquids in the first cube you can access.** TSA screening requires liquids out separately. Put your liquids cube at the top of the bag or in an external pocket.

**Q: Do packing cubes actually save space?**
Compression cubes (double-zipper) reduce clothing volume by 30–40%. Regular cubes organize without compressing. For carry-on travel, compression matters.

**Q: What size cubes fit a standard carry-on?**
Look for carry-on-sized cubes designed for a 22" × 14" × 9" interior. Checked-luggage cubes are too large.

**Q: How many for a week-long trip?**
Three to four: one large compression cube for clothes, one medium for underwear/socks, one small for accessories. A 4-set gives useful flexibility.

**Q: Are expensive cubes worth it?**
For frequent travelers, yes — Eagle Creek's lifetime warranty means they pay for themselves over time. Casual travelers: start with a budget Gonex set at $30.

**Q: Can I use them in checked luggage too?**
Yes — same organizational benefits, just use larger cube sizes. Great for hotels with limited drawer space.
