## Buying Guide: What to Look for in a Robot Vacuum

### Navigation Type
- **Random bounce:** Cheapest but unpredictable coverage, misses corners
- **Gyroscopic:** Basic grid patterns, better than random but no true mapping
- **LiDAR:** Gold standard — creates precise floor maps, handles multi-room homes reliably
- **Camera + AI:** Premium option, best obstacle avoidance (iRobot, Roborock premium lines)

### Suction Power (Pa)
- Under 1,500 Pa: Fine for light dust on hardwood
- 1,500–2,500 Pa: Good for most homes including low-pile carpet
- 2,500+ Pa: Handles thick carpet and embedded pet hair

### Self-Emptying Docks
Self-emptying bases typically hold 45–60 days of debris. Worth the extra $80–150 if you have pets or hate emptying the bin every 2–3 cleanings.

### Runtime vs. Home Size
Most robot vacuums cover 1,000–2,000 sq ft per charge. For larger homes, look for:
- Recharge and resume: Docks, charges, picks up where it left off
- Mapping: Room-level scheduling so you can clean specific areas only

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should a robot vacuum run?**
For pet owners: daily. For dust-only homes: 3–4 times per week. The advantage of robot vacuums is low-effort frequent cleaning that prevents buildup.

**Can robot vacuums handle thick rugs?**
Most modern LiDAR models handle rugs up to 0.8 inches (2 cm). High-pile shag rugs can trap wheels — check the climb height specification before buying.

**Do I need a separate mop?**
Combo vacuum-mop units work well on hard floors but don't replace a deep mop. They're best for daily maintenance mopping, not weekly deep cleaning.

**Are cheap robot vacuums worth it?**
Models under $150 with random navigation create frustration — they miss areas, get stuck repeatedly, and require babysitting. We recommend spending at least $199 for basic smart navigation (LiDAR or gyroscopic).
