## Oscillating vs. Sonic: What Actually Matters

The electric toothbrush market splits into two cleaning technologies:

**Oscillating-rotating (Oral-B style):** A round brush head spins in alternating directions while vibrating. Operates at ~8,000 oscillations per minute. Research consistently shows superior plaque removal, especially along the gumline. Brush heads are small — easier to maneuver around back molars.

**Sonic (Philips Sonicare style):** A rectangular brush head vibrates at 30,000–40,000 strokes per minute, creating fluid dynamics that disrupt plaque slightly beyond the brush tip. Gentler feeling in the mouth. Many people find the sensation less aggressive than oscillating.

The clinical difference between the two is small at equivalent price points — either technology dramatically outperforms manual brushing. Your preference often comes down to how you want the brush to feel.

Other specs that matter at this price range:

- **Pressure sensor:** Alerts you when pressing too hard. Most people brush with 2–3x the necessary pressure, causing gum recession over time. A pressure sensor is worth prioritizing.
- **Battery life:** Entry-level brushes: 1–2 weeks per charge. Mid-range: 3–4 weeks. For travel, battery life matters more than it does for home use.
- **Brush head availability:** A great brush is worthless if the replacement heads are hard to find or expensive. Oral-B and Sonicare heads are available at every drugstore; off-brand heads are Amazon-only.
- **Timer/quadpacer:** Most decent brushes have a 2-minute timer with 30-second quadrant alerts. This is table stakes at this price range — only buy brushes that include it.

## What to Look For When Buying

**Brush head replacement cost matters more than handle price:** Many buyers optimize for handle cost but overlook that Oral-B and Sonicare replacement heads run $8–$12 each and should be replaced every 3 months. Over 2 years, that's $64–$96 in heads alone. Factor this into the total cost of ownership.

**Pressure sensor vs. no sensor:** If your dentist has mentioned gum recession or you know you brush hard, the pressure sensor is worth paying for. If you're a light brusher, skip it and save.

**Battery type:** Most brushes in this price range use NiMH batteries that charge by induction. Lithium-ion batteries (USB-C charging, longer cycle life) are starting to appear in entry-level brushes — a meaningful upgrade if you travel.

**Mode count:** 5 cleaning modes sounds impressive, but most users default to the standard "Clean" mode 100% of the time. Don't pay extra for modes you won't use. The features that matter daily: timer, quadpacer, and pressure sensor.
