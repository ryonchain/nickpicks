## Types of Ice Cream Makers

### Frozen Bowl (Canister-Style)

The most common type. A double-walled bowl with liquid in the wall must be frozen overnight (or 24 hours) before use. Then you add your ice cream base, run the machine for 20–30 minutes, and transfer to the freezer to firm up.

**Pros:** Inexpensive; simple to use  
**Cons:** Requires 24-hour planning; can only make one batch before re-freezing the bowl  
**Price:** $25–$90

### Compressor Machines

Built-in refrigeration unit — no pre-freezing required. Load your base, press start, and the machine does everything. You can make multiple batches back to back.

**Pros:** No planning required; multiple batches; often softer, creamier result  
**Cons:** Significantly more expensive; heavier and larger footprint  
**Price:** $200–$500+

### Salt-and-Ice Machines

Traditional-style machines that use a combination of ice and rock salt in an outer bucket to freeze an inner canister. Produces a very creamy, old-fashioned result.

**Pros:** Large batch capacity; classic process; very creamy texture  
**Cons:** Requires ice and rock salt supply; messier operation  
**Price:** $35–$80

## Key Features That Determine Ice Cream Quality

### Motor Power and Churning Speed

A stronger motor churns the mix more consistently, incorporating more air (overrun) and producing smoother texture. Budget models with weak motors can produce icy, uneven results. Look for:

- **Canister machines:** 60–100W motors
- **Compressor machines:** 150–200W motors

### Capacity

Standard batch sizes range from 1 quart to 2 quarts. For a family of 4, 1.5–2 quarts is ideal. For individuals or couples, 1 quart is enough — and smaller machines are cheaper.

### Compressor Cooling Temperature

For compressor machines, look for models that reach -22°C to -30°C for the fastest freezing and creamiest texture.

### Additional Modes

Better machines offer modes for gelato (denser, less air), sorbet, frozen yogurt, and sherbet — each with slightly different churn timing.

## Ice Cream Maker Buying Guide

### Canister vs. Compressor: The Real Decision

The biggest choice isn't brand — it's canister vs. compressor.

**Choose a canister machine if:**
- You bake ice cream occasionally (once or twice a week max)
- You don't mind planning 24 hours ahead
- Budget is under $100

**Choose a compressor machine if:**
- You make ice cream frequently (multiple times a week)
- You want to make multiple batches back to back
- You're willing to invest $200+ for convenience

### How to Make Creamier Ice Cream

Regardless of machine type, these techniques improve creaminess:

- **Chill your base** in the refrigerator 1–2 hours before churning (or use a custard-based recipe)
- **Use heavy cream** (not half-and-half) for richest result
- **Don't over-churn** — stop at soft-serve stage and transfer to freezer
- **Add a tablespoon of alcohol** (vodka, amaretto) to lower freezing point slightly and keep texture scoopable

## FAQ: Ice Cream Makers

**How long does it take to make homemade ice cream?**
With a canister machine: 24 hours to freeze the bowl + 20–30 minutes to churn + 2 hours in freezer to firm = plan for 26+ hours total. With a compressor: 30–45 minutes from mix to soft-serve.

**Why is my homemade ice cream icy, not creamy?**
Usually the base wasn't cold enough before churning, the bowl wasn't frozen solid, or the mix has too much water. Add an extra egg yolk (richer custard) or more cream.

**Can I make dairy-free ice cream?**
Yes. Coconut milk, cashew milk, and oat milk bases all work well in ice cream makers. The Ninja CREAMi is particularly good with non-dairy recipes.

**How long does homemade ice cream keep?**
Homemade ice cream is best within 1–2 weeks. Unlike commercial ice cream, it lacks stabilizers, so it develops ice crystals faster. Store in airtight pint containers.

**Does the Ninja CREAMi make real ice cream?**
It produces a very creamy result using a different process (shaving a frozen solid). Many users prefer the texture, especially for high-protein or low-calorie recipes.
