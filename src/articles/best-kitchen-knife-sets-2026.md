## What Makes a Quality Knife

### Steel Types

**German steel (high-carbon stainless):**
- 56–58° HRC hardness
- More flexible — resists chipping
- Easier to sharpen
- Best for general home use

**Japanese steel:**
- 60–65° HRC hardness
- Harder = sharper edge retention
- More brittle — chips if misused
- Best for experienced cooks

### Handle Materials

- **Wood:** Traditional, beautiful, requires drying/oiling
- **Synthetic (Fibrox, POM):** Durable, dishwasher-safe (though we recommend hand-washing)
- **Resin-pakkawood:** Premium look with moisture resistance
- **Full-tang metal:** Chef's choice, best balance

### Forged vs. Stamped

**Forged:** Cut from a rod of steel, heated and shaped. Heavier, more durable, usually includes a bolster. Examples: Wüsthof, Henckels.

**Stamped:** Cut from a sheet of steel. Lighter, often equally sharp, less expensive. Examples: Victorinox, Global.

## Knife Maintenance: Make Them Last

1. **Hand wash only** — dishwashers dull edges and damage handles
2. **Hone weekly** with a honing rod — realigns the edge between sharpenings
3. **Sharpen 1-2x per year** — use a whetstone or professional sharpener
4. **Use a wood cutting board** — glass and stone destroy edges
5. **Store properly** — knife block, magnetic strip, or blade guards (not loose in drawers)

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need to buy a full set?**
A: No. Most professional chefs rely on 3 knives: a chef's knife, paring knife, and bread knife. Start with these.

**Q: How long should knife sets last?**
A: Quality German knives last 20–30+ years with proper care. Budget knives typically last 5–10 years.

**Q: Are expensive knife sets worth it?**
A: For cooks who cook 5+ times per week, yes. Better steel holds an edge longer, meaning less time sharpening and better results. Budget sets work but require more maintenance.

**Q: What's the best knife for beginners?**
