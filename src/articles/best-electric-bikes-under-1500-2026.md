## What to Look for in an E-Bike Under $1500

### Motor Type and Placement
Hub motors (in the wheel) are simpler, cheaper to maintain, and fine for flat-to-moderate terrain. Mid-drive motors (at the pedal cranks) are heavier but handle hills better and feel more natural. Under $1500, most bikes use hub motors; mid-drive at this price range is a meaningful upgrade.

### Torque Sensor vs. Cadence Sensor
Cadence sensors activate assist when the cranks spin — they can feel on/off and jerky. Torque sensors measure how hard you push and scale assist proportionally — they feel like a natural extension of your effort. Worth paying a premium for if ride feel matters.

### Battery Capacity (Wh)
More Wh = more range, but also more weight and cost. 400–500Wh handles 20–35 mile commutes. 600Wh+ covers longer rides or hillier terrain. Manufacturer range claims assume pedal assist 1–2; real-world range on assist 3–5 is 30–50% less.

### Class 1, 2, or 3
- **Class 1:** Motor assists only while pedaling, up to 20 mph. Allowed everywhere bikes are allowed.
- **Class 2:** Throttle-only up to 20 mph. Restricted from some trails.
- **Class 3:** Motor assists while pedaling up to 28 mph. May require registration in some states.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to register an electric bike?**
In most US states, Class 1 and Class 2 e-bikes under 750W do not require registration or a license. Class 3 bikes have varying requirements by state. Check your state's DMV website.

**How long do e-bike batteries last?**
Most lithium batteries last 500–1000 charge cycles before noticeable degradation. At one charge per week, that's 10–20 years. Storing batteries at 50% charge in cool temperatures extends lifespan.

**Can I ride an e-bike in rain?**
Most e-bikes are splash-resistant (IP65 rating or similar). Heavy rain riding is fine; submersion is not. Avoid pressure washing the motor or battery.

**What's the real-world range?**
Manufacturer claims assume flat terrain, moderate assist, and a 170 lb rider. Multiply by 0.5–0.7 for hilly terrain, heavier riders, or higher assist levels for a realistic range estimate.
