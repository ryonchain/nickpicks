## 3D Printing Beginner's Guide

### Which Filament Should You Start With?

**PLA (Polylactic Acid):** Start here. Prints at low temperatures (190–220°C), doesn't warp, smells mild, biodegradable. Excellent for models, household objects, and learning. Weakness: brittle at impacts, degrades in direct sunlight.

**PETG:** The second step. Stronger than PLA, slightly flexible, better temperature resistance. Minimal warping. Good for functional parts.

**ABS:** Requires enclosure. Strong, temperature-resistant, smooth finish. Warps without proper environment. Not recommended for beginners.

**TPU:** Flexible filament for phone cases, gaskets, vibration dampeners. Prints slowly, can be finicky. Worth trying after PLA.

### Understanding Build Volume

Build volume = the maximum size object the printer can make in one piece:
- 180mm cube (A1 Mini): enough for phone stands, small figurines, many household parts
- 220mm cube (Ender 3): most common hobby parts and projects
- 256mm cube (P1S): large functional parts, full-size prosthetic hands

Most prints are well under these limits — you can print larger objects in multiple sections and join them.

### Printing Speed: What to Expect

| Claimed Speed | Real-World Quality Speed |
|---|---|
| 250mm/s max | 80–150mm/s for good quality |
| 500mm/s max | 150–250mm/s for good quality |

Maximum speed produces draft quality. For display-quality prints, expect 60–120mm/s for most layer heights and materials.

## FAQ

**How long does a 3D print take?**
A small object (phone stand, small figurine) takes 1–3 hours. A medium object (phone case, practical household part) takes 3–8 hours. A large detailed model can take 12–40+ hours. Print time depends heavily on size, layer height, and infill percentage.

**What can I actually make with a 3D printer?**
Household items that break (brackets, clips, knobs, replacement parts), functional tools (jigs, organizers, cable management), gaming miniatures, cosplay props, engineering prototypes, custom phone cases, plant pots, art, jewelry. The practical answer: anything that fits in the build volume and doesn't require structural strength beyond what plastics provide.

**Is 3D printing difficult to learn?**
Modern printers (especially Bambu Lab) have reduced the learning curve significantly. Basic PLA printing is achievable on day one. Mastering multi-material, engineering materials, and complex supports takes weeks to months of experience.
