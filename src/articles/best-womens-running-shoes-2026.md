## How to Choose the Right Running Shoe

### Step 1: Know Your Gait

| Gait Type | What It Means | Shoe Type |
|---|---|---|
| Neutral | Foot lands under body, minimal roll | Neutral shoe |
| Overpronation | Foot rolls inward | Stability shoe |
| Supination (underpronation) | Foot rolls outward | Cushioned neutral shoe |

**Gait test at home:** Look at your old running shoes. Excessive wear on the inner heel = overpronation. Wear on the outer edge = supination. Even wear = neutral.

### Step 2: Know Your Distance

| Distance | Cushioning Level |
|---|---|
| 5K / speed work | Lightweight, firm (Kinvara, Rebel) |
| 10K / tempo runs | Medium cushioning (Ghost) |
| Half / full marathon | Max cushioning (Bondi) |

### Step 3: Know Your Surface

- **Road:** Any of the above
- **Track:** Racing flats with spikes or low-stack trainers
- **Trail:** Different category — look for HOKA Speedgoat, Brooks Cascadia, Salomon Speedcross

## Running Shoe Fit Guide

- **Thumb's width** of space between longest toe and front of shoe
- **Heel holds** firmly without slipping
- **Midfoot snug** but not tight — no pinching across top of foot
- **Toe box wide enough** that toes can wiggle freely

Feet swell during runs — size up 0.5 if between sizes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I replace running shoes?**
Every 300–500 miles depending on cushioning. Signs you need new shoes: the midsole feels flat, you feel more soreness after runs than usual, or the outsole has worn through in spots.

**Should I wear the same shoes for running and the gym?**
No. Running shoes are designed for forward motion — lateral stability for gym work (squats, side lunges) requires a flat, wider sole like a trainer or cross-trainer. Switching shoes prevents lateral ankle injury.

**Is it worth going to a running specialty store?**
Yes, for a first purchase. Running store staff do free gait analysis and can recommend models for your specific biomechanics. Try on 3–4 pairs before committing.
