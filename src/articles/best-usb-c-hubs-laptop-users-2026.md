## USB-C Hub Buying Guide

### Understanding Display Output

| HDMI Version | Max Resolution | Good For |
|-------------|---------------|---------|
| HDMI 1.4 via hub | 4K@30Hz | Documents, static work |
| HDMI 2.0 via hub | 4K@60Hz | Video, design, gaming |
| DisplayPort 1.4 via hub | 4K@144Hz / 8K | High-refresh displays |
| Thunderbolt (native) | 6K@60Hz | Apple Pro Display XDR |

### Power Delivery: How Much Do You Need?

| Laptop | Minimum PD for Full Charging | Recommended |
|--------|------------------------------|-------------|
| MacBook Air (any M-series) | 30W | 60W+ |
| MacBook Pro 14" | 67W | 96W |
| MacBook Pro 16" | 96W | 140W (may need Thunderbolt dock) |
| Dell XPS 13/15 | 45W | 65W+ |
| Windows laptops (general) | 45W–65W | 100W for safety |

### MacBook-Specific Compatibility Notes

MacBooks use Thunderbolt 3/4 ports that are physically USB-C. Any USB-C hub works — but to unlock:
- **Dual 4K@60Hz displays**: Requires Thunderbolt 3/4 hub (not just USB-C)
- **Single 4K@60Hz display**: Standard USB-C 3.2 Gen 2 hub is sufficient
- **Pro Display XDR at full res**: Requires native Thunderbolt 4 dock

## FAQs

**Why does my hub get warm during use?**
Normal — power conversion for PD passthrough generates heat, and video output processing also adds thermal load. Hubs should feel warm, not hot. If a hub is uncomfortably hot, it may be drawing more power than rated (reduce connected devices or PD load).

**Can a USB-C hub replace a Thunderbolt dock?**
For most tasks, yes. The only cases a Thunderbolt dock is necessary: driving two 4K@60Hz displays simultaneously from a MacBook, achieving full bandwidth to an external NVMe enclosure, or connecting Thunderbolt-specific peripherals (like certain audio interfaces).

**Will a USB-C hub slow down my laptop?**
Not meaningfully for typical tasks. Under maximum load (4K display + fast SSD + 100W PD simultaneously), some bandwidth contention exists — but for standard office use, the difference is imperceptible.
