## Buying Guide

### Door Frame vs. Wall-Mounted: Which Should You Choose?

**Choose door frame if you:**
- Rent your home
- Want to avoid putting holes in walls
- Travel frequently (removable)
- Budget is under $50

**Choose wall-mounted if you:**
- Own your home
- Weigh over 225 lbs or plan to do weighted pull-ups
- Want zero movement during kipping or dynamic movements
- Are building a dedicated home gym space

### Weight Capacity and Safety

For regular pull-ups, capacity should exceed your bodyweight by 50–100 lbs minimum. For kipping pull-ups, muscle-ups, or weighted pull-ups, add 2× your bodyweight to your target capacity.

**Weighted pull-up example:** 200 lb athlete + 45 lb weight vest = 245 lb load. A 300 lb rated bar has very little margin — choose a 400+ lb rated option.

### Grip Width and Options

**Wide grip (30"+):** Emphasizes lat width development
**Shoulder-width (22"–26"):** Best strength-to-ROM ratio for most people
**Close/neutral grip (12"–16"):** More bicep involvement, easier on wrists

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is a door frame pull-up bar safe?**
Yes, when used as directed. The key requirement is a door frame with 3.5"+ of molding depth. Hollow-core doors or thin metal door frames may not be compatible. Check the bar's door frame requirements before buying. Never leave a door frame bar installed while a door is open — the door swinging could dislodge it.

**Can I install a wall-mounted bar in drywall?**
You must mount into wall studs, not drywall alone. Drywall anchors cannot safely support body weight under dynamic loading. Use a stud finder to locate studs (typically 16" or 24" apart) and use lag screws of at least 3.5" length.

**What exercises can I do with a pull-up bar?**
Pull-ups, chin-ups, neutral grip pull-ups, hanging knee raises, L-sits, toes-to-bar, hanging leg raises, muscle-ups (on wall-mounted bars with enough clearance), and one-arm pull-up progressions. A single bar offers more upper body and core exercises than most gym machines.
