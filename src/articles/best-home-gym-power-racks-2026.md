## Power Rack vs. Squat Stand vs. Half Rack

**Squat stand (a-frame):** Lightest, cheapest, most portable. Two vertical uprights. No safety bars — must bail forward if you fail a lift. Best for experienced lifters or those using spotters.

**Half rack:** Two uprights + safety bars. More protection than squat stand. Can add pull-up bar.

**Full cage/power rack:** Four uprights forming a box with safety bars and pull-up bar. Safest option — bail in any direction. Best for solo training.

If you train alone without a spotter, get a power rack. The safety bars save your life (and your floor) on failed squats.

## What to Look For

**Steel gauge and uprights:** 3×3" 11-gauge is the gold standard (Rogue, Titan). 2×2" 12-14-gauge is entry-level but adequate for most home gym users under 350 lb total load.

**Weight capacity:** 600 lb handles any realistic home gym load. 1,000 lb+ is for those planning to compete or load extreme amounts.

**Full cage vs. squat stand:** Solo trainers should choose a full cage with safety bars. Squat stands are for experienced lifters with spotters or confidence in their bail technique.

**Hole spacing:** Westside (1" spacing in bench zone) is the industry standard for flexible bench press setup. Non-standard spacing limits positioning options.

**Accessory ecosystem:** Rogue > Titan > Rep Fitness > everyone else. More accessories means more exercises and future expansion.
