## Buying Guide: What Actually Matters

**FDA clearance vs. FDA approval:** FDA-cleared means the device is substantially equivalent to an existing cleared device and has met accuracy requirements. This is the standard for pulse oximeters. "FDA registered" or "FDA certified" are marketing terms that mean nothing about accuracy validation — avoid devices that use this language.

**Spot check vs. continuous monitoring:** Fingertip oximeters are for spot checks (single readings). Ring and wrist oximeters are for continuous monitoring (overnight, during exercise, during illness). Choose based on your use case.

**Perfusion index (PI):** A higher PI means more blood flow reaching the finger and a more reliable reading. The Masimo MightySat and Innovo Deluxe display PI — if it's below 0.4%, the reading may be unreliable.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use a pulse oximeter during exercise?**
Standard fingertip oximeters are unreliable during movement — motion artifact distorts the reading. The Masimo MightySat uses advanced signal processing that maintains accuracy during some movement. For continuous exercise monitoring, a chest strap or wrist-based device is more appropriate.

**How do I know if my pulse oximeter is accurate?**
Compare your reading to a hospital-grade device if possible. As a basic check: if you get consistent readings of 97–100% at rest while feeling well, the device is likely working. Any consistent readings below 94% at rest warrant physician evaluation regardless of device brand.
