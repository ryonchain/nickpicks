## What to Know Before Buying a Soundbar

### HDMI ARC vs. Optical vs. Bluetooth
**HDMI ARC/eARC** is the best connection — single cable carries audio and lets your TV remote control soundbar volume. **Optical** is universal and high-quality but only carries stereo/Dolby Digital (not Atmos). **Bluetooth** is wireless but adds latency — fine for music, noticeable for video sync.

### Dolby Atmos vs. Dolby Digital — What's the Difference?
**Dolby Digital**: Standard surround sound (5.1, 7.1). All picks here support this.
**Dolby Atmos**: Adds height channels — sound comes from overhead. Requires content with Atmos audio track (Netflix, Disney+, Apple TV+) AND a soundbar with upward-firing drivers. Most sub-$300 soundbars don't truly support Atmos (only virtual processing). The Samsung HW-Q600B (~$350) is the cheapest genuine Atmos bar.

### Wireless Subwoofer vs. No Sub
A wireless subwoofer dramatically changes bass response. The difference between the Sonos Ray (no sub) and Samsung HW-B550 (with sub) is night-and-day for action movies and music. If you care about bass, don't skip the sub.

### Room Size Matters
- Under 200 sq ft: 100–200W is plenty (Sonos Ray, TCL Alto 8+)
- 200–400 sq ft: 200–300W (LG S60Q, Yamaha YAS-209)
- 400+ sq ft: 300W+ (Samsung HW-B550) or step up to the HW-Q800B

## FAQ

**Do I need a soundbar if I have a good TV?**
Yes. Even premium OLED TVs ship with mediocre speakers (10–20W, downward-firing). TV manufacturers sacrifice audio for thin bezels. A $200 soundbar dramatically outperforms any TV's built-in audio.

**Can a soundbar replace a full surround sound system?**
For most people, yes. A quality 2.1 or 3.1 soundbar with a subwoofer delivers 90% of the home theater experience at 20% of the cost and complexity. Only serious audiophiles or dedicated home theater rooms benefit from full 5.1/7.1 receiver setups.

**Where should I place a soundbar?**
Centered below the TV, pointed at the listening position. Front-facing is better than angled. The soundbar should not be inside a closed TV cabinet — it needs open air projection.

**What's the difference between 2.1, 3.1, and 5.1 soundbars?**
The numbers refer to channels: main speakers (first number) + subwoofer (second number). 2.1 = left + right speakers + sub. 3.1 adds a center channel for dialogue. 5.1 adds rear surround speakers. More channels = more immersive, but diminishing returns after 3.1 for typical room setups.
