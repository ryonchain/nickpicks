## Smartwatch Buying Guide: What to Look for Under $200

### Operating System
**watchOS** works only with iPhone but delivers the most polished app ecosystem. **Wear OS** (Samsung, Google) works best with Android but supports iPhone with limitations. **Proprietary OS** (Garmin, Fitbit, Amazfit) typically offers longer battery life but fewer third-party apps. Choose based on your phone ecosystem first.

### Battery Life
Battery life varies wildly in this price range: 1.5 days to 14 days. If you want sleep tracking (highly recommended for health insights), you need a watch that either charges fast enough to top up during the day, or has enough capacity to last through the night without charging.

### Health Sensors
Essential sensors at this price point: heart rate, SpO2 (blood oxygen), GPS. Nice-to-have: ECG, skin temperature, EDA (stress). Watch the marketing carefully — "100+ health metrics" often means the same few sensors measuring different things.

### Display Quality
AMOLED displays are significantly more vibrant and easier to read than LCD. Always-on display is useful in day-to-day life but cuts battery life dramatically (often by 50%). Look for at least 300+ nits of brightness for outdoor visibility.

### Water Resistance
For swimming, you need at least 5 ATM water resistance. For showering only, IP68 is sufficient. Don't assume any rating — check the specific model's spec sheet.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the best smartwatch under $200 for Android users?**
The Samsung Galaxy Watch FE is the best overall for Android, with full Wear OS support, Google Pay, and excellent Samsung Health tracking. The Garmin Venu Sq 2 is better if fitness tracking is your top priority.

**Can I use an Apple Watch with an Android phone?**
No. Apple Watches require an iPhone to set up and function. All notification, health syncing, and app features require iOS. If you use Android, look at Samsung Galaxy Watch, Garmin, or Amazfit instead.

**How accurate is smartwatch heart rate monitoring?**
Optical heart rate sensors in wrist-worn smartwatches are generally accurate within 2–5 BPM during steady-state activity (walking, running). They become less accurate during high-intensity interval training or exercises with significant wrist movement. For clinical-grade accuracy, a chest strap HR monitor is required.

**Do smartwatches work without a phone?**
Basic functionality (steps, time, notifications if connected) works without a phone nearby, but you'll need your phone for setup, GPS accuracy, and app syncing. LTE-enabled watches (typically $100+ more) can stream music and take calls independently.

**How long do smartwatches last before they become obsolete?**
Most smartwatches receive OS updates and feature support for 3–5 years. Hardware-wise, battery degradation is the main concern — after 2–3 years of daily charging, most batteries will hold noticeably less charge. Budget models from less established brands may see shorter software support windows.
