## Powered vs. Passive Bookshelf Speakers

### Powered (Active) Speakers
Built-in amplifier — connect directly to audio source. Simpler setup.
- **Best for:** Desk use, plug-and-play simplicity, Bluetooth streaming
- **Drawbacks:** Can't upgrade the amp; internal amp quality varies
- **Budget range:** $80–$500

### Passive Speakers
No built-in amp — require a separate stereo receiver or amplifier.
- **Best for:** Music enthusiasts, home theater front channels, long-term systems
- **Drawbacks:** Additional cost for receiver; more cables and components
- **Budget range:** $70–$500+ (speakers only)

**For most beginners:** Powered speakers (Edifier, Audioengine). For music enthusiasts: passive speakers with a quality receiver (Yamaha RS202, Denon PMA-600NE).

## Placement Matters as Much as the Speakers

- **Tweeters at ear level** when seated — most important placement rule
- **Equal distance from each wall** for stereo imaging (both speakers same distance from side walls)
- **6–12 inches from rear wall** for most speakers (more for rear-ported designs)
- **Toe-in toward listening position** slightly (angled 15–25° inward) improves center image

A great speaker on a bad placement sounds worse than a mediocre speaker placed correctly.

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need a subwoofer with bookshelf speakers?**
A: For music: often no, if the woofer reaches below 60Hz (Klipsch RP-600M II, Q Acoustics 3020i). For movies: yes — the low frequencies in film soundtracks require a dedicated subwoofer.

**Q: What amplifier should I pair with passive speakers?**
A: Entry-level: Yamaha RS202 ($150) or Sony STRDH190 ($130). Mid-range: NAD D3020 ($400) or Denon PMA-600NE ($300). Match amplifier watts to speaker sensitivity — high-sensitivity speakers (like Klipsch) sound good with low-powered amps.

**Q: Can bookshelf speakers be used for home theater?**
A: Yes. A pair of bookshelf speakers as front L/R channels is the foundation of a good stereo or 5.1 system. Add a center channel speaker and subwoofer to expand.

**Q: What's the difference in sound between $100 and $500 speakers?**
A: At $100, you get good frequency coverage with somewhat compressed dynamics and imaging. At $300–500, imaging expands (instruments have defined positions in 3D space), detail in complex passages improves, and the sound becomes less fatiguing at high volumes.
