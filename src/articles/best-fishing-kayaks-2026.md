## Fishing Kayak Buying Guide

### Sit-On-Top vs. Sit-Inside

**Sit-on-top (SOT):** The overwhelming choice for fishing — more stable, easier to re-enter from the water, better for standing, and self-draining. Self-rescue is simpler.

**Sit-inside:** Better in cold weather (skirt keeps you dry), more efficient paddling, but harder to fish from and less stable for casting.

### Paddle vs. Pedal Drive

**Paddle kayaks:** Lower cost, lighter, simpler. Hands free for short periods only.

**Pedal drive:** Hands always free for fishing. Covers more water, less fatiguing. More expensive ($400–800 premium), heavier, and can't access very shallow water.

### Stability

Measured in two ways:
- **Primary stability:** How stable the kayak feels when you first sit down
- **Secondary stability:** How stable when you lean to the edge (important for fighting large fish)

Fishing kayaks prioritize primary stability for confident fishing and standing. Wider hulls (30"+) are most stable; narrower hulls are faster.

### Weight Capacity

Calculate: Your body weight + gear weight + fish (tournament anglers add an extra 50–100 lbs here). Buy a kayak rated at least 100 lbs above your realistic loaded weight.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is kayak fishing dangerous?**
With proper safety gear (PFD, leash, communication device) and appropriate conditions, kayak fishing is safe for most adults. Don't fish in high wind or strong current until you're comfortable with your kayak's handling.

**What's the best kayak for fishing in the ocean?**
For open ocean/nearshore fishing, look for 12'+ length for better tracking, a pedal drive for hands-free maneuvering, and bright colors for visibility. Consider a sit-on-top with self-rescue skills.

**Do I need a fishing license for kayak fishing?**
Yes — a fishing kayak doesn't exempt you from state fishing license requirements. Check your state's fish and wildlife agency for current requirements.
