## Why Cast Iron Still Beats Nonstick

A good nonstick pan lasts 2–3 years before the coating degrades. A cast iron skillet, cared for properly, outlasts the cook by generations. The Lodge pan on Amazon's bestseller list has 70,000+ five-star reviews because the same basic design has worked for 130 years.

Beyond longevity, cast iron has cooking properties no nonstick can match:
- **Searing:** Cast iron retains heat better than stainless steel, producing restaurant-quality crust on steak and chicken
- **Oven-safe at any temperature:** No plastic handles, no coating that degrades above 400°F — goes from stovetop to 600°F oven without issue
- **Gets better with use:** Each time you cook with fat in cast iron, the polymerized oil layer (seasoning) gets thicker and more nonstick

The tradeoffs: cast iron is heavy (5–8 lbs for a 10-inch pan), reacts with acidic foods before it's fully seasoned, and takes longer to heat up than thin stainless steel. For anyone willing to work within those constraints, no pan produces better food.

## Buying Guide

**10-inch vs. 12-inch.** A 10-inch skillet handles eggs, a two-person steak, cornbread, and most everyday tasks. A 12-inch handles larger proteins and family-size cooking but weighs 7+ lbs. Start with 10-inch unless you cook for four or more regularly.

**How to season cast iron.** Rinse new pan, dry completely on the stovetop over medium heat, apply a thin coat of vegetable oil or flaxseed oil to all surfaces, wipe almost completely off with a clean cloth (too much oil causes sticky buildup), then bake upside-down at 450°F for one hour. Repeat twice before first cook. After that, cooking with fat is sufficient maintenance.

**What to avoid.** Soap (occasional light use is fine; avoid scrubbing with it), soaking in water, cooking highly acidic foods before the pan is well-seasoned, and putting a hot pan in cold water (warping risk).

**The rust question.** Surface rust happens when cast iron stays wet. It's easily fixed with steel wool to remove rust, re-seasoning with oil, and more careful drying going forward. Rust does not ruin cast iron.

## FAQ

**Can I use cast iron on an induction cooktop?**
Yes. Cast iron is magnetic and works on all induction surfaces. The flat bottom contact is important — older, warped cast iron may not work on flat induction cooktops.

**How do I clean cast iron without soap?**
Rinse with hot water, scrub with a Lodge chain mail scrubber or plastic scrub brush, dry immediately over medium heat on the stovetop, then apply a thin coat of oil while still warm. Wipe excess before storing.

**Why does my cast iron stick?**
Common causes: insufficient seasoning, cooking too high heat too fast (cold food hitting a barely-warm pan), or cooking on a pebbly surface before it's fully built up. Solution: cook at medium heat with adequate fat until seasoning develops.

**Is cast iron safe for everyday use?**
Yes. The small amount of iron that transfers to food is generally below concern levels and some argue it benefits people who are iron-deficient. PFOA and PTFE (teflon chemicals) are not present.
