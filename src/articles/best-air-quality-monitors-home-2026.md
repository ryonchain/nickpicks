## Why Indoor Air Quality Monitoring Matters

Indoor air is typically 2–5 times more polluted than outdoor air, according to EPA data. Cooking, cleaning products, furniture off-gassing, pets, and poor ventilation all degrade the air you breathe at home. Monitoring the key pollutants tells you when to open windows, when to run an air purifier, and whether there are chronic sources of contamination. Key pollutants to track:

**PM2.5 (fine particulate matter):** Particles under 2.5 microns penetrate deep into lung tissue. Sources include cooking smoke, candles, wildfires, and outdoor traffic. The most important pollutant to monitor for respiratory health.

**VOCs (volatile organic compounds):** Emitted by cleaning products, paints, furniture, and adhesives. Chronic VOC exposure is linked to respiratory irritation and headaches. Spike during cleaning sessions or after furniture delivery.

**CO2:** High indoor CO2 (above 1,000 ppm) degrades cognitive performance, causes fatigue, and indicates insufficient ventilation. Open-plan offices and bedrooms with doors closed are common problem areas.

**Radon:** Radioactive gas that seeps from soil and accumulates in basements and lower floors. Second leading cause of lung cancer after smoking. Requires dedicated radon sensor — most basic monitors don't include it.

**Humidity:** Relative humidity above 60% promotes mold growth; below 30% causes dry skin, static, and respiratory irritation. Optimal range: 40–50%.

## Acting on Your Air Quality Data

**High PM2.5 during cooking:** Turn on the range hood at the highest setting before cooking. Even gas cooking generates PM2.5 spikes. Opening a kitchen window helps significantly if outdoor air is clean.

**High CO2 in bedrooms:** CO2 rises above 1,000 ppm in closed bedrooms after 2–3 hours of sleep. A small fan moving air under a closed door, or cracking the window slightly, reduces CO2 accumulation that causes next-morning grogginess.

**VOC spikes after cleaning:** Most household cleaners, including "natural" options, spike VOC readings for 1–2 hours after use. Ventilate immediately and let readings return to baseline before closing windows.

**Using data to run air purifiers:** Pair your monitor with a smart air purifier (Coway, Winix, IQAir, Blueair) and set automations to turn the purifier on when PM2.5 exceeds 12 µg/m³. This eliminates constant manual management.

**Radon action level:** EPA recommends taking action when radon levels exceed 4 pCi/L. Sub-slab depressurization systems (installed by radon professionals) reduce levels to under 2 pCi/L in most cases.
