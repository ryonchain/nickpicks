## Buying Guide: What to Look for in a Home Air Quality Monitor

### Which Pollutants Matter Most?

**PM2.5** (fine particulate matter) is the single most important pollutant for respiratory health. Every home should track it — especially during wildfire season or in urban areas.

**CO2** levels above 1,000 ppm cause measurable cognitive decline. High CO2 is a sign of poor ventilation and is often the reason office meetings leave everyone feeling sluggish.

**VOCs** (volatile organic compounds) off-gas from paint, furniture, cleaning products, and flooring. They're hard to eliminate but easy to detect — and ventilating when levels spike is the immediate fix.

**Radon** is an odorless gas that causes lung cancer — the second-leading cause after smoking. If you live in a radon-prone region, it's worth monitoring. The Airthings View Plus is the only monitor on this list with a radon sensor.

**Formaldehyde** matters most in newly built or renovated homes. New furniture, flooring, and insulation off-gas formaldehyde for months. The Temtop M2000C is the best tool for this specific need.

### Display vs. App-Only

Monitors with built-in screens give you at-a-glance readings without pulling out your phone. App-only models (like the Awair) are better for smart home automation but require your phone to check current levels.

### Accuracy: Laser vs. NDIR Sensors

For CO2, NDIR (non-dispersive infrared) sensors are significantly more accurate than "equivalent CO2" estimation via TVOC sensors. If CO2 matters to you — for ventilation, productivity, or sleep quality — look for NDIR explicitly.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is a healthy indoor AQI level?**
AQI 0–50 is "Good" — the EPA's target for indoor air. 51–100 is "Moderate" — acceptable for most people. Above 100, sensitive groups (children, elderly, people with asthma) should take action to improve ventilation or filter.

**Do air quality monitors detect carbon monoxide (CO)?**
Most consumer air quality monitors do not detect CO — that requires a separate CO alarm (which is also a legal requirement in most US states). CO monitors and AQI monitors are different devices.

**How do I improve air quality at home?**
Open windows when outdoor AQI permits, run an air purifier with a HEPA filter, change HVAC filters every 90 days, and ventilate when cooking. The monitor tells you when — the purifier, ventilation, and habits fix it.

**Where should I place an air quality monitor?**
Breathing-zone height (4–5 feet) in the room where you spend the most time — usually the bedroom or living room. Avoid direct sunlight and placement near cooking appliances, which will skew readings.

**Do air quality monitors require calibration?**
Consumer monitors don't require user calibration but do drift over time. Most are accurate to spec for 2–3 years before sensors degrade. Professional and industrial monitors require periodic recalibration.
