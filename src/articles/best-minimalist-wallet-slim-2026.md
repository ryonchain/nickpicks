## Why Most Men Are Switching to Minimalist Wallets

The bifold wallet has been the default for 80 years. It works, but most men are carrying 3–5 cards regularly and filling the remaining slots with old receipts, cards they never use, and loyalty cards for stores they've visited twice. The result: a wallet that's thicker than it needs to be, sits uncomfortably in a back pocket, and causes posture problems for people who sit on it eight hours a day.

Minimalist wallets solve the actual problem: carry the cards and cash you actually need, in the smallest possible package, in a front pocket.

**What to look for:**
- **Card capacity:** 4–8 cards covers most use cases; anything over 12 defeats the minimalist purpose
- **Cash handling:** Some designs have no cash slot; assess honestly how often you use cash
- **RFID blocking:** Prevents contactless card readers from skimming cards in your pocket
- **Front pocket fit:** The point of going minimalist — shouldn't bulge visibly through pants
- **Material:** Aluminum, titanium, carbon fiber, or quality leather; avoid low-grade plastics

## Buying Guide

**How many cards do you actually carry?** Before buying, empty your current wallet and count active cards. Most people carry 2–4 regularly, 2–3 rarely, and 3+ "just in case" cards they haven't used in months. A 6-card capacity covers 95% of real-world use.

**Cash vs. cashless.** If you regularly pay cash at farmers markets, parking meters, or local restaurants, make sure your wallet has a cash system you'll actually use. The Ridge's exterior strap works but is awkward for thick bills. Bellroy's interior cash pocket is more natural.

**RFID blocking: necessary or marketing?**  Contactless card skimming in public spaces does happen, particularly in crowded international transit. RFID blocking is a $0 addition on well-designed metal wallets and minor peace of mind on leather alternatives. It's not critical in suburban US daily life, but it doesn't add bulk so there's no reason not to have it.

**Front pocket vs. back pocket.** The entire value proposition of a slim wallet is front pocket carry — it's more secure, more ergonomic (sitting on a wallet causes lumbar misalignment over time), and prevents the rear pocket bulge that looks unprofessional. If you'll still carry it in your back pocket, you haven't gained much by going minimalist.

## FAQ

**What's the thinnest wallet available?**
The Ridge Wallet at 6 cards runs about 15mm. The Secrid Cardprotector alone (no outer leather) runs 8mm for 4–6 cards. Single-sleeve card holders (titanium or carbon fiber plate holders) can hit 3–5mm for 2–3 cards — functional but limited.

**Can I use a minimalist wallet as a travel wallet?**
Yes, with planning. Most minimalist wallets hold a passport card (same size as a credit card) but not a full passport booklet. For international travel, pair a minimalist wallet with a travel document holder worn under clothing.

**How do I transition from a full bifold to a minimalist wallet?**
Spend one week carrying only the cards you actually use. Most people discover they use 3–5 regularly. Remove the rest. You're ready for a minimalist wallet.

**Are minimalist wallets airport security-friendly?**
Yes. Metal wallets (Ridge, Secrid) should go in the bin through security like any metal item. They don't trigger body scanners differently than keys or a phone.
