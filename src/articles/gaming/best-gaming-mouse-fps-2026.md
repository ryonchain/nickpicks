## Buying Guide

### Sensor: What Matters for FPS

Modern flagship sensors (HERO 25K, Focus Pro 30K, PixArt 3610) are all essentially perfect for FPS at standard DPI settings (400–1600). The differences between them are imperceptible in real gameplay. What to actually evaluate:

- **Acceleration:** Any acceleration (intentional or unintentional) means aim is inconsistent at different speeds. All five picks here have zero acceleration.
- **IPS (inches per second):** How fast you can flick before the sensor loses tracking. 200+ IPS covers any realistic human movement speed. 750 IPS (Razer Focus Pro) handles extreme flick shots.
- **LOD (lift-off distance):** How high you can lift the mouse before tracking stops. Lower is better for low-sensitivity players who frequently reposition.

### DPI: What Settings Are Right for FPS

- **Competitive FPS (CS2, Valorant, Apex Legends):** 400–800 DPI is standard for accurate tracking at lower sensitivity. Lower DPI rewards muscle memory and reduces jitter.
- **Fast-paced battle royale:** 800–1600 DPI is common — easier reorientation, some accuracy tradeoff.
- **Casual/higher sensitivity:** 1600–3200 DPI. Faster cursor movement, higher margin for imprecision.

Don't chase high DPI numbers. 400 DPI with a high-quality sensor outperforms 12,000 DPI on a cheap sensor every time.

### Weight: Do You Need Ultralight?

Ultralight mice (sub-70g) reduce arm fatigue during long sessions and improve micro-adjustment speed for flick shots. The difference is most noticeable when comparing a 120g mouse to a 60g mouse — less so between 70g and 65g. Unless you're grinding 6+ hours daily, any mouse under 90g is fine.

### Wired vs. Wireless in 2026

2.4GHz wireless from premium brands (Logitech LIGHTSPEED, Razer HyperSpeed, SteelSeries 2.4GHz) is indistinguishable from wired in terms of latency. The only practical reason to choose wired:
- Budget constraint (good wireless starts at $50–$80)
- Tournament rules requiring wired peripherals (rare in 2026)
- Battery anxiety

For home gaming, wireless is the better experience. For competitive LAN events, wired eliminates any variable.

## FAQ

### What DPI should I use for FPS games?

Most competitive FPS players use 400–800 DPI. Lower DPI requires larger mouse movements (lower sensitivity setting in-game), which improves consistency and reduces small unintentional movements. Experiment with 400 DPI at 2.0 in-game sensitivity and adjust from there — don't start at 1600 DPI and wonder why your aim is inconsistent.

### Does mouse shape matter for FPS?

Yes significantly. Mouse shape determines grip consistency — if your hand doesn't settle into the same position every time you pick up the mouse, your aim point shifts. Ergonomic right-hand shapes (DeathAdder, Zowie EC) suit palm and claw grips for medium-large hands. Ambidextrous shapes (G Pro Superlight, Aerox 3) suit claw and fingertip grips across hand sizes.

### Is wireless mouse lag a real problem for competitive FPS?

With 2.4GHz wireless from premium brands, no. LIGHTSPEED, HyperSpeed, and SteelSeries wireless all achieve 1ms or lower latency — identical to wired. The concern was valid with early Bluetooth gaming mice. In 2026, only Bluetooth wireless (10–15ms) is problematic for competitive gaming.

### How long do gaming mouse switches last?

Premium switches (Omron, Huano, LIGHTFORCE optical) are rated for 50–90 million clicks. At 500 clicks per hour of gaming, 50 million clicks = 100,000 hours of gaming. Practically, mice fail from other causes (cable fraying, scroll wheel wear) before switches reach their rated lifespan.

### Should I get a mouse bungee?

A mouse bungee keeps the USB cable elevated and tension-free, simulating wireless performance with wired mice. If you're using a wired mouse like the Zowie EC2-C for competitive gaming, a bungee is a $15 quality-of-life upgrade worth having.
