## Buying Guide

### Resolution vs. GPU Pairing

Don't buy a monitor your GPU can't drive:
- **1080p 144–280Hz:** GTX 1660 Super / RX 5500 XT and above
- **1440p 144–165Hz:** RTX 3060 / RX 6600 XT and above
- **1440p 240Hz:** RTX 3070 / RX 6700 XT and above
- **4K 60–120Hz:** RTX 3080 / RX 6900 XT and above
- **4K 144Hz:** RTX 4080 / RX 7900 XTX and above

Buying a 4K 144Hz monitor with a mid-range GPU results in playing at 1440p or lower anyway — you're paying for resolution you can't use.

### Refresh Rate: What You'll Actually Notice

- **60Hz → 144Hz:** Enormous, immediately perceptible difference. Do this upgrade first.
- **144Hz → 240Hz:** Meaningful for competitive FPS; less noticeable in casual gaming.
- **240Hz → 360Hz:** Only detectable under controlled conditions and at professional skill levels.

### Panel Types: Simplified

- **IPS:** Best color accuracy and viewing angles. Moderate contrast. Best all-rounder for mixed gaming/productivity.
- **VA:** Best contrast (better dark scenes). Slower response times (some smearing in fast motion). Best for single-player and console gaming.
- **TN:** Fastest response, worst color, narrow viewing angles. Only for hardcore competitive FPS where response time is the only priority.
- **OLED:** Perfect contrast, near-zero response. Burn-in risk. Best for cinematic gaming.

### G-Sync vs. FreeSync

Both G-Sync and FreeSync reduce tearing and stuttering by syncing monitor refresh to GPU output. Key differences:
- **G-Sync (hardware module):** NVIDIA-exclusive, typically ~$100 premium over comparable FreeSync monitors. Higher certification standards.
- **G-Sync Compatible:** NVIDIA certifies certain FreeSync monitors to work without hardware module. Performance is usually equivalent.
- **FreeSync Premium Pro:** AMD certification tier. Works with most modern NVIDIA cards via G-Sync Compatible as well.

Practical advice: G-Sync Compatible FreeSync monitors work fine with NVIDIA cards. Don't pay a premium for hardware G-Sync unless you've specifically verified a technical need.

## FAQ

### Is 4K worth it for gaming in 2026?

For single-player cinematic games (open world, narrative-driven) at a typical 27–32" screen distance: yes, the sharpness improvement over 1440p is visible and meaningful. For competitive multiplayer gaming: 1440p or 1080p at higher refresh rates is a better tradeoff. 4K requires significantly more GPU power to maintain playable frame rates.

### What's the best monitor size for gaming?

27" is the sweet spot for desktop gaming at a typical 24"–30" viewing distance. 24"–25" is better for competitive FPS (easier to track targets at periphery). 32" works well for console gaming from a couch or for curved ultrawide setups.

### Do I need HDR in a gaming monitor?

DisplayHDR 400 (most gaming monitors): essentially no perceptible HDR benefit. DisplayHDR 600+: minor improvement in HDR scenes. Mini-LED or OLED with high local dimming: genuine HDR benefit visible in dark scene performance. Budget-to-mid-range gaming monitors should ignore HDR specs — they're marketing.

### Can I use a gaming monitor with PS5 or Xbox Series X?

Yes. For full 4K@120Hz support on PS5/Xbox, the monitor must have HDMI 2.1 ports. Many gaming monitors use HDMI 2.0 (max 4K@60Hz or 1440p@120Hz). Check the HDMI spec carefully before buying for console use.
