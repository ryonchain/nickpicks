## Buying Guide

### Powered vs. Bus-Powered Hubs

This is the most important decision:

**Bus-powered hubs** draw power from the USB port on your PC or laptop. Total available power is 4.5W (USB 3.0 standard) to 7.5W (USB 3.1) — shared across all ports. Suitable for: keyboards, mice, webcams, headsets, low-power accessories. Not suitable for: external hard drives (need 900mA+), charging phones at full speed, powering LED strips.

**Externally powered hubs** come with their own power adapter. Each port can deliver full power independently. Suitable for: everything, including power-hungry devices. Required for: any gaming desk with 5+ simultaneous devices or any single device that draws significant current (external HDDs, game capture cards, streaming lights).

**Rule:** If you have 4 or more devices and any of them need charging or draw significant power, get an externally powered hub.

### USB 3.0 vs. USB 3.2 Gen 2: Does Speed Matter?

- **USB 3.0 (5Gbps):** Handles 625 MB/s theoretical — sufficient for keyboards, mice, webcams (up to 4K), most gaming peripherals, and external HDDs.
- **USB 3.2 Gen 2 (10Gbps):** Handles 1,250 MB/s theoretical — needed for external NVMe SSDs, 8K webcams, fast capture cards.

For standard gaming peripherals: 5Gbps is sufficient. If you're transferring game clips from an external NVMe or running high-bitrate capture, 10Gbps matters.

### Bandwidth Sharing: The Hidden Problem

Most USB hubs connect all ports to a single USB controller — meaning all ports share bandwidth. A 10-port USB 3.0 hub often connects through a single 5Gbps USB link, giving each port an effective 500Mbps max when all ports are in use. For gaming peripherals (mouse, keyboard, headset), this doesn't matter — they use minimal bandwidth. For data transfer alongside gaming, look for hubs with multiple USB controllers or confirm the host connection bandwidth.

### Wireless Dongle Interference

USB 3.0 hubs emit RF interference in the 2.4GHz range, which affects wireless dongles (mouse, keyboard, headset) connected nearby. Best practice: plug wireless dongles directly into the PC's rear USB ports, not into a hub. Run wired peripherals through the hub. If you must use a dongle on a hub, use a short USB extension cable to move the dongle away from the hub's circuit board.

### USB-C vs. USB-A Host Connection

The hub connects to your PC via either USB-A or USB-C (from the PC side):
- **USB-A:** Connects to USB-A port on PC — universal but limited to USB 3.0 bandwidth (5Gbps total through that link)
- **USB-C:** Connects to USB-C port on PC — can use USB 3.2 Gen 2 (10Gbps) or Thunderbolt 3/4 (40Gbps) bandwidth, enabling higher aggregate speed

If your PC has Thunderbolt 3/4 or USB4 ports, use a Thunderbolt-compatible hub for maximum bandwidth.

## FAQ

### Can I plug my gaming mouse into a USB hub?

Yes. Gaming mice use minimal bandwidth (well under 1Mbps) and don't require dedicated USB ports. However, wireless dongles (for wireless mice) should be plugged directly into the PC's rear USB ports to avoid 2.4GHz interference from the hub's circuitry.

### Will a USB hub cause input lag on my keyboard or mouse?

No — peripheral input data is transmitted over USB in microseconds and uses negligible bandwidth. The USB hub adds no perceptible latency for gaming peripherals. Input lag concerns apply to display connections and audio, not USB HID devices (keyboard, mouse, controller).

### Do I need a powered hub for a gaming setup?

If you're connecting 5+ devices, charging a phone, using external storage, or running LED controllers and capture cards — yes, get a powered hub. For simpler setups (keyboard, mouse, headset, controller), a quality bus-powered hub works fine.

### Can I use two USB hubs to expand further?

Daisy-chaining two USB hubs reduces available bandwidth and increases the risk of device drop-outs. The USB specification allows chaining up to 7 tiers, but in practice two bus-powered hubs in series is where problems start. Better solution: connect each hub to a separate USB port on your PC's rear I/O panel, giving each its own bandwidth allocation.

### How do I know if my USB hub is causing problems?

Common symptoms of a failing or overloaded USB hub: devices drop off randomly, controller disconnects during gaming, charging speeds are slow, USB audio cuts out. Test by temporarily connecting devices directly to the PC's rear ports — if problems disappear, the hub is the cause.
