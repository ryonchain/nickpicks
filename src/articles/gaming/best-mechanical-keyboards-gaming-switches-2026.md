## Buying Guide

### Switch Type: The Most Important Decision

**Linear switches** (Red, Speed Silver, OmniPoint): Smooth keypress with no tactile bump. Most popular for gaming — especially FPS — because there's no resistance mid-travel. Quieter than clicky. Best for: gaming-first users, office environments, fast typists.

**Tactile switches** (Brown, Aqua, G Pro Tactile): Physical bump at actuation point without audible click. Balance between gaming and typing. Best for: mixed work/gaming users, those who want feedback without noise.

**Clicky switches** (Blue, Green): Audible click + tactile bump at actuation. Most satisfying typing feel. Not ideal for gaming — the tactile bump creates resistance. Best for: typists who game occasionally, users in private spaces.

**For competitive gaming: linear every time.** Speed Silver (Cherry) or OmniPoint (SteelSeries) for fastest actuation. Red switches for comfortable all-day play.

### Form Factor: How Much Space Do You Have?

- **Full-size (100%):** Numpad included. Takes most desk space. Best for: work + gaming, spreadsheets, games that use numpad binds.
- **Tenkeyless (TKL / 80%):** No numpad. Most popular for gaming — more mouse room, easier transport. Best for: FPS, battle royale, esports.
- **75% / 65%:** Compressed layouts with function row or arrow key modifications. Best for: small desks, travel.
- **60%:** No function row or arrow keys. Compact but steep learning curve. Best for: experienced users with space constraints.

### Polling Rate: Does 8,000Hz Matter?

At 1,000Hz (1ms response), inputs are imperceptible for most gameplay. At 8,000Hz (0.125ms), the theoretical improvement exists but is only measurable in controlled conditions at the highest competitive levels. For ranked competitive gaming: 8,000Hz is a marginal but real edge. For everyone else: 1,000Hz is sufficient. Don't pay a significant premium for 8,000Hz unless you're already at the ceiling of other hardware.

### Hot-Swap: Worth It?

Hot-swap lets you change switches without soldering. If you're new to mechanical keyboards and unsure which switch you prefer, hot-swap keyboards let you experiment without commitment. The Logitech G Pro X and Keychron Q5 both support hot-swap. Worth paying for if you're switch-curious.

## FAQ

### What switches are best for FPS gaming?

Linear switches with low actuation force and short travel are optimal for FPS. Cherry MX Speed Silver (1.2mm actuation), SteelSeries OmniPoint 2.0 (adjustable to 0.2mm), and Gateron Yellow (lighter than Red) are all top choices. Avoid tactile or clicky switches — the bump creates resistance that slows rapid keypresses.

### Is a mechanical keyboard worth it over membrane?

For gaming, yes. Mechanical switches are faster, more accurate, and last longer (50–100 million keystrokes vs. 5–10 million for membrane). The tactile or linear feedback reduces missed inputs. For casual gaming, membrane works fine — but once you switch to mechanical, returning to membrane feels like typing through a pillow.

### What's the difference between TKL and full-size for gaming?

TKL (tenkeyless) removes the numpad, freeing roughly 3 inches of desk space for mouse movement. Most competitive FPS and battle royale players prefer TKL or smaller — more mouse room directly improves aim at low sensitivity. Full-size is better for productivity, strategy games, and users with larger desks.

### Does RGB lighting affect gaming performance?

No. RGB is purely cosmetic. Some keyboards offer software features (like key lighting for in-game health indicators) but these are gimmicks for most players. RGB does affect battery life on wireless keyboards — if you're on wireless, turning off RGB extends battery by 30–50%.

### Are wireless mechanical keyboards viable for competitive gaming?

Yes, in 2026. 2.4GHz wireless from major brands (SteelSeries, Logitech, Corsair) delivers sub-1ms latency — identical to wired in practice. The SteelSeries Apex Pro wireless and Logitech G915 are proven competitive wireless options. Bluetooth adds 10–15ms latency and is not suitable for competitive play.
