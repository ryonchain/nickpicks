## Buying Guide

### 1080p vs. 1440p: Which Is Right for You?

**Choose 1080p if:**
- You have a mid-range GPU (RTX 4060 Ti or below) — 1080p lets you hit higher frame rates
- You play competitive titles (CS2, Valorant) where 240fps matters more than pixel density
- Budget is under $200 and you want to maximize refresh rate
- Your monitor is 24 inches or smaller — 1080p looks fine at 24 inches

**Choose 1440p if:**
- You have a mid-high or high-end GPU (RTX 4070+, RX 7700 XT+)
- You play single-player, RPG, or open-world games where image quality matters
- Your monitor is 27 inches — 1080p looks soft at 27 inches, 1440p is noticeably sharper
- You use the monitor for work alongside gaming

### Refresh Rate: 60Hz vs. 144Hz vs. 165Hz

The jump from 60Hz to 144Hz is the most impactful refresh rate upgrade you can make — motion clarity difference is immediately obvious. The jump from 144Hz to 165Hz is marginal. The jump from 165Hz to 240Hz is real but less impactful than 60→144Hz. Budget priority: 144Hz minimum for gaming. 165Hz is a bonus, not a necessity.

### Panel Type: TN vs. IPS vs. VA

- **TN:** Fastest response (1ms GtG), worst colors and viewing angles, lowest cost. Best for: competitive gaming at 1080p.
- **IPS:** Balanced response (1–4ms GtG), good colors and viewing angles, most popular for gaming. Best for: most gaming scenarios.
- **VA:** High contrast ratios (3000:1 vs. IPS's 1000:1), slower response (5ms GtG typical), ghosting in dark fast motion. Best for: HDR content, dark environments.

For most gamers in 2026: IPS is the right choice. VA ghosting is notable in dark fast-action games. TN is only justified for ultra-competitive 1080p gaming.

### Response Time Marketing: GtG vs. MPRT

- **GtG (gray-to-gray):** The true pixel response time — how long it takes a pixel to switch color. Lower is better.
- **MPRT (moving picture response time):** Uses backlight strobing to create perceived sharpness. Not a true pixel response metric.

Marketing often shows MPRT specs because they're lower numbers. Always look for GtG response time for accurate comparison.

## FAQ

### What resolution is best for gaming on a budget?

1440p at 27 inches is the sweet spot in 2026. GPU prices have normalized, and mid-range GPUs handle 1440p at 100+ fps in most titles. If your GPU can't sustain 60fps at 1440p, drop to 1080p on the same monitor — you'll still benefit from the sharper base resolution over a native 1080p panel.

### Is adaptive sync (FreeSync/G-Sync) necessary?

For any monitor below 240Hz, yes. Adaptive sync eliminates screen tearing and reduces stutter without the input latency of VSync. G-Sync Compatible monitors work with NVIDIA GPUs. FreeSync monitors work with AMD GPUs. Most modern FreeSync monitors are G-Sync Compatible. All five picks here support adaptive sync.

### How do I connect a gaming monitor to my GPU?

Use DisplayPort 1.4 for 1440p at 165Hz+ — it carries higher bandwidth than HDMI 2.0. HDMI 2.0 supports 1440p at 144Hz. For PS5 or Xbox, use HDMI 2.1 on supported monitors (most budget monitors don't have HDMI 2.1 — check before buying for console use).

### Does monitor brand matter?

LG, BenQ, AOC, Gigabyte, and ViewSonic all produce reliable panels. BenQ Zowie has the strongest reputation for competitive gaming products. LG Nano IPS panels are consistently excellent. AOC and Gigabyte offer strong value. Avoid off-brand budget monitors — panel quality variance is high and returns are painful.

### Should I buy a 24-inch or 27-inch gaming monitor?

At 1080p: 24-inch for sharper pixel density. At 1440p: 27-inch is the native sweet spot. For competitive FPS players, 24-inch at 1080p is preferred — it's closer to the center of your field of view, requiring less eye movement to see all corners. For single-player and mixed-use: 27-inch 1440p wins on visual experience.
