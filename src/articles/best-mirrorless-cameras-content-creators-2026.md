## Mirrorless Camera Buying Guide for Beginners

### APS-C vs Full Frame: The Right Call for Beginners

Full-frame cameras (Sony A7 series, Canon R6, Nikon Z6) have larger sensors that perform better in low light and produce shallower depth of field. But they're heavier, more expensive, and their lenses cost significantly more. For beginners and content creators, APS-C is the better starting point in 2026 — the image quality gap vs full-frame has almost closed, and the ecosystem cost advantage is substantial.

### In-Body Image Stabilization (IBIS): Does It Matter?

For photography: less critical — cameras with fast shutter speeds freeze motion regardless of stabilization. For video: very important. IBIS eliminates the micro-jitter of handheld video that makes footage look amateurish. If video is a primary use case, prioritize cameras with IBIS (Sony a6700, Fujifilm X-S20). If budget is a constraint, combine a camera without IBIS with a gimbal for professional-looking video.

### Lens Investment: The Long-Term Cost

Camera bodies are replaced every 3–5 years. Lenses last decades. The lens ecosystem matters more than any single camera feature. Canon's RF mount and Sony's E-mount have the largest third-party lens libraries. Fujifilm's X-mount has a carefully curated first-party library. Nikon's Z-mount is growing rapidly. Choose your mount thoughtfully — it determines your lens options for years.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need to shoot RAW as a beginner?**
No. RAW gives maximum editing flexibility but requires processing in Lightroom or similar software before sharing. JPEGs from these cameras are excellent — especially Fujifilm's film simulations. Start with JPEG, learn the camera, and move to RAW when you start wanting more editing control.

**What lens should I buy first?**
The kit lens (18-45mm or 16-50mm equivalent depending on brand) is the right starting point. It covers everyday focal lengths and is optically good enough to learn on. After 6 months, you'll know whether you want a faster prime (portrait/low light) or a longer zoom (sports/wildlife).

**How much should I spend on a first mirrorless camera?**
$700–$900 is the sweet spot in 2026. The Canon R50 and Nikon Z30 at $760 deliver genuine professional-quality results. Spending less gets you older sensors or significantly reduced features; spending more at the beginner stage means paying for features you won't use for another year.
