## Choosing the Right Pull-Up Bar Setup

### Installation Types Compared

| Type | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|------|------|------|---------|
| Doorframe (no drill) | No damage, portable | Limited weight, one position | Renters, apartments |
| Ceiling Mount | High clearance | Requires joist | Basements, garages |
| Wall Mount | Most versatile, very strong | Permanent, needs studs | Dedicated gyms |
| Freestanding | No installation, portable | Large footprint, lower capacity | Shared living spaces |

### Grip Positions and What They Train
- **Wide grip pull-up:** Lats and rear delts
- **Close neutral grip:** Biceps and lower lats
- **Supinated (chin-up):** Biceps emphasis with lat support
- **Neutral parallel grip:** Reduced wrist stress, balanced arm/back activation

### Safety Checklist Before You Buy
1. Check door frame width (most no-drill bars require 24–36 in)
2. Verify your door frame's weight limit (older trim may not support 300 lbs)
3. Check ceiling height for wall mounts (need 8 ft minimum for full range of motion)
4. Confirm stud spacing for wall mounts (16-in spacing is standard)

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Will a doorframe pull-up bar damage my door frame?**
No-drill bars rely on leverage, not fasteners. If installed correctly (according to manufacturer instructions), they don't damage door frames. Over time, repeated use can dent soft wood trim, so check your trim type before purchasing.

**What pull-up bar should a beginner start with?**
Start with a doorframe no-drill bar. It costs $20–40, takes seconds to set up, and handles beginner loads. Upgrade to a wall mount when you're doing weighted pull-ups or need to increase training volume.

**How much weight can a doorframe pull-up bar hold?**
Most doorframe bars are rated 250–300 lbs. This is the combined weight of the user plus any added weight (weighted vest, belt). If you're close to the rating, go with a wall mount instead.
