## Why Your Laptop Position Matters More Than Your Chair

Most home office ergonomics advice focuses on chairs and desks while ignoring the most common source of neck and shoulder strain: a laptop screen at a 15–20 degree downward viewing angle. A laptop stand raises the screen to eye level, which:

- Eliminates the forward head posture that causes upper trap and neck tension
- Positions the screen at the recommended 20–28 inches from eyes (arm's length)
- Requires an external keyboard and mouse, which positions your arms at a natural angle

Key factors to evaluate:

- **Height and angle adjustability:** Fixed-height stands (like the mStand) set one optimal position — great for a dedicated desk, inflexible for shared spaces. Adjustable stands (Nulaxy, Boyata) offer 5–10 height settings and adjustable angle, accommodating different desk heights and chairs.
- **Material:** Aluminum stands are lighter, more durable, and dissipate heat from the laptop chassis. Plastic stands are cheaper but less stable under heavier laptops (15–17 inch) and flex noticeably. Zinc alloy falls in between.
- **Ventilation slots:** Laptops that run hot (gaming laptops, older MacBook Pros) need airflow from below. Open grid or slot designs allow passive airflow; solid surfaces trap heat and can reduce performance on thermally-constrained machines.
- **Portability:** Fixed stands for home; foldable or collapsible stands for travel. The best portable stands compress to <1 inch thick and weigh under 1 lb.
- **Weight capacity:** Most laptop stands are rated for 22–44 lbs, far more than any laptop requires. Only matters for massive 17-inch gaming laptops; even the heaviest consumer laptop (~6 lbs) is well within any stand's rating.
- **Laptop size compatibility:** Most stands accommodate 10–17 inch laptops. Check that the rear lip or grip can hold your specific laptop width at your chosen height and angle.

## What to Look For When Buying

**Eye level is the ergonomic target.** The top of your laptop screen should be at or slightly below eye level. Most adjustable stands can achieve this; fixed stands may not, depending on your desk/chair combination. Measure before buying.

**You need an external keyboard if you use a stand.** Raising the screen requires typing on an external keyboard — otherwise your arms are elevated into an uncomfortable position. Budget for a keyboard alongside the stand.

**Aluminum is worth the slight premium over plastic.** Aluminum stands dissipate heat from the laptop chassis, stay more stable under heavy laptops, and last years without cracking or flexing. The price difference at this market segment is usually $5–10.

**Fixed vs. adjustable depends on your workspace.** Single dedicated desk: fixed stand is fine, simpler, and usually more stable. Standing desk, shared space, or multiple workspaces: adjustable is necessary.
