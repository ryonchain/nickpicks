## Burr Grinder Basics: What Matters

### Conical vs Flat Burr

**Conical burrs** (most home grinders): bean travels downward through a cone-shaped inner burr and a ring-shaped outer burr. Good for a range of grind sizes, less heat generated, easier to clean. Most home grinders under $500 use conical burrs.

**Flat burrs** (higher-end grinders): two flat parallel rings grind coffee horizontally. More uniform particle size distribution, bimodal grind curve preferred by some espresso enthusiasts. Requires more careful alignment. Better for dialing in espresso.

### RPM and Heat Generation

Lower RPM motors generate less heat during grinding, which preserves volatile aromatic compounds. Gear-reduction motors (like the Baratza Encore) run at lower RPM for better flavor preservation vs direct-drive high-RPM motors.

### Grind Settings: Steps vs Stepless

**Stepped** grinders have fixed grind settings — easier to return to a saved position. **Stepless** grinders offer infinite adjustment — more precision, but harder to replicate a specific setting between sessions.

## What Grinder Is NOT Worth Buying

**Blade grinders under $30:** The Krups F203 and Hamilton Beach equivalents chop randomly. Avoid them entirely if you care about extraction quality.

**"Burr" grinders under $50:** Most sub-$50 burr grinders use ceramic disc burrs mounted on cheap motors with poor alignment. They produce marginally better results than blade grinders. The minimum for actual burr quality is $75–$80 (Timemore C3 Pro range).

**Grinder + brewer combo units:** Cuisinart and similar all-in-one units integrate a grinder with a drip machine. The grinders in these units are universally mediocre — usually equivalent to a $40 blade grinder. Buy them separately.
