## Webcam Buying Guide for Remote Meetings

### Resolution vs Sensor Quality: The Real Difference

Camera companies advertise resolution (4K, 1080p) because it's a simple number. The more meaningful specs for video call quality are:
- **Sensor size:** Larger = better low-light. 1/2" is standard; 1/1.2" is excellent.
- **Aperture (f-number):** Lower f-number = more light. f/1.7 beats f/2.8 in low light.
- **Bit rate:** How much data the processed image contains. 4K at 20Mbps looks better than 4K at 5Mbps.

### Lighting: The Real Performance Multiplier

A ring light or key light ($30–$80) will improve your video call appearance more than a $200 webcam upgrade in poor lighting. If you haven't upgraded your lighting yet, do that first — then invest in a better webcam once your lighting is solid.

### Privacy: Hardware vs Software Shutters

Software-based "camera off" in Zoom or Teams is reliable, but hardware shutters provide physical lens blocking that no software exploit can bypass. Worth considering for security-sensitive remote workers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use a mirrorless camera as a webcam?**
Yes — HDMI capture cards (Elgato Cam Link, $89) let you use a Sony, Canon, or Nikon mirrorless as a webcam. Image quality will exceed any dedicated webcam, but at significantly more cost and setup complexity.

**Do 4K webcams work with MacBooks?**
Yes. All webcams in this guide work via USB and are plug-and-play on macOS without additional drivers. Logitech's G Hub software provides additional control but isn't required.

**What internet speed do I need for 4K video calls?**
Video platforms currently cap streams below true 4K, so your upload speed is rarely the limiting factor. Zoom HD video calls use approximately 1.5 Mbps upload. A 10+ Mbps upload connection handles any video call quality.
