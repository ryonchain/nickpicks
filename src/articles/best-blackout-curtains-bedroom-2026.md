# Best Blackout Curtains for Bedroom in 2026

Blackout curtains block light for daytime sleep (shift workers, babies), improve sleep quality by eliminating street light and morning sun, and reduce heat gain in summer and heat loss in winter. The key distinction: "room darkening" blocks 80–95% of light. "True blackout" blocks 99–100% and typically uses a triple-weave or liner construction.

---

## True Blackout vs. Room Darkening

| Type | Light Blocked | Construction | Best For |
|------|--------------|-------------|----------|
| True blackout | 99–100% | Triple-weave or blackout liner | Daytime sleepers, nurseries, media rooms |
| Room darkening | 80–95% | Double-weave | General bedrooms, light control without total darkness |
| Light filtering | 40–60% | Sheer or linen weave | Privacy without blocking light |

For most adults who just want better sleep, room darkening (80–95%) is adequate. For daytime sleepers, babies, or home theaters — go true blackout.

## Measuring and Hanging

**Width:** For full coverage, use 2–2.5x the window width in curtain fabric. Two 52" panels = 104" of fabric for a 42"–52" window.

**Length:** 
- Sill length: Ends at window sill. Casual look.
- Floor length: 0.5"–1" from floor. Standard for bedrooms.
- Puddle length: 3–4" extra on floor. Formal/luxurious look.

**Hardware:** Standard grommet curtains work with most rods. Pinch-pleat headers require specific rod and rings — check before buying.

## Energy Savings

Blackout curtains reduce heat gain in summer (reducing AC load) and heat loss in winter (reducing heating load). Studies show 10–25% reduction in heating/cooling energy for windows with thermal curtains vs. uncovered windows. The thermal layer in most blackout curtains is the same technology used in energy-efficient window treatments.
