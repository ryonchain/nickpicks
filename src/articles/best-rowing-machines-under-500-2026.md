## Rowing Machine Buying Guide

### Resistance Types Explained

**Air resistance (e.g., Concept2, Stamina):**
- Resistance increases with rowing speed/power
- Best replication of on-water rowing feel
- Slightly louder
- Preferred by serious rowers and athletes

**Magnetic resistance (e.g., Sunny):**
- Fixed resistance levels controlled by dial
- Quieter operation
- Consistent feel regardless of rowing speed
- Great for home use where noise is a concern

**Water resistance (e.g., WaterRower):**
- Tank with paddles creates resistance via water
- Most realistic feel
- Beautiful aesthetics
- Usually $600–$1,200+

### Space Considerations

Most rowing machines are 8–9 feet long when in use. Measure your space before buying. Foldable models (NordicTrack RW500) fold to much smaller dimensions for storage but require 8+ feet of clear space during a session.

### Weight Capacity

Most sub-$500 rowers support 220–300 lbs. Check specs if you're over 200 lbs to ensure you're within the rated capacity for safe use.

## FAQ

**Is rowing good for weight loss?**
Yes — rowing is one of the highest-calorie-burning low-impact exercises available. A 180-lb person burns approximately 500–700 calories per hour rowing at moderate intensity.

**How loud are rowing machines?**
Magnetic rowers (Sunny): very quiet — gym neighbors won't hear you. Air rowers (Stamina): moderate fan noise, similar to a loud fan. Water rowers: moderate splashing sound. All are apartment-appropriate at normal hours.

**Do I need a subscription for my rowing machine?**
Only if you buy a connected rower like the Hydrow Wave or NordicTrack RW500. Traditional rowers (Sunny, Stamina) have no subscription requirements — just basic LCD monitors.

**What muscles does rowing work?**
Rowing engages legs (quads, hamstrings, glutes) during the drive phase, core throughout, and back and arms (lats, rhomboids, biceps) during the finish. Approximately 65–70% legs, 20% core, 10–15% arms.
