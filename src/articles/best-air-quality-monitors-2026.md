## What Indoor Air Pollutants Actually Matter

**CO2**: High CO2 (above 1,000 ppm) causes cognitive impairment — difficulty concentrating, fatigue, headaches. Bedrooms and home offices with poor ventilation often hit 1,500–2,000 ppm. Opening a window drops it to ~400 ppm (outdoor level) within minutes.

**PM2.5**: Fine particulate matter from cooking, candles, wildfires, and dust. At elevated levels it irritates respiratory and cardiovascular systems. Cooking on a gas stove without a hood can spike PM2.5 to 5–10x safe levels.

**VOCs**: Chemicals off-gassed from furniture, paint, cleaning products, and adhesives. Acute exposure causes eye/nose irritation. Chronic exposure from new furniture in a closed room is measurable for weeks.

**Radon**: Radioactive gas from soil that accumulates in basements. The #2 cause of lung cancer in the US. Undetectable without a specialized sensor.

**Humidity**: High humidity (above 60%) promotes mold growth. Below 30% causes dry air discomfort and static.

## Buying Guide: Choosing an Air Quality Monitor

### Do You Need Radon Monitoring?

Radon is colorless, odorless, and the second-leading cause of lung cancer. If your home has a basement or ground-level rooms, testing for radon is recommended — the EPA suggests testing every 2 years. Only Airthings and a few specialists offer consumer radon sensors. If radon is a priority, start there.

### CO2 vs. VOC vs. PM2.5

**CO2** tells you when a room needs fresh air — the most actionable single measurement. **VOCs** indicate chemical off-gassing from furniture and cleaning products. **PM2.5** reflects cooking, smoking, wildfire smoke, and dust. Ideally, monitor all three. If choosing one, CO2 has the most immediate impact on comfort and cognition.

### Display vs. App-Only

Monitors with real-time displays are more accessible — you can glance at readings without unlocking your phone. App-only monitors (like the basic Wave Plus) require you to check readings actively. For common areas, a display helps. For bedrooms, app access is usually sufficient.

### Calibration and Accuracy

Consumer monitors use electrochemical and NDIR (non-dispersive infrared) sensors that drift over time. NDIR CO2 sensors (used by Govee and Awair) self-calibrate based on outdoor air levels — important for accuracy over months and years. Radon sensors require weeks of averaging to produce accurate readings.

## FAQ

**Where should I place an air quality monitor?**
Place it at breathing height (4–6 feet) in the room you spend the most time in. Avoid placement directly next to windows or vents — you want to measure room air, not supply air.

**What CO2 level is dangerous?**
400–700 ppm is outdoor/well-ventilated indoor air. 1,000 ppm causes mild cognitive effects. Above 2,500 ppm causes significant impairment. Buildings typically stay below 1,500 ppm with normal ventilation.

**Can I use this to decide when to run my air purifier?**
Yes — this is one of the best use cases. Many monitors support IFTTT or direct integrations with smart purifiers. Set an automation to run the purifier when PM2.5 exceeds a threshold.

**How accurate are consumer air quality monitors?**
Within ±10–20% for most sensors. They're accurate enough for relative comparisons and trend tracking but not for regulatory compliance purposes. For radon testing, Airthings sensors are validated against lab standards.
