## Why Grow Light Spectrum Matters More Than Wattage

The most common grow light mistake is buying by wattage. A 1000W incandescent bulb will not grow plants well; a 100W full-spectrum LED panel will. What plants need is photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) in the right wavelengths — primarily blue (400–500nm) for vegetative growth and red (600–700nm) for flowering, with a broader "white" spectrum that supports all growth stages.

Modern full-spectrum LED panels achieve this with a mix of diode types that produce a balanced output across the entire PAR range. The metric to compare is **PPFD (Photosynthetic Photon Flux Density)** measured in μmol/m²/s — essentially, how many useful light photons hit your plants per second per square meter. Most leafy greens and herbs thrive at 200–400 PPFD; fruiting plants and high-light orchids want 400–800 PPFD; cannabis requires 600–1000+ PPFD. A product claiming "1000W equivalent" tells you nothing about actual PPFD at canopy level.

**Key factors:**
- **Spectrum:** Full-spectrum (3000–6500K white + red) covers all growth stages; red-blue "blurple" lights are outdated
- **PPFD at coverage distance:** Check the PPFD map, not just peak numbers
- **Coverage area:** Manufacturer claims are usually optimistic; halve them for fruiting plants
- **Timer:** Built-in programmable timer is essential for consistent photoperiods
- **Dimmer:** Adjustable intensity is valuable for seedlings (lower) vs. mature plants (higher)

## Buyer's Guide

### How to Match a Grow Light to Your Plants

**Low-light plants** (pothos, snake plants, ZZ plants, philodendrons): These plants evolved in forest understory conditions and thrive in 50–150 PPFD. A GE BR30 bulb or a single Barrina T5 strip is more than sufficient — these plants can actually be burned by high-PPFD panels at close range.

**Medium-light plants** (herbs, leafy greens, tropical houseplants): 150–400 PPFD is ideal. Barrina T5 strips at 6–12 inches, or a Mars Hydro TS 1000 / Spider Farmer SF-1000 panel at 24–30 inches above the canopy, cover this range perfectly.

**High-light plants** (tomatoes, peppers, fruiting crops, succulents, orchids): 400–800+ PPFD required. The Mars Hydro TS 1000 or SF-1000 at 12–18 inches handles this well. Position the panel closer for fruiting and monitor for light burn (bleached or curled leaves near the top).

### How Long Should You Run Grow Lights?

Most plants need 12–18 hours of light per day under artificial lighting. Seedlings benefit from 16–18 hours during early development. Vegetative herbs and leafy greens do well at 14–16 hours. Flowering plants often require a 12/12 hour light/dark cycle to trigger blooming. A built-in or plug-in timer set to your target photoperiod is essential — consistent light cycles are more important than running lights longer.

### Hang Height and Light Burn

More is not always better. Grow lights positioned too close to the canopy cause light burn: bleached, yellowed, or curled leaves, particularly at the top of the plant. Start with the manufacturer's recommended height (usually 18–24 inches for panels, 6–12 inches for strips) and lower gradually while monitoring plant response over 2–3 days. If tips curl or bleach, raise the light.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use a regular LED bulb for plants?**

Standard white LED bulbs provide some useful spectrum but lack the red wavelengths (660nm) that optimize photosynthesis efficiency. Plants will survive under regular LEDs but grow more slowly than under purpose-built grow lights. For plants you want to thrive and grow aggressively, a full-spectrum grow light is worth the minimal cost difference.

**How close should a grow light be to plants?**

Panel-style lights: 18–24 inches for most plants, 12–18 inches for high-light crops. Strip lights: 6–12 inches for seedlings and herbs. Adjust based on plant response — if new growth is compact and dark green, the light is appropriate. If growth is leggy and reaching toward the light, it's too far. If leaves bleach or curl, it's too close.

**What's the difference between full-spectrum and blurple grow lights?**

"Blurple" lights emit only red and blue wavelengths, appearing purple-pink to the human eye. Full-spectrum lights include white wavelengths across the visible range, which supports more balanced plant development and is easier on human eyes. Modern full-spectrum LEDs perform better than blurple at equivalent wattage — the blurple design is effectively obsolete in 2026.

**Do grow lights work for succulents?**

Yes — succulents that stretch toward windows (etiolation) in low-light conditions will compact and color up correctly under adequate grow lighting. Use a full-spectrum grow light at 6–12 inches for 12–16 hours daily. Succulents tolerate higher light intensity than most plants, so positioning closer to a Mars Hydro or Spider Farmer panel is appropriate.

**Can grow lights replace natural sunlight completely?**

Technically yes, but at significant energy cost for high-light plants. Full sunlight delivers around 1500–2000 PPFD in direct exposure — replicating that with artificial lighting requires very high-wattage setups. For most indoor crops (herbs, greens, low-to-medium-light houseplants), grow lights are a complete substitute. For sun-loving fruiting crops, supplementing with whatever natural light is available is more practical than trying to replace full sun entirely.
