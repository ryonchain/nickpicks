## Why MIPS Matters

MIPS (Multi-directional Impact Protection System) is a low-friction layer inside the helmet that allows the head to rotate slightly on impact — mimicking the brain's own protective fluid. This rotation absorbs rotational forces that straight-line EPS foam doesn't address.

Rotational forces cause most serious brain injuries in cycling crashes. Virginia Tech's STAR Evaluation System consistently gives MIPS helmets significantly better ratings than non-MIPS equivalents. In 2026, we recommend avoiding non-MIPS helmets at any price point — MIPS is now available at $50–60.

## Key Specs to Evaluate

**Retention system:** A good dial-adjusted fit system (BOA, Roc Loc, Turnfit) allows micro-adjustments while riding. Loose helmets don't protect you properly. Test the fit with gloves on — that's how you'll be adjusting it on the bike.

**Ventilation:** Road helmets balance aerodynamics with airflow. More vents = more airflow = more comfort on hot days, but also slightly more drag. Endurance riders prioritize ventilation; racers prioritize aero.

**Weight:** Sub-300g is excellent. Sub-250g is ultralight. Weight matters on long climbs and all-day rides where neck fatigue accumulates.

**Aerodynamics:** Look for wind tunnel testing data or third-party aero tests. Aero-optimized helmets (fewer vents, smooth surfaces) can save 10–40 seconds over a 40km time trial. Only relevant if you race or care about speed.

## Helmet Fit: The Most Critical Factor

A poorly fitting helmet is dangerous regardless of price. Key fit checkpoints:

1. **Level placement:** The helmet should sit level, covering the forehead 2 fingers above the eyebrows
2. **Side straps form a "Y":** Straps should meet just below the ear
3. **Buckle position:** Tight enough that you can barely fit two fingers under the chin strap
4. **Shake test:** Properly fitted helmet should not rock forward/backward more than 1 inch

Measure your head circumference before buying (tape measure above ears around the widest point). Most adult helmets: S (52–56cm), M (55–59cm), L (58–63cm).

## When to Replace Your Helmet

Replace your helmet after any significant impact — even if there's no visible damage. EPS foam compresses permanently in a crash; the helmet is no longer protective even if it looks fine.

Also replace after 5 years of regular use. UV degradation and wear degrade the foam and shell over time.

## FAQ

**Q: Is MIPS worth the extra cost?**
A: Yes, unambiguously. MIPS helmets consistently outperform non-MIPS in independent testing (Virginia Tech, Consumer Reports). The price premium is typically $20–30. It's the most important helmet technology since the EPS foam shell.

**Q: Does helmet aerodynamics matter for recreational cyclists?**
A: At speeds under 25 mph on casual rides, the difference is negligible. At race pace (25+ mph) over long distances, aero helmets offer measurable time savings. For sportives and gran fondos, an aero helmet is worth considering. For Sunday rides, prioritize ventilation and comfort.

**Q: Can I use a mountain bike helmet on a road bike?**
A: Technically yes, but road-specific helmets are lighter, more aerodynamic, and better ventilated for road riding speeds. MTB helmets are heavier and designed for lower-speed trail impacts. Use the right tool for the right job.
