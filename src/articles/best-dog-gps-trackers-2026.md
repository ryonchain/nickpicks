## What to Actually Evaluate in a Dog GPS Tracker

The hardware price is only part of the cost equation — most trackers require ongoing monthly subscriptions to access cellular networks, and those costs compound significantly over a dog's lifetime.

- **Battery life:** Budget trackers run 1–3 days at normal update rates. Premium trackers with power-saving modes can push 1–2 weeks. Check the battery life at the update frequency you'll actually use (live tracking every few seconds vs. every 2–3 minutes).
- **Subscription cost:** Plans range from ~$5/month (Tractive) to ~$12/month (Whistle) on annual plans. Over 5 years, that difference is $420. Check what coverage (countries, carriers) the plan includes.
- **Location accuracy:** All GPS trackers use the same constellation of satellites; accuracy depends on cellular network density and how aggressively the device updates. Most are accurate to within 10–30 feet in open areas. Dense urban or heavily wooded areas degrade accuracy across all brands.
- **Geofence alerts:** Most trackers allow you to draw virtual boundaries on a map and receive push notifications when your dog exits them. Check whether geofences are available on the basic subscription tier or locked behind premium plans.
- **Water resistance:** Dogs swim, play in rain, and drink from puddles. Minimum IP55 (dust and water jet resistant) is acceptable; IP67 (submersion to 1 meter) is better for water-loving breeds.
- **Collar weight:** A tracker that's too heavy for a small dog is uncomfortable and affects natural movement. Under 1 oz for dogs under 20 lbs; up to 2 oz is acceptable for medium to large dogs. Weigh against your dog's size before buying.

## What to Look For When Buying

**Calculate total cost of ownership over 3–5 years.** A $30 tracker with a $12/month subscription costs $462 over 3 years. A $100 tracker at $5/month costs $280 over the same period. Don't optimize for hardware price alone.

**Match tracker weight to dog size.** General rule: tracker weight should not exceed 5% of the dog's body weight. A 10 lb dog should wear nothing heavier than 0.5 oz — which limits most trackers to the Jiobit or AirTag. A 50 lb dog handles 2 oz without issue.

**GPS accuracy in your geography.** Open terrain (suburbs, parks, hiking): all GPS trackers perform well. Dense urban canyons and forested areas: expect 30–100 foot accuracy variance across all brands. No tracker overcomes GPS satellite physics.

**Check carrier coverage in your area.** Most trackers run on one or two cellular networks. If you live in a rural area with spotty LTE coverage, verify which network the tracker uses and whether it has coverage where your dog might escape.
