## How to Choose a Dog GPS Tracker

### Network Type
- **Cellular (LTE):** Best for suburban and urban use. Requires a subscription but works anywhere with cell service.
- **Satellite (GPS):** Best for rural/wilderness. No subscription, but update intervals are longer.
- **Bluetooth:** Short range only (< 300 ft). Works well for home use; not suitable as primary tracker.

### Subscription Costs
Factor in total annual cost. A $50 tracker with a $15/month subscription costs $230/year. A $149 tracker at $8/month costs $245/year. Often the higher-quality tracker wins on total value.

### Battery Life
Short battery life means daily or every-other-day charging — easy to forget when you need it most. Look for 3+ days of everyday battery life.

### Size and Weight
A tracker should be no more than 5–10% of your dog's body weight. For dogs under 10 lbs, stick to ultra-light options like the Jiobit.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do dog GPS trackers work without cell service?**
Only satellite-based trackers (like Garmin Alpha) work in zero-cell areas. All cellular-based trackers require coverage.

**Can GPS trackers replace microchips?**
No — GPS trackers require battery power and can be removed. Microchips are permanent and mandatory for vet/shelter ID. Use both.

**How accurate are dog GPS trackers?**
Most cellular trackers are accurate to 10–30 feet under open sky. Expect 50–100 feet in dense urban areas with building interference.
