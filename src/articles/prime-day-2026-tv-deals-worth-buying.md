## How to Spot a Real TV Deal on Prime Day

Retailers inflate TV prices 4–6 weeks before major sales events. Here's how to see through it:

- **Check price history.** A "50% off" TV that was inflated in May isn't a deal. Reference the lowest price from the past 90 days, not the inflated MSRP.
- **Panel type matters.** OLED delivers perfect blacks and infinite contrast. QLED/Mini-LED competes on brightness. Don't let marketing blur the difference.
- **Model year.** TV manufacturers release new models in spring. Prime Day often clears prior-year inventory at genuine discounts — a 2025 model at 30% off often beats a 2026 model at 5% off.
- **Size sweet spot.** 55" and 65" see the deepest discounts because they're the highest-volume sellers. 75"+ discounts are shallower percentage-wise but still meaningful in dollars.

## What TV Size Should You Buy?

| Room size | Viewing distance | Recommended size |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Small bedroom | 6–8 feet | 43"–50" |
| Living room / main TV | 8–12 feet | 55"–65" |
| Large living room | 12–15 feet | 75"–85" |
| Home theater / dedicated room | 15+ feet | 85"+ or projector |

The most common Prime Day purchase regret is buying too small. If you're on the edge between sizes, go bigger — you won't regret it.

## What to Skip on Prime Day

- **8K TVs.** No meaningful 8K content exists for consumers. The upscaling is impressive but not worth the price premium when 4K content looks better on a top-tier 4K TV.
- **1080p TVs.** 4K is the new baseline. At 43" and above, the pixel density difference is visible. Don't buy 1080p in 2026.
- **No-name brands.** Vizio, Westinghouse, and similar budget brands frequently appear at very low prices on Prime Day. Panel quality, software longevity, and input lag put them behind TCL and Hisense at comparable prices.
