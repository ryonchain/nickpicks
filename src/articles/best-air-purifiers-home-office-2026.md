## Why Air Quality Matters for Remote Workers

Indoor air is often 2–5× more polluted than outdoor air. Dust, pet dander, VOCs from furniture and carpets, mold spores, and fine particulate matter (PM2.5) all accumulate in enclosed rooms — especially home offices with few windows and limited airflow.

Research from Harvard shows that cognitive function drops measurably in high-CO2 and high-PM2.5 environments. If you're experiencing afternoon brain fog or persistent fatigue at your desk, air quality is worth investigating.

A good HEPA air purifier removes 99.97% of particles 0.3 microns or larger, including:
- Dust and dust mites
- Pet dander and pollen
- Smoke particles (including wildfire smoke)
- Mold spores
- Some bacteria and viruses

This guide covers six of the best air purifiers for home offices, ranked by noise level, CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate), and value per dollar.

## What to Look for in a Home Office Air Purifier

**CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate):** Measures cubic feet per minute of cleaned air at max fan speed. For a 150 sq ft home office, a CADR of 150+ is sufficient. For 200–300 sq ft, aim for CADR 200+.

**True HEPA vs "HEPA-type":** True HEPA (H13 or H14) captures 99.97% of 0.3-micron particles. HEPA-type filters are marketing copy — their actual filtration varies widely. Only buy True HEPA.

**Noise level:** Measured in decibels (dB). For a working environment, you want the unit quiet enough on its "sleep" or "low" setting. Under 35 dB is whisper-quiet. Under 45 dB is acceptable for background work. Over 55 dB = noticeable distraction.

**Filter replacement cost:** Some purifiers sell cheap but have $60–$80/year filter costs. Factor this into the total cost of ownership.

**Coverage area:** Match the unit's rated sq footage to your actual room size. Manufacturers often overstate coverage by 20–30%.
