## What Makes a Ring Light Worth Buying

Four specs determine lighting quality for video:

- **Size and Output:** Larger rings produce softer, more flattering light by spreading illumination across a bigger surface area. An 18-inch ring at 1,800 lux is dramatically more flattering than a 6-inch ring at 1,800 lux — the bigger source wraps around facial shadows naturally.
- **Color Temperature Range:** 3,200K (warm) to 5,600K (daylight) is the standard range. Daylight (5,500–5,600K) matches natural window light and looks most professional on camera. Being able to blend warm and cool creates custom skin tone rendering.
- **CRI (Color Rendering Index):** CRI 90+ means colors in the frame are reproduced accurately. Lights with CRI below 85 produce a greenish or yellowish cast that's unflattering on skin tones regardless of white balance settings.
- **Dimmer Control and Flicker:** Lights that flicker (common in cheap models) cause banding artifacts in recorded video. Look for PWM-free dimming or app-controlled stepless dimming at all brightness levels.

## Buying Guide: What to Actually Look For

### Ring Size and Subject Distance
The flattering catch-light (circular reflection visible in eyes) is more prominent from larger rings at appropriate distances. For a webcam/phone at 2–3 feet, a 10-inch ring works. For a camera at 4–6 feet, use 18 inches. Closer placement of a smaller light produces a harder, less flattering quality.

### Color Temperature for Skin Tones
5,500K–5,600K (daylight) renders most skin tones accurately on camera. Warmer settings (3,200K) add a flattering glow but can wash out pale complexions. If your room has warm ambient light (incandescent bulbs), match it with 3,200K or use daylight and turn off the ambient sources.

### PWM Flicker and Video Recording
Cheap LED drivers use PWM (pulse width modulation) dimming that flickers at frequencies invisible to the eye but visible to camera sensors — especially at 120Hz or 240fps slow motion. Look for "flicker-free" specifically in specs, or test by filming your hand in front of the light at your target frame rate.

### Stand Stability and Height
For an 18-inch ring with a camera center-mounted, the combined weight is substantial. Lightweight stands tip easily with a camera on top. Invest in a stand with a weight bag hook or use a sandbag on the base. Floor stands should reach at least 6 feet for eye-level positioning when seated.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Should the ring light go in front of me or to the side?**
Directly in front of you (camera centered in the ring) produces the classic catch-light look with even facial illumination. Side placement creates more dramatic shadows — appropriate for mood/gaming content but less flattering for professional video calls.

**What color temperature should I use for video calls?**
5,500K (daylight) is most professional for most video call environments. If your room has warm lighting, you can mix — keep the ring at 4,000–5,000K to avoid looking artificially blue compared to the background.

**Can ring lights cause eye strain?**
Staring directly at any bright LED light causes temporary eye strain. Ring lights are designed to be positioned facing you but slightly above eye level — the glare is reduced by the ring shape. CRI 95+ bulbs produce fuller-spectrum light that's gentler on extended viewing than CRI 80 LEDs.

**Do I need a ring light for Zoom calls?**
A ring light dramatically improves the quality of webcam footage in typical home office conditions. Overhead fluorescent or incandescent lighting casts unflattering shadows. Even a $35 ring light positioned in front of you produces noticeably more professional-looking results.
