## Buyer's Guide

### Adjustable vs. Fixed Dumbbells

**Adjustable Pros:**
- Replace a full rack in one product
- Space efficient
- More economical (per lb equivalent)
- Easy to change weight between sets

**Adjustable Cons:**
- Slower to change than dedicated fixed weights
- Mechanical mechanism can fail
- Longer/bulkier than fixed
- Single pair limitation (can't do supersets with different weights simultaneously)

**Fixed Pros:**
- Best durability — no mechanisms to break
- Fastest weight selection
- Natural feel and balance
- Can do supersets with different weights

**Fixed Cons:**
- Takes up much more space
- Higher total cost for a full range

### What Weight Range Do I Need?

**Beginners (0–6 months):**
- Women: 5–25 lbs covers most exercises
- Men: 10–40 lbs covers most exercises

**Intermediate (6+ months):**
- Women: Up to 35–50 lbs for squats/lunges
- Men: Up to 60–80 lbs for compound movements

### Best Exercises with Dumbbells
1. Dumbbell bench press
2. Bent-over row

4. Bicep curl
5. Tricep kickback

### Dumbbell Storage Options

- **Horizontal rack**: More stable, stores full sets

## FAQ

**Are adjustable dumbbells worth it?**
Yes, for most home gym owners. The space savings and cost per pound are compelling. Bowflex SelectTech 552 replaces 15 pairs of dumbbells in the footprint of one pair.

**What size dumbbells should I start with?**
For beginners: women start with 5–15 lbs, men with 10–25 lbs for most exercises. Buy adjustable to grow with you.

**Can I build muscle with just dumbbells?**
Absolutely. Dumbbells activate stabilizer muscles that barbells don't, and the full range of motion is superior for hypertrophy. Many serious athletes train primarily with dumbbells.

**How much do dumbbells cost per pound?**
Hex dumbbells typically run $1.00–$1.50/lb. Adjustable dumbbell sets work out to $0.80–$1.50/lb-equivalent. Budget sets from Amazon/CAP are cheaper; premium neoprene and chrome are more expensive.

**Are rubber or iron dumbbells better?**
Rubber-coated (hex or round): Better for floors, less noise, good grip, slightly more expensive.
Iron/chrome: Classic look, durable, no rubber degradation over time, slightly cheaper.
