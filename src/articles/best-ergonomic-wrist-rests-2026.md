## Wrist Rest Buying Guide

### The Right Wrist Rest Position

A wrist rest is not for resting your wrists while typing — it's for resting your wrists during pauses. While actively typing, your wrists should float slightly above the rest. The rest catches your wrists during natural breaks. If you're pressing down on the rest while typing, it's positioned too high or being used incorrectly.

### Gel vs. Memory Foam vs. Foam

| Material | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|----------|------|------|---------|
| Gel | Cooler, conforms well, firmer support | Can feel stiff when cold, heavier | All-day office users |
| Memory foam | Soft, conforms over time, lightweight | Retains heat, compresses with age | Gamers, budget users |
| Standard foam | Lightest, cheapest | Bottoms out quickly, least support | Occasional light use |

## FAQs

**Does a wrist rest actually prevent carpal tunnel?**
Wrist rests alone don't prevent carpal tunnel — which is primarily caused by prolonged flexion/extension of the wrist during typing, not lack of support during pauses. However, they reduce pressure on the carpal tunnel during rest periods and encourage better wrist posture. Combined with proper keyboard height, frequent breaks, and neutral wrist position while typing, they're part of a complete ergonomics strategy.

**How long do gel wrist rests last?**
Quality gel rests (GELPRO, Kensington) maintain their support for 3–5 years under daily use. Memory foam compresses faster — typically 1–2 years before noticeable flattening. Replace any wrist rest that has lost its ability to support your wrist above the desk surface.

**Should my wrist rest height match my keyboard height?**
Yes — your wrist rest should bring your wrist to approximately the same plane as your keyboard's key surface, so the wrist maintains a neutral (straight) position from forearm through hand. Most wrist rests are 0.6"–1.0" high, appropriate for standard keyboards. Mechanical keyboard users with high-profile switches may need a taller rest or an angled (sloped) version.
