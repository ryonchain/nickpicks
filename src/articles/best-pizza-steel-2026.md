## Pizza Steel vs. Pizza Stone: What the Science Says

This isn't a preference debate — it's thermodynamics. Pizza stone (ceramic or cordierite) is a poor heat conductor. Pizza steel (solid steel plate) conducts heat rapidly. The relevant comparison:

| Material | Thermal Conductivity (W/m·K) |
|---|---|
| Steel | ~50 |
| Cast iron | ~40 |
| Ceramic/stone | ~3 |

When your raw pizza dough touches a 550°F steel surface, heat transfers immediately into the dough — you get spring (rapid puff), browning, and crust char in 4–6 minutes rather than 8–12 on stone. The top of the pizza bakes from oven radiant heat; the bottom bakes from the steel's conducted heat. Steel wins the bottom-crust race every time.

Pizza stone's one argument: it can absorb moisture from the dough, which some bakers believe contributes to an airier, chewier crust. In practice, the difference is imperceptible — especially in a home oven that can't replicate the 900°F+ floor temperatures of a Neapolitan wood-fired oven.

**When to use a pizza stone instead:** If you break things regularly and don't want to invest in a 15-lb steel plate, a $25 stone is fine. It will survive drops that would dent steel (though stones crack too). But if you're asking what makes better pizza, steel wins.

## Technique: Getting the Best Results from a Pizza Steel

**Preheat aggressively.** Place the steel on the upper third of the oven (or directly under the broiler element for the last 2 minutes of cook). Preheat for 45–60 minutes at your oven's maximum temperature. The steel takes much longer to reach full temperature than the oven's air.

**Use a pizza peel.** Launching a raw pizza onto a hot steel without a peel is extremely difficult and dangerous. A wooden peel for assembly and a thin metal peel for retrieval is the professional setup. A single perforated aluminum peel ($25–40) handles both tasks adequately.

**Broiler finish.** After 4–5 minutes on the steel, switch on the broiler for 60–90 seconds to char the top of the pizza while the bottom finishes crisping. Watch it constantly during this step.

**Dough hydration.** Higher hydration doughs (65–70% for Neapolitan style) puff more dramatically on steel than low-hydration doughs. If you're using store-bought pizza dough, add 1–2 tablespoons of water to the dough before shaping for better results.

## FAQ

**Can I use a pizza steel for bread?**
Yes — this is one of the best uses of a pizza steel. Preheat the steel, place a Dutch oven on top during the first 20 minutes of baking (to create steam), and you get exceptional oven spring and crust. Many serious bread bakers use a steel as their primary baking surface.

**Will a pizza steel rust?**
Carbon steel rusts if left wet or stored without oil. Keep it dry, apply a thin coat of oil after use and washing, and store it out of humidity. Surface rust is easily removed with steel wool and re-seasoning.

**What's the maximum oven temperature a pizza steel can handle?**
Steel can handle any domestic oven temperature (500–550°F maximum for most home ovens) without damage. It's safe in commercial ovens up to 700°F+. Never use a steel in a microwave.

**How long does a pizza steel last?**
Indefinitely with proper care. There's nothing to wear out — it's a solid plate of steel. Properly maintained, a Baking Steel will outlast your oven.
