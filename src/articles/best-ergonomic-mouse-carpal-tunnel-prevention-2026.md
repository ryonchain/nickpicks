Carpal tunnel syndrome and repetitive strain injury (RSI) from mouse use affects an estimated 3–5% of desk workers annually. The two main culprits are forearm pronation (rotating your arm flat to use a standard horizontal mouse) and ulnar deviation (bending your wrist sideways to click). The right ergonomic mouse addresses one or both of these. Here's what to know before buying.

---

## Understanding the Ergonomic Mouse Types

### Vertical Mouse
Holds the hand in a "handshake" position — thumb up, arm in neutral rotation. Eliminates forearm pronation. Best for users whose primary pain is in the forearm and outer wrist. Requires an adjustment period of 3–7 days. Most popular ergonomic mouse type.

### Trackball Mouse
The mouse stays stationary — you move the cursor by rolling a ball with your thumb or fingers. Eliminates ALL wrist and arm movement. Best for users with severe wrist injury, limited desk space, or who need to minimize movement to zero. Longer learning curve (1–2 weeks).

### Angled/Contoured Mouse
Slightly angled (20–30°) compared to a standard mouse. Not as aggressive as vertical mice but more compatible with existing habits. Good as a transition step.

## Will an Ergonomic Mouse Actually Help Carpal Tunnel?

**Short answer:** An ergonomic mouse reduces the risk of developing wrist/forearm RSI and can relieve existing mild symptoms. It is not a medical cure for diagnosed carpal tunnel syndrome.

The clinical evidence supports:
- Vertical mice reduce forearm muscle activity (pronation) by 10–20%
- Trackball mice reduce wrist deviation and overall arm movement significantly
- Both types show improvement in user-reported discomfort over standard mice in controlled studies

If you have **diagnosed carpal tunnel syndrome**, see a hand therapist. An ergonomic mouse is one tool among several (wrist splints, stretching, activity modification, in severe cases surgery). It won't resolve severe median nerve compression alone.

## Ergonomic Mouse Setup Tips

**DPI matters more than you think.** Higher DPI = less physical mouse movement to cover the same screen distance. Setting DPI to 1200–1600 reduces total wrist travel distance significantly. Most users default to 800 DPI, which requires more movement.

**Desk and chair height alignment:** Even the best ergonomic mouse won't help if your desk is too high. Your elbow should be at 90°, forearm parallel to the floor. If your shoulder is raised while mousing, the desk is too high for you.

**Wrist rest vs no wrist rest:** Ergonomic opinion is split. During active mouse movement, a wrist rest creates friction that forces the wrist to deviate. Between clicks (resting), a wrist rest relieves pressure. Best practice: use a wrist rest for resting, lift off it while moving.

**Alternate hands:** The least-discussed RSI prevention strategy. Training your non-dominant hand for routine mouse tasks (scrolling, window clicking) distributes load. Takes 2–4 weeks to build skill but dramatically reduces dominant hand cumulative strain.
