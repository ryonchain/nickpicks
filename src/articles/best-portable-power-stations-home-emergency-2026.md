## Why a Portable Power Station Beats a Gas Generator for Home Backup

A gas generator can power more watts, but portable power stations win on every dimension that matters for home emergency use: no carbon monoxide risk (safe indoors), near-silent operation, zero fuel storage, instant-on when the power cuts, and the ability to recharge from solar panels if the outage runs multi-day.

For a home backup unit, focus on four specs. **Capacity (Wh)** determines how long you can run essentials—a 1000Wh unit runs a standard refrigerator for 6-8 hours, while 2000Wh gets you through 12-16 hours. **AC output wattage** sets what you can actually power; 2000W continuous handles a refrigerator, CPAP, device charging, and fans simultaneously. **Recharge speed** matters because a unit that takes 10 hours to recharge from AC is a liability during an extended outage—look for units that recharge in under 2 hours. **Expandability** is increasingly valuable: units that accept external battery modules let you start at one capacity and grow.

## Buyer's Guide

### How Much Capacity Do You Actually Need?

Start with your critical loads—the things you absolutely need running during a power outage.

**Refrigerator:** 100-200W continuous, meaning a 2000Wh station lasts 10-20 hours before needing recharge. A standard full-size fridge runs in the 150W range; assume 12-15 hours on a 2kWh unit.

**CPAP machine:** 30-50W (without humidifier), making it extremely frugal. A 1000Wh unit can run a CPAP through 2-3 nights. With humidifier, multiply by roughly 3x.

**Window AC unit:** 900-1500W, the most power-hungry home appliance you will likely want to power. Even a 2000Wh unit only runs it for 1-2 hours continuously—pair with solar recharging or cycle it on and off.

**Device charging + lighting:** 50-200W combined for phones, laptops, and LED lights. Manageable from even a 500Wh unit.

For most households, **1000-1500Wh** handles the critical loads through a standard 8-12 hour overnight outage. **2000Wh+** buys genuine multi-day self-sufficiency when paired with solar panels.

---

### UPS Mode vs. Standby Mode

Several units (EcoFlow DELTA 2 Max, Anker SOLIX F1200) offer a UPS (Uninterruptible Power Supply) mode: the station stays connected to wall power, charges to a set level, and automatically switches to battery output in milliseconds when grid power drops. This is ideal for sensitive electronics like NAS drives and medical equipment that cannot tolerate even a brief interruption.

Standard standby mode simply keeps the unit charged so you can plug in devices when the outage hits. UPS mode requires the station to remain plugged in at all times but provides seamless protection for the devices connected to it.

---

### Solar Pairing Basics

For genuine self-sufficiency during extended outages, solar recharging is non-negotiable. A basic calculation: divide the station's capacity by your solar panel wattage, then account for 5-6 peak sun hours per day and 75-80% system efficiency.

Example: 2000Wh station ÷ 400W of panels × (1 / 0.80 efficiency) = 6.25 hours of peak sun for a full recharge. In most US locations, that is achievable in a single summer day, and possible over 1.5-2 days in winter or cloudy conditions.

Match your solar panel wattage to the station's max solar input—there is no benefit to installing panels that exceed the input ceiling, and you lose money on unused panel capacity.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can a portable power station run my home refrigerator during an outage?**

Yes—any unit with 2000W+ AC output and 1500Wh+ capacity will run a standard home refrigerator. A typical full-size fridge draws 150W continuously, so a 2000Wh station provides roughly 12-15 hours of runtime before needing recharge. To extend this, pair with solar panels to maintain the charge cycle. Compact or older refrigerators draw more—check the nameplate wattage on your specific model.

**Is it safe to use a portable power station indoors?**

Absolutely—this is one of the primary advantages over gas generators. Portable power stations produce zero emissions and operate near-silently, making them completely safe for indoor use including bedrooms for overnight medical equipment. Gas generators produce deadly carbon monoxide and must never be used indoors. Keep your station at room temperature (60-90°F) for optimal performance.

**How often should I charge and discharge the battery?**

LFP batteries benefit from regular cycling. Rather than leaving it fully charged for months, run it down to 20-30% every 2-3 months and recharge to full. Most manufacturers recommend storing LFP units at 40-80% state of charge for long-term storage. Many units include a maintenance mode that automatically maintains optimal charge levels with minimal user intervention.

**Can I connect a whole-home automatic transfer switch to a portable power station?**

Not typically for most portable units—automatic transfer switches (ATS) are designed for hardwired whole-home generators with specific output specs. However, some manufacturers (EcoFlow, Bluetti) offer smart home integration panels designed for permanent installation that work with their larger units. For whole-home backup, a dedicated home battery system like the Tesla Powerwall is a better fit than a portable unit.

**What is the difference between continuous watts and surge watts?**

Continuous watts is the sustained power output the inverter can deliver indefinitely. Surge watts is the brief peak output (typically lasting 50-500ms) that handles the startup spike of motor-driven appliances like refrigerators, air conditioners, and power tools. A compressor motor may need 3-5x its running wattage to start—so a fridge that runs at 150W may need a 600W surge to start the compressor. Always match your devices' starting wattage (not just running wattage) to the station's surge spec.
