## What to Look for in a High Chair

### Safety First
Look for JPMA certification (Juvenile Products Manufacturers Association). All high chairs should have:
- 5-point harness (shoulder, waist, crotch straps)
- Crotch post to prevent baby from slipping under the tray
- Stable base that won't tip

### Cleaning Reality
Fabric-padded seats look nice in product photos; they become stained within weeks. Leatherette or smooth plastic wipes down in seconds. Unless you plan to cover with a liner, choose an easy-clean surface.

### Adjustability
Look for adjustable seat height (to fit different tables), adjustable footrest (supports proper posture), and recline positions if you want to feed a newborn.

### Longevity
A high chair used 3x/day for 2 years is 2,190 meals. Budget chairs ($50–$80) often wobble or break before year 2. Spending $150–$300 typically pays off in durability.

## FAQ

**Q: When can my baby use a high chair?**
A: Most babies are ready around 4–6 months when they can hold their head upright independently. Look for a chair with a reclining position if you want to start earlier.

**Q: Do I need a high chair if I have a booster seat?**
A: Booster seats (strapped to a dining chair) work well for older babies/toddlers who can sit independently. For babies under 1 year, a full high chair provides better support.

**Q: How do I clean mold from a high chair?**
A: Prevention is key. Remove the cover and wash weekly. For existing mold, use white vinegar diluted 1:1 with water, then dry completely. Consider switching to a non-fabric chair.

**Q: What age do kids stop using high chairs?**
A: Usually 2–3 years old, when they're stable enough to sit in a regular chair or booster seat.
