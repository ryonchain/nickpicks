## Electric Grill Buying Guide

**Indoor vs. outdoor use:**
True indoor use requires a smoke-reducing design with a drip tray that catches grease before it hits the heating element. Regular outdoor electric grills will smoke indoors on fatty proteins.

**Temperature:**
Look for grills that reach at least 450°F for searing. Many budget models top out at 375°F — enough for chicken and vegetables but won't give you a proper steak sear.

**Grate material:**
- **Cast iron:** Best heat retention and sear marks. Heavier, requires seasoning.
- **Porcelain enamel:** Easy clean, rust-resistant, good heat distribution.
- **Non-stick:** Easiest maintenance, but coatings wear faster at high heat.

**Size:**
- Indoor countertop: 50–100 sq inches cooking area (1–2 people)
- Balcony/patio: 200–300 sq inches (2–4 people)
- Full outdoor: 300+ sq inches (4–6 people)

**Wattage:**
Higher wattage = faster heat-up and higher max temps:
- 1,200–1,500W: Entry-level, slower
- 1,500–1,800W: Mid-range, adequate
- 1,800–2,000W: Premium, fastest recovery between items

## FAQ

**Can an electric grill taste like a real BBQ?**
Electric grills won't replicate smoke flavor — that's charcoal and wood territory. But a quality electric grill at high heat produces excellent grill marks, proper Maillard crust, and genuinely great-tasting food.

**Are electric grills allowed on apartment balconies?**
Most apartment buildings allow electric grills on balconies — but always check your lease/HOA rules first. Gas and charcoal are typically prohibited; electric is almost always permitted.

**How do I clean an electric grill?**
Let it cool fully. Remove the grates and drip tray (most are dishwasher-safe). Wipe the interior with a damp cloth. Never submerge the electrical components.

**What's the best protein to start with on an electric grill?**
Chicken thighs are forgiving and showcase what a good electric grill can do. Burgers are the real test — if your grill reaches 475°F+, you'll get a satisfying crust.
