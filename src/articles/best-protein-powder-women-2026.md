## How to Choose the Right Protein Powder

### Whey vs Plant-Based

- **Whey isolate:** Fastest-absorbing, highest protein density, best for post-workout recovery. Contains dairy.
- **Whey concentrate:** Similar benefits, slightly higher carbs/fat, and cheaper.
- **Plant-based:** Best for vegans or dairy-intolerant. Pea + rice blends match whey amino acid profiles.
- **Collagen:** Best for skin/joint benefits; not a complete protein — pair with whey or plant.

### What to Avoid

- **Protein blends with amino acid spiking:** Low-quality brands add cheap amino acids (glycine, taurine) to inflate protein counts. Look for "whey isolate" or "hydrolyzed whey" as the first ingredient.
- **Excess sugar:** Any protein powder with more than 5g sugar per serving is adding unnecessary calories.
- **Proprietary blends:** If the label says "Protein Matrix 28g" without telling you how much of each ingredient, avoid it.

### How Much Protein Do Women Need?

Research supports 0.7–1.0g of protein per pound of body weight for active women. For a 140-lb woman doing 4× weekly workouts, that's 100–140g/day. Most women get 60–80g from food — protein powder fills the gap.

## FAQ

**Is whey protein good for women?**
Yes — whey is the most research-backed protein supplement for muscle retention and recovery. The "women's formula" marketing is usually just a higher price on the same product.

**What protein powder won't cause bloating?**
Whey isolate (not concentrate) is lowest in lactose. Plant-based proteins from pea are generally well-tolerated. Avoid sugar alcohols (xylitol, sorbitol) which cause GI distress.

**Can protein powder help with weight loss?**
Protein is the most satiating macronutrient — it reduces hunger. A protein shake replacing a 500-calorie meal is effective for weight management. The protein powder itself doesn't cause weight loss; the calorie deficit does.
