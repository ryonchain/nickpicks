  - question: "What causes hairballs in cats?"
    answer: "Cats swallow loose hair when grooming — the tongue's backward-facing barbs (papillae) trap hair, which gets swallowed. Most hair passes through the GI tract normally, but some accumulates in the stomach. When the mass becomes too large to pass, the cat regurgitates it — often in the classic cylindrical tube shape. Long-haired cats, heavy groomers, and cats with anxiety-related over-grooming are most prone to hairballs."
  - question: "How often is it normal for a cat to have hairballs?"
    answer: "Most cats have hairballs occasionally — typically 1–3 times per month for long-haired breeds, less frequently for short-haired cats. More than once per week suggests excessive hair ingestion or GI motility issues. Frequent unsuccessful retching (without producing a hairball) can indicate a blockage rather than a normal hairball and warrants immediate veterinary attention."
  - question: "When is a hairball an emergency?"
    answer: "Seek veterinary care immediately if your cat: retches repeatedly without producing a hairball, shows loss of appetite lasting more than 24 hours, appears lethargic or in pain, has a distended abdomen, or shows signs of constipation alongside retching. These can indicate a GI obstruction (hairball block) or other serious condition. A hairball that cannot be passed can cause life-threatening intestinal blockage."

## The Prevention Approach (Most Effective Long-Term)

**Regular grooming** removes loose hair before it's swallowed — the single most effective hairball prevention measure. For long-haired cats, daily brushing can reduce hairball frequency by over 50%.

**Deshedding tools:** The FURminator removes undercoat hair that causes the most hairball accumulation. Weekly use for long-haired cats, biweekly for short-haired.

**Hydration:** Cats on wet food diets have better GI motility than cats eating only dry food. Adding water or switching partially to wet food can reduce hairball frequency.

**Environmental enrichment:** Cats that over-groom due to stress or boredom benefit from increased play, environmental enrichment, and anxiety management.

## When Home Remedies Aren't Enough

See your vet if:
- Hairball frequency suddenly increases without diet or grooming changes
- Cat appears to be gagging frequently without producing hairballs
- You notice blood in vomit or feces
- Cat is losing weight alongside hairball issues
- Constipation accompanies hairball retching

These can signal inflammatory bowel disease, GI lymphoma (common in older cats), or motility disorders that require diagnosis and medical management.
