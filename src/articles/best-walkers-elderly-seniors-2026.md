# Best Walkers for Elderly and Seniors in 2026

Walkers provide stability for people who need more support than a cane but retain significant walking ability. The main decision: standard walker (lift-and-place), two-wheel walker, or four-wheel rollator with hand brakes and a built-in seat. Each suits a different mobility profile.

---

## Walker Types at a Glance

| Type | How It Works | Best For |
|------|-------------|----------|
| Standard (no wheels) | Lift, place, step | Maximum stability; slowest |
| Two-wheel walker | Front wheels roll, back legs drag | Medium stability, easier movement |
| Rollator (4 wheels + brakes) | All wheels roll; hand brakes to stop | More mobile users; distance walking |
| Knee walker | Padded platform for injured leg | Post-foot/ankle surgery only |

## How to Choose the Right Walker Height

Incorrect handle height is the most common setup error. Correct height: standing upright with arms at sides, the handle should be at wrist height. Handles too low cause hunching; too high causes shoulder strain and reduces stability.

**General guide by user height:**
- 5'0"–5'3": Handle height 32"–34"
- 5'4"–5'7": Handle height 35"–37"
- 5'8"–6'0": Handle height 37"–38"
- 6'0"+: Handle height 38"–40" (verify rollator adjusts this high)

---

## Rollator Brakes Explained

**Loop brakes:** Squeeze to engage. Safe default — if you fall forward, hands naturally grip and brakes engage.

**Push-down brakes:** Push handle down to lock. Good for sitting, but can be awkward during a fall.

**Recommendation:** Loop brakes for most users.
