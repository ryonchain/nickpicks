## Buying Guide: How to Choose Garden Tools

**Handle ergonomics:** The single biggest quality differentiator. Ergonomic handles (Radius, Felco, WOLF-Garten) reduce wrist and forearm strain over long sessions. If you garden more than an hour per session, ergonomics are worth paying for.

**Steel quality:** Forged steel heads (Felco, WOLF-Garten, Fiskars) are stronger and stay sharper longer than stamped steel (budget sets). For trowels and cultivators, the difference is most visible in rocky or compacted soil where cheap tools bend.

**Handle material:** Fiberglass handles are stronger and lighter than wood. Wood is traditional and easy to repair but can dry and crack. Polymer handles (Radius) are lightest and resist rot. All are serviceable — it's more about preference.

**Repairability:** Premium tools (Felco, WOLF-Garten) sell replacement parts — blades, springs, grips. This is worth paying for if you want tools that last decades. Budget tools are disposable by design.

**Set vs. individual:** Sets offer convenience and matched handle aesthetics. Individual tools let you get the best in each category. For serious gardeners, mixing specific individual picks usually beats any single set.

## FAQ

**How do I keep garden tools sharp?**
For pruners: use a whetstone or diamond file on the beveled edge after every few sessions. For trowels and hoes: a metal file keeps the edge functional. Clean and dry tools after every use — rust dulls edges fast.

**Should I oil wooden garden tool handles?**
Yes. Linseed oil applied once or twice a season protects wood from cracking and drying. Avoid leaving wood-handled tools outside in rain or sun for extended periods.

**How often do pruner blades need replacing?**
For premium pruners like the Felco 2: replacement blades are available and often restore the tool to like-new performance. Budget pruners generally aren't worth resharpening — replace the whole tool.

**What's the difference between bypass and anvil pruners?**
Bypass pruners cut with a scissor action — one sharp blade passes a flat blade. They make cleaner cuts with less crushing, which is better for plant health. Anvil pruners have one blade that cuts against a flat surface — more leverage for dead wood, but more likely to crush living stems.
