## Tripod Buying Guide

### The Key Specs That Actually Matter

**Packed length:** For travel, you want the tripod to fit in or on your bag without sticking out awkwardly. Under 18" packed is genuinely travel-friendly. 20"+ means it needs its own bag or to be strapped externally.

**Weight capacity:** Use 2× your camera + heaviest lens weight as the minimum rating. A camera that's at the rated limit of the head will introduce micro-vibrations at longer exposures.

**Leg locks:** Twist-lock (rotate to lock) is faster to operate than flip-lock (lever to lock). Both are reliable when maintained; flip-lock loosens faster with heavy use.

**Height without center column:** The center column extends height but introduces vibration. If you shoot long exposures or video, the tripod height without extending the column is what matters — look for a leg height that works at your needed height without relying on the column.

### Carbon Fiber vs Aluminum

Carbon fiber is lighter (15–25% weight savings) and dampens vibrations slightly better. Aluminum is heavier but more durable against impacts and dings. For most photographers, aluminum is the right choice at any price under $200. Carbon fiber earns its premium for frequent travelers and landscape photographers working in cold conditions where aluminum conducts cold uncomfortably.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What quick-release system should I use?**
Arca-Swiss is the industry standard for interoperability — plates from one brand work with heads from other brands. Manfrotto's RC2 system is only compatible with Manfrotto heads. If you might mix brands or upgrade components, Arca-Swiss is the better long-term choice.

**Can I use a camera tripod for phone shooting?**
Yes — phone mounts that clip onto any standard tripod ball head are inexpensive (~$10–$20). If you shoot primarily with a phone but want the option to add a camera later, a camera-rated tripod with a phone mount adapter is more versatile than a phone-specific tripod.

**How low should a tripod go?**
For macro, flat-lay, and landscape photography, low minimum height matters. The best low-angle trick: reverse the center column (available on most tripods) to mount the camera upside-down between the legs, reaching within 2–3 inches of the ground. Check if your tripod supports reversed center column before buying.
