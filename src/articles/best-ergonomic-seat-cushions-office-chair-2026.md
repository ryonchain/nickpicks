## How Seat Cushions Improve Posture and Reduce Pain

When a chair seat compresses or provides insufficient support, the pelvis tilts backward (posterior pelvic tilt). This flattens the lumbar spine's natural inward curve, increases compression on the posterior intervertebral discs, and creates the characteristic lower back ache of a long desk day.

A well-designed seat cushion works by:

1. **Elevating and leveling the ischial tuberosities** (sit bones), which relieves pressure concentrations
2. **Supporting a slight anterior pelvic tilt**, which restores the lumbar curve and reduces disc compression
3. **Eliminating coccyx contact** (via the U-shape cutout), which prevents tailbone pain during extended sitting

The cumulative effect is measurable: correctly positioned, a quality seat cushion can reduce lumbar load during sitting by 15–30% compared to an unsupported compressed chair.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the best ergonomic seat cushion for an office chair?**
The Everlasting Comfort Seat Cushion is the best overall — high-density memory foam, U-shaped coccyx cutout, non-slip base, and a machine-washable cover. It outperforms most office chair stock foam at 10% of the price of a new chair.

**Do seat cushions really help with back pain?**
Yes, when the cushion corrects your pelvic posture. A wedge-style or forward-tilted cushion encourages the lumbar spine's natural inward curve, reducing disc compression and back muscle strain. Flat comfort cushions reduce pressure without correcting posture.

**What type of cushion is best for long hours of sitting?**
High-density memory foam (or gel + memory foam) for 6+ hours of daily use. Avoid cheap soft foam that compresses flat within weeks. The cushions in this roundup all use high-density or gel-infused foam rated for 12+ months of daily use.

**Should I get a coccyx cutout cushion?**
If you experience tailbone pressure or pain during sitting — yes, the cutout eliminates direct coccyx contact entirely. For general comfort without specific tailbone issues, a standard cushion shape is fine.

**How thick should a seat cushion be?**
3–4 inches for daily office use. Thinner compresses too quickly; thicker noticeably raises your seated height and may require lowering your chair (which can be the right trade-off for shorter users with high desks).
