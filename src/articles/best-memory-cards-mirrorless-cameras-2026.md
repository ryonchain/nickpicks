## Understanding Memory Card Specs

Before the picks, here's what the numbers mean:

### Read vs. Write Speed
**Read speed** is how fast data transfers from card to computer. **Write speed** is what matters while shooting — it determines how quickly the buffer clears for the next burst. Always check write speed, not just read speed.

### UHS Class (UHS-I vs UHS-II)
UHS-II cards have a second row of pins and max out around 312 MB/s. UHS-I cards peak at ~104 MB/s. If your camera has a UHS-II slot, use a UHS-II card in it. If it's UHS-I only, a UHS-II card works but performs at UHS-I speeds.

### Video Speed Class (V30, V60, V90)
The V number is the **minimum sustained write speed** in MB/s. V30 (30 MB/s) handles 4K video. V60 (60 MB/s) handles high-bitrate 4K and 6K. V90 (90 MB/s) handles 8K and RAW video recording. For stills-only shooters, V30 is sufficient.

### CFexpress Type A vs Type B
CFexpress A is smaller (similar to SD) and used in Sony Alpha bodies. CFexpress B is larger and used in pro-tier Nikon Z and Canon EOS R bodies for even higher write speeds.

## Buying Guide: How to Pick the Right Memory Card

### Step 1: Identify your camera's card slot(s)
Check your camera manual. It will specify: SD (UHS-I), UHS-II SD, CFexpress Type A, CFexpress Type B, XQD, or dual-slot configurations. This single step eliminates most buying mistakes.

### Step 2: Match the speed to your shooting style
Landscape and portrait photographers who don't shoot long bursts can use V30 cards without issue. Sports and wildlife photographers who shoot 10+ fps in RAW need V60 or faster cards to keep the buffer from filling during action sequences.

### Step 3: Buy a fast card reader too
A fast card speeds up your workflow both in-camera (buffer clearing) and at the computer (file transfer). A USB 2.0 reader will bottleneck a 300 MB/s UHS-II card. Buy the matching reader tier.

### Step 4: Avoid counterfeit cards
Always buy from Amazon's sold-by or fulfilled-by Amazon listings, or from authorized camera retailers. Counterfeit SanDisk and Lexar cards are common on marketplace listings. Stick to reputable sellers.

## FAQ

**Q: What memory card do I need for 4K video?**
For 4K video, use a V30 card at minimum (30 MB/s sustained write). For high-bitrate 4K (100+ Mbps) or 6K recording, use V60. For 8K or RAW video, use V90 or CFexpress.

**Q: Is a 256GB card better than two 128GB cards?**
Two smaller cards give you redundancy during a shoot — if one fails, you haven't lost everything. Many dual-slot cameras can write to both cards simultaneously for backup. For critical shoots, two cards is the professional approach.

**Q: Do memory cards expire or wear out?**
Flash memory has a finite write cycle count (typically millions of cycles for modern cards). In practice, most photographers replace cards due to capacity constraints long before hitting write-cycle limits. Cards used for heavy video recording do accumulate writes faster.

**Q: How much storage do I need per shoot?**
A 24MP RAW file is approximately 25–30 MB. A 64GB card holds roughly 2,000–2,500 RAW files. For a full day wedding shoot (1,000+ images + video), 128GB in slot 1 with 128GB backup in slot 2 is the standard pro approach.

**Q: Can I use a CFexpress card as a regular SD card?**
No. CFexpress cards use a different physical connector and are not backward-compatible with SD slots. CFexpress Type A cards are the same physical size as SD but have a different pin layout and require a CFexpress A slot or adapter.
