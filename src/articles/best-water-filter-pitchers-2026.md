## Water Filter Pitcher Buying Guide

### Understanding NSF Certifications

NSF International independently certifies water filters. Look for these specific certifications:
- **NSF 42:** Reduces chlorine taste and odor
- **NSF 53:** Reduces health-relevant contaminants including lead, VOCs, mercury
- **NSF 401:** Reduces emerging contaminants (pharmaceuticals, hormones)
- **NSF P473:** Reduces PFAS (perfluoroalkyl substances — "forever chemicals")

**Important:** A filter certified for NSF 42 only reduces taste/odor. If you need lead or PFAS reduction, verify NSF 53 and P473 certification specifically.

### Do You Need to Know What's in Your Water?

Before choosing a filter, consider getting a water quality test:
- **Free option:** Request your utility's annual Consumer Confidence Report (required by EPA for public water systems). Lists what's tested for and levels found.
- **Paid option:** Mail-in water tests from labs like Tap Score ($35–$200) test for specific contaminants not in utility reports.

If your area has known issues (lead pipes, agricultural area, industrial nearby), invest in Clearly Filtered or equivalent.

Water filter pitchers are convenient but limited by capacity. An under-sink filter (like Aquasana or iSpring) provides unlimited filtered water on demand. If you use more than 2–3 pitchers per day or want to filter cooking water as well as drinking water, an under-sink filter has better long-term economics.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Does Brita remove lead?**
The Brita Standard filter does NOT significantly reduce lead. For lead removal, choose PUR PLUS, Clearly Filtered, or Brita's own Longlast+ filter (which does add lead reduction). If you have old pipes, this distinction matters.

**How often should I change my water filter?**
Follow the manufacturer's recommended schedule (typically every 2 months or 40–100 gallons). Skipping changes doesn't just reduce effectiveness — a clogged filter can actually release trapped contaminants back into filtered water.

**Does filtered water taste better?**
Yes, noticeably. Chlorine is the biggest contributor to tap water taste and odor, and all certified pitchers significantly reduce it. Most people notice an immediate improvement in taste.

**Can I use a pitcher filter for well water?**
Standard pitcher filters are not designed for well water, which may contain bacteria, nitrates, iron, or other contaminants not addressed by carbon filters. Well water should be professionally tested, and a whole-house or reverse osmosis system is usually recommended.

**Are the plastics safe?**
All reputable water filter pitchers are BPA-free. Most use polypropylene or Tritan plastic. If you're concerned about plastics leaching, Clearly Filtered uses premium Tritan plastic with the fewest plastic-related concerns of any pitcher on this list.
