## Frequently Asked Questions

### What hiking gear is actually necessary vs. nice-to-have?
Non-negotiables for any hike over 2 miles: water (or hydration pack), headlamp, and first aid kit. Trekking poles become important on hikes with significant descent or loose terrain. A pack large enough to carry layers, food, and safety gear is essential for hikes over 5 miles or 3+ hours.

### How much water should I bring on a hike?
The general rule is 0.5L per hour of hiking activity in moderate temperatures. Add 25% in heat or high exertion. For a 4-hour hike, carry 2L minimum; use a 3L reservoir if you'll be in hot conditions or aren't sure of water sources. Never rely on finding water on trail without a purification method.

### Are trekking poles worth it for beginner hikers?
Especially for beginners — and especially on descents. Trekking poles reduce the shock on knee joints during downhill sections (the hardest part of most day hikes on your body) and provide balance on rocky or root-covered terrain. If you've ever experienced knee soreness after a hike, poles will dramatically reduce it.

### What's the best hiking backpack size for day hikes?
15–25L is the ideal range for day hikes. Under 15L gets tight for all-day outings with layers and food. Over 30L becomes cumbersome for single-day use. The Osprey Daylite Plus at 20L is the most recommended size for most day hikers.

### Can I use a regular flashlight instead of a headlamp?
Technically yes, but a headlamp is significantly better — it keeps your hands free for trekking poles, scrambling, and map reading. A handheld flashlight is a second choice for emergencies but should never be your primary light source on trail.
