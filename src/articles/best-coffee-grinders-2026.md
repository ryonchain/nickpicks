# Best Coffee Grinders 2026: Burr vs. Blade — Which Should You Buy?

Grinding coffee fresh is the single biggest upgrade you can make to your home coffee routine. Pre-ground coffee starts going stale within minutes of opening the bag. Grinding immediately before brewing preserves volatile aromatic compounds that define a coffee's character — the difference is noticeable in your first sip.

But not all grinders are equal. Blade grinders chop unevenly, producing a mix of coarse chunks and fine powder that results in inconsistent extraction. Burr grinders crush beans between two abrasive surfaces, producing a uniform grind size that brews evenly. If you're serious about coffee — even just drip — a burr grinder is the right choice. Here's how to find yours.

## Buying Guide: Choosing the Right Coffee Grinder

### Burr Grinder Types

**Conical burr grinders** (Baratza Encore, Breville Smart Grinder Pro) have a cone-shaped inner burr that nests inside a ring outer burr. Versatile, durable, and suitable for all brew methods including espresso. Slightly more affordable and more heat-tolerant than flat burrs.

**Flat burr grinders** (Fellow Ode Gen 2) have two parallel rings of burrs that produce extremely uniform particle sizes — coveted for filter coffee for the "sweet" flavor profile they develop. More expensive and typically optimized for filter only or espresso only, not both.

**Hand grinders** (Timemore C3) use conical burrs powered by your arm. Slower, but produce excellent results and are genuinely portable.

### Grind Settings: How Many Do You Need?

- **For drip coffee only:** 5–10 settings is sufficient
- **For pour-over + French press:** 10–20 settings to dial in different recipes
- **For espresso:** 30+ settings (espresso requires very fine adjustments to nail extraction)

### Single-Dose vs. Hopper Feeding

**Hopper-fed grinders** (Baratza Encore, Breville) hold a full bag of beans in an open hopper. Convenient for daily grinders who use the same beans. Beans exposed to air degrade faster.

**Single-dose grinders** (Fellow Ode Gen 2) are designed to grind only the beans you need for one brew. Better for enthusiasts who dial in different coffees daily or want to minimize stale bean exposure.

### Matching Grinder to Brew Method

| Brew Method | Grind Size | Recommended Grinder |
|-------------|-----------|-------------------|
| Espresso | Very fine | Baratza Encore ESP, Breville SGP |

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Is a burr grinder really better than a blade grinder?**
Yes — definitively. Burr grinders crush beans to a consistent size; blade grinders chop unevenly. Consistent grind size means even extraction, which means better-tasting coffee. Even the cheapest burr grinder (Timemore C3) produces better coffee than an expensive blade grinder.

**How often should I clean my coffee grinder?**
Weekly for heavy users (a quick brush of the burrs and grind chute), monthly for light users. A buildup of stale coffee oils makes fresh-ground coffee taste dull and slightly rancid. Most burr grinders include a cleaning brush.

**Can I use a coffee grinder for spices?**
You can — blade grinders are often used as spice grinders. However, spice flavors (especially cumin, chili) transfer to coffee and are difficult to remove. Keep a separate grinder for spices, or use the Hamilton Beach as a dedicated spice grinder.

**What grind size for pour-over coffee?**
Medium to medium-coarse — similar to coarse sea salt. Too fine causes over-extraction (bitter, astringent); too coarse causes under-extraction (sour, thin). Use the Fellow Ode Gen 2 or Baratza Encore for the best pour-over grind consistency.

**Do I need an expensive grinder for good espresso?**
For home espresso, the Baratza Encore ESP (~$169) and Breville Smart Grinder Pro (~$199) both produce acceptable results. Competition-level espresso benefits from $300+ grinders (Niche Zero, Eureka Mignon), but the flavor difference becomes meaningful only at very high espresso standards.
