## Our Top Picks

### 1. Squier Affinity Precision Bass — Best Beginner Bass Overall

**Price: ~$300**

Squier is Fender's budget subsidiary, and the Affinity series is Squier's second tier — significantly better than the Bullet series in hardware, fretwork, and finish quality. The Precision Bass (P-Bass) is the standard blueprint for bass guitar: split single-coil pickup, simple controls (volume + tone), and an alder body that produces the fundamental, punchy tone you hear on thousands of records.

**Why the P-Bass is the benchmark:**
- **Split single-coil pickup** (Precision pickup) — hum-canceling, defined low-end, less midrange color than Jazz Bass
- Alder body — consistent density, resonant
- C-shape maple neck — comfortable for most hand sizes
- 20 medium jumbo frets — easier note articulation than vintage narrow frets
- Three-saddle adjustable bridge — simple intonation setup
- Ships in multiple colors

**P-Bass vs. Jazz Bass:** The Precision has a thicker, punchier tone. The Jazz Bass has a thinner neck and more bite in the mids. Beginners who don't know which to pick: choose the P-Bass.

**Best for:** Any beginner who wants the defining bass tone — from Motown to punk to classic rock.

### 2. Squier Affinity Jazz Bass — Best for Versatility

**Price: ~$280**

The Jazz Bass shape has a thinner neck at the nut (1.5" vs. 1.625" on the P-Bass) — noticeably easier to play for smaller hands or guitarists transitioning to bass. The two single-coil pickups produce a brighter, more scooped tone that works better for funk, slap bass, and styles where midrange cut is the goal.

**Jazz Bass characteristics:**
- **Two single-coil pickups** (neck + bridge) — blend between them for tonal variety
- Thinner neck profile — easier for smaller hands and guitarists switching to bass
- 1.5" nut width — noticeably narrower than P-Bass
- Alder body, maple neck, rosewood fingerboard

**Limitation:** Two single-coil pickups produce hum when soloed. Run both pickups at even volume to cancel hum (a Jazz Bass quirk inherited from the original 1960 design).

**Best for:** Funk players, slap bass learners, guitarists transitioning to bass who want a familiar thin neck.

### 5. Squier Mini P Bass — Best for Short-Scale and Small Hands

**Price: ~$220**

Short-scale basses (30" scale vs. 34" standard) have a shorter distance between the nut and bridge, which reduces string tension. This makes them:
- Physically easier to play for small hands
- Better for guitarists who want to switch to bass (closer to guitar string tension)
- Physically smaller — appropriate for children and teens

The Squier Mini P Bass is a genuine Precision Bass with a 28.6" scale. It's not a toy — the same alder body and ceramic pickup as the full-size Affinity series, just compact.

**Best for:** Ages 10–14, guitarists switching to bass, anyone with small hands who finds full-scale bass uncomfortable.

## Amp Recommendations for Beginners

A bass requires a **bass amp**, not a guitar amp. Running bass through a guitar speaker damages the speaker (guitar speakers are not rated for the low frequencies bass produces).

**Budget practice amps ($80–130):**
- Fender Rumble 15 — 15W, 8" speaker, clean tone, headphone jack
- Ampeg BA-108v2 — 30W, 8" speaker, Ampeg tone character
- Orange Crush Bass 25 — 25W, 8" speaker, built-in tuner

**Entry studio amps ($200–300):**
- Fender Rumble 40 — 40W, 10" speaker, 3-band EQ, USB audio out
- Hartke HD25 — 25W, 2× 6.5" speakers, punchy tone
