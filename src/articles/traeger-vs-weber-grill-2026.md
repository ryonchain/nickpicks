Traeger vs Weber — two legendary brands that actually make very different types of grills. Traeger makes wood pellet smokers. Weber makes gas and charcoal grills. The question isn't really which brand is better — it's which cooking style fits your backyard and cooking goals.

---

## Quick Verdict

- **Buy Traeger** if: You want low-and-slow BBQ smoking with authentic wood-smoke flavor. Set it, forget it, and come back to perfectly smoked brisket, ribs, or salmon.
- **Buy Weber** if: You want fast, high-heat grilling with the convenience of gas or the tradition of charcoal. Weekend burgers, steaks, and chicken in 20 minutes.

---

## How They Work

**Traeger (Pellet Grill):** Burns compressed hardwood pellets fed automatically by an auger from a hopper. Generates indirect heat and wood smoke — creating the low-and-slow BBQ environment needed for smoking. WiFIRE controller maintains precise temperatures. Most models run 165°F–500°F. Does smoke, bake, roast, braise, and grill.

**Weber Gas (Spirit/Genesis):** Burns propane or natural gas with direct burner flame. Produces high, instant heat for traditional grilling. Grill-only function. 300°F–700°F+ range.

**Weber Charcoal (Kettle):** Burn charcoal for high heat or extended indirect cooking. Can smoke with the right setup but requires more attention.

---

## Smoke Flavor

**Traeger:** Deep, consistent wood smoke flavor. The pellet grill is designed around smoke, and the flavor shows. Briskets, pulled pork, ribs, and salmon from a Traeger are legitimately excellent — comparable to dedicated offset smokers with far less babysitting.

**Weber Gas:** Very little smoke flavor. The convenience of gas means no wood burning.

**Weber Charcoal Kettle:** More flavor than gas — charcoal adds character. With wood chunks added, you can approximate smoking, though not as effortlessly as Traeger.

**Winner:** Traeger for smoke flavor.

---

## Convenience

**Traeger:** Set the temperature, load the pellet hopper, walk away. The WiFIRE app lets you monitor and adjust from your phone. A 10-lb brisket at 225°F takes 12–14 hours — start it at 8pm and check in via app overnight. Minimal attention needed.

**Weber Gas:** Light burners, preheat in 10 minutes, grill, done. Fastest path from raw to cooked.

**Weber Charcoal:** Light charcoal (20-30 minutes to heat), grill, clean up ash. More involved than gas but manageable.

**Winner:** Weber Gas for speed. Traeger for effortless long cooks. Weber Charcoal is most involved.

---

## Versatility

**Traeger:** Smoke, bake, roast, braise, grill, sear (with GrillGrate inserts or sear box on Pro series). Can cook almost anything. Baking a pizza, roasting vegetables, or smoking a whole turkey are all doable.

**Weber Gas:** Grill, and with a lid, indirect roast. Can add wood chips for limited smoke. Less smoke depth than Traeger.

**Weber Kettle:** Grill, indirect roast, and with the SnokeyMountain setup — serious smoking. More versatile than gas in the right hands.

**Winner:** Traeger for versatility.

## Ongoing Costs

**Traeger:** Wood pellets cost ~$20–$25 per 20-lb bag. A long cook (12-hour brisket) uses about 10 lbs of pellets. Annual pellet cost for regular users: $100–$300.

**Weber Gas:** Propane refill ~$20 per tank. One tank lasts 10–15 hours of cooking. Annual cost for regular users: $80–$200.

**Weber Charcoal:** Charcoal briquettes cost ~$15–$25 per 20-lb bag. Annual cost: $60–$150.

**Winner:** Charcoal is cheapest. Gas and pellets are comparable.

## The Verdict

**Buy a Traeger Pro 575** if you want to get into smoking and BBQ without the steep learning curve of an offset smoker. Set-and-forget convenience plus genuine smoke flavor is a compelling combination. Worth the price if you'll use it weekly.

**Buy a Weber Spirit II or Genesis** if you grill burgers, chicken, steaks, and want the fastest path to dinner. Gas grills are practical, reliable, and fast.
