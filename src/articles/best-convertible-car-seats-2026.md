## Convertible vs. Infant Car Seat: Which Should You Buy?

**Choose a convertible if:**
- You want one seat to last from birth through booster age
- You don't need to carry baby in the seat (convertibles stay in the car)
- You have a larger vehicle (convertibles are heavier and wider)

**Choose an infant-only seat if:**
- You have a newborn and want the best fit for small babies
- You'll frequently transfer a sleeping baby between car and home
- You're building a travel system with a compatible stroller

Many families start with an infant seat, then transition to a convertible around 12 months when baby outgrows the infant seat.

## Key Buying Factors

### Rear-Facing Weight Limits
AAP recommends keeping children rear-facing as long as possible. Premium seats rear-face to 40–50 lbs; budget seats often limit to 35–40 lbs.

### Forward-Facing Limits
Harness weight limits range from 40 lbs to 65 lbs among top seats. Higher limits extend the harness stage, which is safer than transitioning to a booster early.

### Installation Method
LATCH (anchors in your vehicle) and seatbelt installation are both valid. ClickTight (Britax) and similar systems make seatbelt installation nearly foolproof.

### Width
Important if you have a compact vehicle or multiple seats. Narrow seats (Clek Foonf at 17") enable 3-across configurations.

## FAQ

**Q: How long should my child ride rear-facing?**
A: Until they reach the maximum height or weight limit of their rear-facing seat. The AAP says "as long as possible" — typically age 2–4 for most children.

**Q: Do convertible seats expire?**
A: Yes. Check the expiration date on the seat label — typically 6–10 years from manufacture date.

**Q: Can I use a convertible seat from birth?**
A: Yes, with the right insert. However, newborns (especially under 7 lbs) fit better in infant-only seats with proper newborn inserts.

**Q: Does my car need LATCH to use a convertible seat?**
A: No. All seats can be installed using the vehicle's seatbelt. LATCH is simply an alternative method.
