## Oscillating vs. Sonic: What the Difference Means

**Oscillating-rotating (Oral-B):** The round head rotates back and forth at 8,800 oscillations per minute, cleaning one tooth at a time. The motion mirrors proper manual brushing technique. Studies show slight edge in plaque and gingivitis reduction.

**Sonic (Philips Sonicare):** The brush head vibrates laterally at 31,000 strokes per minute. The high frequency creates fluid dynamics that clean between teeth and below the gumline without direct contact. Better for people with gum sensitivity and large interdental gaps.

**Which is clinically better?** Multiple meta-analyses show oscillating-rotating has a slight edge in plaque removal; sonic has a slight edge in gum health (gingivitis reduction). The difference is small — technique consistency matters more than technology choice. Pick the one you'll actually use consistently.

## Buying Guide: Choosing an Electric Toothbrush

### Brush Head Replacement Costs Add Up

Brush heads should be replaced every 3 months. Factor in ongoing costs when comparing brands:
- Oral-B CrossAction (Pro 1000): ~$7–10 per head
- Oral-B iO heads: ~$10–14 per head
- Sonicare heads: ~$8–12 per head
- Quip brush heads: ~$5 per head (subscription model)

Subscribe and Save (Amazon) typically reduces head costs by 10–15%.

### Pressure Sensors Are Worth Paying For

Overbrushing is the most common cause of gum recession, and it's irreversible. A pressure sensor that lights up red when you're pressing too hard prevents this. If you're between models, the one with a pressure sensor is almost always the better choice — even if it means spending $30 more.

### The 2-Minute Timer Is Non-Negotiable

Most people brush for 45–60 seconds. Dentists recommend 2 minutes (30 seconds per quadrant). Any electric toothbrush without a 2-minute timer is missing the most useful behavioral feature. All five models in this guide include it.

### Modes Beyond "Daily Clean" Are Nice But Not Critical

One mode (daily clean) delivers clinical results. Additional modes (whitening, sensitive, gum care) are useful if specific dental conditions require them. Don't pay a large premium just for mode count if your dentist hasn't identified a specific need.

## FAQ: Electric Toothbrushes

**Are electric toothbrushes better than manual?**
Yes — clinical evidence consistently shows 21% more plaque removal and 11% less gingivitis compared to manual brushes at 3 months. The advantage comes from consistent 2-minute timing and optimized brush head motion, not more scrubbing force.

**How often should I replace brush heads?**
Every 3 months, or when bristles visibly fray. Frayed bristles lose cleaning contact with tooth surfaces. Set a calendar reminder; most people forget.

**Can children use electric toothbrushes?**
Yes, from age 3 with parental supervision. Use a children's brush head with extra-soft bristles. Electric brushes can make brushing more engaging for reluctant kids. Avoid high-power modes on baby teeth.

**Are electric toothbrushes safe for braces?**
Yes — use an Orthodontic brush head designed for braces. Oral-B makes specific ortho heads; Sonicare's standard brush heads are also safe at lower intensities.

**Do electric toothbrushes whiten teeth?**
Mechanical polishing from electric brushes helps remove surface staining (coffee, tea, wine) more effectively than manual brushing. They don't penetrate enamel like chemical whitening treatments. For visible whitening results, a whitening mode plus whitening toothpaste helps, but bleaching trays or whitening strips are more effective.
