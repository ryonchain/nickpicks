## What Makes a Coffee Grinder Worth Buying

Four specs determine grind quality:

- **Burr vs. Blade:** Blade grinders chop beans randomly, producing uneven particles that brew simultaneously fast (fine dust = over-extracted bitter) and slow (large chunks = under-extracted sour). Burr grinders crush beans between two abrasive surfaces, producing uniform particle size that unlocks consistent flavor extraction.
- **Conical vs. Flat Burr:** Conical burrs are the standard for home grinders — quieter, lower RPM (less heat buildup), and self-cleaning. Flat burrs (used in commercial espresso machines) are more uniform at high precision but require more calibration and cleaning.
- **Grind Steps/Settings:** More steps = finer precision. A 40-step grinder (Baratza Encore) is excellent for drip and pour-over; a stepless grinder (Comandante) allows micro-adjustments critical for espresso dialing.
- **RPM:** Lower RPM generates less heat, which matters for preserving volatile aromatic compounds that evaporate at elevated temperatures. Under 400 RPM is ideal; above 1,000 RPM compromises flavor at high volumes.

## Buying Guide: What to Actually Look For

### Matching Grinder to Brew Method
Coarser grinds (French press, cold brew) are forgiving — a 15-setting grinder works fine. Finer grinds for espresso require precision — a single step adjustment changes extraction time by seconds and flavor significantly. Espresso grinders need 50+ settings or stepless adjustment. Drip and pour-over sit in the middle; 15–40 steps is adequate.

### Grind Retention and Freshness
Grind retention is the amount of ground coffee left inside the grinder between uses. A grinder with 2g retention contaminates the next batch with stale coffee; single-dose designs (Fellow Ode, Comandante) grind only what you need. For daily espresso drinkers, low retention matters more than for drip drinkers who grind in larger batches.

### Blade Grinder: When Is It Acceptable?
Blade grinders are acceptable for coarse French press where uneven extraction is partly masked by immersion brewing. They are not acceptable for espresso, pour-over, Aeropress, or any method where grind consistency directly affects flavor. A $30 entry-level conical burr grinder (Cuisinart DBM-8) is a meaningful upgrade over any blade grinder.

### Bean Freshness Matters More Than the Grinder
The best grinder in the world cannot fix stale beans. Fresh-roasted beans (bought within 2 weeks of roast date, stored in an airtight container) make a larger flavor difference than upgrading from a $100 grinder to a $300 one. Look for roast dates on bags; avoid supermarket beans with only "best by" dates.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**How often should I clean my coffee grinder?**
Grind 1 tablespoon of plain dry rice through the grinder monthly — it sweeps out oil-coated stale grounds from the burrs. Disassemble and brush the burrs with a stiff cleaning brush quarterly. Never wash burrs with water.

**Can I grind spices in a coffee grinder?**
Blade grinders are commonly used for spices. Burr grinders should not be used for spices — spice oils contaminate the burrs and produce off-flavors in subsequent coffee grinding that are difficult to remove.

**What's the ideal grind for different brew methods?**
Extra coarse: cold brew. Coarse: French press. Medium-coarse: Chemex. Medium: drip, flat-bottom pour-over. Medium-fine: Aeropress, Moka pot. Fine: espresso. Extra fine: Turkish coffee.

**Does grind size affect caffeine content?**
Finer grinds extract more caffeine (and more bitterness) in the same brew time. The caffeine content per bean is fixed — grind size affects how much is extracted into your cup during the brew window, not the total available.
