## Mechanical Keyboard Buying Guide

### Switches: Red vs. Brown vs. Blue

**Red (Linear):** Smooth keystroke with no tactile bump or click. Consistent actuation through the full press. Quieter than clicky switches. Best for: gaming (fast repeated keystrokes), users who don't want to feel the actuation point.

**Brown (Tactile):** Tactile bump at the actuation point (you feel when the key registers) with no audible click. The office "middle ground." Best for: office use where you want tactile feedback without annoying coworkers.

**Blue (Clicky):** Tactile bump AND audible click sound. Satisfying but loud — audible through microphones, in offices, and to anyone nearby. Best for: home offices or dedicated gaming rooms where noise is not a concern.

**Yellow/Speed (Linear):** Lighter actuation force than Red, shorter travel. Best for: fast typists and competitive gaming.

### Hotswap: Why It Matters

Hotswap PCBs let you pull switches with a switch puller and press in new ones — no soldering. This matters because: (1) you can try different switches without buying a new keyboard, (2) you can replace a damaged switch, and (3) you can upgrade switches as your preferences evolve. In 2026, paying for a non-hotswap keyboard is a false economy unless you're certain you'll never want to change switches.

### Form Factor

**Full-size (100%):** Includes numpad. Best for: spreadsheet work, data entry. Largest footprint.
**Tenkeyless (TKL, 87%):** No numpad. Better mouse ergonomics. Most popular for office/gaming balance.
**75%:** Compact with arrow keys and function row. Good for small desks.
**65%:** Arrow keys, no function row. Very compact.
**60%:** Minimal — no arrows, no function row. Smallest; requires key combinations for navigation.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the difference between red, brown, and blue switches?**
Red switches are linear (smooth, no bump, quieter). Brown switches are tactile (you feel a bump at actuation, no click sound). Blue switches are clicky (bump + audible click). For home offices: brown or red. For home-only use where noise doesn't matter: blue. For gaming: red or yellow linear for fastest response.

**Are mechanical keyboards worth it for typing (not gaming)?**
Yes — mechanical keyboards improve typing speed, accuracy, and reduce finger fatigue for most users. The tactile feedback of brown switches in particular helps you type more lightly (you know the key registered, so you stop pressing before the bottom). The longevity (50–100 million keystrokes vs. 5 million for membrane) also makes them cost-effective over time.

**What is hotswap and do I need it?**
Hotswap means you can swap switches without soldering. You need it if: (1) you're new to mechanical keyboards and might want to try different switches, (2) you want the option to replace damaged switches yourself, or (3) you want to upgrade your switches later. If you're certain about your switch preference and are experienced, a soldered board is fine — but hotswap has essentially no downside.

**Are wireless mechanical keyboards reliable for gaming?**
Yes — in 2026, 2.4 GHz wireless keyboards (not just Bluetooth) have latency indistinguishable from wired in controlled tests. Bluetooth has marginally more latency and is better for office/multi-device use. For competitive gaming, 2.4 GHz wireless is equivalent to wired. The Keychron Q3 Pro's 2.4 GHz mode is effectively lag-free.
