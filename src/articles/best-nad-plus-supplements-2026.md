    answer: "NAD+ (nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide) is a coenzyme found in every cell of the body — essential for energy metabolism (converting food to ATP), DNA repair (via PARP enzymes), and the activation of sirtuins (proteins involved in longevity signaling).   - question: "What is the difference between NMN and NR?"
    answer: "Both NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) and NR (nicotinamide riboside) are NAD+ precursors that raise intracellular NAD+ levels, but through different pathways. NR → NMN → NAD+ (NR enters cells directly and converts to NMN). NMN → NAD+ (some evidence NMN requires conversion to NR first before entering cells). In practice, both raise NAD+ effectively in human studies. Key differences: NMN has slightly more recent human clinical data and can be taken in higher doses; NR (NIAGEN) has been studied longer in humans with a well-established safety profile. Several head-to-head studies show comparable NAD+ elevation."
