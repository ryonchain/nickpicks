## Types of Vacuum Sealers

**External (edge) sealers:** Most home models. Insert the bag, the machine removes air and heat-seals the edge. Great for dry and semi-moist foods. Can't handle liquids well (liquids get sucked into the pump).

**Chamber sealers:** The bag goes entirely inside a chamber. Air is removed from the entire chamber, so liquids stay in the bag (no suction). Used commercially. Better performance but larger and more expensive.

## What You Can Vacuum Seal

**Extends shelf life dramatically:**

| Food | Regular Storage | Vacuum Sealed |
|------|----------------|---------------|
| Fresh meat (refrigerator) | 3-5 days | 2 weeks |
| Fresh meat (freezer) | 6 months | 2-3 years |
| Hard cheese | 1-2 weeks | 4-8 months |
| Coffee beans | 2-4 weeks (open) | 6-12 months |
| Dry goods (flour, pasta) | 1 year | 2+ years |
| Berries (refrigerator) | 3-5 days | 1-2 weeks |

**Cannot vacuum seal effectively:**
- Carbonated liquids
- Garlic in oil (botulism risk in anaerobic environments)
- Soft cheeses (crushes under pressure)
- Whole raw mushrooms (off-gassing)

## FAQ

**Q: Is vacuum sealing worth it?**
A: For people who buy in bulk, meal prep, or throw away food regularly — absolutely. A sealer typically pays for itself in 3-6 months of reduced food waste and bulk purchase savings.

**Q: Can I vacuum seal fresh garlic?**
A: No — garlic in oil sealed anaerobically creates botulism risk. Always refrigerate and use within a week, or freeze first.

**Q: Do I need special bags?**
A: You need textured/embossed bags (smooth bags won't seal properly). Generic brands work fine.

**Q: Can I reuse vacuum sealer bags?**
A: Yes, for non-meat foods. Wash, dry completely, and re-seal. Not recommended for raw meat due to bacteria.
