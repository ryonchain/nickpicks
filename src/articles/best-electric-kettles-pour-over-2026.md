## What Makes a Pour-Over Kettle Different

Standard electric kettles heat to boiling and pour fast — fine for tea bags and instant oatmeal. Pour-over coffee needs two things a standard kettle can't provide:

**Temperature precision.** Coffee extraction is temperature-sensitive. Boiling water (212°F) extracts too aggressively and produces harsh, bitter coffee. The ideal window for most pour-overs is 195–205°F. A variable temperature kettle lets you dial in the exact temperature — especially useful when you're dialing in a new bean or roast level.

**A gooseneck spout.** The thin, curved neck creates a narrow, controllable stream. This lets you bloom grounds evenly (saturating all grounds uniformly before the main pour), pour in slow concentric circles, and control flow rate deliberately. A fast-pour standard spout makes all of this nearly impossible.

**Temperature hold.** A good pour-over takes 3–4 minutes. You want the water at temperature throughout, not cooling as you pour. Hold mode keeps the water at exactly your set temperature for as long as needed.

| Roast Level | Recommended Temperature | Why |
|-------------|------------------------|-----|
| Light | 200–205°F | Light roasts need higher temps to fully extract |
| Medium | 195–200°F | Sweet spot for balanced extraction |
| Dark | 190–195°F | Lower temp prevents harsh bitter notes |
