## What Makes a Good Bedroom Space Heater

### Noise Level
This is the #1 consideration most buyers overlook. Fan-based ceramic heaters are always audible to some degree — the question is whether the noise is at an acceptable level for sleep. Oil-filled radiators (De'Longhi) and bladeless designs (Dyson) are the quietest. Look for "quiet operation" claims from reputable brands, not generic no-name units.

### Thermostat Control
A thermostat that cycles the heater off when the room reaches your target temperature is essential for overnight use. Without it, the heater runs continuously — wasting electricity and potentially creating a too-warm room at 2 AM.

### Timer Function
A sleep timer (4-hour, 8-hour, or custom) lets you fall asleep with the heater on and have it shut off automatically. Prevents running a heater all night if you wake up warm or kick off covers.

### Safety Certifications
Look for UL or ETL certification. All heaters on this list are certified. Tip-over protection and overheat protection are mandatory — not optional features.

### Placement
Keep the heater at least 3 feet from the bed, bedding, and curtains. Never put it under a nightstand or enclosed in furniture. Always plug directly into the wall — not an extension cord.

## Bedroom Space Heater FAQs

**Is it safe to sleep with a space heater on?**
Yes, with caveats: use a thermostat-controlled heater, keep it 3 feet from any flammable material, plug directly into a wall outlet, and choose a unit with tip-over and overheat protection. The De'Longhi oil-filled radiator is the safest overnight choice on this list.

**What wattage do I need for a bedroom?**
Most bedrooms (150–300 sq ft) work well with a 750W–1,500W heater. Use 1,500W for cold rooms or those without good insulation. 500W personal heaters (like the Honeywell HCE100) are fine for small rooms with mild winters.

**Will a space heater raise my electric bill significantly?**
At 1,500W and 8 hours nightly, a bedroom heater costs roughly $25–$40/month at average U.S. rates. If it replaces whole-house heating in a cold room, it may actually save money by letting you lower the thermostat elsewhere.

**Can I put a space heater in a bedroom closet or under a bed?**
Never. Heaters require open air circulation and clearance from all sides. Enclosed spaces are a serious fire risk.
