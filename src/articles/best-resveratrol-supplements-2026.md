    answer: "Resveratrol is a polyphenol compound produced by plants (particularly grapes, Japanese knotweed, and peanuts) in response to stress. It gained scientific prominence from studies showing it activates SIRT1 — a sirtuin protein linked to longevity and metabolic regulation. Evidence-supported benefits include: cardiovascular protection (reduces LDL oxidation, improves endothelial function), anti-inflammatory effects (inhibits NF-κB and COX-2), metabolic benefits (improves insulin sensitivity in some populations), and neuroprotection.   - question: "Why is resveratrol bioavailability such a problem?"
    answer: "Resveratrol is absorbed efficiently (~70% absorbed in the GI tract) but undergoes rapid metabolism — converted to sulfate and glucuronide conjugates in the intestinal epithelium and liver, reducing circulating free resveratrol to minimal levels. This is why eating red wine provides almost no meaningful resveratrol dose despite the popular narrative (a glass of wine contains ~1–2mg resveratrol; studies use 100–1,000mg). Solutions: (1) nanotechnology or liposomal encapsulation increases bioavailability; (2) micronized trans-resveratrol absorbs faster than standard powder; (3) pairing with piperine inhibits the glucuronidation that clears resveratrol rapidly."
  - question: "What is the difference between trans-resveratrol and cis-resveratrol?"
    answer: "Trans-resveratrol is the biologically active form — it's the isomer that activates SIRT1 and demonstrates the clinical effects studied in research. Cis-resveratrol is the inactive form. Many products list total 'resveratrol' content without specifying the isomer ratio. Always look for 'trans-resveratrol' specifically — products from Japanese knotweed extract that specify trans-resveratrol at ≥98% are the highest quality commercial source."
  - question: "Does drinking red wine provide resveratrol benefits?"
