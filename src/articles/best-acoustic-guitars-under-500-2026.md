## Understanding Tonewoods

### Tops (most important for tone)

| Tonewood | Sound Character | Best For |
|---|---|---|
| Sitka Spruce | Bright, articulate, balanced | Strumming, versatile players |
| Cedar | Warm, soft, immediate response | Fingerpicking, light touch |
| Mahogany | Warm, dry, focused midrange | Blues, singer-songwriter |

### Back & Sides (important but secondary)

| Tonewood | Sound Character |
|---|---|
| Rosewood | Rich, complex, broad frequency range |
| Mahogany | Warm, punchy, dry midrange |
| Nato/Walnut | Budget rosewood substitute — similar profile |
| Wild Cherry | Between mahogany and rosewood |

### Solid vs Laminate

**Solid wood:** Resonates freely, opens up with age, sounds better over time. The top is most important — a solid top with laminate back/sides (like the FG800) is a good compromise.

**Laminate:** Multiple thin layers bonded together. More resistant to humidity changes, less resonant, doesn't improve with age. Used in most sub-$200 guitars.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What's the best first acoustic guitar?**
The Yamaha FG800 at $250. Solid top, reliable tuners, consistent factory setup. It won't embarrass you when you improve.

**Should I buy new or used?**
Used acoustic guitars in good condition at $200–$400 often offer more guitar-per-dollar than new equivalents. Check the neck angle, tuning stability, and any top cracks before buying.

**Do I need a pickup/electronics?**
Only if you plan to perform live. For home practice and recording directly into a microphone, a plain acoustic is ideal.
