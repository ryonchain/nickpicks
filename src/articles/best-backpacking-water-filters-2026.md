## Filter vs. Purifier: Understanding the Difference

| Technology | Removes | Misses | Best For |
|------------|---------|--------|----------|
| Mechanical filter (Sawyer, LifeStraw) | Bacteria, protozoa, microplastics | Viruses | US/Canada backcountry |
| UV purifier (SteriPen) | Bacteria, protozoa, viruses | Heavy sediment | International travel, fast-moving |
| Chemical treatment (iodine, Aquatabs) | All pathogens | Heavy sediment | Emergency backup |
| Purifier pump (MSR Guardian) | Everything including viruses | Nothing | International, military use |

**For North American backcountry (typical use):** A mechanical filter (Sawyer Squeeze, BeFree) is fully adequate. Giardia and Cryptosporidium are the primary risks; viruses are rare in wilderness water.

**For international travel or areas with human contamination:** Choose UV or a purifier pump.

## Water Sources and Field Tips

### Best water sources (cleanest first):
1. High-elevation snowmelt streams with no upstream human activity
2. Moving streams far from trailheads
3. Lakes at distance from camps
4. Any still water with upstream human activity (filter carefully)

### Keep your filter flowing:
- Backflush after every use before storage
- Store moist but not frozen (freezing destroys hollow fiber filters)
- If flow rate drops, backflush with more force or soak in warm water

### Carry a backup:
Even reliable filters clog, freeze, or fall in mud. Pack Aquatabs (chlorine tablets) as a backup purification option. Under 1 oz, treats 40 liters.

## FAQ

**Q: Is tap water safe enough to skip a filter while backpacking?**
A: No water source in the backcountry should be assumed safe without treatment, including streams that look clear. Giardia and Crypto cysts are invisible and can survive in cold, clear water.

**Q: How do I know when to replace my filter?**
A: Sawyer and similar hollow fiber filters have a 100,000-gallon rating — recreational hikers rarely reach this. The functional signal is flow rate dropping significantly even after backflushing. Most hikers replace filters every 2–5 years based on use.

**Q: Can I use a backpacking filter in the city during an emergency?**
A: A mechanical filter removes bacteria and protozoa from tap or floodwater. For utility water outages, this covers biological contamination. Chemical contamination (industrial runoff) is not removed by mechanical filters.

**Q: Does filtering add any chemical taste?**
A: No — mechanical filters are neutral tasting, unlike iodine tablets. Some users report that treated water from Katadyn BeFree has a slightly different taste due to the hollow fiber media, but this is minimal.
