## Why a Good Headlamp Matters

A headlamp is the most-used piece of camping gear after your sleeping bag and stove — and the most often bought as an afterthought. A cheap headlamp fails you at exactly the wrong moment: dim when you need it bright, battery dead at 2 a.m., uncomfortable on your head after two hours, fogged in the rain.

Quality headlamps deliver the three things that matter: **adequate brightness with good control** (not just a maximum lumen number, but real usability across dim, task, and flood settings), **battery reliability** (either long AAA runtime or fast rechargeable with accurate battery indicators), and **waterproofing** (IPX4 splash-proof is the minimum; IPX8 submersion resistance is better for real outdoor use).

## Buyer's Guide

### How Many Lumens Do You Actually Need?

- **50–150 lumens:** Camp tasks, reading, walking known terrain at night. Sufficient for most casual camping.
- **200–400 lumens:** Hiking on marked trails after dark, technical camp setup, climbing routes in low light. The sweet spot for most outdoor users.
- **400–750 lumens:** Trail running, off-trail navigation, group lighting. Clearly visible at 100+ meters.
- **750+ lumens:** High-speed trail running on unmarked terrain, technical mountaineering, search and rescue. Exceptional distance range, shorter battery life.

Most campers overestimate their lumen needs. 400 lumens is genuinely bright. Buy what you need, not the maximum spec.

### Battery Life vs. Peak Brightness

Peak lumen ratings are often measured for 30 seconds of "burst" mode — not sustained output. Check the runtime at the lumens you actually plan to use. A headlamp with 400 lm peak and 200 lm sustained is more useful than one with 1000 lm peak and a dead battery in an hour. Look for ANSI/FL1 standardized runtime graphs when researching — they show real-world brightness reduction over time.

### Waterproofing Ratings

- **IPX4:** Splash resistant — protected against rain and drips from any direction. Fine for most hiking and camping.
- **IPX6:** Protected against strong water jets. Heavy rain, stream crossings.
- **IPX7/8:** Submersion resistant at 1–1.5m. Necessary if you're swimming or crossing deep water while wearing the headlamp.

For most camping and hiking, IPX4 is adequate. Kayakers, canyoneers, and technical creek hikers should prioritize IPX7 or 8.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the best headlamp for running at night?**
Trail running needs high lumens (500+), a secure headband that doesn't bounce, and light weight. The BioLite HeadLamp 750 (2.4 oz, rear-balanced, 750 lm) and Fenix HM65R (1400 lm, IPX8, 3.5 oz) are the top two for running. The BioLite wins on comfort and weight; the Fenix wins on illumination distance.

**How long do AAA batteries last in a headlamp?**
At medium brightness (100–150 lumens), quality alkaline AAAs typically last 10–15 hours in a modern LED headlamp. At maximum output, runtime drops to 2–4 hours. In cold temperatures, lithium batteries significantly outperform alkalines — a critical consideration for winter and high-altitude hiking where alkalines can drop 50% capacity in cold conditions.

**Should I choose rechargeable or AAA-powered headlamps?**
Rechargeable headlamps are more economical and convenient for regular campers who remember to charge before trips. AAA-powered headlamps are better for expedition and emergency use where you can carry spare batteries and can't always recharge. The best solution for serious users is a headlamp like the Petzl Actik Core that supports both — rechargeable primary with AAA backup.
