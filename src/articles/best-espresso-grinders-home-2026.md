## Why Your Grinder Matters More Than Your Machine

Espresso extraction is sensitive to particle size distribution in ways filter coffee is not. At the 9-bar pressure of espresso:

- **Too coarse:** Water flows through too fast (under-extracted) — sour, watery shots
- **Too fine:** Water can't flow through uniformly (over-extracted) — bitter, harsh shots
- **Inconsistent particle distribution:** Part of the puck extracts correctly while another part over- or under-extracts — complex off-flavors that can't be fixed by adjusting one variable

Burr grinders crush coffee between two precision burrs, producing consistent particle sizes. Blade grinders chop randomly — producing everything from powder to chunky pieces in the same dose. You cannot make excellent espresso with a blade grinder.

## Key Specs to Evaluate

**Burr type:**
- *Flat burrs:* Two parallel circular burrs. Produce distinct, narrow particle distribution. The choice for specialty coffee shops and most home baristas who want maximum consistency.
- *Conical burrs:* Cone-shaped inner burr inside a ring burr. Wider particle distribution (some say adds complexity/sweetness). More forgiving to dial in for some. Less retention (grounds don't get stuck in the grinder chamber).

**Burr size:** Larger burrs run cooler (less heat damage to coffee) and grind faster. 64mm and up is the home premium tier; 83mm+ is commercial territory.

**Stepless vs. stepped adjustment:**
- *Stepless:* Infinite fine-tuning of grind size. Essential for espresso where a 0.1mm change in grind can shift extraction significantly. Required for serious home espresso.
- *Stepped:* Fixed click positions. Easier to return to a setting but limits micro-adjustment.

**Retention:** How many grams of coffee stay inside the grinder between doses. Low retention (<0.5g) means less waste and fresher doses. High retention grinders require flushing old grounds before each dose.

## Dialing In Your Espresso: A Quick Guide

Once you have a quality burr grinder:

1. **Start coarser than you think** — espresso grind is very fine; start in the ballpark to avoid immediately choking the machine
2. **Target shot time:** For a 1:2 ratio (18g coffee → 36g espresso), aim for 25–30 seconds extraction time
3. **Too fast (under 20 sec):** Grind finer
4. **Too slow (over 40 sec):** Grind coarser
5. **Adjust one variable at a time** — grind size OR dose, not both simultaneously
6. **Use a scale for dose and yield** — a 0.1g dose scale and a cup-catching scale are essential for reproducibility

## FAQ

**Q: Do I need a separate espresso grinder, or can I use my coffee grinder?**
A: If your grinder has a stepless adjustment and gets fine enough for espresso (Turkish grind fine), you may be able to use it. Most filter coffee grinders don't get fine enough or have enough adjustment resolution for espresso. An espresso-specific grinder is recommended.

**Q: Is a $170 espresso grinder good enough?**
A: The Baratza Encore ESP at $170 produces significantly better espresso than any blade grinder, and good enough for most beginners. You'll notice the limitation as your technique improves — the stepped adjustment becomes frustrating when you want to micro-adjust between settings.

**Q: Should I buy a manual espresso grinder?**
A: Manual grinders (like the Comandante or 1Zpresso) produce excellent grind quality for espresso at lower prices than equivalent electric grinders. The trade-off is physical effort (60+ turns per dose) and time. For those with physical limitations, electric is necessary.
