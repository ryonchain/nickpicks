## Buying Guide: What to Consider

### Keys vs. Pads vs. Both

**Keys only** (Komplete Kontrol M32, A61): Best for producers who primarily write melodic content — leads, chords, bass lines, classical-influenced productions.

**Keys + Pads** (MPK Mini, MiniLab 3): Best all-rounder for producers who do both — beat programming on pads, melody on keys.

**Pads only** (Launchpad X, Akai MPD): Best for producers whose primary workflow is beat programming, sampling, and clip launching.

### Mini Keys vs. Full-Size Keys

**Mini keys** are smaller than standard piano keys. Advantages: compact, portable, affordable. Disadvantages: cramped for chord playing, harder for pianists to use intuitively.

**Full-size keys** match standard piano dimensions. Advantages: comfortable chord playing, familiar feel for piano players. Disadvantages: larger, heavier, more expensive.

For a small desk, mini-key controllers are the practical choice. If you play piano or intend to play full chords, get full-size keys.

### Weighted vs. Semi-Weighted vs. Synth Action

- **Weighted** (Arturia KeyLab 88): Simulates acoustic piano key resistance via hammer mechanism. Best for pianists. Heaviest.
- **Semi-weighted** (Komplete Kontrol A61, KeyLab Essential 61): Spring resistance + some weight. Good balance of playability and portability.
- **Synth action** (MPK Mini, MiniLab 3, most budget controllers): Light, springy keys. No real weight. Fine for programming and melody; less expressive for performance.

### DAW Integration

Most controllers work with all DAWs via MIDI. But deep integration varies:
- **Ableton Live**: APC Key 25 MK2, Launchpad X (native support in Ableton)
- **Logic Pro**: Native Instruments Komplete Kontrol (deep integration via NKS protocol)
- **FL Studio**: Akai MPK series maps automatically
- **Universal**: Arturia MiniLab (Arturia Analog Lab bridges all DAWs)
