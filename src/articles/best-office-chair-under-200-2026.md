## What Actually Matters in an Office Chair

### Lumbar Support

The lower back (lumbar region) is where most desk workers develop chronic pain. Good lumbar support maintains the natural curve of your lower spine. Look for:
- Adjustable lumbar height (can be moved up or down to match your back)
- Adequate firmness (soft lumbar padding compresses quickly and loses effect)

Fixed lumbar positions are a compromise — they may or may not match your anatomy.

### Seat Height Range

Standard desk chairs adjust between 17-21 inches. If you're shorter (under 5'4") or taller (over 6'2"), check that the height range covers your seated position with feet flat on the floor.

### Seat Depth and Width

Most under-$200 chairs have a seat depth of 17-19 inches and width of 19-21 inches. If you're broader or have longer legs, check these measurements — narrow seats or too-short depth are uncomfortable within hours.

### Mesh vs. Foam Seat

**Mesh back:** Standard in ergonomic chairs. Breathes well, doesn't trap heat.

**Foam/cushioned seat:** More comfortable initially. Better long-term for prolonged sitting (3+ hours) versus mesh seats, which can develop pressure points.

**Best:** Mesh back + foam or well-padded seat.

## When to Spend More

**Spend up to $400** if you sit 8+ hours daily in your primary work chair. The jump from $200 to $300-400 brings synchronous tilt, better lumbar adjustment systems, and 5-year warranties that matter for daily professional use.

**Consider refurbished Herman Miller or Steelcase** (~$400-600 refurbished). A used Aeron or Leap V2 in good condition beats any $200 chair and often includes manufacturer warranties. Check office liquidation sales or eBay from reputable sellers.
