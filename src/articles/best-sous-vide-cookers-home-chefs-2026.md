## Sous Vide Temperature Guide

The biggest advantage of sous vide is precision. These are the target temperatures for common proteins:

| Protein | Doneness | Temperature | Time |
|---------|----------|-------------|------|
| Steak (1" thick) | Rare | 125°F / 52°C | 1–2.5 hrs |
| Steak (1" thick) | Medium Rare | 130°F / 54°C | 1–4 hrs |
| Steak (1" thick) | Medium | 140°F / 60°C | 1–4 hrs |
| Chicken breast | Juicy + safe | 145°F / 63°C | 1.5–4 hrs |
| Pork tenderloin | Perfect | 140°F / 60°C | 1–4 hrs |
| Salmon fillet | Silky | 122°F / 50°C | 30–45 min |
| Soft-boiled eggs | Custardy | 167°F / 75°C | 13 min |
| Pasteurized eggs | Runny yolk | 135°F / 57°C | 75 min |

**Note:** Always sear proteins after sous vide for color and crust. Pat dry first, then 90 seconds per side in a very hot cast iron pan.

## What You Need to Get Started

### Container
A 12-quart Cambro polycarbonate container is the standard home sous vide vessel. The clear walls let you see the food, the wide opening fits most circulators, and it handles temperatures up to 212°F. A lid with a cutout for the circulator reduces evaporation on long cooks.

### Bags
Zip-lock freezer bags work well using the water displacement method: seal the bag except one corner, lower it into the water until the air is pushed out, then seal. For serious cooks, a vacuum sealer like the FoodSaver FM2000 removes air completely and prevents floating.

### Searing Setup
A cast iron skillet on high heat, a torch, or a grill at maximum temperature. The goal is Maillard browning in the shortest time possible — 60–90 seconds per side — without raising the core temperature.
