## Telescope Types Explained

### Refractor (Lens)
- Uses lenses to focus light
- Good for: Moon, planets, bright star clusters
- Limitation: Chromatic aberration (color fringing) on cheaper models
- Maintenance: Sealed optics, no collimation needed
- Best for: Visual purity in daytime/casual use

### Reflector (Mirror)
- Uses mirrors to focus light (Newtonian design)
- Good for: Planets, galaxies, nebulae—excellent light-gathering
- Limitation: Requires occasional collimation (alignment); open tube
- Maintenance: Low; mirrors need occasional realignment
- Best for: Best bang-for-aperture in beginner astronomy

### Schmidt-Cassegrain (SCT)
- Hybrid design; compact for the aperture
- Good for: All-purpose astronomy, astrophotography
- Limitation: Highest cost per aperture; complex for true beginners
- Best for: Serious amateurs who want compact, versatile, long-term option

### Aperture Rule
Aperture (diameter of the objective lens or mirror) determines how much light you collect. More aperture = fainter objects visible = more detail on bright ones. The priority sequence: aperture first, then mount quality, then everything else.

## Buying Mistakes to Avoid

1. **"High magnification" claims:** Ignore magnification marketing. Aperture matters; magnification is secondary and changeable with eyepieces.
2. **Cheap plastic mounts:** A wobbly mount ruins every observation. Spend on mount quality.
3. **Department store telescopes:** Any telescope under $80 at a department store is likely to disappoint.
4. **Ignoring used equipment:** The Orion SpaceProbe 130ST and Celestron NexStar frequently appear on Craigslist and Facebook Marketplace at 50–70% of retail. Optics don't wear out.
