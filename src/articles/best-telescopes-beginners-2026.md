## Before You Buy: The Most Common Mistake

**Don't buy by magnification.** "450x power!" claims on $30 telescopes are marketing nonsense. A small lens at high magnification shows a blurry, useless image. Aperture (lens/mirror diameter) is what matters — more light gathered = brighter, clearer images.

## Buying Guide

### Aperture: The Only Spec That Matters

| Aperture | What You'll See |
|---------|----------------|
| 60–70mm | Moon details, Saturn's rings, Jupiter's moons |
| 80–100mm | Cloud bands on Jupiter, Saturn's Cassini division |
| 114–130mm | Mars features, some nebulae, star clusters |
| 150–200mm+ | Galaxies, nebulae details, excellent planetary |

Rule of thumb: more aperture is always better. Budget accordingly.

### Mount Types for Beginners

**Alt-Azimuth (easiest):** Moves up-down and left-right. Simplest to use. Objects drift out of view as Earth rotates — nudge to recenter. Best for beginners.

**Equatorial:** Aligns with Earth's rotation axis. One-axis tracking keeps objects centered with a single knob. Requires polar alignment setup. Better for serious use.

**GoTo computerized:** Motor-driven, finds objects automatically. Most convenient but most expensive and requires alignment.

### Essential Accessories to Buy

- **Extra eyepieces:** Most telescopes come with 1–2. A set (6mm, 10mm, 25mm) dramatically expands magnification range. ~$30–80 for quality set.
- **Red flashlight:** Preserves night vision while consulting star charts.
- **Smartphone mount:** Photograph what you see through the eyepiece.
- **Stellarium app (free):** Best free planetarium app for finding objects.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What can I see with a beginner telescope?**
Moon (craters, mountains, valleys), Saturn (rings, Titan moon), Jupiter (cloud bands, 4 large moons), Mars (polar ice cap when close), Venus (phases), star clusters, and bright nebulae.

**Do I need dark skies?**
Dark skies dramatically improve deep-sky viewing (nebulae, galaxies). The Moon, planets, and star clusters are fine from suburbs.

**Is astronomy hard to learn?**
The sky navigation learning curve is real but short. With a star chart app and 2–3 sessions, you'll learn to find major objects. GoTo mounts skip this entirely.
