Percussion therapy works by delivering rapid, targeted pressure deep into muscle tissue — reducing delayed onset muscle soreness (DOMS), improving blood flow, and reducing recovery time between training sessions. But not all massage guns are equal. The meaningful differences are amplitude (depth of stroke), stall force (pressure you can apply before the motor slows), and whether the device actually reaches the muscle depth needed.

Marketing obscures these specs constantly. Here's what to actually look for.

---

## Key Specs That Actually Matter

**Amplitude (mm):** The depth of each percussion stroke. Standard is 10–12mm. Deep tissue therapy starts at 14mm. Theragun's 16mm is industry-leading. If an ad doesn't list amplitude, be skeptical.

**Stall force (lbs):** How much pressure you can press into a muscle before the motor slows. Under 30 lbs is fine for light use. 40–60 lbs is needed for dense muscle groups (glutes, quads, lats).

**RPM range:** Percussion speed measured in revolutions per minute. Most devices run 1,750–3,200 RPM. Variable speed matters for different muscle groups.

**Noise (dB):** Loud motors are friction-inefficient and signal cheaper motors. Premium guns run 45–65 dB (quiet conversation to moderate). Budget guns can hit 80+ dB (power tools).

## When to See a Physical Therapist Instead

Massage guns are recovery tools, not medical devices. Acute injuries, nerve pain, joint pain, and post-surgical recovery require professional assessment. Percussion directly over injured tissue, joints, or the spine should be avoided. When in doubt, consult a physical therapist before adding percussion therapy to a recovery protocol.
