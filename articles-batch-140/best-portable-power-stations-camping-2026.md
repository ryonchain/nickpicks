## Do You Actually Need a Power Station at Campsites?

Power stations earn their keep in several camping scenarios. For campers with electric coolers or 12V fridges, a power station extends run time significantly compared to car batteries. For those who rely on CPAP machines, a power station is a medical necessity. For remote work or digital nomad camping, it powers laptops reliably. And for families with kids, it charges enough devices to maintain sanity on multi-day trips without hunting for campground electric hookups.

The calculus is simpler than the spec sheets suggest: **capacity (Wh)** tells you how much energy you have. A 500Wh unit can charge a smartphone roughly 40+ times, run LED camp lights for days, or power a 40W 12V cooler for about 12 hours. If that's enough for your trip, buy accordingly.

## Buyer's Guide

### How Much Capacity Do You Actually Need for Camping?

Run this quick calculation: add up the wattage of everything you'll run and multiply by hours of use. A smartphone charges at ~18W and takes ~1.5 hours: 27Wh per charge. A 12V cooler draws ~40W continuously: 960Wh per 24 hours. A CPAP machine without humidifier draws ~30–60W per night: 270–540Wh. LED camp lights draw 10–20W for 4 hours: 40–80Wh.

For phone charging + LED lighting over a 2-night trip, 200Wh is adequate. Add a CPAP and you need 500+Wh. Add a 12V fridge and you're in the 1000+Wh territory.

### Solar Charging at Camp: Is It Worth It?

Solar makes sense on trips of 3+ days in sunny climates where you want power independence. The math is simple: a 100W panel charges at roughly 70–80W of real-world output, averaging 5–6 peak sun hours per day = 350–480Wh per day. Match your unit's solar input capacity to your panel output for fastest charging. For weekend camping, a quick wall charge before you leave is more reliable and faster than solar — solar earns its value on extended trips.

### AC vs. DC Output: What Matters for Camping

Most camping devices run on DC — your phone, LED lights, 12V fridge. If you specifically need to run AC-only devices (CPAP, laptop with AC adapter, kitchen appliances), an inverter is required. All units on this list have built-in inverters for AC output. AC output runs warm, is less efficient than DC output, and draws more from the battery — use DC output whenever your device supports it to extend runtime.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can a portable power station run a CPAP machine?**
Yes — most CPAP machines draw 30–60W per night without humidifier (the humidifier can triple power draw). A 300Wh unit will run a standard CPAP for one night. A 500Wh unit covers two nights. Always check your specific CPAP's power requirements and use the 12V DC adapter if your machine supports it — DC is more efficient than AC through an inverter.

**Can I charge a power station while using it?**
Yes, most portable power stations support pass-through charging — you can use devices while the unit charges via AC or solar. Check your unit's manual for limitations, as some units restrict pass-through to prevent battery damage.

**How do I safely use a power station in a tent?**
Portable power stations are safe to use inside a tent — they produce no fumes and no combustion risk. Keep them upright, away from direct moisture (rain, condensation), and ensure adequate ventilation to prevent heat buildup during high-load use. Do not charge via AC inside a tent due to cord trip hazard — charge at the car or campsite table, then carry in for overnight use.
