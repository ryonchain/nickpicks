## Why Use Trekking Poles?

Research consistently shows that trekking poles reduce joint stress — particularly in the knees — on downhill terrain. Studies report 20–25% reduction in lower extremity muscle fatigue with pole use on descents. On ascents, poles engage your upper body and distribute load more evenly. For hikers with existing knee issues, poles are often the difference between comfortable and painful miles.

Beyond joint protection, poles improve balance on wet rocks, stream crossings, and loose terrain. They extend your reach on tricky steps and serve double duty as tent poles for ultralight shelters.

The main trade-off is that poles require hand use — some hikers find them intrusive on easy terrain and prefer to carry them collapsible most of the time. The best poles are the ones that disappear into your rhythm when you need them and collapse small when you don't.

## Buyer's Guide

### Adjustable vs. Fixed-Length Poles

Adjustable poles (twist-lock or lever-lock) let you shorten poles for uphill sections (plant closer, more power) and lengthen for downhill (more forward reach for balance). This is meaningful on technical terrain with significant elevation change. Fixed-length poles (like the Black Diamond Distance Z) are lighter, simpler, and faster to deploy — the right call for trail running and fastpacking where you're moving at consistent pace and terrain.

For most hikers, adjustable is better. Set your poles to approximately elbow height when your arm is at 90°, then shorten 5–10cm for steep climbs and lengthen 5–10cm for steep descents.

### Aluminum vs. Carbon Fiber

**Aluminum** is more durable against impact — it dents rather than shatters. For rocky terrain, scrambling, and heavy use, aluminum handles abuse better. It is heavier (typically 20–24 oz vs. 10–16 oz per pair for carbon) and vibrates more on hard surfaces.

**Carbon fiber** is lighter, vibrates less (absorbs trail shock better), and feels more refined. It is more brittle — a sharp lateral impact on a rock can crack the shaft. For hikers who are careful with their gear and prioritize weight, carbon is worth it.

### Grip Materials

**Cork** is the traditional premium grip — absorbs sweat, molds to your hand over time, warm in cold weather. Best for multi-day hiking where hand fatigue accumulates. **Foam/EVA** is used in lighter and trail-running poles — quick-drying, sweat-wicking, comfortable for shorter high-intensity use. **Rubber** handles cold temperatures well but causes blisters on long hikes — mostly found on winter or budget poles.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What length trekking poles do I need?**
Set length to a starting point of elbow height when your arm is at 90° at your side. For most hikers this is between 110–130cm. Adjust down 5–10cm on steep uphill and up 5–10cm on steep downhill for optimal power transfer. If buying fixed-length poles, measure your elbow height standing on flat ground and choose the nearest available size.

**Can I fly with trekking poles as carry-on?**
Trekking poles are prohibited in carry-on bags by TSA — the sharp tips classify them as prohibited items. They must be checked. Most airlines classify them as sports equipment under standard baggage rules. If you need poles at your destination, purchase at an outdoor retailer there or ship ahead. Foldable poles at 13–15 inches are still too long for carry-on per TSA policy, but their short length makes checking them easier.

**How do I replace trekking pole tips?**
Tips thread on or press on depending on the brand. Most manufacturers sell replacement carbide tip sets for $8–$15. Tips typically last 400–800 miles of use depending on terrain. Replace when you notice the tip is worn smooth — you'll feel the difference on rock surfaces where the bite is gone.
