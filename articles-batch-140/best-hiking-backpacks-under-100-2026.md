## What to Look for in a Hiking Backpack

The single most important feature in any hiking backpack is **fit**. A poorly fitting pack makes a 5-mile hike miserable regardless of price. Look for adjustable torso length, a hip belt that sits on your iliac crest (hip bones), and shoulder straps that follow your shoulders without pinching. Many budget packs skip torso length adjustment — this is a real limitation for people who aren't average height.

**Capacity** is measured in liters. For day hiking, 20–30L is appropriate. For overnight trips, 40–55L fits a sleeping bag, pad, and 2–3 days of food and gear. For a week or longer, you need 55–70L or more.

**Frame type** matters for loads over 20 lbs. Frameless packs save weight but transfer load poorly. Internal frame packs (most backpacks you'll find) have aluminum stays or a frame sheet that transfers weight to your hips — critical for multi-day loads.

## Buyer's Guide

### How to Size a Hiking Backpack

Measure your torso length — from the C7 vertebra at the base of your neck to the iliac crest of your hip — while sitting upright. This is the primary sizing dimension, not your height. Most packs come in XS/S/M/L torso sizes. Incorrect torso fit is the single most common reason hikers find packs uncomfortable.

The hip belt should wrap around your hip bones (iliac crest), with the shoulder straps following the curve of your shoulders without gaps. The load lifter straps (at the top of the shoulder straps) should angle at roughly 45° when adjusted correctly.

### Pack Capacity Guide

- **15–25L:** Day hiking, trail running
- **30–45L:** Overnight trips, ultralight multi-day
- **50–65L:** Standard 2–5 night backpacking
- **65L+:** Extended trips, family camping with gear for kids

### What Features to Skip vs. Keep

Features worth paying for: suspension system quality (aluminum stays vs. fiberglass vs. none makes a real difference), hip belt padding, breathable back panel, hydration compatibility, and external gear loops for trekking poles and ice axes. Features that add weight and cost without meaningful benefit for most hikers: integrated rain covers (pack covers are lighter and cheaper to buy separately), excessive zipper pockets, and proprietary "smart" features.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Do I need a hip belt for day hiking?**
For loads under 15 lbs, a hip belt provides comfort but isn't essential. Above 15 lbs, a hip belt is important — it transfers pack weight from your shoulders to your stronger hips and core, dramatically reducing fatigue on long hikes. Always cinch the hip belt first when putting on a loaded pack.

**What is the best hiking backpack brand for beginners?**
For beginners on a budget, Teton Sports and Kelty offer the best value. For beginners willing to spend up to $100, Osprey is the gold standard for fit and durability. Osprey's sizing assistance and All Mighty Guarantee make it the best choice if you're uncertain about fit.

**Can I bring a hiking backpack as carry-on luggage?**
A 20–25L day pack almost always qualifies as a personal item or small carry-on. Larger backpacking packs (40L+) typically don't fit in overhead bins and must be checked. Roll-top access and panel-loader packs are easier to pack efficiently for carry-on use.
