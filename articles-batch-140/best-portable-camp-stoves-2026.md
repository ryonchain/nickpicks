## Propane vs. Canister vs. Multi-Fuel: Which Stove Type Do You Need?

The three main fuel types for camping stoves each fit different situations.

**Propane (canister, often 1 lb green Coleman canisters)** is the go-to for car camping and large base camp stoves. Propane burns hot, is widely available at gas stations and hardware stores, and powers two-burner stoves that let you cook real meals. Downside: the canisters are heavy and bulky for backpacking.

**Isobutane-propane canisters** (MSR, Jetboil, Snow Peak) are the backpacker's choice. Small, lightweight threaded canisters feed compact stoves that boil water fast. Performance drops in cold temperatures — below 20°F these stoves struggle. Canisters are available at outdoor shops, less common in rural gas stations.

**Multi-fuel stoves** (MSR WhisperLite) burn white gas, diesel, and other liquid fuels — ideal for international expeditions and extreme cold. They require priming and more maintenance, so they're overkill for domestic camping.

## Buyer's Guide

### Backpacking vs. Car Camping Stoves

If you're backpacking, weight is the priority. Canister stoves (MSR PocketRocket 2, Jetboil Flash) are the only practical options — propane stoves weigh 10–12 lbs before fuel. If you're car camping, weight is irrelevant: prioritize BTU output, two-burner capacity, and wind protection.

### Fuel Availability

Before any trip, confirm your fuel source is available at your destination. Isobutane canisters are stocked at REI, outdoor retailers, and some sporting goods stores, but not every rural gas station. Propane 1 lb green canisters are the most universally available camping fuel — most large gas stations, Walmart, and hardware stores carry them. Bulk propane (20 lb tank) is cheapest per BTU and available at most hardware stores; requires a hose adapter.

### BTU Output and What It Means

BTU (British Thermal Unit) measures heat output. For reference, a typical kitchen gas stove burner is about 10,000–15,000 BTU. For camping, 10,000 BTU per burner is sufficient for most cooking. 20,000 BTU gives you much faster boil times and more heat for searing or high-heat cooking. Ultralight canister stoves run 7,000–10,000 BTU, which is enough for boiling water and simple meals.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What fuel do I need for an MSR PocketRocket?**
The PocketRocket 2 uses threaded isobutane-propane fuel canisters. Common compatible brands include MSR IsoPro, Jetboil Jetpower, Primus Power Gas, and Snow Peak GigaPower. Use only these threaded canister types — not the Coleman green 1 lb propane canisters, which use a different valve system.

**Can I use a camp stove inside a tent?**
No. Never use a fuel-burning stove inside a tent or enclosed shelter. Carbon monoxide poisoning is a serious risk, and the open flame creates a fire hazard. Always cook outside or in a well-ventilated shelter. Some people use stoves in tent vestibules, but this is still risky — CO builds up faster than expected. Use a camp stove on a stable surface at least 3 feet from any flammable material.

**How long does a 1 lb propane canister last?**
On a two-burner propane stove at medium heat, a 1 lb canister typically lasts about 1–2 hours of cooking time. For a weekend of camping with moderate cooking, plan for 2–3 canisters or use a larger bulk tank with an adapter hose.
