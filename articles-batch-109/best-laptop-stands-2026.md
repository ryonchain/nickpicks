# Best Laptop Stands 2026

Using a laptop without a stand puts your screen roughly 8–12 inches below eye level. That forces you to crane your neck forward and down, loading the cervical spine with what biomechanists call "tech neck" — 10–12 lbs of additional force per inch of forward head position. Over years, this causes real structural damage.

A laptop stand that raises your screen to eye level costs $20–$150 and fixes this completely. It's one of the most ergonomic improvements you can make to your workspace.

## What You Need Alongside a Laptop Stand

**External keyboard and mouse:** Once your laptop is at eye level, the built-in keyboard and trackpad are too high to use comfortably. An external keyboard ($30–$100) and mouse ($20–$60) complete the ergonomic setup. Budget: add $50–$100 total.

**If using a clamshell setup:** Laptop lid closed, laptop acting as a second monitor or just powering external displays. The stand just needs to hold the laptop securely at a convenient angle; height doesn't matter as much.

**For dual-display work:** Laptop at eye level with an external monitor beside it — or laptop in clamshell with one larger external monitor.

---

## What Makes a Good Laptop Stand

**Height range:** Your laptop screen should reach eye level when sitting upright. Most people need 10–15 inches of elevation from desk surface. Check the stand's maximum height.

**Angle vs height:** Some stands only adjust angle (tilt), not height — useful for typing-angle improvement but not for eye-level elevation. For true ergonomics, you need height adjustment.

**Stability:** A stand that vibrates when you type or slides when adjusting is a daily frustration. Look for rubber pads, wide base, and solid locking mechanisms.

**Laptop compatibility:** Width — can it fit your 16" MacBook Pro? Weight capacity — most stands handle 2–8 lbs; ultrabooks are light, some pro laptops are heavier.

**Portability:** Foldable stands for bag carry vs heavier solid stands for permanent desk setups.

3. External mouse: $20–$60

This eliminates tech neck, reduces wrist strain (keyboard at proper height), and improves posture more than any other single workspace change.

**Monitor position:** Top of screen at eye level, screen 20–28" from your face (arm's length). Tilt screen back 10–20° so the top of the screen is slightly farther away than the bottom.

**Keyboard position:** Elbows at 90–110°, wrists neutral (not bent up or down), shoulders relaxed. If the keyboard is too high (desk too high for your chair), add a keyboard tray.

## For MacBook Users: Special Considerations

**USB-C hub requirement:** MacBook Air and Pro (M-series) have 2–3 USB-C/Thunderbolt ports. To use an external monitor, keyboard, and mouse simultaneously, you need a USB-C hub or Thunderbolt dock ($30–$150).

**Clamshell mode:** MacBook can drive an external monitor with lid closed. The stand holds it at a convenient angle. Requires the MacBook to be connected to power when in clamshell.

**Aluminum scratch risk:** MacBook's aluminum body scratches. Only use stands with silicone or rubber contact pads — not bare metal or plastic.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**What height should my laptop screen be at?**
Eye level or slightly below when sitting with correct posture. Measure from your desk surface to your eyes — that's roughly the height the top of your screen should reach.

**Do I need a laptop stand if I have a monitor arm?**
If your laptop is in clamshell mode (lid closed, external display only), no. If your laptop is your only display and you want it at eye level, yes.

**Will a laptop stand affect my laptop's temperature?**
Raising a laptop at an angle improves airflow to the bottom vents — typically 5–10°C cooler under load. The Amazon Basics ventilated stand goes further with a mesh surface. Either way, a stand almost always improves thermals.
